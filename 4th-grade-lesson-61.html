üßê Part 1: Before You Read

In Oz, we followed a girl swept away from home. In Treasure Island, we followed a boy dragged into adventure. Now we are following a DOG ripped from everything he knows. Notice the pattern: all three stories start with a character losing their world.


Think about this: Can an animal be the main character of a serious story? Can we understand what an animal feels even though they cannot talk?


What to look for:

  • How does London describe Buck‚Äôs life BEFORE he is stolen?
  • What happens to Buck in this chapter? How is he treated?
  • How does London show us what Buck is thinking and feeling without dialogue?

üìñ Part 2: Today‚Äôs Task

Open your digital copy of The Call of the Wild and read Chapter 1: Into the Primitive.


Warning: this chapter is intense. Buck is kidnapped, beaten, and shipped north in a cage. Jack London does not sugarcoat anything. This book respects you enough to show you the truth about how the world can be.


Reading tip: London uses THIRD-PERSON LIMITED POV. That means the narrator is outside the story (uses he not I) but stays close to Buck‚Äôs thoughts and feelings. It is like a camera hovering right over Buck‚Äôs shoulder.

üí¨ Part 3: Word of the Day

primitive (adjective/noun)

Relating to the earliest, most basic state of something. Wild, uncivilized, raw. The opposite of tame or civilized.


From the story: The chapter title is Into the Primitive. Buck is being dragged out of his comfortable, civilized life and into a primitive, wild world where only the strong survive.


Think about it: What does it mean to go from civilized to primitive? Is primitive always bad, or can it also mean powerful and free?
