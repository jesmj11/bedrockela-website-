Midas and the Golden Touch

A Tale from Ancient Greek Mythology

King Midas was not a bad man.

He was friendly and generous and well-liked by his people, and his one weakness — a love of gold, its color and weight and the way it caught light — was, as weaknesses go, not the most dangerous kind. He had a treasury full of it and was always happy to see more.

One morning his servants brought him an old man they'd found asleep in the royal garden. He was peculiar-looking — round red face, vine leaves tangled in his hair, the unmistakable air of someone who had been having an excellent party somewhere and wandered off. They'd tied him up, not knowing what else to do.

Midas looked at him and recognized him immediately: Silenus, the companion and old tutor of Dionysus, the god of wine and celebration. He freed the old man at once, drew him a warm bath, had a proper meal prepared, and entertained him cheerfully for ten days before sending him safely back to Dionysus.

📚 Reading (Part 4 of 4)

Midas and the Golden Touch

He was the richest man who ever lived for about four hours. He was much happier after he stopped being that.

The Greeks told the story of Midas not as a warning against greed exactly — Midas wasn't greedy in a cruel way, just enthusiastic about something in a direction that happened to be wrong. They told it as a reminder that getting what you think you want has consequences you cannot fully imagine in the moment of wanting it.

They also pointed out that his daughter came back.

The gold in the river stayed. That part didn't undo itself.
