🎯 Vocabulary Practice

augury

The Roman practice of reading signs from the gods — especially the flight of birds — to determine the right course of action.

sacred

Set apart as holy — belonging to the gods, not to be violated or treated as ordinary.

heir

The person who is next in line to receive a title, throne, or inheritance.

Romulus and Remus

Romulus and Remus A Tale from Roman Mythology Rome's symbol is a wolf. Not an eagle, not a lion, not a sun — a wolf, nursing two human boys. The Romans put her on their coins and their standards and their monuments, and they were proud of her, and they wanted you to understand what it meant: Rome came from something wild and fierce and dangerous, and it never entirely forgot that. Here is the story. A king named Numitor ruled the city of Alba Longa, in the hills south of where Rome would one day stand. His brother Amulius wanted the throne. He took it. To make sure no heir of Numitor's could challenge him, he forced Numitor's daughter Rhea Silvia into the priesthood — made her a Vestal Virgin, sworn to a life without marriage or children. The god Mars had other plans. Rhea Silvia gave birth to twin boys. She said their father was Mars. Amulius had them placed in a basket and set in the Tiber River to drown. The basket floated. It came to rest at the foot of a fig tree on a hillside — the Ficus Ruminalis, the tree the Romans would mark as sacred for centuries. A she-wolf found it there. She had recently lost her own cubs and was full of milk. She looked at the two babies and did what the situation required: she fed them and kept them warm. A woodpecker brought food. Both the wolf and the woodpecker were sacred to Mars. The god, the Romans said, was watching. A shepherd named Faustulus found them eventually and brought them home to his wife. They raised the boys as their own. Their names were Romulus and Remus. The boys grew up strong and restless and good at being in charge, which is a quality that makes life interesting when you don't yet know who you are. They led bands of young men through the hills, cleared bandits from the shepherd roads, protected the flocks. Remus was captured once in a raid and brought before the old displaced king Numitor. Something about the young man's bearing made Numitor ask careful questions. Faustulus arrived with the truth. The brothers united with their grandfather, overthrew Amulius, and restored Numitor to his throne. Then they went to build their own city. They were not made for other men's kingdoms. They chose the seven hills along the Tiber and disagreed immediately about which one. They settled it by augury — watching the flight of birds for a sign from the gods. Remus saw six vultures from the Aventine Hill. Romulus saw twelve from the Palatine. Twelve was more. Romulus began to mark out his city's boundary, digging the sacred trench, turning up the first soil, building the first wall. Remus mocked the wall. It was small, he said. He jumped over it. The Romans told this moment several ways. In some versions it is Romulus who kills him, furious at the insult to the sacred boundary. In others it is one of Romulus's men who strikes first. In all of them, Remus dies, and Romulus is responsible. He grieved. The Romans were careful to tell you this — they did not make him a monster or pretend he felt nothing. He buried his brother with full honors and named a gate of the new city after him. Then he built the city. He called it Rome, after himself. He built its walls and its institutions and its first laws. He invited anyone who needed a fresh start — fugitives, exiles, runaways, people with nothing and nowhere else to go — to come and be Roman. Rome would take anyone willing to work and fight and belong to it. This was Rome's first policy, and in many ways its most important one. The city that conquered the world began as a place for people nobody else wanted. The wolf nursed them. That was the beginning. Everything else was built on that — on the idea that something wild and fierce had looked at two abandoned children and decided they were worth saving. The Romans never forgot where they came from. They put the wolf on everything to make sure no one else forgot either.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

1. Romulus killed his brother over a boundary violation and then grieved and buried him with full honors. Do you think grief changes the meaning of what he did — or does grief not change what something was? Can you mourn someone and still have caused their death?

2. Rome began as a city for people nobody else wanted — fugitives, exiles, runaways. What does it mean to build a national identity on that foundation? How is that different from a nation built on heritage or bloodline? What might it mean for how Rome thought about citizenship?

3. The she-wolf nursing the twins is Rome's most famous image. The Romans chose that image deliberately — something wild and dangerous being the thing that saved Rome's founders. What does it say about a civilization that its founding myth centers on being fed by a predator?

📰 Informational Text

Connect Myth to Reality

The She-Wolf Symbol of Rome

The Capitoline Wolf statue in Rome (showing a wolf nursing Romulus and Remus) is one of the most famous symbols of the city. The wolf part dates to ancient times, though the twins were added during the Renaissance.

Wolves were sacred to Mars, the god of war and father of Romulus. Romans saw wolves as symbols of strength, courage, and survival - perfect for a city built by warriors.

The phrase 'raised by wolves' comes from this story and means someone who grew up tough, wild, and able to survive anything. It became a metaphor for Roman toughness and military prowess.

Archaeological evidence shows that Romans really did revere wolves. Wolf imagery appears on coins, armor, shields, and monuments throughout Roman history. The founding myth shaped Roman identity for over 1,000 years.

💡 The Lesson

Foundation myths matter because they tell us what a culture values. Rome chose a story about abandoned twins raised by a wolf and founded through violence - this reflects Roman values of toughness, resilience, and military strength.
