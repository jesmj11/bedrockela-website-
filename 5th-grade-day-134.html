Cupid and Psyche

Cupid and Psyche A Tale from Roman Mythology There was once a king with three daughters, and the youngest was so beautiful that people began neglecting the shrines of Venus and worshipping Psyche instead. Venus noticed. She sent her son Cupid — the god of love, winged and armed with arrows that caused whoever they struck to fall irresistibly in love — to find Psyche and make her fall in love with the most wretched creature alive. This was meant as a punishment for the girl's beauty drawing away worship that belonged to a goddess. But when Cupid saw Psyche, he scratched himself with his own arrow — by accident, or by choice; the story is deliberately ambiguous — and fell in love with her instead. Psyche's family received a terrifying oracle: she was destined to marry a creature that even the gods feared. She was to be dressed as a bride and left on a mountain and wait. The family wept and carried out the oracle. She stood on the mountaintop alone. The west wind Zephyr came softly and carried her down to a valley where a palace stood — built by divine hands, attended by invisible servants, furnished with everything a person could want. A voice came to her in the dark each night: her husband, who told her she could have everything except one thing. She must never see his face. Psyche was happy and also, eventually, lonely. She asked if her sisters could visit. Her husband warned her quietly that this was a bad idea. She insisted. The sisters came, saw the palace and the invisible servants and the extraordinary life, and were consumed by envy dressed as concern. Her husband must be a monster, they said — why else would he hide? They urged her urgently to take a lamp and a knife to bed that night. She did. The lamp showed her Cupid — not a monster but the most beautiful being she had ever seen, golden-winged, peacefully asleep. The lamp shook in her hands. A drop of hot oil fell on his shoulder. He woke. He saw the lamp and the knife. He looked at her for a moment and said nothing. He left. The palace vanished. Psyche was alone in a field with her life dissolved around her. She went to Venus directly and asked what she had to do. Venus, who was still angry, gave her four tasks, each impossible. Sort an enormous pile of mixed seeds by morning. Psyche wept, and ants sorted the seeds for her. Gather golden wool from violent sheep. Psyche wept, and a reed by the river told her to wait until the sheep slept and gather the wool from the thorns. Fill a crystal vessel from an unreachable waterfall guarded by dragons. Psyche wept, and Jupiter's eagle took the vessel and filled it for her. Go to the underworld and bring back a box of Proserpina's beauty. Psyche did not weep at this one. She went. She did everything she was told to do in the underworld — the instructions had been specific — until the very last moment, on the way back out, when she opened the box. Deadly sleep fell over her like a stone. Cupid found her on the road. He woke her. He went to Jupiter and asked permission to make Psyche immortal. Jupiter agreed — and took the opportunity to settle the whole affair properly, making the marriage official and giving Venus grandchildren to be pleased about. Psyche was made a goddess. She and Cupid were married on Olympus. Her name means soul. His means love. The Romans, who thought carefully about what stories meant, were deliberate about this: the story of Cupid and Psyche is the story of love and the soul finding each other — and the point is that the finding requires going through everything. The tasks, the mistakes, the impossible journey to the underworld and back. Love doesn't come to you. You go to it. And you have to be worth the trip.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

1. Psyche opened the box from the underworld at the very last moment, after completing every other impossible task. The story doesn't fully explain why — curiosity? exhaustion? something else? Why do you think she did it? What is it about forbidden things that holds on even when you've come so far?

2. Cupid's name means love; Psyche's name means soul. The Romans told their story as the story of love and soul finding each other, but through ordeals, not instantly. What do you think the Romans believed love required in order to be real? Is there something about difficulty that the story says love needs?

3. Venus was angry because people were worshipping Psyche's beauty instead of hers. She used that anger to design tasks that ended up making Psyche divine. Did Venus's punishment accomplish what she intended — or did it accomplish something else entirely? Can an attempt to harm someone accidentally make them stronger?

✨ Examples:

SIMPLE: Rome conquered.

COMPOUND: Rome conquered, and Greece submitted.

COMPLEX: Although Greece fought, Rome conquered.
