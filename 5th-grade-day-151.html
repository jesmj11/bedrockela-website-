A Tale from After Baldur died, everything in Asgard became quieter. Not peaceful-quiet. The other kind.

The gods knew who was responsible. Not Hodr, blind Hodr, who had thrown the dart not knowing what it was — Hodr was grief-stricken and blameless. It was Loki who had found the mistletoe. It was Loki who had guided the hand. It was Loki, in the cave, who had refused to weep.

Loki disappeared.

He had been around long enough to know what was coming, and he was fast enough to get a head start. He built a house in the mountains with four doors facing four directions so he could always see who was approaching. During the days he turned himself into a salmon and hid in a waterfall. At night he sat by the fire and worked on a fishing net, thinking about how the gods might try to catch him.

He looked up one night and saw torches on the distant hills.

The Death of Baldur and Loki's Punishment

Loki turned back into himself and looked at the gods standing around him on the riverbank and knew that there was nowhere left to go.

They bound him in a cave beneath the earth. The bonds were made from the entrails of his own son — transformed into iron — and they would not break. They positioned him over two sharp stones. Above him, fixed to the ceiling of the cave, a serpent dripped venom onto his face.

His wife Sigyn was there. Nobody asked her to stay. She didn't leave. She found a bowl and held it above his face, catching the drops. She had been there ever since. She would be there until the end of everything.

When the bowl filled up and she had to step away to empty it, the venom hit Loki's face. The pain of it was enormous. He thrashed against the chains and the earth shook — all of Midgard shaking from the movement of a bound god in a cave beneath it.

That is what earthquakes are, the Norse said. That is what they always were.

The gods knew all of this. Some of them felt something complicated about it — a being who had been their companion, their problem-solver, their impossible wildcard, now under the earth in the dark. A being who had done something unforgivable. Both of these things were true at once, and the Norse sat with both of them without resolving either.

Sigyn was still there with her bowl.

The cave was still dark.

📰 Informational Text

Connect Myth to Reality

Viking Punishment and Imprisonment: Justice in a Harsh World

Viking punishments were harsh by modern standards, but they were designed for a society without prisons. Most crimes were settled with fines (called 'wergild' or 'man-price'), exile, or execution — but some criminals were physically restrained or confined.

Archaeologists have found several Viking-age skeletons buried in chains or with bound limbs. One famous find in Denmark showed a man buried in a bog with his legs broken and folded under him — possibly a criminal or a captive. These finds suggest that restraint and confinement, while uncommon, did happen.

Viking laws (recorded in texts like the Grágás from Iceland) described different punishments for different crimes: theft might cost you a hand, murder might require payment to the victim's family, and betraying your chieftain could mean outlawry or death.

The concept of a family member staying with a condemned person — like Loki's wife Sigyn holding the bowl — reflects the real Norse value of loyalty even in the worst circumstances. Family bonds were sacred, and abandoning someone (even a criminal) brought shame.

Some sagas describe people imprisoned in caves or isolated islands as punishment. While rare, this shows that the Norse understood isolation as a form of torture — which makes Loki's binding even more psychologically horrible.

💡 The Lesson

Loki's punishment — bound and isolated but not killed — reflects real Viking practices of dealing with people who couldn't be allowed back into society but were too important (or too dangerous) to simply execute. Justice wasn't just about revenge; it was about removing threats while maintaining social order.

✏️ Grammar

Week 31 Focus: Verb Tenses: Past, Present, and Shifting Between Them

📚 What You're Learning:

Most stories are told in past tense. But sometimes writers shift to present tense to make a moment feel immediate and dramatic. Be intentional about when you shift tenses.

✨ Examples:

PAST: Tyr fed Fenrir every day, and the wolf trusted him.

PRESENT: Tyr steps forward. He knows what will happen. He places his hand in Fenrir's mouth.

MIXED (purposeful): Tyr fed Fenrir for years. Now he stands before the wolf, and his hand is steady.
