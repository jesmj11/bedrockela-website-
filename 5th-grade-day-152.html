A Tale from The sword was called Gram, and it had been broken and reforged, and it was so sharp that when Sigurd tested it in the river it cut a tuft of wool floating on the current clean in two.

He had a dragon to kill.

The dragon's name was Fafnir. He had not always been a dragon. Once he had been a man — the son of a farmer, ordinary except for his strength and his greed. He had killed his own father for a hoard of cursed gold, then transformed himself over years into something that matched the darkness inside him: scales, venom, a terrible size. He lay on the treasure in the wilderness, breathing poison fog, and the curse on the gold kept working on him from within.

The man who had reforged Sigurd's sword was named Regin, and he was Fafnir's brother, which explained the sword and also the problem.

"Kill Fafnir," said Regin. "Bring me the treasure."

Sigurd went to find the path the dragon used between his lair and the river where he drank. He began to dig a pit in the middle of it. An old man wandered up — one eye, wide-brimmed hat, the look of someone who knows far more than he's going to say. He suggested that Sigurd also dig drainage ditches around the main pit, so the blood would flow away and not drown him when it came.

Sigurd dug the pit and the drainage ditches. He climbed in and waited.

📚 Reading (Part 2 of 4)

Sigurd and the Dragon Fafnir

The ground shook. Fafnir came.

He was very large. He moved the way things move that have forgotten they were ever anything else, heavy and certain and trailing poison in the air around him. He passed over the pit.

Sigurd drove Gram upward with everything he had.

Fafnir stopped.

He looked down at the pit, at the figure in it, at the sword. He lowered his massive head and said, with the dignity of something dying slowly and knowing it: "Who are you? Who sent you?"

Sigurd told him his name.

Fafnir spent his last breaths giving a warning. The gold was cursed. Everyone who touched it died. It had destroyed their father. It had destroyed Fafnir himself. Leave it where it lies.

Sigurd listened politely. Then he took the treasure.

He roasted Fafnir's heart for Regin, as Regin had asked. While he cooked it, he pressed his finger to it to test if it was done, burned himself, and put his finger in his mouth.

Dragon blood.

In the trees above him, several birds were having a very clear conversation.

"Regin is going to kill him," one said.

"Obviously," said another. "He's just waiting for the heart."

"He should take the treasure, cut off Regin's head, and ride for the sleeping Valkyrie on the mountain."

The birds went on talking. Sigurd listened.

He made a decision.

He rode on with the treasure — the cursed gold and the ring at its center called Andvaranaut, which a dwarf had once cursed with every ounce of spite he had: everyone who holds this will be destroyed by it.

Sigurd rode through fire to a mountaintop, where a figure lay sleeping in full armor. He cut through the armor and found a woman — a Valkyrie named Brynhild, put to sleep by Odin for defying his will, surrounded by fire that only someone who feared nothing could ride through.

He woke her.

She sat up and looked at him, this young man who had just killed a dragon and ridden through fire to find her, and she saw what he was. He saw what she was.

📚 Reading (Part 4 of 4)

Sigurd and the Dragon Fafnir

He gave her Andvaranaut as a pledge of his promise to come back.

He had just given her the cursed ring. He didn't know that yet.

He rode on. The curse went with him. The curse worked the way curses do — not through thunder and lightning, but through ordinary human choices, each one understandable, all of them terrible together. A drink that made him forget. A marriage to the wrong person. Brynhild waking up and finding herself somewhere else, with someone else, unable to understand why he had abandoned her.

The end was dark.

The Norse knew it was coming when they started the story. They told it anyway, every word, every tragedy.

Because Sigurd had killed the dragon. He had ridden through the fire. He had woken the sleeper on the mountain.

Those things were real. The curse couldn't take them back.

Some things you do are yours forever, even if everything else falls apart.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

Show what you understood

1. Fafnir warned Sigurd to leave the gold behind — told him directly that the curse was real and everyone who took it would be destroyed. Sigurd took it anyway. Why do you think he didn't listen? Have you ever been warned about something and chosen not to listen? What happened?

2. The curse on the gold didn't work through dramatic magic — it worked through ordinary human choices, each one understandable, all of them terrible together. What does that suggest about how the Norse thought curses and bad luck really operated in the world?

3. Sigurd killed the dragon, rode through fire, and woke Brynhild — but the story still ends in tragedy. Does a hero have to succeed in order to be a hero? What made Sigurd heroic, even if the ending was dark?

📰 Informational Text

Connect Myth to Reality

Viking Treasure and the Curse of Gold

The Norse told many stories about cursed treasure, and archaeology reveals why: wealth was genuinely dangerous in Viking culture. A hoard of gold or silver could attract raiders, cause family feuds, or lead to murder. 'Cursed gold' was a way of expressing real risks.

Vikings buried treasure for safekeeping, but if the owner died without revealing the location, that wealth was lost forever. Thousands of Viking hoards have been discovered — some recently, some centuries ago — containing silver coins, jewelry, and ingots buried around 900-1100 AD.

One of the most famous finds is the Hiddensee treasure from Germany: 16 pieces of gold jewelry weighing over a pound. Another is the Galloway Hoard from Scotland, containing over 100 items including rare artifacts from Ireland, Scandinavia, and Central Asia. These prove that Vikings accumulated enormous wealth through trade and raiding.

Treasure could tear families apart. Inheritance disputes were common, and many sagas describe brothers, cousins, and uncles fighting over gold. The 'curse' of treasure wasn't supernatural — it was the very real human tendency toward greed and betrayal when wealth is involved.

Archaeological evidence shows that some Viking chieftains were buried with massive amounts of treasure — ships, weapons, jewelry, and even sacrificed slaves. This 'grave wealth' could have fed entire communities but was instead buried with the dead, showing that status and honor sometimes mattered more than practical wealth.

💡 The Lesson

Fafnir's hoard and the curse that follows anyone who takes it reflects the real Viking understanding that gold can corrupt, divide, and destroy. The myth is a warning: wealth without wisdom is dangerous.

🎨 Language Skills

Week 31 Focus: Connotation: Words That Carry Feelings

📚 What You're Learning:

Denotation = dictionary definition. Connotation = the feeling a word gives you. Writers choose words carefully for their emotional weight.

✨ Examples:

NEUTRAL: Loki looked at the gods. | NEGATIVE: Loki glared at the gods. | POSITIVE: Loki gazed at the gods.

NEUTRAL: Fenrir was big. | SCARY: Fenrir was monstrous. | AWE-INSPIRING: Fenrir was magnificent.

NEUTRAL: Sigyn stayed with Loki. | LOVING: Sigyn remained faithful to Loki. | STUBBORN: Sigyn refused to leave Loki.
