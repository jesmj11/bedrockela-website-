The Three Princes and Princess Nouronnihar • Part 1 of 4

The Sultan had three sons and one problem: all three of them were in love with the same person. The person was Princess Nouronnihar, his niece, who had grown up in the palace and was, by any measure, worth being in love with. She was clever and kind and the kind of person who made every room better by being in it. The three princes — Houssain, Ali, and Ahmed — were all perfectly good men who each had a reasonable argument for why they were the right choice. The Sultan could not decide. He thought about it for many nights. Then he called all three before him and announced the solution. "You will each go out into the world for one year," he said. "Travel wherever you like. Bring back the most extraordinary, the most remarkable, the most useful thing you can find. When you return, I will choose." The brothers agreed. They walked out of the palace together, down the road, and when it split into three, they embraced, promised to meet back in a year, and went their separate ways. Prince Houssain, the eldest, traveled east to a great trading city. In the market he found a carpet seller with a plain-looking square of wool. The price was enormous. The property, the seller explained, was this: sit on it, wish to be somewhere, and you'd be there — anywhere in the world, instantly. Houssain paid without bargaining. He had seen enough to know that useful things rarely look impressive. Prince Ali, the middle son, went west to a city of craftsmen. In a back alley he found an old man with a carved ivory tube. Look through it, the old man said, and you'd see whatever you wished to see — any person, any place, any event happening right now anywhere on earth. Ali paid the high price and tucked it carefully in his bag. Prince Ahmed, the youngest, went south through desert and mountains to a market at the edge of a great forest. There he found a single golden apple. The seller explained: any person who smelled this apple, however ill, however close to death, would recover completely. Ahmed protected that apple like it was made of something more precious than gold. A year later, the brothers met at the crossroads. They were glad to see each other and swapped stories and admired each other's treasures and were generally pleased with how well the mission had gone. Then Ali raised his ivory tube and looked through it idly — and stopped. "Brothers," he said. They looked at him. "Look at Nouronnihar." They each looked through the tube in turn. In the palace far away, Nouronnihar lay surrounded by weeping attendants. She had fallen terribly ill while they were gone. The physicians had tried everything. She was dying. Houssain didn't need to be asked twice. He pulled out the carpet. "All three of us. Now." They sat together. Houssain said the word. The crossroads vanished and the palace appeared around them, and they were kneeling beside Nouronnihar's bed before anyone in the palace had time to notice. Ahmed held the apple beneath her nose. Color came back into her face. Her eyes opened. By evening she was sitting up, asking what had happened and why her cousins were all there at once looking like they'd run a very long distance. The Sultan arrived and was overjoyed and then immediately faced with the original problem, which had not gone away: all three princes had used their treasures to save her, and all three treasures had been necessary. Which one deserved the princess? The princes debated this themselves. Houssain argued that without the carpet, they would have arrived too late. Ahmed argued that without the apple, she would have died regardless of how quickly they arrived. Ali argued that without the tube, they wouldn't have known she was dying at all. They were all right. This was the problem. The Sultan devised an archery contest — the prince who shot the furthest would win. Houssain and Ali shot their arrows great distances. Ahmed shot his and they could not find it at all. He went to look for it and found it near a pavilion in the hills, beside a woman named Peri Banou who had clearly enchanted it to land there so Ahmed would come looking. She was remarkable in every way, and by the time Ahmed figured out this was a setup, he didn't particularly mind. He came back to the palace without the princess. Ali received Princess Nouronnihar as the Sultan had promised. And Ahmed, who had lost the contest he hadn't really known he was in, went back to the hills to find Peri Banou, which turned out to be a significantly more interesting story than winning a straightforward archery contest. Houssain, who had genuinely loved Nouronnihar, sat with his grief honestly and then decided to become a hermit and spend his life in contemplation, which suited him better than he expected. The Sultan kept all three treasures. He was a practical man. The lesson, if there is one, is that it is very difficult to judge who deserves credit when everyone contributed something essential. And that sometimes losing what you were competing for leads you somewhere better than winning would have. Ahmed would have agreed, if anyone had asked him. He was very happy in those hills.

📰 Arabian Medicine and Hospitals

In the 9th and 10th centuries, while Europe had few medical resources, the Islamic world built sophisticated hospitals. Baghdad's first hospital opened in 805 CE, with separate wards for different illnesses, a pharmacy, a library, and lecture rooms for teaching.

These hospitals treated everyone — rich and poor, citizen and foreigner — without charge. They had specialized departments: one for fevers, one for eye diseases, one for surgery, one for mental illness. Doctors made rounds, examining patients and recording symptoms. Medical students learned by observing experienced physicians.

Arabian physicians made important discoveries. Al-Razi (known in Europe as Rhazes) identified the difference between measles and smallpox. Ibn Sina (known as Avicenna) wrote 'The Canon of Medicine,' a comprehensive medical encyclopedia used in European universities for 600 years.

Arabian doctors pioneered surgical instruments, understood the importance of hygiene, and recognized that diseases could spread through contamination. They established pharmacy as a profession separate from medicine, with trained pharmacists preparing precise doses.

When European scholars translated Arabian medical texts in the 12th century, this knowledge revolutionized Western medicine. Modern hospitals, medical schools, and pharmacy practices all carry forward ideas first developed in the medieval Islamic world.
