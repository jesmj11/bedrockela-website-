📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

Chapter 1: The Road to the Castle

My name is Jonathan Harker, and I am a young lawyer from London. I am writing this in my journal because I want to remember everything — the good days and the strange days alike. And the days I am about to describe were among the strangest of my life.

My employer, Mr. Hawkins, sent me on an important assignment. A nobleman from a distant country called Transylvania wished to buy a house in England. He had chosen an old estate called Carfax, near London, and he needed a lawyer to handle the paperwork. Mr. Hawkins thought I was just the man for it — young, hardworking, and eager to prove myself. He was right that I was eager.

I traveled by train across France and into Eastern Europe, watching the landscape change through the train window — from flat farmland to rolling hills to jagged mountains draped in pine forest. The food changed, the language changed, and the people changed too. They were warm and welcoming, but there was an uneasiness that I could not quite explain.

When I asked about my destination — a castle owned by a Count Dracula — people's faces changed. They fell quiet. An old woman pressed a string of garlic flowers into my hands and made a sign that I did not understand.

📰 Informational Text

Connect Story to Reality

The Real Dracula: Vlad the Impaler

The character Count Dracula was inspired by a real historical figure: Vlad III, Prince of Wallachia (a region in modern-day Romania), who lived from 1431 to 1476. He was known as Vlad the Impaler because of his brutal method of punishing enemies.

Vlad ruled during a dangerous time when the Ottoman Empire was expanding into Europe. He defended his homeland fiercely, using terror tactics to discourage invaders. While his methods were cruel, many Romanians today consider him a national hero who protected his people from foreign conquest.

The name 'Dracula' comes from his father's title: Vlad II Dracul, meaning 'Vlad the Dragon.' The suffix '-a' means 'son of,' so Dracula literally means 'son of the dragon.' The dragon was actually a symbol of a knightly order Vlad's father belonged to — the Order of the Dragon, dedicated to defending Christianity.

Bram Stoker, the author of Dracula, read about Vlad while researching Eastern European history. He was fascinated by the fearsome reputation and the dramatic name. However, Stoker's vampire count has little in common with the real Vlad — the historical prince was never associated with vampire legends during his lifetime. Those stories came later, blending Vlad's dark reputation with ancient folklore about the undead.

Interestingly, Vlad's castle (Poenari Fortress) and the tourist attraction Bran Castle both exist in Romania today. While Bran Castle is marketed as 'Dracula's Castle,' historians believe Stoker's description was based on his imagination, not any specific building.

✏️ Grammar

Week 16 Focus: Complex Sentences with Subordinating Conjunctions

📚 What You're Learning:

A complex sentence has an independent clause (complete thought) and a dependent clause (incomplete thought connected with words like 'because', 'although', 'while', 'when', 'if', 'since').

✨ Examples:

Jonathan traveled to Transylvania because he needed to complete an assignment for his employer.

Although the villagers warned him, Jonathan continued to the castle.

The Count watched Jonathan carefully while he ate his dinner.
