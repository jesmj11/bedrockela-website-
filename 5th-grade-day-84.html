📚 Reading (Part 2 of 3)

Chapter 5: Mina's Worry

Three months have passed. His letters stopped halfway through his journey. I have written to his employer, to the train companies, to every contact I can think of. No one has heard anything.

I tell myself there are reasonable explanations. The mail is slow in that part of the world. He may have had to stay longer. He is resourceful and sensible, and nothing bad has happened to him.

I tell myself this every night before I go to sleep.

My dearest friend in the world is a woman named Lucy Westenra. She is bright and funny and the most good-natured person I know. Lucy has three young men all very much in love with her — a doctor named John Seward, a good-natured American named Quincey Morris, and an English nobleman named Arthur Holmwood. She has chosen Arthur, and they are newly engaged.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

Show what you understood

1. Mina writes that she tells herself 'reasonable explanations' for why Jonathan hasn't returned, but she writes this in her journal each night before sleep. What does this tell you about how worried she really is? Why might she need to write these reassurances down?

2. A ship arrives in Whitby during a storm with no living crew and fifty boxes of 'dark foreign earth' in the hold. Knowing what we learned from Jonathan's chapters, why is this arrival so ominous? What has arrived in England?

3. Mina describes her friend Lucy as 'bright and funny and the most good-natured person I know,' but then immediately says 'Lucy began walking in her sleep.' Why might the author introduce a happy, innocent character right before something bad starts happening to her? How does this make you feel as a reader?

📰 Informational Text

Connect Story to Reality

Bram Stoker: The Man Behind Dracula

Abraham 'Bram' Stoker was born in 1847 in Dublin, Ireland. As a child, he was frequently sick and bedridden, spending years unable to walk or play like other children. During this time, his mother told him ghost stories and Irish folk tales — dark, imaginative stories that would later influence his writing.

Mysteriously, when Bram was about seven years old, he suddenly recovered and became unusually strong and athletic. He attended Trinity College Dublin, where he excelled in athletics and became a champion runner and weightlifter. This transformation from sickly child to athletic young man fascinated him throughout his life.

Stoker never planned to become a novelist. He worked as a civil servant (a government employee) and wrote theater reviews in his spare time. His reviews caught the attention of Henry Irving, one of the most famous actors of the Victorian era. Irving hired Stoker as business manager for the Lyceum Theatre in London — a job Stoker held for 27 years.

Working in theater exposed Stoker to drama, storytelling, and spectacle. He met artists, writers, and travelers who shared stories from around the world. He also worked exhausting hours managing the theater's business affairs, traveling, and organizing tours. Writing was something he did late at night after everyone else had gone home.

Stoker published several novels and short story collections, but Dracula (1897) was his masterpiece. He spent seven years researching it, reading about Eastern European history, vampire folklore, geography, and travel routes. He kept detailed notes on everything from train schedules to castle architecture.

Interestingly, Stoker never visited Eastern Europe himself. His descriptions of Transylvania came entirely from books, maps, and travelers' accounts — yet they felt so real that readers believed he'd been there.

Stoker died in 1912, largely forgotten. Dracula sold moderately well but wasn't a huge success during his lifetime. It was only later — especially after the 1931 film starring Bela Lugosi — that Dracula became a cultural phenomenon. Today, it's one of the most influential horror novels ever written, adapted countless times for film, television, and theater.

Bram Stoker created a character that would outlive him by over a century, becoming a permanent part of world culture.

✏️ Grammar

Week 17 Focus: Complex Sentences with Subordinating Conjunctions

📚 What You're Learning:

A complex sentence has an independent clause (complete thought) and a dependent clause (incomplete thought connected with words like 'because', 'although', 'while', 'when', 'if', 'since').

✨ Examples:

Jonathan traveled to Transylvania because he needed to complete an assignment for his employer.

Although the villagers warned him, Jonathan continued to the castle.

The Count watched Jonathan carefully while he ate his dinner.
