Professor Van Helsing returned to Whitby several times over the following weeks, and each time he brought flowers. Garlic flowers, specifically — bunches and bunches of them, which he arranged around Lucy's room, hung above the windows, and wove into a wreath for her neck.

Lucy's mother thought this was eccentric. Lucy thought it was funny, and teased Van Helsing gently about his "smelly decorations."

But I noticed that on the nights when the garlic was in place and the windows were closed, Lucy slept soundly. Her color improved. She seemed more herself.

Then one night, Lucy's mother — not understanding the reason for the garlic — opened the windows to let in fresh air and moved the flowers because she thought they smelled too strong.

📰 Informational Text

Connect Story to Reality

Transylvania: Real Geography, Real History

Transylvania is a real region in central Romania, surrounded by the Carpathian Mountains. Its name comes from Latin: 'trans' (across) and 'silva' (forest), meaning 'across the forest' or 'beyond the forest.' The name itself sounds mysterious — a land beyond the woods, isolated and remote.

Historically, Transylvania was always a borderland — a place between empires. At different times it was controlled by the Kingdom of Hungary, the Ottoman Empire, and later became part of Romania. This position made it both culturally rich (Hungarian, Romanian, Saxon German, and Roma communities all lived there) and politically unstable.

The Carpathian Mountains that surround Transylvania are genuine wilderness. Dense forests, high peaks, deep valleys, and harsh winters made the region difficult to access. Even today, parts of the Carpathians are home to Europe's largest populations of brown bears, wolves, and lynx — the same predators that prowled there in medieval times.

Medieval Transylvania was dotted with fortified castles and churches. Because the region faced frequent invasions, communities built strong defensive structures. Many of these castles still stand today, perched on cliff edges or mountain passes, looking just as dramatic as Stoker described.

The Saxon Germans who settled in Transylvania during the Middle Ages built distinctive fortified churches — entire church complexes surrounded by thick walls where villagers could shelter during attacks. UNESCO now recognizes several of these as World Heritage Sites for their unique architecture and historical importance.

One of Transylvania's most famous legends is about Prince Vlad III (Vlad the Impaler), whose fortress at Poenari Castle was a real mountain stronghold. While there's little historical evidence connecting Vlad to vampire legends during his lifetime, his reputation for cruelty and the dramatic landscape of his territory made him perfect inspiration for Stoker's Count Dracula.

Today, Transylvania embraces its Gothic reputation. Tourism based on Dracula brings visitors from around the world. However, locals often note the irony: Dracula the vampire is fiction, created by an Irish author who never visited Romania, yet the story has become permanently attached to their real homeland.

The actual Transylvania is a beautiful region with medieval towns, mountain hiking, traditional crafts, and vibrant culture — quite different from the purely Gothic setting of Stoker's imagination, yet just as interesting.

✏️ Grammar

Week 17 Focus: Using Commas in Complex Sentences

📚 What You're Learning:

When a dependent clause comes BEFORE the independent clause, use a comma to separate them. When it comes AFTER, usually no comma is needed. Example: 'Although Jonathan was frightened, he continued forward.' vs 'Jonathan continued forward although he was frightened.'

✨ Examples:

When the Count appeared in the doorway, Jonathan felt a chill run down his spine.

Jonathan felt a chill run down his spine when the Count appeared in the doorway.

Because Mina organized all the records, the group could see patterns they had missed before.
