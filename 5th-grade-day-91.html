📰 Informational Text

Connect Story to Reality

Medical Science in the 1890s: Dr. Seward's Asylum

In Dracula, Dr. John Seward runs a private asylum (a hospital for people with mental illness) right next door to Count Dracula's property at Carfax. This wasn't just a plot convenience — private asylums were common in Victorian England, and the treatment of mental illness was very different from today.

The 1890s was a transitional period in psychiatry (the medical study of mental illness). Doctors were beginning to view mental illness as a medical condition rather than moral failing or demonic possession, but treatment was still primitive and often harmful.

Dr. Seward uses a phonograph — a newly invented device that recorded sound onto wax cylinders — to keep his medical notes. This was cutting-edge technology in the 1890s! The phonograph had only been invented in 1877, and using it for medical records showed Dr. Seward as a modern, scientific practitioner.

However, Victorian asylum treatments would horrify us today. 'Restraint' (tying patients down or using straitjackets) was common. 'Hydrotherapy' involved shocking patients with ice-cold water or forcing them into long baths. Some doctors used medications like laudanum (liquid opium) or bromides (sedatives) liberally, often creating addiction.

The theory behind many treatments was that mental illness came from physical problems: 'bad blood,' inflammation of the brain, or imbalanced body chemistry. Some doctors performed bloodletting (deliberately removing blood) or prescribed purging (forced vomiting). Others tried experimental electrical treatments.

Asylums varied widely in quality. Wealthy patients in private asylums like Dr. Seward's might receive relatively humane care — comfortable rooms, good food, and gentle treatment. Poor patients in public asylums often faced overcrowding, neglect, and harsh conditions.

One of Dr. Seward's patients, Renfield, displays what we might today recognize as symptoms of psychosis — delusions, obsessive behaviors, and break from reality. Victorian doctors would have called this 'mania' or 'lunacy.' They had no effective medications; the antipsychotic drugs that help people with these conditions today weren't developed until the 1950s.

Interestingly, Stoker portrays Dr. Seward sympathetically — he cares about his patients, keeps detailed observations, and tries to understand rather than simply restrain. This reflects the progressive side of Victorian psychiatry: doctors who genuinely wanted to help, even though their tools were limited.

Modern psychiatry has been revolutionized by medication, therapy techniques, brain imaging, and better understanding of how the brain works. People with mental illness today have treatment options and legal protections that didn't exist in the 1890s.

✏️ Grammar

Week 18 Focus: Using Commas in Complex Sentences

📚 What You're Learning:

When a dependent clause comes BEFORE the independent clause, use a comma to separate them. When it comes AFTER, usually no comma is needed. Example: 'Although Jonathan was frightened, he continued forward.' vs 'Jonathan continued forward although he was frightened.'

✨ Examples:

When the Count appeared in the doorway, Jonathan felt a chill run down his spine.

Jonathan felt a chill run down his spine when the Count appeared in the doorway.

Because Mina organized all the records, the group could see patterns they had missed before.
