Today's New Words

Learn these 3 words before reading. They'll appear in the Norse creation story!

1. void

Definition: A completely empty space — nothing at all, not even air or light.

As used in the story: "Ginnungagap was the great void, the nothing in the middle between fire and ice."

2. primordial

Definition: Existing at the very beginning of time, before almost everything else.

As used in the story: "The primordial cold of Niflheim and the primordial fire of Muspelheim were the first two things that existed."

3. realm

Definition: A kingdom or world with its own rules and creatures.

As used in the story: "The gods built Asgard as their realm, high above the human realm of Midgard."

📚 Reading: In the Beginning

The Norse Creation Myth

Before the world existed, there were exactly two things. And they hated each other.

In the north: Niflheim. Ice. Darkness. Fog so cold it bit. Eleven rivers that crawled outward from a spring called Hvergelmir and froze solid before they got anywhere interesting. If you had stood at the edge of Niflheim — which you couldn't, because you didn't exist yet — you would have felt your eyelashes freeze off within seconds.

In the south: Muspelheim. Fire. Roaring, howling, everything-is-on-fire fire. A giant named Surt stood at its border holding a flaming sword, waiting for the end of the world with the patience of someone who has been waiting since before the world started and doesn't mind waiting until after it ends.

Between them: nothing. A yawning gap called Ginnungagap. The great empty void.

Then the cold crept south. The fire crept north. They met in the middle. The ice melted.

And from that melting, something enormous hauled itself upright, shook the frost from its beard, and blinked.

His name was Ymir. He was the first frost giant — bad-tempered, enormous, and not particularly bright. While he slept, other frost giants sweated out of him the way breath fogs in cold air, and those giants had more giants, and suddenly the empty void was full of enormous bickering creatures.

Also crawling out of the melting ice was a cow. An enormous, untroubled cow named Audhumbla. She had four udders and she fed Ymir with her milk, and she fed herself by licking the salty ice.

As Audhumbla licked, something started appearing in the block she was working on. Day one: hair. Day two: a head. Day three: a full person stepped out of the ice, blinking, looking around at the frost giants with an expression that said clearly: I am not one of these.

His name was Buri. He was the first of the gods.

Buri had a son named Bor. Bor had three sons: Odin, Vili, and Ve. The three brothers looked at Ymir snoring in the middle of everything, frost giants multiplying in every direction, and had a conversation that went approximately like this:

"We should build something better."
"Agreed."
"We'll need materials."

They looked at Ymir. Ymir did not survive the meeting.

From his body, the brothers made the world. His flesh became the earth — all of it, every hill and plain and valley. His blood became the oceans and rivers. His bones became mountains. His teeth became boulders. His skull became the dome of the sky, which they propped up at the four corners with four dwarves named North, South, East, and West, who have been standing there ever since holding up the sky and presumably wishing they had said no.

His brains became clouds. His eyebrows became a great wall around the middle world to keep out whatever was left of the giants.

Then the brothers found two trees lying on the ground — an ash and an elm, just lying there, not doing much. Odin breathed life into them. Vili gave them understanding and the ability to move. Ve gave them senses, voices, color in their faces.

From the ash came a man named Ask. From the elm came a woman named Embla. They looked at each other. They looked at the world — the mountains made of bones, the sea made of blood, the sky made of a dead giant's skull — and somehow found it beautiful.

The brothers built their own realm above: Asgard, the home of the gods, connected to the human world of Midgard by a rainbow bridge called Bifrost. They built great halls. They lit the sky with sparks from Muspelheim. They set the sun and moon running across the sky, chased by wolves who would catch them at the end of everything.

And holding all of it together — all nine worlds, from the fiery south to the frozen north, from the gods at the top to the dead at the bottom — was Yggdrasil, the World Tree. An ash so enormous that its branches sheltered the gods and its roots reached into three different wells. A squirrel named Ratatoskr spent every single day running up and down its trunk carrying insults between the eagle at the top and the serpent gnawing at the bottom.

Nobody asked him to do this. He just did it.

The world was built. The people were walking around in it. The gods were in their hall.

And somewhere at the border of Muspelheim, Surt was still standing, still holding his flaming sword, still waiting. He was very patient.

📰 Informational Text

Connect Myth to Reality

How the Universe Actually Formed: The Big Bang

The Norse imagined creation beginning with ice and fire meeting in a void. Surprisingly, scientists have discovered that the universe DID begin from something like a void — but their story is even stranger than the Norse myth!

About 13.8 billion years ago, the entire universe was compressed into a single point smaller than an atom. Then it exploded in what scientists call the Big Bang. In less than a second, the universe expanded faster than the speed of light, growing from microscopic to larger than a galaxy.

The first few minutes were crucial: The universe was incredibly hot — billions of degrees. As it expanded and cooled, the first atoms formed: mostly hydrogen and helium, the same elements that make up stars today.

After about 380,000 years, the universe cooled enough for light to travel through space for the first time. Before this, the universe was opaque — you couldn't see anything even if you were there! This ancient light still exists today as background radiation that scientists can detect.

The first stars formed about 100 million years later, lighting up the dark universe. These stars were massive — much bigger than our Sun — and when they exploded, they created heavier elements like carbon, oxygen, and iron. Every atom in your body (except hydrogen) was forged inside an ancient star!

Comparing the Stories

Norse Myth
  • Started with a void (Ginnungagap)
  • Fire and ice met and melted
  • First creature: Ymir (frost giant)
  • Gods created order from chaos
  • Earth made from a giant's body
Scientific Discovery
  • Started from nearly nothing (singularity)
  • Extreme heat, then rapid cooling
  • First elements: hydrogen & helium
  • Gravity organized matter into galaxies
  • Earth formed from stardust billions of years later

The Lesson: Ancient peoples used the ideas they had — ice, fire, giants, gods — to explain their world. Scientists today use mathematics, telescopes, and experiments. Both groups asked the same fundamental question: How did everything begin? The tools we use to answer that question have changed, but human curiosity remains the same.

✏️ Grammar Workshop

Week 29 Focus: Complex Sentences

What is a Complex Sentence?

A complex sentence has an independent clause (complete thought) AND a dependent clause (incomplete thought that adds information).

Independent clause: "Ymir did not survive."
(Complete sentence — makes sense on its own)

Dependent clause: "When the three brothers looked at him"
(Incomplete — leaves you wondering "what happened?")

Complex sentence: "When the three brothers looked at him, Ymir did not survive."
(Dependent + Independent = Complete meaning)

Common Dependent Clause Starters:

because, when, while, if, although, since, before, after, unless, until
