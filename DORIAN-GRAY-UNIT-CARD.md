## Character Guide

**Dorian Gray** - Beautiful young man whose portrait bears the burden of his sins  
**Lord Henry Wotton** - Charismatic aristocrat whose philosophy corrupts Dorian  
**Basil Hallward** - Artist whose masterpiece enables supernatural corruption  
**Sibyl Vane** - Young actress destroyed by Dorian's heartless cruelty  
**Alan Campbell** - Scientist blackmailed into helping hide Dorian's crimes  
**James Vane** - Sibyl's brother who seeks revenge for her death

## Themes to Explore

1. **Beauty vs. Morality**
   - Can beauty exist without morality?
   - Does physical beauty excuse moral corruption?
   - The cost of prioritizing appearance over character

2. **The Corruption of Innocence**
   - Lord Henry as tempter/corruptor
   - Dorian's transformation from innocent to monster
   - Can corruption be reversed?

3. **Art and Reality**
   - Portrait as mirror of the soul
   - Life imitating art
   - Artist's responsibility for their creation

4. **Hedonism and Consequences**
   - Philosophy of pleasure-seeking
   - Every sin has a price
   - Delayed but inevitable consequences

5. **Appearance vs. Reality**
   - Surface beauty hiding inner corruption
   - Victorian hypocrisy and double lives
   - The portrait reveals hidden truth

## Discussion Questions

1. Who is most responsible for Dorian's corruption: Lord Henry, Basil, or Dorian himself?
2. Is the portrait supernatural or symbolic? What does it represent?
3. Could Dorian have been saved? At what point was redemption impossible?
4. How does Victorian society contribute to Dorian's downfall?
5. What is Oscar Wilde saying about the relationship between art and morality?
6. Compare Lord Henry's philosophy to actual results - was he right?
7. Why does the portrait restore itself after Dorian's death?
8. Is this a cautionary tale or a tragedy? Both?
9. How does this compare to Frankenstein and Jekyll & Hyde?
10. What is the "yellow book" and why is it so corrupting?

## Oscar Wilde Context

**About the Author:**
- Irish playwright, poet, aesthete
- Famous for wit and epigrams
- "Art for art's sake" philosophy
- Scandalous trial and imprisonment (1895)

**The Aesthetic Movement:**
- Art should be beautiful, not moral
- "All art is quite useless" (Wilde's preface)
- Pleasure and beauty as highest values
- Rejection of Victorian moral constraints

**Irony:** Wilde wrote a cautionary tale against his own philosophy - the novel shows consequences of pure aestheticism without morality.

## Notes for Implementation

**Mature themes** - Handle sensitively:
- Hedonism and moral corruption
- Suicide (Sibyl Vane)
- Murder (Basil Hallward)
- Same-sex devotion (Basil's love for Dorian, Wilde's coded language)
- Discuss as literature, not sensationalism

**Victorian context** - Help students understand:
- Extreme social pressure for respectability
- "Double lives" common in Victorian society
- Why Wilde's aestheticism was scandalous
- Relevance to modern "curated personas"

**Philosophical depth** - Guide discussions:
- Lord Henry sounds convincing but is he right?
- Separate Wilde the author from Lord Henry the character
- Consequences prove the moral lesson
- Art vs. morality debate is still relevant

**Perfect Conclusion:**
- Ties together year's themes
- Provides closure (Dorian's death, portrait's restoration)
- Leaves students with big questions
- Celebrates completing challenging literature
