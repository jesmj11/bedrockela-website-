## Unit Overview

## Character Guide

**Robert Walton** - Arctic explorer whose dangerous ambition mirrors Victor's  
**Victor Frankenstein** - Young scientist whose obsession with creating life destroys everything he loves  
**The Creature** - Victor's abandoned creation who becomes both victim and monster  
**Elizabeth Lavenza** - Victor's adopted sister and beloved whose love could save him  
**Professor Waldman** - University teacher who inspires Victor's fatal experiments

## Themes to Explore

1. **Scientific Responsibility**
   - Knowledge without ethics
   - Consequences of unchecked ambition
   - Creator's duty to creation

2. **Nature vs. Nurture**
   - The creature's inherent nature
   - Impact of rejection and isolation
   - Could love have changed the outcome?

3. **Appearance vs. Reality**
   - Physical monstrosity vs. moral monstrosity
   - Who is the real monster?
   - Society's judgment based on appearance

4. **Revenge and Justice**
   - Cycle of violence
   - Personal vengeance vs. legal justice
   - Can either side be justified?

5. **Isolation and Connection**
   - Dangers of isolation (Victor and Creature)
   - Human need for companionship
   - Rejection's devastating effects

---

## Discussion Questions

1. Is Victor Frankenstein a hero, a villain, or a victim?
2. Does the creature have a right to demand a companion?
3. What would have happened if Victor had accepted his creation?
4. Is knowledge always worth pursuing, regardless of consequences?
5. Who bears more guilt: Victor for creating or the creature for killing?
6. How does the frame narrative (Walton's letters) affect the story?
7. What parallels exist between Victor and Walton's ambitions?
8. Can the creature be held morally responsible for his actions?
9. What role does Elizabeth play in the tragedy?
10. What is Mary Shelley warning us about?

## Writing Prompts

**Literary Analysis:**
- Compare Victor Frankenstein to the creature - who is more human?
- Analyze the role of nature imagery (mountains, ice, storms) in the novel
- Explore the theme of dangerous knowledge in Frankenstein
- Discuss the significance of the frame narrative structure

**Argument Essays:**
- Who is the real monster in Frankenstein? Defend your answer.
- Should Victor have created the companion the creature demanded?
- Is scientific progress worth the risk of unintended consequences?
- Can the creature's murders be morally justified?

**Creative Extensions:**
- Write a letter from the creature to humanity
- Create an alternate ending where Victor accepts his creation
- Imagine a trial: The People vs. Victor Frankenstein
- Write the creature's perspective of a key scene

## Notes for Implementation

**This unit is darker than others** - contains themes of death, abandonment, murder, and revenge. Consider:
- Pre-teaching discussion about Gothic literature conventions
- Emphasizing themes of responsibility and consequences
- Connecting to modern bioethics and technology debates
- Supporting students who may find horror elements intense

**Frame narrative structure** - Story within a story (Walton → Victor → Creature). Help students track:
- Who is speaking at any given time
- How multiple perspectives reveal different truths
- Why Shelley chose this complex structure

**Historical context** - Written in 1818 by 18-year-old Mary Shelley:
- Discussions about reanimation were real scientific debates
- Industrial Revolution raised questions about progress
- Women had limited publishing opportunities
- Gothic tradition explored fear of scientific advancement
