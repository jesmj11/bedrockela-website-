## Unit Overview

**Novel:** *The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde* by Robert Louis Stevenson (adapted)  
**Genre:** Gothic Psychological Thriller / Mystery  
**Theme:** Duality of Human Nature, Good vs. Evil, Consequences of Unchecked Desire  
**Total Chapters:** 24  
**Days:** 15 (Mon-Fri for 3 weeks)  
**Assessment Day:** Day 15 (Friday, Week 30)

## Character Guide

**Dr. Henry Jekyll** - Respected physician whose experiments with human nature lead to tragedy  
**Mr. Edward Hyde** - Jekyll's dark alter ego who embodies pure evil  
**Mr. Gabriel John Utterson** - Lawyer and detective who investigates the mystery  
**Dr. Hastie Lanyon** - Jekyll's colleague who witnesses a terrible transformation  
**Mr. Richard Enfield** - Utterson's cousin who first encounters Hyde  
**Sir Danvers Carew** - Prominent politician murdered by Hyde

## Themes to Explore

1. **Duality of Human Nature**
   - Everyone contains both good and evil
   - The struggle to balance opposing forces
   - What happens when we separate them?

2. **Hypocrisy and Victorian Society**
   - Public respectability vs. private desires
   - Social masks we all wear
   - Cost of maintaining appearances

3. **Consequences of Playing God**
   - Scientific experimentation without ethics
   - Trying to control/perfect human nature
   - Parallel to Frankenstein's hubris

4. **Addiction and Loss of Control**
   - Jekyll's addiction to being Hyde
   - Transformation from choice to compulsion
   - Point of no return

5. **Good vs. Evil**
   - Can evil exist without good?
   - Is Hyde purely evil or does he represent natural desires?
   - Can we choose to be only good?

## Discussion Questions

1. Is Mr. Hyde a separate person or is he Dr. Jekyll's true self?
2. Why does Jekyll create Hyde in the first place?
3. Could Jekyll have stopped at any point? When?
4. What does Hyde represent about human nature?
5. Why does Victorian London provide the perfect setting?
6. How does the mystery structure affect the reader's understanding?
7. What role does Utterson play as narrator/detective?
8. Is Jekyll a victim or a villain?
9. What is Stevenson warning us about?
10. How does this story compare to Frankenstein?

## Literary Devices & Symbolism

**Symbolism:**
- **Doors** - Hidden aspects of self, secrets
- **Fog/Darkness** - Moral ambiguity, hidden truth
- **Jekyll's house** - Respectable front vs. back door (Hyde's entrance)
- **The potion** - Temptation, forbidden knowledge
- **Names** - Jekyll (French "je kill" = I kill), Hyde (hide)

**Gothic Elements:**
- Doubles/doppelgängers
- Mystery and suspense
- Urban Gothic (London fog and streets)
- Psychological horror
- Transformation and monstrosity

**Narrative Structure:**
- Mystery/detective format
- Multiple perspectives (Utterson → Lanyon → Jekyll)
- Gradual revelation of truth
- Unreliable narration

## Notes for Implementation

**Mystery structure** - Story unfolds like a detective novel:
- Begin with Utterson's perspective (outsider looking in)
- Gradually reveal clues and evidence
- Build suspense toward shocking revelation
- Final confession provides complete truth

**Psychological depth** - More subtle than Frankenstein:
- Less physical horror, more psychological
- Explores internal struggle
- Relevant to students' own experiences with identity
- Connect to modern understanding of psychology

**Victorian context** - Help students understand:
- Extreme social pressure to maintain respectability
- Double lives many Victorians led
- Why Jekyll couldn't simply indulge desires openly
- Relevance to modern "social media personas"

**Compare/Contrast with Frankenstein:**
- Both deal with dangerous experiments
- Both feature creators destroyed by creations
- Both explore consequences of playing God
- Different approaches: external monster vs. internal
