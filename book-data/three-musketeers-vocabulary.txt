The Three Musketeers

A BedrockELA Adaptation

Vocabulary Guide — 2 Words Per Chapter (48 Words Total)

Chapter 1: The Boy from Gascony

province  (noun)

A region or area of a country, often far from the capital city.

From the story: D'Artagnan came from Gascony, a rough, wild province in the south of France where the people were known for their pride and their tempers.

Think about it: How does coming from a province far from Paris shape who D'Artagnan is? Would he be different if he had grown up in the city?

scabbard  (noun)

A cover or sheath for a sword blade, usually made of leather.

From the story: D'Artagnan's father handed him a sword in a worn leather scabbard — old and plain, but the steel was good.

Think about it: The scabbard is worn but the blade is sharp. How is this like D'Artagnan himself — rough on the outside but strong inside?

Chapter 2: Trouble in Meung

humiliated  (verb/adjective)

Made to feel deeply ashamed or embarrassed, especially in front of others.

From the story: D'Artagnan was humiliated — beaten by the stranger's companions while the man with the scar did not even bother to draw his sword.

Think about it: Why does being humiliated hurt more than being physically hurt? What does this moment do to D'Artagnan's pride?

dignity  (noun)

A sense of pride and self-respect. The quality of being worthy of honor.

From the story: D'Artagnan had lost his letter, his dignity, and very nearly his sword — but he refused to give up.

Think about it: Can you lose your dignity and get it back? How might D'Artagnan rebuild his after this humiliation?

Chapter 3: Three Duels Before Noon

barracks  (noun)

A building or group of buildings where soldiers live and train.

From the story: The headquarters of the King's Musketeers was a grand building near the palace, with a courtyard full of soldiers practicing swordplay.

Think about it: How does the atmosphere of the barracks — confident soldiers, blue cloaks, silver crosses — affect D'Artagnan when he first arrives?

flustered  (adjective)

Nervous, confused, and unable to think clearly because of pressure or embarrassment.

From the story: D'Artagnan was so flustered after accidentally challenging two musketeers that he walked directly into a third one.

Think about it: Being flustered makes D'Artagnan clumsy. Have you ever been so flustered that you made a situation worse by trying to fix it?

Chapter 4: All for One

rivalry  (noun)

An ongoing competition between two groups who consider each other enemies.

From the story: The Cardinal's Guards and the King's Musketeers hated each other. It was the rivalry of the age.

Think about it: What causes rivalries? Are they always bad, or can rivalry push people to be better?

flurry  (noun)

A sudden burst of activity, movement, or action happening all at once.

From the story: D'Artagnan drove the guard backward with a flurry of strikes that surprised even himself.

Think about it: A flurry suggests energy and instinct rather than careful planning. What does fighting in a flurry tell you about D'Artagnan's natural talent?

Chapter 5: The Three Musketeers

extravagant  (adjective)

Spending more than necessary. Over-the-top, showy, and excessive.

From the story: Porthos wore the biggest hats, the shiniest boots, and the most extravagant sword belts in all of Paris.

Think about it: Is being extravagant a flaw or a strength? What does Porthos's extravagance reveal about his personality?

legendary  (adjective)

Famous and admired, often because of remarkable achievements that become the stuff of stories.

From the story: Together, the three musketeers were legendary — the best swordsmen in the king's service and the biggest headache the Cardinal's Guards had ever known.

Think about it: What makes someone legendary? Is it skill alone, or is there something more — loyalty, code, friendship?

Chapter 6: The Captain's Blessing

modesty  (noun)

The quality of not bragging about yourself or your achievements. Being humble.

From the story: The king appreciated D'Artagnan's modesty — it was rare in Paris, where everyone tried to seem more important than they were.

Think about it: D'Artagnan says his friends did most of the work. Is this true modesty, or is it smart strategy? Can it be both?

commission  (noun)

An official document giving someone a military rank or position.

From the story: The king told Tréville to enroll D'Artagnan and, when he had earned it, give him the blue cloak — the commission of a musketeer.

Think about it: Why does the king make D'Artagnan earn the commission instead of giving it to him immediately? What does earning something mean compared to being given it?

Chapter 7: The Queen's Secret

intrigue  (noun)

Secret plotting and scheming, especially in politics. Plans made behind closed doors.

From the story: D'Artagnan stumbled into a world of intrigue — secret plots between queens, cardinals, and dukes that could change the fate of France.

Think about it: Why is political intrigue more dangerous than a sword fight? In intrigue, you often don't know who your enemies are.

consequences  (noun)

The results of an action, especially negative results that follow a mistake.

From the story: If the king discovered his gift was now in England, the consequences for the queen would be terrible.

Think about it: The queen made a small, kind gesture — giving a gift to a friend. But the consequences could be enormous. Can a small action have huge consequences?

Chapter 8: The Cardinal's Plot

intercepted  (verb)

Stopped or caught something or someone before they reached their destination.

From the story: The Cardinal's agents would try to intercept anyone riding for England — blocking roads, setting ambushes, stopping the studs from reaching the queen.

Think about it: In war and politics, intercepting information or messengers can change everything. Why is controlling the road as powerful as controlling the destination?

sacrifice  (noun/verb)

Giving up something valuable for the sake of someone or something more important.

From the story: 'If we are attacked, whoever is still free must keep riding. The mission comes first.' It was a sacrifice they all agreed to make.

Think about it: The musketeers agree to sacrifice themselves so D'Artagnan can complete the mission. What makes someone willing to sacrifice for others?

Chapter 9: The Mission

ambush  (noun/verb)

A surprise attack by people lying in wait in a hidden position.

From the story: Eight men on horseback burst from the trees — a perfectly planned ambush by the Cardinal's agents.

Think about it: An ambush works because the victims don't see it coming. How does the fear of ambush make the entire journey more tense, even in quiet moments?

desperate  (adjective)

Having almost no hope left; willing to try anything because the situation is so bad.

From the story: D'Artagnan rode through the night, desperate and alone, his friends left behind one by one.

Think about it: Desperation can make people reckless — but it can also make them unstoppable. Which is D'Artagnan in this moment?

Chapter 10: D'Artagnan Alone

diplomacy  (noun)

The skill of dealing with people in a sensitive, careful way, especially in politics or between nations.

From the story: Buckingham understood diplomacy — he knew that the diamond studs were not just jewelry but symbols of trust between a queen and a friend.

Think about it: How is diplomacy different from just being polite? When do careful words matter more than swords?

replica  (noun)

An exact copy of something, made to look identical to the original.

From the story: Buckingham's jeweler made two replica diamond studs so perfect that even the queen could not tell them from the originals.

Think about it: A replica looks real but isn't. How does this connect to the bigger theme about appearances in this story?

Chapter 11: The Race to Paris

endurance  (noun)

The ability to keep going through pain, exhaustion, or difficulty without giving up.

From the story: D'Artagnan rode for days without rest, running on nothing but endurance and the knowledge that the queen was counting on him.

Think about it: Endurance is different from strength. Strength wins a fight; endurance wins a journey. Which matters more in this story?

intercepted  (verb (revisited))

You learned this in Chapter 8. Now D'Artagnan is the one being intercepted — agents try to stop him at every town.

From the story: At Boulogne, two men tried to block the road. At Abbeville, a man tried to start a fight. Every attempt to intercept D'Artagnan failed.

Think about it: D'Artagnan avoids every trap. Is this luck, skill, or the fact that he simply refuses to stop for anything?

Chapter 12: The Ball

radiant  (adjective)

Glowing with beauty, confidence, or happiness. Shining brightly.

From the story: The queen entered the ball radiant — her gown was silver and white, and twelve diamond studs blazed around her neck like stars.

Think about it: The queen is radiant because she has been saved. How does inner relief make someone look outwardly beautiful?

foiled  (verb)

Prevented a plan from succeeding. Defeated someone's scheme.

From the story: The Cardinal counted the studs three times. His plan had been foiled — all twelve were there.

Think about it: Being foiled by a teenager from Gascony must be infuriating for the Cardinal. How might this change how Richelieu sees D'Artagnan?

Chapter 13: The Woman in the Shadows

ruthless  (adjective)

Showing no mercy or pity. Willing to do anything to achieve a goal, regardless of who gets hurt.

From the story: Milady was ruthless — she used charm the way others used swords, to get close enough to strike.

Think about it: Why is ruthlessness combined with charm more dangerous than ruthlessness alone? What makes Milady different from a straightforward villain?

deception  (noun)

The act of making someone believe something that is not true. Tricking people deliberately.

From the story: Everything about Milady was deception — the beauty, the gentle voice, the kind smile. All of it was a weapon.

Think about it: How is deception different from lying? Can deception involve telling the truth in a misleading way?

Chapter 14: Athos's Secret

branded  (verb/adjective)

Marked permanently as punishment for a crime, usually with a hot iron.

From the story: Athos discovered his wife had been branded on the shoulder — a mark that revealed her criminal past.

Think about it: A brand is a permanent mark that cannot be hidden forever. How does this connect to the idea that the truth always comes out eventually?

confession  (noun)

The act of telling someone a secret or admitting something painful and private.

From the story: Athos's confession about his past was the deepest secret of a man who kept everything locked inside.

Think about it: Why is confessing a painful secret an act of trust? What does it mean that Athos chose to tell D'Artagnan?

Chapter 15: The Siege of La Rochelle

siege  (noun)

A military operation where an army surrounds a city or fort and waits for the people inside to surrender.

From the story: The siege of La Rochelle would be long and bloody — thousands of soldiers camped outside the walls, waiting.

Think about it: A siege is a test of patience, not just strength. How does the siege test the four friends differently than the ride to England?

endure  (verb)

To suffer through something difficult without giving up. To bear hardship with strength.

From the story: The four friends endured the siege together — the mud, the cold, the danger — and that made all the difference.

Think about it: Athos says 'patience is the hardest kind of strength.' Do you agree? Is enduring harder than fighting?

Chapter 16: The Breakfast in the Bastion

bastion  (noun)

A fortified position or stronghold, especially one that projects outward from a wall or defensive line.

From the story: The ruined bastion sat in no-man's-land, exposed to cannon fire from both sides — the most dangerous place to eat breakfast in France.

Think about it: Why does having breakfast in the most dangerous place possible become an act of courage? How does humor in the face of danger show bravery?

audacity  (noun)

Bold, daring behavior that surprises people, sometimes bordering on recklessness.

From the story: The audacity of eating a casual meal under enemy fire made the breakfast in the bastion the most famous meal of the siege.

Think about it: Is audacity the same as bravery, or is it something wilder? Can audacity be foolish and admirable at the same time?

Chapter 17: Milady's Mission

cunning  (adjective/noun)

Clever in a sneaky, deceptive way. The ability to achieve goals through tricks rather than force.

From the story: Milady was not simply the Cardinal's agent — her cunning meant she always had plans of her own.

Think about it: How is cunning different from intelligence? Can someone be both cunning and intelligent? Is cunning always negative?

patron  (noun)

A powerful person who supports and protects someone, often in exchange for service.

From the story: The Cardinal was Milady's patron — he gave her power and protection, and she carried out his schemes.

Think about it: Is the relationship between patron and agent one of trust or one of use? Does the Cardinal truly trust Milady, or does he just use her?

Chapter 18: The Prisoner

manipulate  (verb)

To control or influence someone cleverly and often unfairly, making them do what you want without realizing it.

From the story: Milady manipulated her young guard with invented tears and false stories of innocence.

Think about it: Manipulation is about making someone believe something that benefits YOU, not them. How can you tell the difference between someone who genuinely needs help and someone who is manipulating you?

calculated  (adjective)

Done deliberately and carefully planned, especially in a cold or strategic way.

From the story: Every word, every tear, every trembling hand was calculated. Milady was performing the role of her life.

Think about it: What makes calculated emotion scarier than genuine emotion? How does knowing that someone's feelings are fake change how you see them?

Chapter 19: The Warning

frantic  (adjective)

Wild with worry, fear, or urgency. Desperate and panicked.

From the story: D'Artagnan was frantic — Milady was free, heading for France, and Constance was in danger.

Think about it: Being frantic can make you act without thinking. Is D'Artagnan's frantic energy helpful or harmful in this moment?

intercepted (revisited)  (verb)

Third time you've seen this word. Information is being intercepted constantly in this story — by spies, messengers, and networks of contacts.

From the story: Aramis's network intercepted messages revealing Milady's location and direction of travel.

Think about it: Information is a weapon in this story. Who controls information controls the outcome. How has intercepting — or failing to intercept — information shaped events throughout the book?

Chapter 20: Too Late

tragedy  (noun)

A very sad event, especially one involving death or suffering. In literature, a story where things end badly despite the hero's best efforts.

From the story: They arrived to find tragedy — Milady had struck, and no amount of riding could undo what had been done.

Think about it: Why do authors include tragedy in adventure stories? What does loss teach characters — and readers — that victory cannot?

vengeance  (noun)

The desire to punish someone who has hurt you or someone you love. Revenge.

From the story: Something harder and more determined appeared in D'Artagnan's eyes — vengeance, cold and clear.

Think about it: Is vengeance the same as justice? Can wanting vengeance be both understandable and dangerous at the same time?

Chapter 21: The Trial of Milady

exile  (noun/verb)

Being forced to leave your country permanently as punishment. Banishment.

From the story: The sentence was exile — Milady would leave France forever, never to return.

Think about it: Is exile mercy or punishment? For someone like Milady, who reinvents herself constantly, is exile even a real consequence?

reckoning  (noun)

A time when someone must face the consequences of their actions. A moment of judgment.

From the story: Athos had been running from a reckoning for years and had finally decided to face it.

Think about it: Why does facing a reckoning require courage? What is Athos finally confronting in this chapter?

Chapter 22: The Cardinal's Offer

adversary  (noun)

An opponent or enemy, especially one you respect.

From the story: The Cardinal recognized D'Artagnan as a worthy adversary — someone who had beaten him fairly.

Think about it: Can you respect your adversary? What does it mean when an enemy offers you a gift instead of punishment?

diplomacy (revisited)  (noun)

You learned this word in Chapter 10. Now D'Artagnan uses diplomacy himself — choosing careful words before the most powerful man in France.

From the story: D'Artagnan said carefully: 'I believe that you and I disagree about how France should be served.' It was the most diplomatic sentence of his life.

Think about it: Compare D'Artagnan's diplomacy here to his recklessness in Chapter 1. How has he changed? Is diplomacy a form of courage?

Chapter 23: One for All

commission (revisited)  (noun)

You learned this word in Chapter 6. Now D'Artagnan holds the actual document in his hands — the thing he has been working toward since leaving Gascony.

From the story: The blank commission sat on the table — a piece of paper that represented everything D'Artagnan had fought for.

Think about it: Why does D'Artagnan try to give the commission away before accepting it? What does this tell you about how he has grown?

ceremony  (noun)

A formal event marking an important occasion, often with rituals and traditions.

From the story: The commissioning ceremony was held in the courtyard, with Monsieur de Tréville himself placing the blue cloak on D'Artagnan's shoulders.

Think about it: Why do ceremonies matter? What does the ritual of receiving the cloak mean beyond just getting a piece of fabric?

Chapter 24: All for One

contentment  (noun)

A deep, quiet feeling of satisfaction and happiness. The feeling that you have everything you need.

From the story: D'Artagnan felt contentment as the four swords met in the fading light — he belonged, and that was enough.

Think about it: Compare D'Artagnan's contentment at the end to his restless ambition at the beginning. Has he gotten what he wanted, or has he discovered that what he wanted was different from what he thought?

legacy  (noun)

Something passed down from the past. The lasting impact of a person's actions and character.

From the story: D'Artagnan drew his father's old sword and raised it to meet the other three — the legacy of a Gascon farmer now carried by a musketeer.

Think about it: D'Artagnan carries his father's sword throughout the entire story. What legacy does his father leave him? What legacy will D'Artagnan leave?
