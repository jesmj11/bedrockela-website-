The Wonderful Wizard of Oz

A BedrockELA Adaptation

Vocabulary Guide — 2 Words Per Chapter (48 Words Total)

Chapter 1: The Cyclone

prairie  (noun)

A large, flat area of grassland with few or no trees.

From the story: Dorothy lived in the middle of the great Kansas prairies, where the land was flat and gray as far as the eye could see.

Think about it: How does living on a flat, empty prairie affect how a person feels? Why might Dorothy feel lonely there?

cyclone  (noun)

A powerful, spinning windstorm that can cause great destruction.

From the story: The cyclone lifted the house right up into the air and carried it away, spinning slowly like a leaf in the wind.

Think about it: Why did Baum choose to start Dorothy's story with a cyclone? What does it do to the story right away?

Chapter 2: The Council with the Munchkins

extraordinary  (adjective)

Very unusual or remarkable. Far beyond what is normal or expected.

From the story: The cyclone had set the house down in a land of extraordinary beauty — bright flowers, green grass, and sparkling water everywhere.

Think about it: What makes something extraordinary instead of just ordinary? Compare Kansas to the land of the Munchkins.

sorceress  (noun)

A woman who practices magic or witchcraft.

From the story: The Witch of the North called Dorothy a noble sorceress, though Dorothy had never done magic in her life.

Think about it: Why do the Munchkins think Dorothy is a sorceress? What does this misunderstanding tell you about how people judge others?

Chapter 3: How Dorothy Saved the Scarecrow

tiresome  (adjective)

Boring and annoying because it lasts too long or happens too often.

From the story: The Scarecrow said it was very tiresome being stuck on a pole all day with nothing to do and no one to talk to.

Think about it: What would be the most tiresome part of being a scarecrow? How does this help you understand why he wants brains?

companion  (noun)

A person or animal you spend time with; a friend who goes with you on a journey.

From the story: Dorothy was glad to have a companion on the road to the Emerald City. Walking alone had been lonely.

Think about it: What makes someone a good companion? What qualities does the Scarecrow bring to the journey?

Chapter 4: The Road Through the Forest

gloomy  (adjective)

Dark, dim, and depressing. A place or mood that feels heavy and sad.

From the story: The forest was gloomy — the trees blocked out the sunlight, and strange sounds echoed through the darkness.

Think about it: How does a gloomy setting make you feel as a reader? Why do authors use dark settings before something important happens?

undergrowth  (noun)

The thick bushes, plants, and small trees that grow beneath the tall trees in a forest.

From the story: They pushed through the undergrowth and found a man made entirely of tin, frozen beside a tree.

Think about it: What details help you picture the undergrowth? How does it make the forest feel more wild and difficult to travel through?

Chapter 5: The Rescue of the Tin Woodman

enchanted  (adjective/verb)

Put under a magical spell. Something that has been changed or affected by magic.

From the story: The Wicked Witch enchanted the Tin Woodman's axe so that it would slip and cut him, piece by piece, until nothing was left of his real body.

Think about it: The word enchanted usually sounds magical and wonderful. How does this story show that enchantment can also be cruel?

tenderly  (adverb)

In a gentle, caring, loving way.

From the story: The Tin Woodman said he once loved a girl tenderly, but without a heart, he could no longer feel love.

Think about it: Why is it sad that someone who used to feel tenderly now claims he cannot feel at all? Do you think he is right about himself?

Chapter 6: The Cowardly Lion

courage  (noun)

The ability to do something even when you are afraid. Being brave when it is hard.

From the story: The Lion said he had no courage, yet he leaped out of the trees to frighten the travelers — which took courage in itself.

Think about it: Is courage the same as not being afraid? Or is courage doing something EVEN THOUGH you are afraid? What do you think?

contradiction  (noun)

When two things are opposite or do not match — when someone says one thing but does another.

From the story: The Lion is a contradiction: he calls himself a coward, but his actions show bravery again and again.

Think about it: Can you find other contradictions in the story? Think about the Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman too.

Chapter 7: The Journey to the Great Oz

comrade  (noun)

A close companion who shares a journey or challenge with you. Like a teammate on an adventure.

From the story: The four travelers became true comrades as they faced dangers together on the road.

Think about it: What is the difference between a friend and a comrade? How do shared challenges make people closer?

obstacle  (noun)

Something that blocks your path or makes progress difficult. A challenge you must overcome.

From the story: The great ditch was the first serious obstacle on the road to the Emerald City.

Think about it: Every adventure story has obstacles. Why are obstacles important to a story? What would happen if the road were easy?

Chapter 8: The Deadly Poppy Field

poisonous  (adjective)

Containing something harmful that can make you sick or cause damage, even if it looks safe.

From the story: The beautiful poppies were poisonous — their sweet scent put any living creature into a sleep so deep they might never wake.

Think about it: Why is it MORE dangerous when something harmful looks beautiful? How does this connect to the theme that things are not always what they seem?

desperate  (adjective)

Feeling that a situation is so bad that you will try anything to fix it. Having almost no hope left.

From the story: The Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman were desperate to save their friends from the deadly flowers.

Think about it: When you are desperate, you sometimes find strength you did not know you had. How does desperation help the Scarecrow and Tin Woodman here?

Chapter 9: The Queen of the Field Mice

debt  (noun)

Something you owe someone because they helped you. An obligation to repay a kindness.

From the story: The Queen of the Field Mice said her subjects were forever in the Tin Woodman's debt for saving her life.

Think about it: Is a debt of kindness the same as a money debt? How do people repay acts of kindness in real life?

scheme  (noun/verb)

A clever plan for getting something done, especially one that involves many steps.

From the story: The Scarecrow came up with a scheme to rescue the Lion: build a cart and have the mice pull it out of the poppy field.

Think about it: The Scarecrow says he has no brains, but he keeps coming up with schemes. What does this tell you?

Chapter 10: The Guardian of the Gates

dazzling  (adjective)

So bright and impressive that it is almost overwhelming. Something that amazes your eyes.

From the story: The Emerald City was dazzling — everything glittered with green light so bright it almost hurt to look at.

Think about it: Why might the author make the Emerald City SO dazzling? Remember gray Kansas. What is the contrast telling you?

spectacles  (noun)

Glasses worn over the eyes. In the Emerald City, green spectacles make everything appear green.

From the story: The Guardian locked green spectacles onto every visitor's head so the brightness of the city would not blind them.

Think about it: If everyone wears green glasses, how do they know what color things REALLY are? What might the author be saying about appearances?

Chapter 11: The Great and Terrible Oz

terrible  (adjective)

In this book, terrible means causing great fear or awe — not just bad. The Wizard is called Great and Terrible because he is powerful and frightening.

From the story: Oz, the Great and Terrible, appeared as a giant floating head with rolling eyes and a booming voice.

Think about it: Why would someone WANT to be called terrible? What is the advantage of making people afraid of you?

chamber  (noun)

A large room, especially one used for official or important purposes.

From the story: Dorothy entered the vast throne chamber alone, her footsteps echoing off the emerald-studded walls.

Think about it: How does the huge, empty chamber make Dorothy feel? How do settings affect the way characters — and readers — feel?

Chapter 12: The Search for the Wicked Witch

domain  (noun)

The territory or area that someone rules or controls.

From the story: The Wicked Witch could see everything that happened in her domain with her one powerful eye.

Think about it: What makes a domain different from just a place? What does it mean to have power over an entire region?

cunning  (adjective)

Clever in a sneaky or deceptive way. Good at getting what you want through tricks.

From the story: The Wicked Witch was cunning — she did not attack all at once but sent her forces one wave at a time.

Think about it: Is cunning the same as smart? Can cunning be used for good, or is it always a villain's quality?

Chapter 13: Dorothy the Prisoner

captive  (noun/adjective)

A person who is held prisoner and cannot leave. Trapped against your will.

From the story: Dorothy was a captive in the Witch's castle, forced to cook and clean from morning until night.

Think about it: How does being a captive test Dorothy's character? What does she do that shows she has not given up?

defiant  (adjective)

Boldly refusing to obey or submit. Standing up against someone more powerful than you.

From the story: Even as a prisoner, Dorothy remained defiant — she refused to be broken by the Witch's cruelty.

Think about it: Where does Dorothy's defiance come from? Is defiance a kind of courage?

Chapter 14: The Melting of the Witch

dissolved  (verb)

Melted away or broke apart into nothing. Disappeared completely.

From the story: The Wicked Witch dissolved into a brown puddle on the kitchen floor, destroyed by nothing more than a bucket of water.

Think about it: The most powerful villain in the story is destroyed by something ordinary. What might the author be saying about the nature of evil?

liberated  (verb/adjective)

Set free from imprisonment or control. Released from someone's power.

From the story: The Winkies were liberated from the Witch's rule and celebrated with tears of joy.

Think about it: How do the Winkies change once they are liberated? What does freedom look like after years of fear?

Chapter 15: The Winged Monkeys

bound  (adjective/verb)

Tied or held by a force you cannot escape. Obligated to obey because of a rule or spell.

From the story: The Winged Monkeys were bound by the magic of the Golden Cap — they had no choice but to obey whoever wore it.

Think about it: Is it fair to punish an entire group for one person's mistake? What does the monkeys' story say about justice?

suspicious  (adjective)

Feeling that something is wrong or not as it seems. Having doubts about whether something is true.

From the story: Dorothy grew suspicious when the monkey king told her that the great Wizard of Oz had been afraid of them.

Think about it: What clues have been building throughout the story that something is not right about the Wizard?

Chapter 16: The Discovery of Oz the Terrible

humbug  (noun)

A fraud or fake. Someone who pretends to be something they are not.

From the story: The great and powerful Oz was nothing but a humbug — a little old man from Omaha hiding behind tricks and machines.

Think about it: Is a humbug always a bad person? Can someone be a fraud and still do some good?

ventriloquism  (noun)

The art of making your voice seem to come from somewhere else, like a puppet or another location.

From the story: The Wizard used ventriloquism to make his booming voice seem to come from the giant head, the fairy, and the ball of fire.

Think about it: The Wizard's power was all about controlling what people saw and heard. What does this say about the difference between real power and the appearance of power?

Chapter 17: The Magic of Oz

symbol  (noun)

An object that represents or stands for something else. Something physical that carries a deeper meaning.

From the story: The brains, heart, and courage the Wizard gave were only symbols — bran and pins, silk and sawdust, colored water — but they helped the characters believe in themselves.

Think about it: Can a symbol have real power even if it is not magic? Why did the symbols work for the Scarecrow, Tin Woodman, and Lion?

confidence  (noun)

The feeling of believing in yourself and your abilities. Trust in your own power.

From the story: What the Wizard really gave them was confidence — the belief that they already had what they were looking for.

Think about it: Is confidence something someone can give you, or must you find it yourself? How does the Wizard's trick actually work?

Chapter 18: The Balloon

inflate  (verb)

To fill something with air or gas so it expands and rises.

From the story: The great green silk balloon was inflated with hot air until it swelled and rose, tugging at its ropes.

Think about it: The balloon is the Wizard's last trick — made of silk and hot air, just like his other illusions. Is this fitting?

vanished  (verb)

Disappeared suddenly and completely, as if into thin air.

From the story: The balloon rose into the sky and vanished, leaving Dorothy behind with no way to get home.

Think about it: How does it feel when something you were counting on vanishes? How does Dorothy handle this moment of lost hope?

Chapter 19: The Journey South

untamed  (adjective)

Wild and not controlled by people. Land or creatures that have not been civilized.

From the story: South of the Emerald City, the land grew untamed — no roads, no fences, just wild grass and tangled forest.

Think about it: How is untamed land both dangerous and beautiful? Compare the untamed south to the controlled Emerald City.

recoiled  (verb)

Pulled back quickly in fear, pain, or surprise.

From the story: The fighting trees recoiled when the Tin Woodman chopped their branches, pulling their limbs tight against their trunks.

Think about it: Even something powerful recoils when it meets something stronger. What makes the Tin Woodman effective against the trees?

Chapter 20: The Dainty China Country

fragile  (adjective)

Easily broken or damaged. Delicate and requiring gentle handling.

From the story: The china people were fragile — so thin and delicate that a single careless step could shatter them forever.

Think about it: What things in real life are fragile? People's feelings? Trust? Friendships? How does the china country connect to bigger ideas?

dainty  (adjective)

Small, delicate, and pretty. Something made with fine, careful detail.

From the story: The dainty china houses had tiny painted shutters and miniature flower boxes no bigger than a thimble.

Think about it: The chapter is called The Dainty China Country. Why is dainty a better word than small here? What feeling does it create?

Chapter 21: The Country of the Quadlings

earnest  (adjective)

Serious, sincere, and determined. Showing that you truly mean what you say.

From the story: Dorothy spoke to Glinda in earnest, telling her story simply and honestly without exaggeration.

Think about it: After meeting a humbug wizard who was all tricks, how is Glinda's honesty different? Why does earnestness matter?

radiant  (adjective)

Shining brightly. Glowing with light, beauty, or happiness.

From the story: Glinda was radiant — her warm eyes and gentle smile made Dorothy feel safe for the first time in weeks.

Think about it: Compare Glinda's radiance to the Wizard's dazzling tricks. What is the difference between real light and fake light?

Chapter 22: Glinda Grants Dorothy's Wish

destiny  (noun)

The future path or purpose that seems meant for someone. Where you are supposed to end up.

From the story: Each of Dorothy's friends found their destiny: the Scarecrow as ruler, the Tin Woodman as emperor, the Lion as king.

Think about it: Did Dorothy's friends find their destinies because of magic, or because of who they already were? What is the difference?

farewell  (noun/interjection)

A goodbye, especially one that is emotional or final. The act of saying goodbye when you may not see someone again.

From the story: The farewells were the hardest part — saying goodbye to friends who had become family.

Think about it: Why are farewells so difficult? What makes a goodbye painful even when you are going somewhere you want to be?

Chapter 23: There's No Place Like Home

reveal  (noun/verb)

To show or make known something that was hidden. A moment when the truth becomes clear.

From the story: Glinda's reveal about the silver shoes was the biggest surprise of all — Dorothy had the power to go home the entire time.

Think about it: Why did Glinda wait to reveal the truth about the shoes? Was it kind or cruel to let Dorothy travel so far first?

bittersweet  (adjective)

Something that is both happy and sad at the same time. Joy mixed with sorrow.

From the story: Leaving Oz was bittersweet — Dorothy was overjoyed to go home, but heartbroken to leave her friends.

Think about it: Can you think of a bittersweet moment in your own life? Why are the most meaningful moments often bittersweet?

Chapter 24: Home Again

embrace  (noun/verb)

To hold someone closely in your arms. A hug that shows deep love or relief.

From the story: Aunt Em swept Dorothy into a fierce embrace, holding her so tightly that Toto yelped between them.

Think about it: Why does a physical embrace mean so much after a long separation? What does Aunt Em's embrace tell Dorothy?

contentment  (noun)

A deep, quiet feeling of happiness and satisfaction. The feeling that you have everything you need.

From the story: Dorothy felt a deep contentment settle inside her as she sat at the kitchen table eating warm bread — she was home, and that was enough.

Think about it: Contentment is different from excitement or thrill. Why is contentment the right word for Dorothy's ending instead of something louder like joy or triumph?
