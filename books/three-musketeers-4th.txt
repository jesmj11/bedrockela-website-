The Three

Musketeers

A BedrockELA Adaptation

Based on the novel by Alexandre Dumas (1844)

Adapted for 4th–5th Grade Readers

Chapter 1: The Boy from Gascony

On the first Monday of April, in the year 1625, a young man rode into the town of Meung on the most ridiculous horse anyone had ever seen.

The horse was yellow. Not golden, not amber — yellow, like a sunflower that had been left in the rain too long. It was old, bony, and walked with a peculiar sideways shuffle, as though it were not entirely sure how legs worked. Its mane stuck out in clumps, and its tail looked like it had been chewed on by something that was not very particular about its food.

The young man on the horse was no more impressive — at first glance. He was eighteen years old, tall and thin, with a long face tanned brown by the sun. He wore clothes that had once been fine but were now faded and patched in several places. At his side hung an enormous sword that was far too big for him. On his head sat a hat that might have been fashionable ten years earlier, in a town that had never been fashionable at all.

His name was D'Artagnan, and he had come from Gascony — a rough, wild province in the south of France where the people were known for three things: their pride, their tempers, and their absolute refusal to be impressed by anyone or anything.

D'Artagnan had all three qualities in abundance.

That morning, his father had stood in the doorway of their small stone farmhouse and given him three gifts. The first was the yellow horse. The second was fifteen gold coins — every coin the family could spare. The third was a piece of advice.

"My son," his father had said, gripping D'Artagnan's shoulders with both hands, "you are going to Paris to seek your fortune. You will present yourself to Monsieur de Tréville, the captain of the King's Musketeers. He is an old friend of mine, from the days when I was young and foolish and carried a sword for the king. Tell him your name. He will remember me."

"Yes, Father."

"You will face many challenges in Paris. Men will try to cheat you, insult you, and fight you. Here is what you must know: never run from a fight, never tolerate an insult, and never — never — betray a friend. Do you understand?"

"Yes, Father."

"One more thing." His father reached behind the door and brought out a sword in a worn leather scabbard. "This was mine. It is old and plain, but the steel is good. Carry it with honor."

D'Artagnan took the sword and drew it. The blade was long and straight, nicked in places from old battles, but still sharp. It felt heavy in his hand — heavier than any sword he had practiced with. But he held it steady and nodded.

"I will make you proud," he said.

His father's eyes glistened. "You already have."

His mother had cried, of course. She had pressed a small pouch of herbs into his hand — lavender, for luck — and kissed both his cheeks and told him to eat properly and wear clean shirts. D'Artagnan had promised everything, knowing he would probably forget most of it by the second day.

And so he rode north, on his ridiculous yellow horse, with his father's sword and his mother's lavender and fifteen gold coins and a heart full of fire, toward the greatest city in the world.

Paris.

Where the King lived, and the musketeers served, and a young man from nowhere could become someone who mattered.

D'Artagnan did not know, as he rode through the April sunshine, that he was about to walk straight into trouble. He did not know that before the day was over, he would be humiliated, robbed of his pride, and left burning with a desire for revenge that would change the course of his life.

But that was the thing about D'Artagnan. Trouble did not frighten him.

Trouble was exactly what he was looking for.

Chapter 2: Trouble in Meung

D'Artagnan stopped at an inn in Meung to water his horse and eat something before the final stretch to Paris. He tied the yellow horse to a post and walked inside, trying to look confident and worldly, which is difficult to do when you are eighteen and your clothes are patched.

He was sitting by the window eating bread and cheese when he heard laughter from the courtyard.

He looked out. Three men stood around his horse, pointing and snickering. The tallest of them — a dark-haired man with a scar on his cheek and an air of cold authority — was describing the horse to his companions with obvious amusement.

"I have never seen such a creature," the man said. "It is the color of a dandelion and appears to move by accident rather than intention."

The other men roared with laughter.

D'Artagnan's blood went hot. His father's voice echoed in his head: Never tolerate an insult.

He threw open the inn door and marched into the courtyard, his hand on his sword. "Sir," he said, his voice shaking with anger, "you are mocking my horse."

The man with the scar turned slowly and looked D'Artagnan up and down. His eyes were sharp and cold, like a hawk studying a mouse. He did not seem impressed.

"I am mocking your horse," the man agreed pleasantly. "It deserves to be mocked."

"That horse was a gift from my father," D'Artagnan said. "And any insult to it is an insult to me."

"Then you are easily insulted," the man said. He turned back to his companions as though D'Artagnan did not exist.

D'Artagnan drew his sword.

What happened next was fast and painful. The man with the scar did not even bother to draw his own weapon. His two companions seized D'Artagnan from behind, knocked the sword from his hand, and beat him with the flats of their blades until he fell to the ground. The innkeeper's servants came running out and dragged D'Artagnan inside, where they dumped him on the kitchen floor.

When D'Artagnan came to his senses, his head was throbbing, his ribs ached, and his pride hurt worst of all. He had been beaten — humiliated — in front of everyone, and the man with the scar had not even considered him worth fighting.

He staggered to the window just in time to see the man mounting a fine black horse. A woman leaned from a carriage window and spoke to him. She was beautiful — pale, with golden hair and eyes that glittered like cold jewels. The man bowed to her, and they exchanged words that D'Artagnan could not hear.

Then they were gone.

D'Artagnan burned. He did not know the man's name. He did not know the woman's name. But he memorized everything: the scar, the cold eyes, the black horse, the woman in the carriage. He would find them again. And when he did, things would be different.

He reached for his father's letter of introduction to Monsieur de Tréville — the letter that was supposed to open doors for him in Paris — and his stomach dropped.

It was gone. Stolen during the beating.

D'Artagnan sank onto a bench and put his head in his hands. He had been in the world for exactly one day, and he had already lost his letter, his dignity, and very nearly his sword. He had nothing left but fifteen coins, a yellow horse, and his father's advice.

Never run from a fight. Never tolerate an insult. Never betray a friend.

"Fine," D'Artagnan said aloud to the empty kitchen. "Paris it is."

He stood up, straightened his battered hat, and walked back out into the daylight. The yellow horse was still tied to the post, looking no worse than before — which was not saying much.

"Come on," D'Artagnan told the horse. "We have work to do."

The horse shuffled sideways and sneezed.

They rode for Paris.

Chapter 3: Three Duels Before Noon

Paris was enormous.

D'Artagnan had never seen so many people in one place. The streets were packed with carriages, horses, merchants, soldiers, beggars, and nobles in silk and lace. Buildings rose three and four stories high, and the noise was overwhelming — the clatter of hooves on cobblestone, the shouts of vendors, the barking of dogs, and underneath it all, a constant hum of life that never stopped.

D'Artagnan sold the yellow horse for three gold coins — which was, honestly, more than it was worth — and found a tiny room above a shop that he could afford for a week. Then he put on his least-patched clothes, strapped on his father's sword, and went to find Monsieur de Tréville.

The headquarters of the King's Musketeers was a grand building near the palace. The courtyard was full of musketeers — tall, confident men in blue cloaks with silver crosses on the chest. They practiced swordplay, polished their boots, and told stories of their adventures. D'Artagnan watched them with burning envy. These were the finest soldiers in France. And he was a nobody from Gascony with no letter of introduction.

But D'Artagnan was not the sort of person who let small problems like having nothing stop him. He walked straight up to the front door and asked to see the captain.

To his shock, Monsieur de Tréville agreed to see him. The captain was a serious man with sharp eyes and a military bearing. He listened as D'Artagnan explained who he was — the son of an old comrade, come to Paris to serve the king.

"I remember your father," Tréville said. "He was a good man and a fine swordsman. I would have welcomed his letter, but I believe your story. I can offer you a place in the guards — not the musketeers, mind you, but the guards. Earn your place, and perhaps one day you will wear the blue cloak."

D'Artagnan's heart soared. It was not everything he wanted, but it was a start.

He was leaving Tréville's office, practically floating with excitement, when he glanced out a window and saw something that turned his blood to ice.

The man with the scar.

Walking calmly through the street below, as though he had every right to exist in the same city as D'Artagnan. The man who had humiliated him in Meung, who had stolen his letter, who had laughed at his horse and his sword and his pride.

D'Artagnan did not think. He bolted down the stairs, crashed through the door, and sprinted into the street after the man — and in his haste, he slammed directly into a musketeer who was walking up the steps.

The musketeer was tall, dark-haired, and carried himself with quiet dignity. He winced sharply when D'Artagnan hit his shoulder.

"Forgive me!" D'Artagnan gasped. "I am in a hurry —"

"You hit my shoulder," the musketeer said through clenched teeth. His face was pale with pain. "That shoulder was wounded in a duel last week, and you have just made it worse."

"I am sorry, truly —"

"Sorry is not enough," the musketeer said coldly. "I will expect you at noon, behind the Luxembourg Palace. Bring your sword."

A duel. D'Artagnan had been challenged to a duel by a musketeer. He had been in Paris less than a day.

"I will be there," D'Artagnan said, because he had promised his father he would never run from a fight.

He continued chasing the man with the scar, but the man had vanished into the crowd. Cursing, D'Artagnan turned a corner too fast and ran straight into another musketeer — a large, broad-shouldered man in an enormous plumed hat who was showing off his new sword belt to a friend.

D'Artagnan's foot caught the edge of the belt, and the elaborate gold clasp snapped clean off.

"My belt!" the big musketeer roared. "Do you know how much that cost?"

"I am terribly sorry —"

"One o'clock," the big musketeer snarled. "Behind the Luxembourg. And prepare to pay for that belt with your blood."

Two duels. D'Artagnan staggered away, his head spinning. He was so flustered that he walked directly into a third musketeer — a slim, handsome man with gentle eyes who was reading a book of poetry.

D'Artagnan stepped on the man's foot.

"Ow," said the musketeer mildly.

"I am so sorry," D'Artagnan said.

"Two o'clock," the man said, still polite but firm. "Behind the Luxembourg. I trust you know the place by now."

Three duels with three musketeers in one morning. D'Artagnan walked back to his tiny room and sat on his bed and stared at the wall.

He was going to die in Paris on his very first day.

Chapter 4: All for One

At noon, D'Artagnan walked behind the Luxembourg Palace and found the first musketeer — the tall, dark-haired one with the wounded shoulder — waiting for him in a quiet clearing among the trees.

"You came," the musketeer said. He seemed mildly surprised.

"I gave my word," D'Artagnan said. He drew his father's sword, and though his hand trembled slightly, his voice did not.

"My name is Athos," the musketeer said. "And I should warn you — even with a wounded shoulder, I am a very good swordsman."

"My name is D'Artagnan of Gascony. And I should warn you — I have nothing to lose."

Athos almost smiled. "Fair enough."

They raised their swords and were about to begin when two more figures appeared through the trees. It was the big musketeer with the plumed hat and the gentle one with the book of poetry.

"Porthos!" Athos said. "Aramis! What are you doing here?"

"I have a duel at one o'clock," Porthos said, jerking his thumb at D'Artagnan. "With this boy."

"And I have one at two," Aramis added pleasantly. "Also with this boy."

The three musketeers looked at D'Artagnan, and then at each other, and then they began to laugh. Even Athos, who did not seem like a man who laughed often, allowed himself a rare smile.

"You challenged all three of us?" Porthos said. "In one morning?"

"It was not entirely on purpose," D'Artagnan admitted.

"I like him," Porthos declared.

"He stepped on my foot," Aramis said. "But I am willing to be charitable."

"Well," Athos said, raising his sword again. "Shall we?"

But before the first blow could be struck, five men in red cloaks appeared at the edge of the clearing. They were guards of the Cardinal — Cardinal Richelieu, the most powerful man in France besides the king himself. The Cardinal's Guards and the King's Musketeers hated each other. It was the rivalry of the age.

"Well, well," said the leader of the guards, a sneer curling his lip. "Three musketeers about to fight a boy. How heroic."

"This is a private matter," Athos said quietly.

"Nothing is private from the Cardinal," the guard replied. "Dueling is forbidden by order of the Cardinal himself. Lay down your swords, or we will arrest you all."

There was a moment of silence. Athos, Porthos, and Aramis exchanged a look — the kind of look that passes between people who have stood together many times before.

"There are five of them," Porthos observed.

"And four of us," Aramis said.

"Three of us," Athos corrected. "The boy is not a musketeer."

D'Artagnan stepped forward. His heart was pounding so hard he could feel it in his throat. His father's sword was heavy in his hand. These were trained soldiers — real fighters, not farmboys. He had no business being here.

But he planted his feet and raised his sword.

"I may not be a musketeer," he said. "But I am on your side."

Athos looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded.

"All for one," Athos said quietly.

"And one for all," Porthos and Aramis answered together.

The fight was wild and furious. Steel rang against steel. D'Artagnan found himself face to face with a guard twice his age and far more experienced. The man was fast — faster than anyone D'Artagnan had ever practiced against. But D'Artagnan was young, and quick, and burning with the kind of desperate energy that comes from having absolutely everything to prove.

He ducked under a swing, spun to the side, and drove the guard backward with a flurry of strikes that surprised even himself. The guard stumbled, and D'Artagnan knocked the sword from his hand.

Meanwhile, Athos fought with cold precision despite his wounded shoulder — every movement efficient, every strike perfectly placed. Porthos fought like a charging bull, roaring and swinging his massive sword in great arcs that sent guards scrambling. Aramis fought elegantly, almost gracefully, as though the whole thing were a dance.

In five minutes, it was over. The Cardinal's Guards were defeated — disarmed, bruised, and thoroughly embarrassed.

D'Artagnan stood panting, his sword arm shaking, his heart hammering. He had fought alongside the King's Musketeers. And he had held his own.

Athos sheathed his sword and looked at D'Artagnan with new eyes. "You fight well," he said. "Who taught you?"

"My father," D'Artagnan said.

"He taught you well." Athos extended his hand. "You are no longer our enemy, D'Artagnan. You are our friend."

Porthos clapped D'Artagnan on the back so hard he nearly fell over. "Welcome to the family, boy!"

Aramis smiled and offered D'Artagnan a handkerchief to wipe the sweat from his face. "You have blood on your cheek," he said. "It is not a good look for dinner."

D'Artagnan looked at the three men — Athos, noble and serious; Porthos, enormous and joyful; Aramis, gentle and sharp — and felt something click into place inside his chest. He had left Gascony alone. He was alone no longer.

"All for one," he said.

"And one for all," they answered.

Chapter 5: The Three Musketeers

Over the next few weeks, D'Artagnan learned everything he could about his three new friends.

Athos was the oldest of the three, and the most mysterious. He was tall, handsome, and carried himself like a nobleman — which, D'Artagnan suspected, he secretly was. Athos rarely spoke about his past. When the others told stories around the fire, Athos listened with a faint, sad smile, as though he were remembering something painful. He drank more wine than was good for him, and sometimes D'Artagnan found him sitting alone in the dark, staring at nothing.

But when Athos drew his sword, every trace of sadness disappeared. He became something magnificent — precise, controlled, and utterly fearless. He was the finest swordsman D'Artagnan had ever seen, and D'Artagnan's father had been very good indeed.

"Athos does not fight because he enjoys it," Aramis told D'Artagnan once. "He fights because it is the one thing that makes him forget."

D'Artagnan wanted to ask what Athos was trying to forget, but something in Aramis's eyes told him not to.

Porthos was the opposite of Athos in nearly every way. Where Athos was quiet, Porthos was loud. Where Athos was spare and elegant, Porthos was enormous and extravagant. He wore the biggest hats, the shiniest boots, and the most ridiculous sword belts in all of Paris. He told stories that grew bigger every time he told them, and he laughed at his own jokes louder than anyone else.

But beneath the bluster, Porthos had a heart the size of his appetite — which was considerable. He would give his last coin to a friend in need, share his last meal with a stranger, and fight anyone who threatened someone smaller than himself. He was vain, yes. He was loud, certainly. But he was also loyal to his bones.

Aramis was the most puzzling of the three. He was slim, soft-spoken, and always impeccably dressed. He read poetry. He wrote letters in beautiful handwriting. He spoke of becoming a priest someday and leaving the world of swords behind.

And yet, when the fighting started, Aramis was as deadly as any of them. He fought with a quiet, lethal grace that belied his gentle manner. He was also the cleverest of the three — the one who noticed details the others missed, who read between the lines, who saw the trap before anyone walked into it.

"Aramis is the most dangerous man I know," Athos said once, in a rare moment of openness. "Because no one ever suspects him."

Together, the three musketeers were legendary. They were the best swordsmen in the king's service, the most loyal friends in all of France, and the biggest headache the Cardinal's Guards had ever known. They moved through Paris like a force of nature — fighting, feasting, and living by a code that was simple and unbreakable.

All for one, and one for all.

It meant that if one of them was in trouble, all of them came. If one was insulted, all were insulted. If one needed money, the others emptied their pockets. If one was challenged to a duel, three swords appeared at his side.

D'Artagnan was not yet a musketeer — he was only a cadet in the guards, the lowest rung of the military ladder. He had no blue cloak, no silver cross, no official rank. But the three musketeers treated him as an equal. They invited him to their meals, included him in their adventures, and taught him everything they knew about swordsmanship, honor, and how to survive in a city where everyone was plotting against everyone else.

"You are one of us," Porthos told him simply. "The cloak will come later. The friendship is now."

D'Artagnan trained every day. He practiced with Athos, who taught him precision and control. He sparred with Porthos, who taught him power and endurance. He studied with Aramis, who taught him strategy and observation.

He was getting better. Faster. Stronger. More confident.

But Paris was a dangerous city, and the greatest danger of all was not in the streets. It was in the palace, where a man in red robes pulled the strings of power like a puppet master.

His name was Cardinal Richelieu, and he was about to change D'Artagnan's life forever.

Chapter 6: The Captain's Blessing

The fame of the fight behind the Luxembourg Palace spread quickly. Four men against five of the Cardinal's Guards — and the four had won without losing a single sword. The story grew with each telling, as stories do. By the end of the week, some people claimed it had been four against ten. By the end of the month, four against twenty.

The king himself heard about it.

King Louis XIII was a complicated man. He depended on Cardinal Richelieu to run the government, but he secretly resented the Cardinal's power. The musketeers were HIS men — loyal to the crown, not to the church — and when they embarrassed the Cardinal's Guards, the king was quietly delighted.

"Bring me this young Gascon," the king told Monsieur de Tréville. "I want to meet the boy who fought alongside my musketeers."

D'Artagnan was terrified. He had never been inside a palace, never stood before a king, never done anything more grand than herd goats in Gascony. He put on his best clothes — which were not very good — and tried to comb his hair into something respectable. Porthos lent him a hat. Aramis lent him a clean collar. Athos simply said, "Stand straight and tell the truth. That is all any king can ask."

The palace was overwhelming. Gold everywhere — on the walls, the ceilings, the frames of every mirror. Courtiers in silk and velvet whispered behind their hands. D'Artagnan felt like the yellow horse all over again: out of place and slightly ridiculous.

But when he stood before the king, something changed inside him. He stopped being afraid. He remembered his father's words: never run from a fight. This was not a fight with swords, but it was a fight nonetheless — a fight to prove that he belonged.

"You are the boy from Gascony?" the king asked. He was younger than D'Artagnan expected, with a thin face and sharp, curious eyes.

"Yes, Your Majesty."

"They tell me you fought five of the Cardinal's Guards alongside three of my best musketeers. Is this true?"

"It is, Your Majesty. Though I must say, Athos, Porthos, and Aramis did most of the work. I merely did my best not to get in their way."

The king smiled. Modesty was rare in Paris, and he appreciated it.

"Tréville tells me you wish to become a musketeer," the king said.

"More than anything, Your Majesty."

"You are young. You are untested. You have no family name, no fortune, and no connections. Why should I allow it?"

D'Artagnan thought for a moment. Then he said, "Because I will never stop trying, Your Majesty. I may not be the best swordsman in France — not yet. But I will work harder than anyone else, fight braver than anyone else, and be more loyal than anyone else. I may have nothing, but I will give everything."

The room was very quiet.

The king nodded slowly. "Tréville," he said. "Enroll this boy in your company of guards. Let him train. Let him prove himself. And when he has earned it — give him the blue cloak."

D'Artagnan's heart nearly burst.

Monsieur de Tréville placed a hand on D'Artagnan's shoulder as they left the throne room. "You did well," the captain said. "The king does not give praise easily. Earn that cloak, and you will have a place in this company for life."

That evening, D'Artagnan sat with Athos, Porthos, and Aramis in a small tavern near the barracks. The fire was warm. The food was simple — bread, cheese, and soup. But D'Artagnan felt richer than any king.

"To D'Artagnan," Porthos said, raising his cup. "The fourth musketeer."

"I'm not a musketeer yet," D'Artagnan protested.

"You will be," Athos said. And the way he said it — quiet, certain, without a shred of doubt — made D'Artagnan believe it too.

They raised their cups together.

"All for one," D'Artagnan said.

"And one for all," they answered.

Outside, the lights of Paris glittered in the darkness, and somewhere in the shadows of the city, a man in red robes was making plans that would test the four friends more than any sword fight ever could.

Chapter 7: The Queen's Secret

Cardinal Richelieu was the most powerful man in France.

He was not the king — that was Louis XIII. But everyone knew that Richelieu held the real power. He controlled the army, the treasury, and the network of spies that stretched across Europe like a web. He was brilliant, ruthless, and absolutely convinced that everything he did was for the good of France.

The king respected Richelieu. He also feared him, just a little. And the queen — Anne of Austria, who had come from Spain to marry Louis — feared the Cardinal most of all. Because the Cardinal did not trust her. She was Spanish, and France and Spain were enemies, and Richelieu believed that anyone with divided loyalties was dangerous.

He was not entirely wrong. The queen had a secret.

She had become friends with the Duke of Buckingham, an English nobleman who had visited Paris on a diplomatic mission. The friendship was innocent — the queen was lonely, far from her homeland, and Buckingham was kind and charming. But in the world of kings and cardinals, an innocent friendship between a French queen and an English duke was the most dangerous thing imaginable.

As a token of friendship, the queen had given the duke a gift: twelve diamond studs, set in blue velvet, that the king had given her on their wedding day. It was a foolish thing to do. If the king discovered that his gift was now in England, the consequences for the queen would be terrible.

D'Artagnan stumbled into this world of secrets by accident.

His new lodgings were above the house of a man named Bonacieux, a mild-mannered cloth merchant whose wife worked as a seamstress for the queen. Constance Bonacieux was clever, brave, and devoted to the queen. When the queen needed help, Constance was the one she turned to.

One evening, D'Artagnan heard a commotion downstairs. He crept to the landing and saw two men dragging Constance Bonacieux toward the door. She was struggling and calling for help.

D'Artagnan did not hesitate. He drew his sword, vaulted down the stairs, and confronted the men. They were agents of the Cardinal — D'Artagnan recognized their red cloaks. He fought them fiercely, and they fled into the night, leaving Constance behind.

"Thank you," Constance said, catching her breath. Her eyes were bright and sharp despite her fear. "You are very brave."

"What did they want with you?" D'Artagnan asked.

Constance hesitated. Then, sensing that D'Artagnan could be trusted, she told him about the diamond studs.

"The queen gave them to Buckingham as a sign of friendship," Constance explained. "The Cardinal has discovered this. He has convinced the king to hold a great ball and to insist that the queen wear the diamond studs. If she cannot produce them, the king will know she gave them away. It will be a disaster."

"When is the ball?" D'Artagnan asked.

"In twelve days."

"And the studs are in England?"

"With the Duke of Buckingham, in London."

D'Artagnan's mind raced. London was far — across the English Channel. The roads between Paris and the coast were controlled by the Cardinal's agents. Anyone trying to reach Buckingham would be hunted every step of the way.

It was impossibly dangerous. It was almost certainly a trap. It was exactly the kind of mission that could get a young man killed.

D'Artagnan grinned.

"I'll go," he said.

Chapter 8: The Cardinal's Plot

D'Artagnan went straight to his friends.

He found them in their usual tavern, Athos brooding over a cup of wine, Porthos admiring his reflection in a spoon, and Aramis composing a letter that was certainly not about theology.

"I need your help," D'Artagnan said. "All three of you."

He told them everything — the queen, the diamond studs, the Cardinal's trap, the ball in twelve days, the need to reach London and back before time ran out.

When he finished, there was a long silence.

"This is extremely dangerous," Athos said.

"Yes," D'Artagnan agreed.

"The roads will be full of the Cardinal's agents," Aramis added. "He has spies everywhere. If he knows about the studs — and clearly he does — he will do everything in his power to stop anyone from reaching Buckingham."

"Yes," D'Artagnan agreed.

"We could be arrested," Porthos said. "Imprisoned. Executed, even. We would be acting against the most powerful man in France."

"Yes," D'Artagnan agreed.

There was another silence. Then Porthos drained his cup, slammed it on the table, and stood up.

"When do we leave?" he asked.

Athos allowed himself one of his rare smiles. "In the morning. We ride at dawn."

"I'll pack lightly," Aramis said. "One book of poetry should suffice."

They planned through the night. The route to the coast was long — through the towns of Chantilly, Beauvais, Amiens, and finally Calais, where they would cross the Channel to England. The Cardinal's agents would be waiting at every stop.

"We ride together as far as we can," Athos said. "But if we are attacked, we cannot all be captured. Whoever is still free must keep riding. The mission comes first."

"The mission comes first," the others echoed.

It was a hard thing to agree to. It meant that if one of them fell, the others would have to leave him behind. It went against everything they believed — all for one, one for all.

But the queen's safety depended on the diamond studs reaching Paris before the ball. There was no room for failure.

D'Artagnan gathered supplies: fresh horses, money, weapons, and a letter from Constance that would identify him to the Duke of Buckingham. He barely slept. Every time he closed his eyes, he saw the road ahead — long, dark, and full of enemies.

At dawn, the four friends mounted their horses in the courtyard of D'Artagnan's lodgings. The morning air was cold, and mist hung in the streets. Paris was still asleep.

Athos pulled his cloak tight and looked at D'Artagnan. "Whatever happens on this road," he said, "remember: you ride for the queen, and you ride with friends. That is more than most men can say."

D'Artagnan nodded. His throat was too tight to speak.

"All for one," Porthos said.

"One for all," they answered.

They spurred their horses and rode out of Paris into the pale morning light, four friends against the most powerful man in France, with twelve days to change the course of history.

Chapter 9: The Mission

The trouble started in Chantilly.

They had been riding hard for two hours when they stopped at an inn to change horses and eat. A stranger approached Porthos as they were leaving and made a remark about the size of his hat. Porthos, who could not tolerate an insult to his wardrobe, challenged the man to a duel on the spot.

"Go!" Porthos shouted to the others as he drew his sword. "I will catch up!"

They hesitated. Every instinct told them to stay and fight beside their friend. But Athos shook his head.

"The mission comes first," he said. "Porthos can handle himself."

They rode on, three instead of four. D'Artagnan looked back once and saw Porthos circling his opponent in the courtyard of the inn, his enormous sword gleaming in the sunlight. Then the road curved, and Porthos was gone.

In Beauvais, they were ambushed.

Eight men on horseback burst from the trees and surrounded them. The Cardinal's agents — D'Artagnan recognized the cold efficiency of their attack. There was no warning, no conversation. Just swords and speed.

Aramis took a ball from a pistol in the shoulder. He slumped in his saddle but stayed mounted.

"Go!" Aramis shouted, clutching his wounded arm. "I'll hold them here! Go!"

Athos and D'Artagnan fought their way through the ring of attackers and galloped away, leaving Aramis behind to face the remaining agents alone. D'Artagnan's heart was in pieces, but he kept riding.

Two friends down. Two left.

They reached Amiens by nightfall, exhausted and desperate. They stopped at an inn for fresh horses and found the innkeeper friendly — too friendly. He offered them wine, praised their bravery, and insisted they rest before continuing.

Athos, who trusted no one, tasted the wine and stopped drinking.

"Drugged," he said quietly.

But it was too late. His vision was already blurring. The innkeeper had poisoned the wine before Athos could detect it.

"Run," Athos whispered to D'Artagnan. "Take the fresh horse. Ride for Calais. Don't stop for anything."

"I can't leave you —"

"You can and you will." Athos's eyes were fierce even as they glazed over. "The mission, D'Artagnan. The queen is counting on you. Go."

D'Artagnan ran. He grabbed the fresh horse, swung into the saddle, and rode into the darkness alone.

Three friends down. D'Artagnan alone.

The road to Calais was the loneliest ride of his life. Every shadow was an enemy. Every sound was an ambush. He rode through the night without stopping, his father's sword on his hip, the letter for Buckingham inside his coat, and the faces of his three friends burning in his mind.

They had sacrificed themselves for him — for the mission, for the queen, for the code they lived by. Porthos, fighting in a courtyard. Aramis, wounded and surrounded. Athos, poisoned and left behind. Each one had given himself so that D'Artagnan could keep riding.

He would not let their sacrifices be wasted.

D'Artagnan reached Calais at dawn. The harbor was gray and cold, and the English Channel stretched before him like a sheet of steel. He found a boat, paid the captain with his last coins, and crossed the water to England.

He was alone in a foreign country, with no money, no friends, and no plan except to find the Duke of Buckingham and save the queen.

But he was D'Artagnan of Gascony. And he had promised.

Chapter 10: D'Artagnan Alone

London was nothing like Paris.

The streets were wider but grayer, and the people spoke a language D'Artagnan barely understood. He had learned a few words of English from Aramis, who spoke four languages, but it was not enough to ask for directions to a duke's palace.

He showed Constance's letter to a merchant near the docks. The man studied the seal, raised his eyebrows, and pointed D'Artagnan toward a grand house near the river.

The Duke of Buckingham's residence was magnificent — marble columns, gleaming windows, and guards in red uniforms at every door. D'Artagnan marched up to the front gate, covered in road dust and sea salt, his clothes torn, his face unshaven, and his eyes wild with exhaustion.

"I must see the Duke of Buckingham," he said. "Immediately."

The guards looked at this ragged young Frenchman and very nearly laughed. But D'Artagnan produced the letter with the queen's seal, and the laughter died instantly. Within minutes, he was standing in a private room before one of the most powerful men in England.

The Duke of Buckingham was tall, elegant, and impeccably dressed. He had dark eyes and a sharp intelligence that D'Artagnan recognized immediately — this was a man who understood power.

"You come from the queen?" Buckingham said, reading the letter. His expression changed as he understood the danger. "The diamond studs. The Cardinal means to expose her."

"The ball is in seven days," D'Artagnan said. "If the queen cannot wear the studs, the king will know she gave them away. You must return them."

Buckingham moved immediately. He unlocked a velvet case and produced the diamond studs — twelve brilliant stones set in a ribbon of blue silk. They were dazzling, even in the dim light of the room.

Then he stopped. His face went pale.

"Two are missing," Buckingham said.

"Missing?"

Buckingham examined the ribbon closely. "The clasps have been cut. Someone has stolen two of the studs." His jaw tightened. "Milady de Winter. She was here last week — a guest at a banquet. She must have taken them. She is an agent of the Cardinal."

D'Artagnan felt the floor shift under him. Two missing studs. The Cardinal's agent had been one step ahead of them.

"How long to replace them?" D'Artagnan asked.

Buckingham was already calling for his jeweler. "Two days," he said. "My jeweler is the finest in London. He can make copies that even the queen herself could not tell from the originals."

"Two days," D'Artagnan repeated. That left five days to cross the Channel and ride back to Paris. It would be close — desperately close.

For two days, D'Artagnan paced the halls of Buckingham's residence like a caged animal. He ate without tasting. He slept in snatches. He checked the jeweler's progress every few hours, watching the old man set tiny diamonds into gold with steady, ancient hands.

On the morning of the third day, the jeweler finished. Twelve diamond studs lay on blue velvet — ten originals and two copies so perfect that even Buckingham could not tell them apart.

"Thank you," D'Artagnan said, tucking the velvet case inside his coat. "I will never forget this."

Buckingham provided a fast horse, a bag of gold, and a letter of passage that would get D'Artagnan through English ports without delay. At the dock, he gripped D'Artagnan's hand.

"Tell the queen that Buckingham is her servant," the duke said. "And tell her that her friendship is the greatest treasure I possess — worth more than any diamonds."

D'Artagnan nodded, mounted his horse, and rode for the coast.

The Channel crossing was rough — gray waves and howling wind — but D'Artagnan barely noticed. All he could think about was the road ahead. Five days to Paris. The Cardinal's agents would be watching every mile.

But D'Artagnan had the studs, a fast horse, and a fire in his chest that no Cardinal could extinguish.

He rode.

Chapter 11: The Race to Paris

The ride back was a nightmare.

D'Artagnan pushed his horse to the limit, changing mounts at every coaching inn. He rode through rain and darkness, eating in the saddle, sleeping only when his eyes refused to stay open. Every hour counted. The ball was in five days. Then four. Then three.

The Cardinal's agents were everywhere. At Boulogne, two men tried to block the road. D'Artagnan spurred his horse straight at them, and they dove out of the way. At Abbeville, a man in a tavern tried to start a fight — obviously trying to delay him. D'Artagnan walked out without a word, which was the hardest thing he had ever done. Every instinct screamed at him to draw his sword, but the mission came first.

On the third night, exhausted beyond anything he had ever experienced, D'Artagnan found Athos.

The musketeer was in the cellar of the inn at Amiens — the same inn where he had been drugged. He was alive, awake, and surrounded by empty wine bottles. He had locked the innkeeper in the cellar with him and refused to let the man leave until he confessed who had paid him to drug the wine.

"The Cardinal's agents paid me twenty gold pieces," the innkeeper whimpered. "Please let me go."

"D'Artagnan!" Athos stood up, swaying slightly. "You are alive!"

"So are you," D'Artagnan said, relief flooding through him. "What about the others?"

"Porthos won his duel in Chantilly and is recovering at the inn — his opponent nicked his leg, nothing serious. Aramis's wound was minor; a farmer's wife bandaged him and he is resting in Beauvais. They are all alive."

D'Artagnan felt tears prick his eyes. His friends were safe. Battered, wounded, scattered across France — but safe.

"Do you have the studs?" Athos asked.

D'Artagnan patted his coat. "All twelve. Two were stolen by a woman named Milady de Winter, but Buckingham's jeweler made perfect copies."

"Milady," Athos said, and something dark and complicated passed across his face. He said nothing more, but D'Artagnan filed the reaction away. There was a story there — a painful one.

"I must get to Paris," D'Artagnan said. "The ball is the day after tomorrow."

"Then ride," Athos said. "I will follow when I can. But you — ride like the wind, D'Artagnan. The queen is depending on you."

D'Artagnan rode. Through the night, through the rain, through his own exhaustion. He ate hard bread and drank water from streams. His legs ached. His back burned. His eyes stung from lack of sleep.

But every time he wanted to stop, he thought of the queen — alone in her palace, waiting for the studs that could save her. He thought of Constance, who had trusted him with this mission. He thought of Athos, Porthos, and Aramis, who had sacrificed themselves so he could keep riding.

He could not let them down. He would not.

On the morning of the ball, D'Artagnan galloped through the gates of Paris covered in mud, half-dead with exhaustion, clutching the velvet case against his chest like a newborn child.

He had made it.

Barely.

Chapter 12: The Ball

D'Artagnan found Constance waiting at her house, frantic with worry. When she saw him — filthy, swaying on his feet, but grinning — she nearly wept with relief.

"You have them?" she asked.

D'Artagnan opened the velvet case. Twelve diamond studs blazed in the morning light.

"Every last one," he said.

Constance snatched the case and ran. She had to get the studs to the queen before the ball that evening. There was no time for thank-yous, no time for explanations. She simply ran, and D'Artagnan watched her go.

Then he collapsed onto a bench and slept for six hours straight.

That evening, the ball was the grandest event Paris had seen in years. The palace blazed with a thousand candles. The finest nobles in France filled the great hall in silks and velvets, dancing and laughing and pretending they were not all terrified of the Cardinal.

D'Artagnan watched from the gallery above, cleaned up and wearing borrowed clothes. He was not important enough to attend the ball itself, but he had found a spot where he could see the great hall below.

The king arrived first. Then the Cardinal, in his red robes, his eyes scanning the room like a hawk.

Then the queen entered.

She was radiant. Her gown was silver and white, her hair was dressed with pearls, and around her neck — on a ribbon of blue velvet — shone twelve diamond studs, blazing like stars.

D'Artagnan held his breath.

The Cardinal leaned toward the king and whispered something. The king frowned, then walked to the queen and studied the studs carefully.

"They are beautiful," the king said. "All twelve."

The Cardinal's face went very still. He counted the studs — once, twice, three times. Twelve. There were supposed to be only ten. His agent, Milady, had stolen two. The queen should have been missing two studs.

But she was not. Somehow, impossibly, all twelve studs were there.

The Cardinal turned away, and for just a moment, D'Artagnan saw something flicker across his face — not anger, exactly, but a cold, calculating respect. Whoever had foiled his plan had done it perfectly.

The queen danced. The king smiled. The court applauded. And high above in the gallery, D'Artagnan leaned against a pillar and breathed.

He had done it. A boy from Gascony — no money, no title, no connections — had outrun the most powerful man in France and saved the queen.

Later that night, D'Artagnan walked through the quiet streets of Paris, too wired to sleep despite his exhaustion. The moon was full, and the city was silver and black.

He found Constance waiting at the corner of her street. She pressed something into his hand — a ring, gold, with a small sapphire.

"From the queen," Constance said. "She cannot thank you publicly, but she wants you to know that she will never forget what you did."

D'Artagnan looked at the ring. It was the most valuable thing he had ever held. But it was not the ring that moved him. It was the knowledge that he had done something that mattered — something brave, something good, something worthy of the cloak he had not yet earned.

"Tell the queen," D'Artagnan said, "that her musketeers are always at her service."

"You are not a musketeer yet," Constance said, smiling.

"I will be," D'Artagnan said.

He walked home through the moonlit streets, and for the first time since leaving Gascony, he felt like he truly belonged in Paris.

But the Cardinal was not finished. D'Artagnan had won this round. The next round would be far more dangerous.

Chapter 13: The Woman in the Shadows

In the weeks following the ball, D'Artagnan began to notice a woman.

She appeared at the edges of his life like a shadow — glimpsed across a crowded street, spotted in a carriage window, mentioned in whispered conversations. She was beautiful in a way that made people stop and stare: golden hair, pale skin, and eyes so blue they seemed almost violet. She moved through Parisian society with effortless grace, charming everyone she met.

Her name was Milady de Winter.

D'Artagnan recognized the name immediately. This was the woman the Duke of Buckingham had mentioned — the one who had stolen two diamond studs from his case. She was an agent of the Cardinal, working in the shadows to carry out his schemes.

"Stay away from her," Athos warned when D'Artagnan mentioned the name.

They were sitting in their usual tavern. Athos's voice was low and hard — harder than D'Artagnan had ever heard it.

"You know her?" D'Artagnan asked.

"I know what she is," Athos said. "She is the most dangerous person in France. More dangerous than the Cardinal himself, because the Cardinal at least acts in the open. Milady works in darkness. She uses charm the way others use swords — to get close enough to strike."

"What do you mean?"

Athos stared into his wine for a long time. "She is not what she appears to be. Nothing about her is real. The beauty, the manners, the gentle voice — they are weapons. She has destroyed better men than you, D'Artagnan. Stay away."

D'Artagnan heard the warning. He understood the danger. But something in Athos's voice bothered him — the way it cracked slightly, the way his hand tightened on his cup, the way his dark eyes flickered with something that looked like grief.

There was history there. Deep, painful history.

D'Artagnan did not ask. He knew Athos well enough to know that pressing would only drive the man deeper into silence. But he watched Milady more carefully after that, and what he saw confirmed Athos's warning.

Milady met with the Cardinal's agents in quiet corners. She passed notes in sealed envelopes. She attended parties where important people said careless things, and those careless words somehow found their way to the Cardinal's ears.

She was a spy. A brilliant, beautiful, utterly ruthless spy.

And D'Artagnan had the uncomfortable feeling that she was watching him, too.

Chapter 14: Athos's Secret

The truth came out one night when Athos had drunk more wine than usual.

The four friends were alone in Athos's rooms. A storm raged outside, rain hammering the windows and wind howling through the streets. Porthos was asleep in a chair, snoring. Aramis was reading by candlelight. D'Artagnan sat on the floor, cleaning his sword.

"I was married once," Athos said suddenly.

D'Artagnan looked up. Athos never talked about his past. In all the months they had been friends, Athos had shared nothing about who he was before Paris — no family, no home, no history. He was like a man who had appeared fully formed, with nothing behind him but shadows.

"She was beautiful," Athos continued, his voice flat and distant, as though he were talking about someone else's life. "The most beautiful woman I had ever seen. I was young — younger than you, D'Artagnan — and I was a fool. I fell in love with her face and her voice and her smile, and I did not look deeper."

Aramis closed his book. Even Porthos stopped snoring, though whether he was awake or simply holding his breath was unclear.

"I married her," Athos said. "I gave her my name, my home, my trust — everything. And then one day, by accident, I discovered the truth. She was a criminal. She had committed terrible crimes — theft, deception, cruelty. She had been branded on the shoulder as punishment — a mark that she had hidden from everyone, including me."

D'Artagnan felt cold. "What did you do?"

"What honor demanded," Athos said. "I cast her out. She was dead to me. I left everything behind — my lands, my title, my name — and came to Paris as a simple musketeer. I buried my past and swore I would never speak of it again."

"And the woman?" D'Artagnan asked, though he already knew the answer. His stomach was tight.

"The woman," Athos said slowly, "is Milady de Winter."

The room was very quiet. Rain streamed down the windows.

"She survived," Athos said. "She changed her name, invented a new history, and found a new patron — the Cardinal. She is cleverer than any of us. She is more ruthless than any of us. And she has no conscience. None."

"She is working for the Cardinal now," D'Artagnan said.

"Yes. And she will not stop until she has destroyed everything and everyone she considers an enemy. That includes the queen, Buckingham, and anyone who has ever stood in her way." Athos's eyes met D'Artagnan's. "That now includes you."

D'Artagnan understood the weight of what Athos had shared. This was not just a warning. It was a confession — the deepest, most painful secret of a man who kept everything locked inside.

"Thank you for telling me," D'Artagnan said.

Athos nodded once. Then he poured himself more wine and said nothing else for the rest of the night.

But something had shifted between them. Athos had trusted D'Artagnan with his most guarded secret. And D'Artagnan understood, perhaps for the first time, that the greatest wounds a man carries are not always the ones you can see.

Chapter 15: The Siege of La Rochelle

War came, as it always does.

The Cardinal ordered the King's armies to march south to the city of La Rochelle, a Protestant stronghold that had been resisting the Cardinal's authority for years. The musketeers were called to fight, and D'Artagnan — as a cadet attached to the company — went with them.

La Rochelle was a walled city on the coast, surrounded by water on one side and the king's army on the other. The siege would be long and bloody. Thousands of soldiers camped in the fields outside the walls, digging trenches, building fortifications, and waiting for the city to surrender.

It was D'Artagnan's first experience of real war, and it was nothing like the stories.

The stories talked about glory and honor and magnificent charges. The reality was mud, cold, bad food, and long stretches of boredom punctuated by sudden, terrifying violence. Cannonballs screamed overhead. Musket fire crackled from the walls. Men D'Artagnan had eaten breakfast with were carried away on stretchers by afternoon.

But the four friends endured it together, and that made all the difference.

Athos was calm under fire — eerily calm, as though the chaos of battle were a relief from the chaos inside his own head. He gave quiet orders, steadied nervous soldiers, and fought with the cold precision that made him legendary.

Porthos was in his element. He was built for war — his enormous strength and boundless energy made him tireless in the trenches. He carried supplies that took two normal men to lift, dug fortifications with the enthusiasm of a boy building a sandcastle, and told jokes so bad that even the enemy must have groaned.

Aramis tended the wounded as often as he fought. He bandaged cuts, set broken bones, and prayed with dying men. The gentle poet who dreamed of becoming a priest was, in the horror of war, exactly the person everyone needed.

D'Artagnan fought bravely. He volunteered for every dangerous mission, led scouting parties into no-man's-land, and never once showed fear — though he felt it, deeply, every single day.

"You do not have to prove yourself this hard," Athos told him one evening, as they sat in the trench watching the sun set over the ocean.

"Yes, I do," D'Artagnan said.

"Why?"

"Because every time I volunteer for something dangerous, every time I fight harder than I need to, I am one step closer to the blue cloak. And I want it, Athos. I want it more than anything."

Athos looked at him for a long time. "You will have it," he said. "But not because of what you do in battle. You will have it because of who you are."

D'Artagnan did not entirely understand. But he kept the words in his heart and returned to the fighting the next day.

Chapter 16: The Breakfast in the Bastion

Halfway through the siege, D'Artagnan needed to tell his friends something important — something he had discovered about Milady and the Cardinal's plans. But spies were everywhere, and every conversation in the camp was overheard by someone.

"We need a private place to talk," D'Artagnan said. "Somewhere no one can listen."

Athos looked out across the battlefield. Between the king's camp and the walls of La Rochelle stood a ruined bastion — a small stone fort that had been destroyed by cannon fire. It sat in no-man's-land, exposed to fire from both sides. No one went near it.

"There," Athos said, pointing to the bastion with a slight smile. "We can talk there."

"That is insane," Porthos said. "That bastion is in range of the city's guns. We could be killed."

"Which is exactly why no one will be listening," Athos said. "Bring food. We will have breakfast."

The idea was absurd. Having a casual breakfast in the middle of a war zone, in a bombed-out fort that could be shelled at any moment — it was the kind of thing only a madman would suggest.

Or a musketeer.

At dawn, the four friends carried a basket of bread, cheese, ham, and wine across the open ground to the ruined bastion. Soldiers in the camp watched them go with open mouths. Officers shouted at them to come back. They ignored everyone.

Inside the bastion, they spread a cloth on a broken wall and laid out their breakfast. Cannonballs whistled overhead. Musket shots cracked from the city walls. Chunks of stone flew from the bastion's crumbling walls.

Athos poured wine as calmly as if they were in a Paris tavern. "Now," he said. "What did you want to tell us?"

"The Cardinal has sent Milady to England," D'Artagnan said, tearing off a piece of bread. "I overheard his agents talking. She has orders to find Buckingham and —"

A cannonball hit the bastion twenty feet away. Stone fragments rained down. Porthos brushed dust off his ham.

"Continue," Aramis said, refilling his cup.

"— and deliver a message threatening him. If Buckingham does not withdraw his support for La Rochelle, the Cardinal will expose secrets that could ruin him."

"Milady is the perfect weapon for this," Athos said grimly. "She is charming enough to get close and ruthless enough to follow through."

"We must warn Buckingham," Aramis said.

"More than that," D'Artagnan said. "Milady also has orders regarding Constance. The Cardinal knows that Constance helped us with the diamond studs. She is in danger."

The mood at the table changed. This was no longer just politics and power games. This was personal.

"We must protect Constance," D'Artagnan said. "And we must stop Milady."

"Agreed," Athos said. There was steel in his voice — the kind of steel that comes from a man confronting something he has been running from for years. "When the siege allows it, we ride."

Another cannonball shattered what remained of the bastion's north wall. The four friends finished their breakfast, packed up the cloth, and walked calmly back across no-man's-land to the camp.

The entire army had been watching. When the four friends strolled back into the trenches, unhurried and unharmed, the soldiers erupted in cheers.

The breakfast in the bastion became the most famous meal of the siege.

Chapter 17: Milady's Mission

While the four friends ate breakfast under cannon fire, Milady de Winter was already at sea.

Her ship cut through the grey waters of the English Channel, carrying her toward England and the Duke of Buckingham. In her luggage was a letter from the Cardinal with very specific instructions. In her mind was a plan far more dangerous than anything the Cardinal had imagined.

Milady was not simply the Cardinal's agent. She was her own agent — a woman of extraordinary intelligence who used everyone around her as tools to achieve her own ends. The Cardinal thought he controlled her. He was wrong. Milady served Milady, and no one else.

Back at the siege, D'Artagnan pieced together what he could about Milady's movements from intercepted messages and the gossip of soldiers. Aramis, whose network of contacts stretched across France, gathered additional information from letters and whispered conversations.

"She reached England three days ago," Aramis reported one evening. "She is staying near Buckingham's estate."

"What is she planning?" Porthos asked.

"Something terrible," Athos said quietly. He knew Milady better than any of them. He knew what she was capable of. "The Cardinal wants a message delivered. But Milady will do more than deliver a message. She never simply does what she is told. She improves upon it — makes it darker, more final."

D'Artagnan felt a chill. "You think she will try to harm Buckingham?"

"I think she will try to destroy him," Athos said. "And anyone else who stands in her way."

They sent warnings to England through every channel they could find — letters carried by trusted messengers, coded notes passed through Aramis's network of contacts, even a message entrusted to the captain of a merchant ship bound for London.

But they could not be sure any of the warnings would arrive in time. The sea was wide, the roads were dangerous, and Milady had a head start.

"We have done what we can from here," Athos said. "The rest depends on others."

D'Artagnan hated waiting. He was a man of action — of riding and fighting and doing things with his own hands. Sitting in a trench while someone he cared about was in danger felt like a slow form of torture.

But Athos taught him something important during those long days of waiting.

"Patience is not weakness," Athos said. "It is the hardest kind of strength. Any fool can charge into battle. It takes real courage to wait, to trust, to hold steady when every part of you wants to run."

D'Artagnan tried to learn this lesson. He was not sure he succeeded.

Chapter 18: The Prisoner

The warnings arrived too late.

Milady reached Buckingham. She delivered the Cardinal's threats. But when Buckingham refused to back down — when he told her that he would never abandon his principles for fear of a cardinal or a spy — Milady's mission changed.

What happened next reached D'Artagnan as a series of fragmented reports, carried across the Channel by slow ships and slower messengers.

Milady had been captured by English authorities who had been warned about her by one of Aramis's messages. She was imprisoned in a country house under guard. For a time, it seemed the danger had passed.

But Milady was not the kind of person who stayed captured.

She studied her guards the way a chess player studies a board. She found the one who was young, impressionable, and lonely. She used her voice — soft, sad, filled with invented sorrow — to win his sympathy. She told him she was an innocent woman, wrongly imprisoned, separated from her family by cruel men.

It was all lies. Every word, every tear, every trembling hand was calculated. Milady was performing the role of her life, and her audience of one believed every moment of it.

The young guard helped her escape.

By the time his commander discovered what had happened, Milady was gone — vanished into the English countryside like smoke.

When this news reached the camp at La Rochelle, D'Artagnan felt the ground shift beneath him. Milady was free. She was angry. She was heading back to France. And she had unfinished business with everyone who had tried to stop her.

"She will come for Constance," D'Artagnan said. He knew it in his bones. Milady would strike at the person D'Artagnan cared about most — not out of strategy, but out of revenge. That was how Milady operated. She did not simply defeat her enemies. She destroyed the things they loved.

"We must find Constance before Milady does," D'Artagnan said. "We must get her somewhere safe."

"The siege —" Porthos began.

"The siege can wait," D'Artagnan said, and the fierceness in his voice surprised even him. "Constance cannot."

Athos nodded. "He is right. This is no longer about politics or queens or cardinals. This is about protecting an innocent person from a monster."

They went to Monsieur de Tréville and requested leave from the siege. Tréville, who understood loyalty better than most, granted it.

"Go," the captain said. "Do what you must. But be careful. The Cardinal has eyes everywhere."

The four friends gathered their weapons, saddled their horses, and rode north — away from the siege, away from the war, toward a danger that was more personal and more terrible than anything they had faced on the battlefield.

Chapter 19: The Warning

They rode hard for Paris, gathering information at every stop. Aramis's network of contacts proved invaluable — at each coaching inn, there was someone who owed him a favor, someone who had heard something, someone who could point them in the right direction.

The picture that emerged was terrifying.

Milady had returned to France. She had been spotted in the north, traveling fast, with purpose. She was heading for a convent near the town of Béthune, where — according to Aramis's sources — Constance had been hidden for her own safety.

"She knows where Constance is," D'Artagnan said, his face white. "How does she know?"

"The Cardinal's spies," Athos said. "He knows everything. And Milady has his full resources behind her."

They pushed their horses harder. D'Artagnan rode at the front, leaning forward in the saddle, willing the horse to go faster. Every minute felt like an hour. Every delay — a lame horse, a blocked road, a bridge under repair — was agony.

On the third day, they met a man on the road who recognized Aramis. He was a monk from a monastery near Béthune, and he carried terrible news.

"A woman arrived at the convent two days ago," the monk said. "Beautiful, with golden hair. She told the Mother Superior she was a friend of the young woman staying there — Constance Bonacieux. The Mother Superior believed her."

"No," D'Artagnan whispered.

"We must ride faster," Athos said.

They abandoned the road and cut across open country, jumping fences and splashing through streams. The horses were foaming and wild-eyed. D'Artagnan's hands were numb on the reins, and his heart was pounding so hard he could hear it over the hoofbeats.

As the sun set on the third day, they saw the convent in the distance — a quiet stone building surrounded by gardens, looking peaceful and safe.

D'Artagnan leaped from his horse before it had fully stopped and ran for the door.

Chapter 20: Too Late

The convent was quiet. Too quiet.

D'Artagnan burst through the door and found the Mother Superior in the front hall, her face streaked with tears.

"Where is Constance?" D'Artagnan demanded.

The old woman could not speak. She simply pointed down the corridor.

D'Artagnan ran. He threw open door after door until he found a small, plain room at the end of the hall. Constance was there, lying on a narrow bed. Her eyes were closed. Her face was pale and still.

On the table beside her sat a cup of wine — half empty.

D'Artagnan fell to his knees beside the bed. "Constance," he said. "Constance, please."

She opened her eyes — barely. They were clouded and unfocused, but she recognized him. A faint smile crossed her lips.

"You came," she whispered.

"I came. I'm here. Everything is going to be fine."

"The woman," Constance murmured. "The beautiful woman. She was so kind. She brought me wine..." Her voice faded.

D'Artagnan looked at the cup. Poison. Milady had come as a friend, shared a drink, and left death behind her. It was exactly how Athos had described her: charm used as a weapon, kindness as a disguise.

Athos, Porthos, and Aramis arrived at the doorway. One look at D'Artagnan's face told them everything.

"No," Porthos said, his voice cracking.

Aramis knelt beside the bed and took Constance's hand. He prayed quietly, his lips moving in words too soft to hear.

Athos stood in the doorway, his face carved from stone. He had known this was possible. He had warned them about Milady, told them what she was capable of. But knowing did not make it easier. Nothing made this easier.

Constance passed away quietly, with D'Artagnan holding her hand and Aramis praying beside her. She was brave to the end — she did not cry out, did not complain, did not curse the woman who had poisoned her. She simply closed her eyes and let go.

D'Artagnan did not move for a long time. The room was very still. The candle flickered. Outside, night had fallen, and the convent garden was silver with moonlight.

When he finally stood, something had changed in his eyes. The boyish eagerness was gone. The reckless grin was gone. What remained was something harder, colder, and more determined than anything his friends had seen in him before.

"Where is Milady?" D'Artagnan asked.

"She left the convent this morning," the Mother Superior said through her tears. "She rode north."

D'Artagnan turned to his friends. "We find her," he said. "We end this."

Athos nodded. His face was grim, but behind the grimness was something like relief — the relief of a man who had been running from a reckoning for years and had finally decided to face it.

"Yes," Athos said. "We end this."

Chapter 21: The Trial of Milady

They found her at a house near the river, three days north of Béthune.

She was alone. Her carriage stood in the courtyard, her bags packed for a journey. She was preparing to flee to a new country, a new name, a new life — as she had done so many times before.

But this time, four men blocked her path.

They brought her to a clearing on the riverbank. It was night. The moon hung low and full, casting silver light across the water. Torches had been planted in the ground, and their flames flickered in the wind.

Milady stood in the center of the clearing, her hands unbound. She was not chained or shackled. There was nowhere to run.

She was still beautiful. Even now, even in the torchlight with four armed men surrounding her, she carried herself with poise and elegance. She looked at each of them in turn — Athos, Porthos, Aramis, D'Artagnan — and her expression was calm. Almost amused.

"So," she said. "A trial."

"Yes," Athos said. His voice was steady, but D'Artagnan could see the effort it cost him. This woman had been his wife. He had loved her once. And now he stood in judgment of her. "You will answer for your crimes."

Athos listed them. The theft of the diamond studs. The manipulation of the Cardinal's power for personal gain. The escape from English custody through deception. And the poisoning of Constance Bonacieux — an innocent woman whose only crime had been loyalty to the queen.

Milady listened to each charge without flinching. When Athos finished, she spoke.

"I have done what I had to do to survive," she said. "The world gave me nothing. Every man I trusted betrayed me. Every hand that was offered came with a price. I made myself powerful because no one else would protect me."

"You poisoned an innocent woman," D'Artagnan said.

Milady's eyes flickered. For just a moment — so brief that D'Artagnan almost missed it — something human passed across her face. Regret? Shame? Or simply the recognition that she had finally gone too far?

"She was in my way," Milady said. But her voice was quieter now.

The four friends deliberated. It did not take long. The evidence was clear. The crimes were real. And the danger Milady posed — to the queen, to France, to anyone who crossed her path — was too great to ignore.

The sentence was exile. Milady would leave France forever. She would be escorted to a ship and sent to a distant land, never to return. If she ever set foot on French soil again, the consequences would be final.

Milady accepted the sentence without protest. Perhaps she understood that exile was mercy. Perhaps she was already planning her next reinvention. With Milady, it was impossible to know.

As the soldiers led her away, she turned back once and looked at Athos. Something passed between them — a look that held years of love, betrayal, grief, and a strange, broken tenderness that neither of them would ever speak of again.

Then she was gone.

D'Artagnan stood by the river long after the others had left. The torches burned low. The water moved on, as water always does — carrying everything with it, leaving nothing behind but the sound.

Chapter 22: The Cardinal's Offer

They returned to Paris expecting punishment. They had left the siege without full permission, pursued a personal vendetta against one of the Cardinal's agents, and conducted a trial with no legal authority. The Cardinal had every reason to destroy them.

D'Artagnan was summoned to the Cardinal's private chambers in the palace. He went alone. Athos, Porthos, and Aramis wanted to accompany him, but D'Artagnan refused.

"This is my fight," he said. "If anyone is punished, it should be me. I started this."

He walked through the palace corridors feeling like a man walking toward the executioner's block. The Cardinal's guards lined the halls, watching him with cold eyes. The doors to the Cardinal's chambers were enormous — dark wood, iron hinges, large enough to make anyone who entered feel small.

Cardinal Richelieu sat behind a desk piled with documents. He was older than D'Artagnan had expected — thin, sharp-featured, with piercing eyes that seemed to see through walls. He wore his red robes like armor.

"Sit down," the Cardinal said.

D'Artagnan sat.

"You have caused me a great deal of trouble," the Cardinal said. His voice was not angry. It was measured, curious, almost respectful. "You retrieved the diamond studs. You warned Buckingham. You pursued and captured my agent. You have interfered with my plans at every turn."

"I did what honor required," D'Artagnan said.

"Honor," the Cardinal repeated. He seemed to taste the word. "An interesting concept. Tell me, young man — do you believe that I am without honor?"

D'Artagnan hesitated. This was a trap. Any answer could be wrong.

"I believe that you serve France," D'Artagnan said carefully. "And I believe that you and I disagree about how France should be served."

The Cardinal's eyes narrowed — not with anger, but with something like appreciation. "A diplomatic answer. You are learning."

He reached into his desk and produced a folded document. "I should have you arrested," the Cardinal said. "I should have all four of you locked in the Bastille for the rest of your lives. You have defied me, embarrassed me, and cost me one of my most valuable agents."

D'Artagnan said nothing. His heart hammered.

"But I am not a petty man," the Cardinal continued. "I recognize talent when I see it. And you, D'Artagnan of Gascony, have talent. You have courage, determination, loyalty, and an irritating refusal to give up that I find... admirable."

He unfolded the document and slid it across the desk. D'Artagnan looked down.

It was a commission. A lieutenant's commission in the King's Musketeers. The blank line for the name was empty.

"Fill in any name you wish," the Cardinal said. "Your own, or one of your friends'. Consider it a peace offering — from an old man who knows how to recognize a worthy opponent."

D'Artagnan stared at the document. This was everything he had wanted since leaving Gascony. The blue cloak. The silver cross. The rank of musketeer.

He picked up the commission, bowed to the Cardinal, and walked out of the palace in a daze.

Chapter 23: One for All

D'Artagnan found his three friends waiting at the tavern. They were trying to look calm, but the worry in their eyes gave them away. When D'Artagnan walked through the door — alive, free, and carrying a piece of paper — all three of them stood up at once.

"Well?" Porthos demanded. "Are we going to prison?"

D'Artagnan laid the commission on the table.

There was a long silence as the three musketeers read it. A lieutenant's commission. Blank. Ready for any name.

"He gave you this?" Athos said, disbelief in his voice.

"He said I could fill in any name. Mine, or one of yours." D'Artagnan pushed the document toward Athos. "It should be yours. You are the best of us."

Athos pushed it back. "I do not want rank. I have had rank before, and it brought me nothing but grief. Give it to Porthos."

Porthos shook his head. "I am too loud and too vain for command. Everyone knows it, including me. Give it to Aramis."

Aramis smiled gently. "I am thinking of leaving the musketeers to enter the church. A commission would be wasted on me. It must be yours, D'Artagnan."

D'Artagnan looked at his friends — each one refusing the honor, each one insisting it go to someone else. This was who they were. This was what "all for one" truly meant. Not just fighting together, but lifting each other up.

"I cannot take this," D'Artagnan said. "Not alone. Everything I have done, I did because of you. Athos taught me to think. Porthos taught me to be strong. Aramis taught me to be clever. Without the three of you, I would still be a boy from Gascony on a yellow horse."

"You were never just a boy from Gascony," Athos said. "You were always a musketeer. You simply needed time to prove it."

D'Artagnan picked up the quill from the table. His hand trembled slightly. He dipped it in ink and, in his best handwriting, wrote his name on the blank line.

D'Artagnan.

He looked up at his friends.

"I accept this," he said. "Not for myself. For us. For everything we have been through and everything we will face together."

Porthos let out a whoop that shook the rafters. Aramis clapped politely. Athos — Athos simply reached across the table and gripped D'Artagnan's hand, and the pressure of his grip said more than any words could.

The tavern keeper brought wine. They drank. They told stories — the yellow horse, the three duels, the fight behind the Luxembourg, the ride to England, the breakfast in the bastion. They laughed at the absurdity of it all — four men against the most powerful cardinal in Europe, and somehow, against all odds, still standing.

"What now?" Porthos asked, refilling his cup.

"Now," Athos said, "D'Artagnan gets his blue cloak."

The commissioning ceremony was held the next morning in the courtyard of the musketeers' headquarters. Monsieur de Tréville himself placed the blue cloak on D'Artagnan's shoulders. The silver cross gleamed on his chest. Around him, the company of musketeers stood at attention.

D'Artagnan looked down at the blue cloak — the cloak he had dreamed of since he was a boy, the cloak that meant he belonged, that he had earned his place among the finest soldiers in France.

Then he looked up at Athos, Porthos, and Aramis, standing together in the front row. Athos nodded. Porthos grinned. Aramis raised an eyebrow that said, about time.

D'Artagnan smiled.

Chapter 24: All for One

That evening, the four friends sat together on the steps of the musketeers' barracks as the sun set over Paris. The sky was painted in shades of gold and crimson, and the rooftops of the city glowed in the fading light. It was one of those rare, perfect moments when the world seems to pause and allow you to feel exactly where you are.

D'Artagnan wore his blue cloak. It was heavier than he had expected — not just the weight of the fabric, but the weight of everything it represented. The battles he had fought. The friends he had made. The boy he had been and the man he was becoming.

"I keep thinking about the day I arrived in Paris," D'Artagnan said. "On that ridiculous yellow horse, with my patched clothes and my father's old sword. I was so sure I was ready for anything."

"You were not ready for anything," Athos said. "You crashed into me, broke Porthos's belt, and stepped on Aramis's foot. All in one morning."

"And challenged all three of us to duels," Porthos added, chuckling. "I have never met anyone so determined to get himself killed."

"Or so determined to make friends," Aramis said.

D'Artagnan laughed. It was true. He had stumbled into the greatest friendships of his life by literally running into people. There was something perfectly D'Artagnan about that — graceless, reckless, and somehow exactly right.

"My father told me three things before I left Gascony," D'Artagnan said. "Never run from a fight. Never tolerate an insult. And never betray a friend."

"Your father was a wise man," Athos said.

"He was. But he forgot to mention the most important thing."

"What is that?"

D'Artagnan looked at his three friends — Athos, who had taught him that true strength is quiet; Porthos, who had taught him that true joy is generous; Aramis, who had taught him that true wisdom is gentle.

"He forgot to tell me that the friends you find along the way are worth more than anything you set out to find."

The sun slipped below the rooftops. The first stars appeared. Paris settled into its evening rhythms — the clatter of carriages, the distant music from a tavern, the laughter of soldiers heading home.

Porthos stood and stretched his enormous frame. "Well," he said, "I suppose we should decide what to do next. We have defeated the Cardinal's plot, saved the queen, survived a siege, and D'Artagnan has his cloak. What adventure is left?"

"All of them," Athos said.

Aramis closed his book and stood. "France is full of trouble. The Cardinal will not rest. There will be new plots, new villains, new dangers."

"Good," Porthos said. "I was getting bored."

D'Artagnan stood last. He looked out over the city — this impossible, beautiful, treacherous city that had tried to chew him up and spit him out on his very first day. He thought about the boy who had ridden in on a yellow horse, full of fire and foolishness, with nothing but a sword and a dream.

That boy was gone. In his place stood a musketeer.

But the fire was still there. It would always be there.

He turned to his friends. "Whatever comes next," he said, "we face it together."

Athos drew his sword. The blade caught the last light of the sunset and gleamed.

"All for one," Athos said.

Porthos drew his. "And one for all."

Aramis drew his. "Always."

D'Artagnan drew his father's old sword — nicked, worn, and still sharp — and raised it to meet the other three. Four blades touched in the fading light, steel ringing against steel, a sound like a promise.

All for one. And one for all.

Below them, Paris glittered in the darkness, full of danger and possibility. And four friends — the finest swordsmen in France — were ready for whatever came next.
