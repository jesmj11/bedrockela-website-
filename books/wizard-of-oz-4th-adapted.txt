The Wonderful

Wizard of Oz

A BedrockELA Adaptation

Based on the novel by L. Frank Baum (1900)

Adapted for 4th–5th Grade Readers

Chapter 1: The Cyclone

Dorothy lived in the middle of the great Kansas prairies with Uncle Henry, who was a farmer, and Aunt Em, who was the farmer's wife. Their house was small — just one room — with a rusty stove, a cupboard, a table, and three or four chairs. There were no curtains, no pictures on the walls, and nothing pretty at all.

Uncle Henry never laughed. He worked from morning till night and rarely spoke. Aunt Em had once been young and cheerful, but years of sun and wind on the dry prairie had taken the color from her eyes and cheeks. She never smiled anymore, either.

It was Toto who made Dorothy laugh. Toto was a small, scrappy black dog with long silky hair and bright little eyes that twinkled on either side of his funny nose. Dorothy loved Toto dearly. He was the one thing in Kansas that was not gray.

Everything around them was gray. The sun had baked the plowed land into a gray mass. Even the paint on the house had faded until it was as dull and gray as everything else. When Aunt Em first came to Kansas, the sun and wind had changed her, too. They had taken the sparkle from her eyes and left them a sober gray; they had taken the red from her cheeks and lips, and they were gray also.

Dorothy often looked out at the flat, gray world and felt something like loneliness, though she did not know the word for it. Toto was not gray. He saved her from growing as gray as everything else around her.

One afternoon, Uncle Henry sat on the doorstep and looked up at the sky, which was even grayer than usual. Dorothy stood in the doorway holding Toto and watching too. From the far north, they heard a low wail of the wind. They could see the long grass bowing in waves before the coming storm.

"There's a cyclone coming, Em," Uncle Henry called to his wife. "I'll go look after the stock." Then he ran toward the sheds where the cows and horses were kept.

Aunt Em dropped what she was doing and hurried to the door. One look at the sky told her the danger was real. "Quick, Dorothy!" she screamed. "Run for the cellar!"

Toto jumped out of Dorothy's arms and hid under the bed. Dorothy started to chase after him. Aunt Em threw open the trapdoor in the floor and climbed down into the small, dark hole beneath the house. Dorothy caught Toto at last and started to follow her aunt.

But she was too late.

Before she was halfway across the room, a terrible shriek came from the wind. The house shook so hard that Dorothy lost her footing and fell to the floor. Then a strange thing happened.

The house tilted, then tilted more, and then — with a great groaning sound — it was lifted right up into the air by the cyclone, as easily as you might pick up a feather. Up and up and up it went, spinning slowly, carried by the mighty wind.

The house was caught in the very center of the cyclone. The wind on every side pressed against it equally, and it was carried along like a balloon, rising higher and higher. It was very dark, and the wind howled terribly around her, but Dorothy found, after the first few moments, that she was not being tossed about. The house swayed gently, like a baby being rocked in a cradle.

Toto did not like it. He ran around the room, barking, but Dorothy sat quite still on the floor and waited to see what would happen.

Once, Toto got too near the open trapdoor and fell in with a little yelp. Dorothy thought she had lost him. But his ear stuck up through the hole, and she grabbed it and pulled him back into the room. Then she closed the trapdoor so no more accidents could happen.

Hours passed. Slowly, Dorothy got over her fright. She felt quite lonely with the wind screaming so loudly around her, but there was nothing to do except sit and wait. Eventually, she crawled across the swaying floor to her bed, and Toto followed her. Dorothy lay down and, despite the swaying and the howling wind, fell fast asleep.

Chapter 2: The Council with the Munchkins

Dorothy was awakened by a sudden jolt. The house had stopped moving. Bright sunshine streamed through the window, flooding the little room with light so cheerful that Dorothy sat up and blinked in surprise.

She ran to the door, opened it, and gave a little cry of wonder.

The cyclone had set the house down gently in a land of extraordinary beauty. Green grass stretched in every direction, dotted with wildflowers of every color — deep reds, brilliant oranges, and purples so rich they looked like they had been painted by hand. Fruit trees heavy with blossoms lined a sparkling brook, and in the distance, she could see rolling hills touched with gold. Birds with bright feathers sang in the branches.

After gray, flat, dead Kansas, it was like stepping into a painting.

"Where am I?" Dorothy whispered.

Toto pressed against her leg and whimpered. He did not know, either.

As Dorothy stood staring, she noticed a group of small people walking toward her. They were about as tall as Dorothy herself, though they were clearly grown-ups. They wore round hats that rose to a point about a foot above their heads, with tiny bells on the brims that tinkled sweetly as they walked.

Three of them were men, dressed all in blue. With them was a very old woman, also dressed in white. Her face was covered in wrinkles, but her eyes were kind. She walked stiffly, as if her knees hurt, and the three men followed her with obvious respect.

The little old woman stopped before Dorothy, bowed low, and said in a sweet voice: "You are welcome, most noble sorceress, to the land of the Munchkins. We are so grateful to you for having killed the Wicked Witch of the East, and for setting our people free from her cruelty."

Dorothy stared at her. "I haven't killed anyone," she said.

"Your house did, anyway," replied the old woman with a gentle laugh. "And that is the same thing. Look!" She pointed to the corner of the house.

Dorothy looked and gave a little scream. Two feet were sticking out from under the corner of the house, wearing silver shoes with pointed toes.

"Oh dear!" Dorothy gasped, putting her hands over her mouth. "The house must have fallen on her. What are we going to do?"

"There is nothing to be done," the old woman said calmly. "She was the Wicked Witch of the East, as I said. She held all the Munchkins in slavery for many years, making them work for her night and day. Now they are free, and they are grateful to you."

"Who are the Munchkins?" Dorothy asked.

"They are the people who live in this land of the East, where the Wicked Witch ruled. I am the Witch of the North, and I am their friend."

Dorothy's eyes went wide. "Are you a real witch?" she asked, feeling a bit nervous.

"Yes, indeed," the woman replied. "But I am a good witch, and the people love me. The Wicked Witch of the East was the bad one. There were four witches in the land of Oz — two good and two wicked. Now that you have killed one wicked witch, there is only one left: the Wicked Witch of the West. But she lives far from here, and I do not think she will bother you."

Dorothy thought about this for a moment. "Aunt Em told me that all witches were wicked," she said.

"Oh no," the Witch of the North replied. "That is a great mistake. I know there are no witches at all in Kansas, but here in Oz we have always had them. The Wicked Witch of the East and the Wicked Witch of the West were terribly evil, that is true. But the Witch of the South and I are good witches, and the people know it."

Dorothy felt relieved, but she also felt tears coming. "I just want to go home," she said. "Can you help me find my way back to Kansas?"

The Munchkins and the Witch of the North looked at each other and shook their heads.

"Where is Kansas?" the Witch asked.

"It's where I live. It's far away from here. Aunt Em will be so worried. Oh, Toto, we are so far from home!"

She began to cry, and the Munchkins, who truly were grateful, cried with her. The men pulled out their handkerchiefs and blew their noses loudly. The Witch of the North thought for a moment, then took off her cap and balanced it on the tip of her nose.

"One, two, three," she counted.

The cap changed into a slate with white chalk writing on it. The Witch read the words aloud: "Let Dorothy go to the City of Emeralds."

"Where is the City of Emeralds?" Dorothy asked.

"It is in the exact center of the land of Oz. It is ruled by the great Wizard of Oz himself."

"Is he a good wizard?" Dorothy asked.

"He is a good wizard. Whether he is a man or not, I cannot tell, for I have never seen him. But he is more powerful than all the rest of us together. He lives in the Emerald City, and perhaps he can help you find your way home."

"How do I get there?" Dorothy asked.

"You must walk. It is a long journey, through a country that is sometimes pleasant and sometimes dark and dangerous. But I will use what magic I know to protect you."

She kissed Dorothy gently on the forehead. Where her lips touched, they left a round, shining mark.

"No one will dare to hurt someone who has been kissed by the Witch of the North," she said. "Now, take the silver shoes from the Wicked Witch. They must have some special power, though I do not know what it is."

Dorothy looked at the silver shoes. They were just her size. She put them on.

"The road to the City of Emeralds is paved with yellow brick," the Witch of the North told her. "You cannot miss it. When you reach the Wizard, do not be afraid. Tell him your story and ask for his help. Goodbye, my dear."

The three Munchkins bowed low and wished her a pleasant journey, and the Witch of the North gave her a friendly nod and spun around on her left heel three times, and then vanished completely. Toto barked in surprise.

Dorothy was alone.

She looked around at the beautiful land — the flowers, the fruit trees, the blue sky — and thought about gray Kansas and Aunt Em.

"Come along, Toto," she said. "We are going to the Emerald City to ask the great Oz to send us home."

She looked down and found the road of bright yellow brick. With Toto trotting beside her, Dorothy began to walk.

Chapter 3: How Dorothy Saved the Scarecrow

Dorothy walked along the yellow brick road, enjoying the beauty around her. The road was smooth and well-kept, and the countryside was green and lovely. The fences along the sides were painted a cheerful blue, and the houses were also blue, for this was still Munchkin country, and blue was the Munchkins' favorite color.

After walking for a while, Dorothy sat down on a fence to rest and eat some bread from her basket. There was a great cornfield beyond the fence, and not far away, she saw a Scarecrow, raised high on a pole to keep the crows away.

Dorothy stared at the Scarecrow curiously. His head was a small sack stuffed with straw, with eyes, a nose, and a mouth painted on in blue to look like a Munchkin. He wore a faded blue suit, old boots, and a pointed blue hat. He was stuffed with straw that stuck out at every seam.

As Dorothy looked at him, she thought she saw one of the painted eyes wink at her.

She blinked. Had she imagined it?

Then the Scarecrow nodded his head in a friendly way.

Dorothy climbed down from the fence and walked right up to him. "Did you just nod at me?" she asked.

"Certainly," answered the Scarecrow. "How do you do?"

"I'm — I'm fine," Dorothy said, quite astonished. She had never talked to a scarecrow before. "How are you?"

"Not very well," said the Scarecrow with a sigh. "It is very tiresome being stuck up here all day and all night with nothing to do. Would you be so kind as to help me down?"

Dorothy reached up and lifted him off the pole. He was very light, being stuffed with straw, and he stumbled when his feet touched the ground but quickly steadied himself.

"Thank you very much," said the Scarecrow. "I feel like a new man."

He stretched his arms and legs and looked around with his painted eyes. "Who are you?" he asked. "And where are you going?"

"My name is Dorothy, and I am going to the Emerald City to ask the great Wizard of Oz to send me home to Kansas."

"Where is the Emerald City?" the Scarecrow asked. "And who is Oz?"

"Don't you know?" Dorothy asked, surprised.

The Scarecrow shook his head sadly. "No, indeed. I don't know anything at all. You see, I am stuffed with straw, so I have no brains."

"Oh," said Dorothy. "I'm sorry."

"It's not so bad during the day," the Scarecrow said. "But at night, when it is dark and I am alone, I have nothing to think about, and that is very hard. If I only had brains, I would know things, and then I would not feel so empty and useless."

Dorothy thought about this for a moment. "If you come with me to the Emerald City," she said, "I will ask Oz to give you some brains."

The Scarecrow's painted face seemed to brighten. "Do you think the Wizard could really do that?"

"I don't know," Dorothy said honestly. "But he is a great Wizard, and the Witch of the North told me he might be able to help me. Perhaps he can help you, too."

"Then I will go with you," the Scarecrow declared. He took a step, then stumbled and nearly fell. Dorothy caught his arm. "I am sorry," he said. "I am not very steady on my feet. My legs do not always do what I want them to."

"That's all right," Dorothy said. "You'll get better with practice."

They started down the yellow brick road together, the Scarecrow wobbling at first but growing steadier with every step. Toto sniffed at the Scarecrow's straw-stuffed legs and barked once, then decided this strange creature was friendly enough and trotted along beside them.

"Tell me about yourself," Dorothy said.

"There is not much to tell," said the Scarecrow. "I was made only yesterday. A farmer painted my face, stuffed me with straw, and put me on that pole. He said I was to keep the crows away, but the crows were not afraid of me at all. One old crow even landed on my shoulder and said, 'Any fool can see that man is only stuffed with straw.' That made me very sad. If I only had brains, even the crows would respect me."

"It seems to me," Dorothy said carefully, "that a man who can talk as cleverly as you do ought to have brains."

"Do you really think so?" the Scarecrow asked eagerly.

"I do," said Dorothy.

The Scarecrow seemed to walk a little taller after that.

As they walked, they came to places where the road was rough and broken, and the Scarecrow sometimes tripped over the uneven bricks. But each time he fell, he picked himself right back up without complaining and kept going. He could not feel pain, he explained, because he was only stuffed with straw. But he did not like falling, because it made him feel foolish.

"Tell me about Kansas," the Scarecrow said after a while.

"It is gray and flat and dry," Dorothy said. "There are no beautiful flowers or green trees like there are here. But it is home, and Aunt Em is there, and that is all that matters."

"I cannot understand why you would want to go back to such a gray place," the Scarecrow said.

"That is because you have no brains," Dorothy said gently. "No matter how dull and gray home is, people who have hearts always want to return to it. There is no place like home."

The Scarecrow sighed. "Of course, I cannot understand it. If your heads were all stuffed with straw, like mine, you would probably all live in beautiful places, and then Kansas would have no people at all. It is fortunate for Kansas that you have brains."

Dorothy smiled and said nothing. She was beginning to like the Scarecrow very much.

Chapter 4: The Road Through the Forest

Dorothy and the Scarecrow walked together along the yellow brick road, and the Scarecrow told Dorothy everything he knew about the world — which was not very much, since he had only been alive for two days.

"I do not mind the walking," the Scarecrow said cheerfully. "My feet do not get tired, since they are stuffed with straw. And the road is interesting, even if I do not understand most of what I see."

"What don't you understand?" Dorothy asked.

"Well, for example — why is the sky blue? And why does water flow downhill instead of up? And what is that bright thing in the sky?"

"That is the sun," Dorothy said, smiling.

"It is very warm," the Scarecrow observed. "I like it. But I am told it is also the thing that sets fire to straw, which makes me nervous."

Dorothy laughed. She was beginning to think the Scarecrow was better company than most people she knew in Kansas. He asked strange questions, but he listened carefully to the answers, and he was always kind and polite.

They passed through Munchkin country, where everything was painted blue. The Munchkin farmers waved at them from their fields. Small blue birds sang in the trees. Dorothy's spirits lifted.

But as the afternoon wore on, the road led them out of the gentle farmland and into a thick, dark forest. The trees grew so close together that their branches tangled overhead, blocking out the sunlight. The cheerful blue fences disappeared, replaced by twisted roots and tangled undergrowth. The bird songs faded. The only sounds were their own footsteps and the creak of branches.

"This forest is very dark," the Scarecrow said. He did not sound frightened — the Scarecrow claimed he was never frightened, since fear required brains and he had none. But he moved a little closer to Dorothy.

"Are there wild animals in this forest?" Dorothy asked.

"I suppose there must be," the Scarecrow said. "But I have never been in a forest before, so I do not know what kinds."

The road was rougher here. Bricks were missing or broken, and roots pushed up through the gaps. The Scarecrow, who was still unsteady on his feet, stumbled every few steps. Each time he fell, Dorothy helped him up, and each time, he thanked her and kept going without complaint.

"Does it hurt when you fall?" Dorothy asked.

"Not at all," the Scarecrow said. "I cannot feel pain. That is one advantage of being stuffed with straw. But I do not like falling. It makes me feel clumsy and foolish, and I would very much like to be neither of those things."

"You are not foolish," Dorothy said firmly.

"That is kind of you to say. But a man without brains cannot be sure of anything, least of all himself."

They walked deeper into the forest. The darkness pressed in around them. Then, through the trees, Dorothy heard a sound — a faint, groaning cry, like someone in terrible pain.

"Did you hear that?" she whispered.

"Yes," the Scarecrow whispered back. "It came from over there."

They pushed through the undergrowth, following the sound. The groaning grew louder. And then, in a small clearing just off the road, they saw him.

A man made entirely of tin.

He stood beside a great tree, his axe raised above his head, frozen mid-swing. His body was dull with rust, and he could not move a single joint. His mouth was rusted shut, but through his clenched tin jaw came that terrible groaning — the sound of someone who had been trapped in the same position for a very, very long time.

"Oh my!" Dorothy cried. "We have to help him!"

Chapter 5: The Rescue of the Tin Woodman

Dorothy found the oil can in a tiny cottage near the frozen man. She hurried back and carefully oiled his jaw first, drop by drop, until it loosened.

"Oh, thank you," the Tin Woodman gasped. His voice was rough and creaky, like a gate that had not been opened in years. "Thank you. I have been standing here for more than a year. If you had not come along, I might have stood here forever."

"A whole year!" Dorothy said. "That's terrible!" She oiled his neck, then his arms, his elbows, his shoulders. The Scarecrow held him steady while Dorothy worked on his legs and knees. Joint by joint, the Tin Woodman came back to life.

He lowered his axe at last, rolled his shoulders, bent his knees, and took one careful step. Then another. His joints squeaked, but they moved.

"I feel like a new man," the Tin Woodman said. "And I owe it all to you."

"How did you get so rusted?" the Scarecrow asked.

"I was chopping that tree when it began to rain. The water rusted my joints, and I could not reach my oil can. So I stood here. And stood. And stood. The sun rose and set, the seasons changed, and I stood. I had nothing to do but think."

"What did you think about?" Dorothy asked.

The Tin Woodman was quiet for a moment. "I thought about the girl I used to love," he said softly. "Before I was made of tin."

"You were not always tin?" the Scarecrow asked, surprised.

"No. I was once a man of flesh and blood — a woodcutter. I fell in love with a beautiful Munchkin girl. She was kind and funny and she laughed at my jokes, and I wanted to marry her more than anything. But the Wicked Witch of the East did not want us to be happy. She enchanted my axe so that it slipped and cut me, piece by piece. First my legs, then my arms, then my head, then my body — and each time, a tinsmith replaced the part with tin."

"How awful," Dorothy whispered.

"The worst part," the Tin Woodman continued, "was that when the tinsmith made me a complete body of tin, he forgot to give me a heart. Without a heart, I could not love the girl anymore. I could not feel anything at all — no happiness, no sadness, no love." He paused. "At least, that is what I am told. And yet, standing here for a year, thinking of her... I felt something. I do not know what to call it. But it hurt."

A single tear rolled down his tin face.

"Careful," the Scarecrow said. "You'll rust again."

Dorothy quickly oiled the tear away. "If you could have anything in the world," she said, "what would it be?"

"A heart," the Tin Woodman said without hesitation. "I would give up everything — my axe, my strength, my tin body — for a real heart. Because without one, nothing else matters."

"We are on our way to the Emerald City," Dorothy said. "I am going to ask the great Wizard of Oz to send me home to Kansas, and the Scarecrow is going to ask for brains. Perhaps the Wizard could give you a heart."

"Do you really think so?" the Tin Woodman asked. Hope flickered in his tin eyes — which, Dorothy thought, was a remarkable thing to see in someone who claimed he could not feel.

"I think it is worth asking," Dorothy said.

"Then I will come with you," the Tin Woodman declared. "But I must ask you to carry my oil can. If it rains, or if I cry, I will need oiling at once."

"I will carry it," Dorothy promised.

So the three of them set off down the yellow brick road, deeper into the dark forest. The Scarecrow stumbled over roots. The Tin Woodman's joints squeaked with every step. And Dorothy walked between them, a girl from Kansas with silver shoes and an oil can, making her way toward a city she had never seen to meet a wizard she was not sure she believed in.

But she had friends, and they had her, and that made all the difference.

Chapter 6: The Cowardly Lion

The forest grew darker and wilder. Strange sounds echoed through the trees — growls, rustling, and the snap of twigs. Dorothy walked closer to her friends.

"How far is it to the Emerald City?" she asked.

"I don't know," said the Tin Woodman. "I have never been there. But my father once told me it was a long, difficult journey."

"I heard that the road passes through dangerous country," the Scarecrow added. "But since I have no brains, I am not afraid."

"And since I have no heart," the Tin Woodman said, "I cannot be afraid either."

Dorothy said nothing. She was the only one in the group who had both brains AND a heart, and she was quite frightened. Toto was frightened too. He pressed against Dorothy's legs and growled at the shadows.

Suddenly, a great beast burst out of the trees with a terrible roar. It was an enormous lion, with a thick golden mane and huge paws. He knocked the Scarecrow spinning with one swipe and struck at the Tin Woodman with his claws, which scraped against the tin but did no real damage.

Then the Lion opened his great mouth and lunged at Toto.

Dorothy did not think. She did not plan. She did not hesitate. She ran forward and slapped the Lion as hard as she could, right on his nose.

"Don't you dare bite Toto!" she cried. "You ought to be ashamed of yourself — a big beast like you, trying to bite a poor little dog!"

The Lion looked shocked. He sat down and rubbed his nose with his paw.

"I didn't bite him," the Lion said in a hurt voice.

"No, but you tried to," Dorothy said. "You are nothing but a big coward."

"I know it," the Lion said, hanging his great head in shame. "I have always known it. I am a disgrace."

Dorothy stared at him. This was not what she had expected.

"But you're a lion," she said. "Lions are supposed to be the king of beasts."

"I know," the Lion said miserably. "Everyone expects me to be brave and fierce. But I am terrified of almost everything. I roar to scare other creatures away, because if they got close, they would see that I am really a coward."

"That's terrible," said the Scarecrow, picking straw out of his clothes.

"It is," the Lion agreed. "My roar is all I have. My heart pounds, my legs shake, and I want to run away. A real king of beasts would not be afraid. But I cannot help it."

"Maybe you just need courage," Dorothy said thoughtfully. "We are on our way to the Emerald City to see the great Wizard of Oz. The Scarecrow is going to ask for brains, and the Tin Woodman wants a heart. Perhaps the Wizard could give you courage."

The Lion's eyes grew wide. "Do you think he could?" he asked. "If I only had courage, I would be everything a lion is supposed to be."

"Come with us," Dorothy said. "You can ask him yourself."

"I will," said the Lion. "Because right now, my life is unbearable. Every day I wake up afraid, and every night I go to sleep ashamed. If the Wizard can fix that, it would be worth any journey."

So the Lion joined the group. He was enormous — Dorothy barely reached his shoulder — and when he walked, the ground trembled slightly under his great paws. The Scarecrow walked on one side of Dorothy, the Tin Woodman on the other, and the Lion padded along behind them. Toto did not like the Lion at first and stayed as far away as possible, but after a while, the little dog decided that any creature who cried about being a coward was probably not very dangerous after all.

"I am curious about something," Dorothy said as they walked. "You say you are a coward, but you jumped out of the trees and attacked all of us. That seems rather brave to me."

"Oh, that," the Lion said, embarrassed. "I was more scared than any of you. My heart was pounding. I just roared and charged because I did not know what else to do."

"But isn't that exactly what courage is?" the Scarecrow asked. "Doing something even when you are afraid?"

The Lion thought about this for a long moment. "No," he said finally. "I think real courage means not being afraid at all."

"I'm not sure about that," Dorothy said. But she did not argue.

They walked on through the forest. Dorothy noticed something: the Scarecrow, who said he had no brains, was the one who figured out the safest path through the trees. The Tin Woodman, who said he had no heart, carefully moved a beetle off the road so it would not be stepped on. And the Lion, who said he had no courage, walked at the back of the group, keeping watch for danger.

Dorothy kept these observations to herself. But she was beginning to wonder if her friends already had exactly what they were looking for, and simply did not know it.

Chapter 7: The Journey to the Great Oz

The next morning, they set off again on the yellow brick road. The forest was still thick around them, but the road was clear, and the four companions walked together in good spirits.

They had not gone far when they came to a great ditch that stretched across the road. It was very wide and very deep, with jagged rocks at the bottom. There was no way around it.

"What shall we do?" Dorothy asked.

The Scarecrow thought for a moment. "We cannot fly, and we cannot climb down and back up — it's too deep. But the Lion is very large and strong. Perhaps he could jump across."

"I can try," the Lion said nervously. "But what about the rest of you?"

"You could carry us, one at a time," the Scarecrow said.

The Lion looked at the ditch. It was a terrifying distance. His legs trembled. But he crouched low, gathered all his strength, and leaped — sailing through the air and landing safely on the other side.

"It wasn't so bad," he called back, though his voice shook.

He jumped back and carried the Scarecrow across first, since he was the lightest. Then Dorothy, holding Toto tightly. Finally, the Tin Woodman, who was the heaviest and made the Lion grunt with the effort. But the great beast managed it.

"There," said the Scarecrow. "Problem solved."

"That was your idea," Dorothy told him. "That was very clever."

"Was it?" the Scarecrow said, surprised. "I suppose it just seemed like common sense."

They continued walking. The forest thinned, and patches of sunlight began to break through the leaves. Then they heard a low, rumbling growl from the trees ahead.

Two great beasts leaped onto the road — fierce, catlike creatures with long teeth and angry eyes. They snarled at the group and crouched, ready to spring.

The Cowardly Lion stepped forward.

"Stay behind me," he said, and his voice came out deeper and stronger than usual. He threw back his great head and let out a roar so loud and so terrible that the leaves shook on the trees and the two beasts stopped cold. They stared at the Lion, then turned and ran back into the forest.

"They're gone," the Lion said, turning around. His legs were shaking. "My heart is still pounding."

"You just saved us all," the Tin Woodman said.

"I was terrified," the Lion admitted.

"You were terrified and you did it anyway," Dorothy said. "That matters more than you think."

They walked on. The forest grew lighter, and soon they emerged into open country filled with wildflowers. A sparkling river ran alongside the road, and they stopped to drink and rest.

"I like this much better than the forest," the Lion said, lying down in the tall grass. "The open air makes me feel braver."

"You were plenty brave in the forest," Dorothy said.

The Lion shook his great mane. "I was pretending."

"Everyone pretends sometimes," said the Tin Woodman quietly. "I pretend I do not feel sad, even though I do."

"And I pretend I do not notice things," said the Scarecrow, "even though I notice quite a lot."

Dorothy looked at her three friends — the one who said he had no brains, the one who said he had no heart, and the one who said he had no courage — and she thought they were the cleverest, kindest, bravest companions anyone could wish for.

"We make a good team," she said.

"We do," the Scarecrow agreed. "Though I cannot imagine why. A girl, a man of straw, a man of tin, and a cowardly lion — we are not exactly an army."

"No," Dorothy said, smiling. "But we are friends. And that is better."

They rested by the river as the afternoon sun warmed them, and Toto chased butterflies through the tall grass, and for a little while, everything was peaceful.

But the yellow brick road stretched on ahead of them, and the Emerald City was still far away. There would be more dangers before they reached it.

Chapter 8: The Deadly Poppy Field

The yellow brick road led them through rolling green meadows now, and the sun shone warm and bright. After the darkness of the forest, it felt wonderful to be in open country again. Dorothy could see for miles in every direction — green fields, blue sky, and in the far distance, a faint shimmer that might have been the Emerald City.

"Look," said the Scarecrow, shading his painted eyes with one stuffed hand. "Flowers."

He was right. Ahead of them, the fields were filled with enormous poppies — great scarlet flowers nodding on their stems, stretching out on both sides of the road as far as they could see. They were beautiful, and their sweet, heavy scent drifted toward the travelers on the breeze.

"How lovely!" Dorothy said, breathing deeply. The smell was rich and thick, like warm honey.

"They are very pretty," the Tin Woodman agreed. "But I have heard that poppies can be dangerous."

"Dangerous?" Dorothy laughed. "They are only flowers."

They walked into the poppy field. The road of yellow brick cut straight through the center of it, and the scarlet flowers pressed close on either side. Their scent grew stronger with every step — sweet and heavy and impossibly thick, filling Dorothy's nose and mouth and lungs.

After a few minutes, Dorothy yawned.

"I feel so sleepy," she said. Her eyelids were heavy. Her feet dragged. The flowers seemed to sway around her, whispering sleep, sleep, sleep.

"We must hurry through this field," the Tin Woodman said urgently. "These flowers are poisonous. Their scent will put any living creature to sleep — a sleep so deep you might never wake up."

"I can barely keep my eyes open," the Lion said. His great head was drooping, and his paws dragged along the bricks. "Must... keep... walking..."

But the poppies were too strong. The Lion stumbled, swayed, and then collapsed in the middle of the road, fast asleep. His enormous body blocked the path completely.

"Run, Dorothy!" the Scarecrow shouted. "Run as fast as you can! We must get you out of this field!"

Dorothy tried to run, but her legs felt like they were made of sand. Toto had already fallen asleep beside the Lion. Dorothy took three more steps, then two, then one — and then she sank down among the scarlet flowers and closed her eyes.

The Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman looked at each other.

"We must carry her," the Scarecrow said.

Neither of them was affected by the poppies. The Scarecrow had no lungs to breathe with, and the Tin Woodman had no flesh to poison. The deadly flowers were harmless to them.

The Tin Woodman lifted Dorothy gently — so gently that the Scarecrow noticed tears forming in his tin eyes. "Careful," the Scarecrow said. "You will rust."

"I cannot help it," the Tin Woodman said. "She looks so small and helpless."

The Scarecrow picked up Toto and tucked him under one arm. Then they began to run, carrying their sleeping friends through the field of poisonous flowers.

They ran and ran. The field seemed to go on forever. The Scarecrow's straw legs were not built for speed, and the Tin Woodman's joints creaked with every stride. But they did not stop.

Finally — finally — they reached the edge of the poppy field and tumbled out onto clean, sweet grass. They laid Dorothy and Toto down gently in the fresh air.

"She's breathing," the Tin Woodman said with relief.

"But what about the Lion?" the Scarecrow asked, looking back at the vast field. The Lion was a distant golden shape among the red flowers, motionless. "He is far too heavy for us to carry."

They stared at each other helplessly. The Lion was their friend. They could not leave him there to sleep forever.

"We need help," the Scarecrow said. "And I think I know where to find it."

Chapter 9: The Queen of the Field Mice

Dorothy and Toto lay sleeping on the grass beyond the poppy field. The Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman stood over them, relieved that they had carried their friends to safety, but sick with worry about the Lion. He lay deep in the field of poisonous flowers, too heavy for them to move, sinking into a sleep from which he might never wake.

"We must do something," the Tin Woodman said. "He is our friend."

"I know," the Scarecrow said. "Let me think."

He thought very hard. The Scarecrow always said he had no brains, but in moments like this, his head seemed to work better than anyone's.

Just then, a wildcat burst from the tall grass, chasing a tiny field mouse. Without thinking, the Tin Woodman raised his axe and brought it down. The wildcat fell, and the mouse was saved.

The little mouse looked up at the Tin Woodman with bright, grateful eyes. "You have saved my life," she said in a small, squeaking voice. "I am the Queen of the Field Mice. My subjects and I are forever in your debt."

"It was nothing," the Tin Woodman said, bowing low. He truly meant it — he simply could not bear to see any creature hurt.

Hundreds of mice appeared from the grass, surrounding their queen. They were tiny — no bigger than Dorothy's thumb — but there were so many of them that the ground seemed to ripple with brown fur.

The Scarecrow's eyes brightened. "Your Majesty," he said, "I have an idea. Our friend the Lion is trapped in the poppy field, asleep. He is far too heavy for us to carry. But if the Tin Woodman were to build a cart, could your mice pull it?"

The Queen tilted her tiny head. "The Lion is very large," she said. "But we are very many. Yes — I believe we could do it."

The Tin Woodman set to work immediately. He was a woodcutter by trade, and even though his body was tin, his skill with an axe was extraordinary. He felled small trees, trimmed branches, and built a rough but sturdy cart in less than an hour.

Meanwhile, the Scarecrow organized the mice. He tied strings of braided grass to the cart and attached them to the mice in long rows — thousands of mice, each one pulling with all its tiny strength.

Getting the Lion onto the cart was the hardest part. He was enormous — as heavy as a boulder and completely limp. The Scarecrow pushed from behind while the Tin Woodman lifted from the side, and inch by inch, groaning and straining, they rolled the great beast onto the wooden platform.

"Now," said the Queen, "pull!"

The mice surged forward. The cart creaked, rocked, and then began to move — slowly at first, then faster, rolling through the poppy field toward clean air. The Scarecrow walked alongside, pushing when the wheels stuck. The Tin Woodman walked on the other side, steadying the Lion when he threatened to slide off.

The poppies swayed around them, their deadly perfume thick in the air. The Scarecrow and Tin Woodman felt nothing — straw and tin do not breathe — but they moved as fast as they could for the sake of the mice, who were flesh and blood and very small.

At last, the cart rolled out of the poppy field and onto clean grass. The mice collapsed in exhaustion, their tiny sides heaving. The Lion lay on the cart, still deeply asleep, but breathing.

"He will wake when the fresh air clears the poison from his lungs," the Queen said. "Give him time."

"Thank you," the Scarecrow said. "You saved our friend's life."

"And your friend saved mine," the Queen replied, nodding at the Tin Woodman. "We are even. But if you ever need us again, come to this field and call. We will come."

The mice disappeared into the grass like a brown river flowing back into the earth. The Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman sat down to wait.

Dorothy woke first. She blinked in the sunlight, saw the Lion on the cart beside her, and sat up in alarm. "What happened?"

The Scarecrow told her everything — the wildcat, the Queen, the cart, the rescue.

"You did all that while I was asleep?" Dorothy asked.

"You needed saving," the Tin Woodman said simply. "So we saved you."

Dorothy felt tears prick her eyes. "You carried me out of the poppy field," she said. "And then you saved the Lion. You did all of this without being asked."

"That is what friends do," the Scarecrow said.

The Lion stirred. He opened one great eye, then the other. He yawned enormously, stretched, and looked around in confusion.

"How did I get here?" he asked.

"A thousand mice pulled you on a cart," Dorothy said.

The Lion blinked. "I must still be dreaming."

"You are not," the Tin Woodman said, smiling. "Welcome back."

Chapter 10: The Guardian of the Gates

They rested in the grass until everyone felt strong again. Then they stood and looked ahead. The yellow brick road climbed a gentle hill, and beyond the hill, something sparkled in the distance — bright and green and impossibly dazzling, like a jewel set into the earth.

"The Emerald City," the Scarecrow whispered.

They hurried forward. With every step, the sparkle grew brighter and more magnificent. Soon they could see the great wall that surrounded the city, studded with emeralds that caught the sunlight and threw it back in brilliant green flashes. The wall was very tall, and behind it, towers and spires rose into the sky, gleaming like they were made of solid light.

"I have never seen anything so beautiful," the Tin Woodman said.

"Neither have I," said the Lion, and for once, he did not seem afraid of anything.

At the gate stood a small man dressed entirely in green — green clothes, green shoes, even green whiskers that curled at the tips. He had sharp, intelligent eyes and a serious expression.

"What do you wish in the Emerald City?" he asked.

"We have come to see the great Oz," Dorothy said.

The man's eyes widened. "Oz? No one has asked to see Oz in many years. He is very powerful and very terrible. If you come on a foolish errand, he may destroy you."

"It is not a foolish errand," Dorothy said. She stood up straight and looked the man in the eye. "I need to get home."

The Guardian studied her — her dusty dress, her silver shoes, the shining mark on her forehead where the Witch of the North had kissed her. He studied the Scarecrow, the Tin Woodman, and the great Lion. He studied Toto, who wagged his tail.

"Very well," the Guardian said at last. "But you must wear these spectacles inside the city. Everyone who enters the Emerald City must wear them, by order of Oz."

He opened a large box filled with green-tinted spectacles of every size. He found pairs for each of them — even Toto, whose tiny spectacles made him look very dignified — and locked them onto their heads with a small golden key.

"Why must we wear them?" Dorothy asked.

"If you did not," the Guardian said, "the brightness and glory of the Emerald City would blind you. The city is so dazzling that no one can look upon it with unprotected eyes."

He turned the key in the great lock, and the gate swung open.

Dorothy gasped.

Everything was green. The streets were green marble. The houses were built of green stone with green glass windows. The sky itself seemed to glow with emerald light through the tinted spectacles. People walked by in green velvet and green silk, eating green candy, selling green lemonade from green carts. A green dog trotted past, followed by a green cat. Even the sun seemed to shine green.

"It's incredible," Dorothy breathed.

The Tin Woodman's tin body reflected green light in every direction. The Lion's golden mane looked almost emerald. The Scarecrow's painted face glowed.

They walked through the streets in wonder. Everything was clean and bright and beautiful. The people smiled at them. Children waved. It felt like walking through the inside of a jewel.

But the Scarecrow, who noticed things even though he said he had no brains, walked quietly and looked around with his painted eyes, and something bothered him.

The spectacles were green. Everything looked green through them. But was the city ACTUALLY green? If everyone always wore green glasses, how would they know what color anything really was?

He kept this thought to himself. But it settled into the back of his straw-stuffed head, and it would not go away.

A soldier with a long green beard led them to the Palace of Oz, which was the grandest building of all — green marble walls, emerald-studded doors, and hallways so bright they seemed to hum with light.

"The great Oz will see you," the soldier said. "But only one at a time. And I warn you — do not make him angry."

The four friends looked at each other.

"I will go first," Dorothy said.

She took a breath, straightened her dress, and walked through the great doors alone.

Chapter 11: The Great and Terrible Oz

A soldier with a long green beard led them through the gleaming streets to the Palace of Oz. The palace was the grandest building Dorothy had ever seen — towering green walls, emerald-studded gates, and corridors so bright they seemed to glow from within.

"The great Oz will see you," the soldier told them, "but only one at a time. Who will go first?"

"I will," said Dorothy.

The soldier led her into a vast, round room. The ceiling was so high she could barely see it. The walls were covered with emeralds, and in the center of the room stood an enormous throne made of green marble.

On the throne sat nothing but a giant head.

It had no body, no arms, no legs — just an enormous head, bigger than the head of the biggest giant. It had no hair, and its eyes rolled slowly, looking down at Dorothy. Its mouth moved.

"I am Oz, the Great and Terrible," the head said in a voice that echoed through the chamber. "Who are you, and why do you seek me?"

Dorothy's knees trembled, but she took a breath and spoke clearly. "I am Dorothy, the small and meek. I have come to ask you to send me back to Kansas, where my Aunt Em and Uncle Henry are. I do not like your country, although it is so beautiful. And I am sure Aunt Em must be terribly worried about me."

The eyes on the great head blinked slowly. "Where did you get those silver shoes?" the voice boomed.

"From the Wicked Witch of the East. My house fell on her when the cyclone brought me here."

"And where did you get that mark on your forehead?"

Dorothy touched the spot where the Witch of the North had kissed her. "The Witch of the North kissed me when she said goodbye and sent me to you."

The great head was silent for a long time. Then it spoke. "I will help you — but only if you do something for me first."

"What must I do?" Dorothy asked.

"You must kill the Wicked Witch of the West."

Dorothy's heart sank. "But I cannot!" she cried. "I am just a girl. I have never killed anything in my life, and I do not want to start now."

"The Wicked Witch of the West is the only truly wicked creature left in this land," the great head said. "You wear the silver shoes that hold her sister's power. You bear the mark of the Witch of the North's kiss, which protects you. You are stronger than you know. Kill the Witch, and I will send you home."

Dorothy left the throne room with tears in her eyes.

The Scarecrow went next. When he entered the great room, the throne was empty. Instead, a beautiful fairy floated in the air above it, with shimmering wings and a crown of jewels.

"I am Oz, the Great and Terrible," the fairy said. "Why do you seek me?"

"I want brains," the Scarecrow said. "My head is stuffed with straw, and I wish to have real brains so I can think properly."

"Kill the Wicked Witch of the West," the fairy said, "and I will give you brains — the finest brains in the land of Oz."

The Tin Woodman went third. He found on the throne a terrible beast, covered in thick fur, with the head of a rhinoceros and five long arms. Five eyes of flame stared down at him.

"I am Oz, the Great and Terrible," the beast growled. "Why do you seek me?"

"I want a heart," the Tin Woodman said. "I am made of tin and cannot feel. I want to love and be loved."

"Kill the Wicked Witch of the West," the beast said, "and I will give you a heart."

The Cowardly Lion went last. When he entered, the throne held nothing but a ball of fire, so bright and fierce that he could barely look at it. The room was unbearably hot.

"I am Oz, the Great and Terrible," the fire roared. "Why do you seek me?"

"I — I want courage," the Lion stammered, crouching low. "I am a coward, and I want to be brave."

"Kill the Wicked Witch of the West," the fire blazed, "and I will give you courage."

The four companions gathered in a hallway afterward and compared their experiences.

"He appeared as a giant head to me," Dorothy said.

"A beautiful fairy to me," the Scarecrow said.

"A terrible beast to me," the Tin Woodman said.

"A ball of fire to me," the Lion said.

They stared at each other.

"Why would he look different to each of us?" the Scarecrow asked slowly.

No one had an answer. But a seed of doubt had been planted, and it sat quietly in each of their minds.

"He wants us to kill the Wicked Witch," Dorothy said unhappily. "How can we possibly do that?"

"We must try," the Scarecrow said. "It is the only way to get what we need."

"I do not like it," the Tin Woodman said. "But Dorothy needs to get home. So we will go."

"Together," the Lion said. His voice shook, but he said it anyway. "We will go together."

Dorothy looked at her friends — the Scarecrow standing tall, the Tin Woodman gripping his axe, the Lion trembling but determined — and she felt something warm spread through her chest.

"Together," she agreed.

Chapter 12: The Search for the Wicked Witch

The Wicked Witch of the West had only one eye, but it was as powerful as a telescope. She could see everything that happened in her domain, no matter how far away. So when Dorothy and her friends set out from the Emerald City heading west, the Witch saw them coming.

"Fools," she muttered. She sat on her throne in her dark castle and planned her attack.

First, she called a pack of forty great wolves. "Go to those strangers and tear them to pieces," she commanded.

The wolves ran howling across the plains. The Tin Woodman heard them coming.

"Stand behind me," he said. He planted his feet wide, raised his axe, and waited. When the wolves came, snarling and snapping, the Tin Woodman swung his axe with terrible skill. He was tireless, because he was made of tin, and the wolves could not bite through his metal body. One by one, they fell before him until not a single wolf remained.

When the Witch saw what had happened, she flew into a rage. She sent forty crows — huge, black, vicious crows — with orders to peck out the strangers' eyes.

The Scarecrow saw them coming. "Lie down," he told the others. "I will handle this."

He stood in the middle of the road, spread his arms wide, and waited. When the crows dove at him, they found nothing but straw. The Scarecrow grabbed them one by one, wringing their necks with his stuffed hands. They could not hurt him, because he felt no pain. Soon the crows were finished.

The Witch screamed with fury. She sent a swarm of black bees — thousands of them — to sting the strangers to death.

"Quick!" the Scarecrow said. "Take my straw and cover Dorothy, Toto, and the Lion!"

The Tin Woodman pulled handfuls of straw from the Scarecrow's body and piled it over the others, hiding them completely. The bees found only the Tin Woodman standing in the road, and when they stung his tin body, their stingers broke and they fell to the ground.

The Scarecrow was a sorry sight afterward — flat and empty, his clothes hanging loosely. But Dorothy and the Tin Woodman stuffed him full again, and he declared himself as good as new.

"Three attacks," Dorothy said. "Do you think she will stop?"

"No," the Scarecrow said quietly. "She will not stop until she destroys us or we destroy her."

He was right. The Witch had one final weapon — the most terrible of all. In her castle, locked in a cupboard, was a golden cap. Whoever wore it could command the Winged Monkeys three times, and three times only. The Witch had already used the cap twice. This would be her last command.

She placed the cap on her head and spoke the magic words. The sky darkened. A rushing sound filled the air. Then they appeared — dozens of enormous monkeys with powerful wings, swooping down from the clouds like a nightmare.

The Winged Monkeys seized the Tin Woodman and carried him high into the air, then dropped him onto sharp rocks far below. His body was battered and dented, his arms and legs bent at terrible angles. They pulled every wisp of straw from the Scarecrow's body and threw his empty clothes into the top of a tall tree. They tied the Lion with thick ropes and carried him to the Witch's castle, where they locked him in a small iron cage.

But when the leader of the Winged Monkeys reached for Dorothy, he saw the mark on her forehead — the shining spot where the Witch of the North had kissed her.

"We cannot harm this girl," the monkey said. "She is protected by the power of good. We will carry her to the Witch, but we cannot hurt her."

So the Winged Monkeys carried Dorothy and Toto gently to the Witch's castle and set them down at the gate. The Witch was waiting.

She looked at Dorothy with her one terrible eye and smiled a cruel smile. Then she saw the silver shoes on Dorothy's feet, and her smile vanished. She knew those shoes held great power — perhaps enough to destroy her.

The Witch was afraid of Dorothy, though she would never show it. She was afraid of the dark, she was afraid of water, and she was deeply afraid of the silver shoes. But she was clever and cruel, and she had ruled the western lands for many years.

"You will be my servant," the Witch told Dorothy. "You will cook and clean and scrub the floors."

"I will not," Dorothy said. She was frightened — more frightened than she had been in the poppy field, or the dark forest, or even the throne room of Oz. But she planted her feet and looked the Witch in her one eye. "My friends will come for me."

"Your friends are destroyed," the Witch said. "The Tin Woodman is smashed. The Scarecrow is scattered. The Lion is in chains. You are alone."

Dorothy felt her heart break. But she did not cry — not in front of the Witch.

"I am not alone," she said quietly. "I have Toto."

The little dog pressed against her leg and growled at the Witch, and for that one small moment, Dorothy did not feel afraid at all.

Chapter 13: Dorothy the Prisoner

The Wicked Witch of the West kept Dorothy as her prisoner. She set her to work in the kitchen — scrubbing floors, washing pots, carrying water, sweeping ashes from the great fireplace. The work was hard and the Witch was cruel, but Dorothy did not break.

Every day, Dorothy checked on the Cowardly Lion, who was locked in an iron cage in the courtyard. The Witch was trying to starve him into pulling her carriage like a horse, but the Lion refused.

"I will not serve her," the Lion said through the bars. His voice was hoarse, but his eyes were fierce. "She can keep me in this cage forever, but I will not bow to a bully."

"That is very brave of you," Dorothy said, pushing food through the bars. She had saved scraps from the kitchen — bread, vegetables, a few pieces of meat she had hidden in her apron.

"Or very stubborn," the Lion said. "I am not sure there is a difference."

Dorothy smiled despite everything. Even here, even caged, the Lion was still himself.

The days were long and lonely. Dorothy missed the Scarecrow's clever conversation and the Tin Woodman's gentle kindness. She missed the open road and the sunshine and the yellow brick road stretching ahead of her full of possibility. In the Witch's castle, there was no possibility. There was only the same gray routine, day after day: scrub, sweep, carry, serve.

But Dorothy watched. And Dorothy planned.

She noticed that the Witch was afraid of certain things. The Witch never went near water — she washed herself with sand and kept every door and window shut when it rained. She flinched when Dorothy carried buckets from the well, and she screamed if even a single drop splashed in her direction.

Dorothy also noticed the silver shoes. The Witch stared at them constantly — with hunger, with fear, with a desperate longing that made her one eye burn. She wanted those shoes more than anything.

One day, the Witch tried to steal them. She placed an invisible iron bar across the kitchen floor, and when Dorothy walked past carrying a bucket, she tripped and fell. One of the silver shoes flew off her foot, and the Witch snatched it up.

"Give that back!" Dorothy cried. "That is mine!"

"It is mine now," the Witch said, clutching the shoe with both hands. "And soon I will have the other one, too."

Dorothy was furious — more furious than she had ever been in her life. The silver shoes were hers. The Witch of the North had given them to her. They were the last thing she had from the beginning of her journey, the last connection to the good people who had helped her.

"You are a wicked, wicked creature," Dorothy said. "And I am not afraid of you."

The Witch laughed. But there was something nervous in her laugh, because she could see the mark shining on Dorothy's forehead — the kiss of the Witch of the North — and she knew that as long as that mark remained, she could not truly harm the girl.

Dorothy lay awake that night, stroking Toto's ears and thinking. She still had one silver shoe. The Witch had the other. The Witch was afraid of water. And tomorrow, Dorothy would have to carry water from the well again, as she did every day.

An idea was forming — not a plan, exactly, but a feeling. A sense that something was about to change.

She held Toto close and waited for morning.

Chapter 14: The Melting of the Witch

The next morning, Dorothy filled her bucket at the well and carried it into the kitchen. The Witch was waiting for her, wearing the stolen silver shoe on one foot and nothing on the other. She was in a foul mood.

"Scrub faster!" the Witch snapped. "And when you are finished with the kitchen, scrub the hallway. And when you are finished with the hallway, scrub the stairs. I want every stone in this castle clean enough to eat from."

Dorothy scrubbed. The water sloshed in the bucket, and the Witch kept her distance, watching from the doorway with her one terrible eye.

It happened quickly.

The Witch lunged forward, trying to snatch the second silver shoe from Dorothy's foot. Dorothy jumped back. The bucket tipped. Water splashed across the floor — and across the Witch.

The Witch screamed.

It was not a scream of anger. It was a scream of pure terror.

"What have you done?" the Witch shrieked. She was shrinking. Her black robes were collapsing around her. Her body was dissolving, melting away like brown sugar in the rain. "Don't you know that water will be the end of me?"

"I didn't know!" Dorothy cried, her heart hammering. "I'm sorry!"

"I have been wicked in my day," the Witch wailed, her voice fading as she grew smaller and smaller. "But I never thought a little girl would be the end of me. Oh — I'm melting! Melting! What a world... what a world..."

Her voice trailed off into nothing. Where the Wicked Witch of the West had stood, there was now only a brown puddle on the kitchen floor and a pointed black hat floating in it.

Dorothy stood very still. The kitchen was silent. Toto crept out from under the table and sniffed at the puddle. Even he seemed surprised.

"It was an accident," Dorothy whispered. She had not meant to do it. She had been carrying water, and the Witch had lunged, and the bucket had tipped. That was all.

She picked up the silver shoe from the brown puddle, wiped it clean, and put it back on her foot. Both silver shoes were hers again.

Then Dorothy took a deep breath and walked out of the kitchen.

She found the Winkies in the courtyard. They were the people of the western land — small, quiet, frightened people who had served the Witch for years out of terror. They had watched Dorothy go into the kitchen carrying a bucket of water and heard the Witch's scream.

"The Witch is dead," Dorothy told them.

For a moment, no one moved. Then one Winkie began to cry. Then another. Then they were all crying and laughing at the same time, hugging each other and jumping up and down. Years of fear and cruelty dissolved in an instant, like the Witch herself.

Dorothy ran to the Lion's cage and broke the lock. The great beast bounded out, stretching his cramped legs and shaking his mane.

"Is it true?" the Lion asked. "She is really gone?"

"She is really gone," Dorothy said.

"Then we must find the Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman," the Lion said. "We must put our friends back together."

The Winkies were eager to help. They sent their best tinsmiths to find the Tin Woodman on the rocks where the Winged Monkeys had dropped him. He was badly dented — his arms bent at wrong angles, his legs twisted, one side of his body nearly flat. But the tinsmiths were the finest in Oz, and they worked for three days, hammering and shaping and polishing, until the Tin Woodman looked almost as good as new.

When he opened his eyes and saw Dorothy, a tear rolled down his cheek. "I thought I would never see you again," he said.

"Don't cry," Dorothy said, reaching for the oil can. "You'll rust."

"Some tears are worth the rust," the Tin Woodman said.

The Winkies found the Scarecrow's clothes in the top of a tall tree, empty and flat. They stuffed him with fresh, clean straw — the finest straw in the western land — and sewed him up carefully. When his painted eyes blinked open, the Scarecrow looked around and said, "Well. That was thoroughly unpleasant. But I must say, new straw feels wonderful. The old straw was getting quite lumpy."

Dorothy threw her arms around him. Then around the Tin Woodman. Then around the Lion. Toto barked and ran in circles around all of them.

They were together again. And the Wicked Witch was gone.

"Now," Dorothy said, "let us go back to the Emerald City and tell Oz."

Chapter 15: The Winged Monkeys

On the journey back to the Emerald City, Dorothy reached into her basket and pulled out the golden cap she had found in the Witch's castle.

"What a curious thing," she said, turning it over in her hands. It was soft and beautiful, with a circle of diamonds and rubies around the brim.

"That is the Golden Cap," the Lion said. "I have heard stories about it. Whoever wears it can command the Winged Monkeys three times."

"The Winged Monkeys?" Dorothy shuddered. She remembered all too well how they had battered the Tin Woodman, scattered the Scarecrow, and caged the Lion. "I don't want anything to do with them."

"But they are not evil," the Lion said. "They are bound by the cap's magic. They must obey whoever wears it. The Witch commanded them because she had the cap. Now you have it."

Dorothy looked at the cap doubtfully. "I suppose we could use them to fly us back to the Emerald City. The road is so long, and we are all tired."

"That is a very clever idea," the Scarecrow said.

Dorothy put on the Golden Cap and spoke the magic words that were written inside the brim. The sky darkened, the wind picked up, and with a great rushing of wings, the Winged Monkeys appeared — dozens of them, circling overhead.

Their leader landed before Dorothy and bowed. "What do you command?" he asked.

"Please carry us to the Emerald City," Dorothy said.

"It shall be done," said the monkey king.

The monkeys lifted the travelers gently into the air. Dorothy rode on the back of the king himself, with Toto in her lap. The Scarecrow and the Tin Woodman were each carried by four monkeys, and eight strong monkeys bore the heavy Lion between them.

As they flew, Dorothy looked down at the beautiful land of Oz spread below her — the green fields, the sparkling rivers, the dark forests, and far behind them, the gray ruins of the Witch's castle. Ahead, the Emerald City gleamed like a jewel.

"May I ask you something?" Dorothy said to the monkey king.

"Of course."

"Why did you serve the Wicked Witch? Were you afraid of her?"

The monkey king sighed. "It is a long story," he said. "We Winged Monkeys were once free — the happiest creatures in all of Oz. We played and flew and caused mischief wherever we went. But long ago, my grandfather played a prank on a powerful sorceress, and she was furious. She enchanted the Golden Cap and bound our people to obey whoever wore it, three times for each owner."

"That seems like a harsh punishment for a prank," Dorothy said.

"It was," said the monkey king. "The cap passed from owner to owner. Some used us wisely. Some used us cruelly. The Wicked Witch was the worst. She used her first command to enslave the Winkies. She used her second to drive the Wizard of Oz out of the western lands. This — carrying you — would have been her third, but she used it on your friends instead."

"Wait," Dorothy said. "You said she drove the Wizard away? The great and powerful Oz?"

"Yes," the monkey king said. "The Wizard was afraid of us. He fled back to his Emerald City and never returned to the west."

Dorothy thought about this. The great and powerful Oz — afraid of monkeys? That did not sound like someone who was truly all-powerful.

She tucked this information away and said nothing to the others. But it added to the doubt that had been growing in her mind since the throne room, where the Wizard had appeared in four different forms.

The monkeys set them down gently before the gates of the Emerald City. The leader bowed once more.

"You have two commands remaining," he told Dorothy. "Call us when you need us."

Then the Winged Monkeys soared back into the sky and vanished.

Dorothy tucked the Golden Cap into her basket and straightened her dress. "Well," she said, "let us go tell the Wizard that we have done what he asked."

The Guardian of the Gates gave them their green spectacles, and they walked once more through the gleaming streets to the Palace of Oz. The green-bearded soldier led them to the great throne room.

"We have killed the Wicked Witch of the West," Dorothy announced to the empty room. "We have come to claim your promises."

There was no answer. The throne sat empty.

"Come back tomorrow," a voice boomed from nowhere. "The Wizard is busy."

"Busy?" the Scarecrow said. "We killed a witch for him, and he is busy?"

"Come back tomorrow," the voice repeated.

They had no choice. They returned to their rooms in the palace, disappointed but not defeated.

"He will see us tomorrow," Dorothy said. "And when he does, he will keep his word."

But as she lay in bed that night, stroking Toto's ears, Dorothy thought about what the monkey king had told her. A wizard who was afraid of monkeys. A wizard who looked different to everyone. A wizard who hid behind a locked door and a booming voice.

Something was not right. And tomorrow, Dorothy intended to find out what it was.

Chapter 16: The Discovery of Oz the Terrible

The next morning, Dorothy and her friends returned to the throne room. Once again, the great voice boomed: "Come back tomorrow!"

"No," Dorothy said.

The room went silent.

"We have done what you asked," Dorothy said, and her voice was steady even though her knees were not. "We killed the Wicked Witch of the West. You made us a promise. If you do not keep it, you are a very bad wizard."

"And if you do not keep your promises," the Scarecrow added, "then you are not a great wizard at all."

The Lion let out a roar — not because he was brave, but because he was angry and frightened, and roaring was the only thing he could think to do. The sound shook the walls of the throne room. Toto, startled by the noise, jumped sideways and knocked over a tall screen that stood in the corner.

Behind the screen was a little old man.

He was small and bald, with a wrinkled face and spectacles perched on his nose. He wore a plain green coat, and he was sitting behind a table covered with knobs, levers, and strange mechanical devices. Above the table hung a large round shape that looked very much like the giant head Dorothy had seen on her first visit — except now she could see the wires holding it up and the bellows that made its mouth move.

The little man stared at them with wide, frightened eyes.

"Who are you?" Dorothy demanded.

"I," the little man said in a very small voice, "am Oz, the Great and Terrible."

He did not look great. He did not look terrible. He looked like someone's grandfather caught doing something he should not.

"You?" the Lion growled. "You are the Wizard?"

"Please don't be angry," the man said, shrinking back. "Please, please don't hurt me. I'll explain everything."

"You're a humbug," the Scarecrow said. It was not a question.

"Yes," the man admitted miserably. "I am a humbug. I am not a wizard at all. I never was."

Dorothy felt the floor tilt beneath her. Everything she had believed — the great and powerful Oz who could grant wishes, who could send her home — was a lie.

"But the giant head," she said. "The fairy, the beast, the ball of fire —"

"Tricks," the man said. He pointed to his table of devices. "Ventriloquism for the voice. A paper-mâché head for the giant face. A costume for the beast. Chemicals for the fire. I am a very good trickster, you see. That is my real talent."

"How could you deceive everyone?" the Tin Woodman asked. His voice was not angry. It was hurt. "The people of this city worship you."

The old man sighed deeply and sank into his chair. "I suppose I should tell you my story," he said. "Please, sit down. It is not a long story, but it is an embarrassing one."

They sat. Even the Lion lay down, though he kept one eye on the little man.

"I was born in Omaha, Nebraska," the man began. "I was a circus performer — a balloonist. I traveled with the circus, going up in a hot-air balloon and doing tricks for the crowds. One day, the ropes of my balloon came untied while I was high in the air, and the wind carried me far, far away. I floated for days. When I finally came down, I landed here — in the land of Oz."

"Just like me," Dorothy said softly. "The wind brought me here, too."

"Yes," the man said. "When I landed, the people saw me floating down from the sky and believed I was a great wizard. I was too frightened to tell them the truth. So I let them believe it. I told them to build the Emerald City, and I had everyone wear green spectacles — which make everything look green, whether it is or not."

"So the city isn't really green?" Dorothy asked.

"It is a normal city," the man said. "Some of it is green. Most of it is not. But everyone wears the spectacles, so everyone believes."

"You have been lying to them for years," the Scarecrow said.

"I have," the man said. "And I am deeply ashamed. But I was afraid. If anyone found out I was not a real wizard, the two Wicked Witches would have destroyed me. So I hid behind my tricks and my locked doors and hoped no one would ever look behind the screen."

He looked at them with watery eyes. "I never thought anyone would actually kill a witch. When you did, I was terrified — because now you would come to me expecting miracles, and I have none to give."

Dorothy felt many things at once: anger that she had been lied to, sadness that her hope of getting home had been stolen, and — underneath it all — a strange sympathy for this frightened little man from Nebraska who had been pretending for so long.

"Can you give the Scarecrow brains?" she asked.

"No," the man said.

"Can you give the Tin Woodman a heart?"

"No."

"Can you give the Lion courage?"

"No."

"Can you send me home?"

The man paused. "I don't know," he said. "But I will try."

It was not much. But it was honest, and after so many lies, honesty felt like something.

Chapter 17: The Magic of Oz

The next morning, the little man who called himself Oz sent for the Scarecrow.

"I cannot give you real brains," the man said. "But I can give you something that might work just as well."

He opened the Scarecrow's head and removed the straw. Then he mixed together bran, pins, and needles, and stuffed the mixture inside. He sewed the head shut and stepped back.

"There," the man said. "Bran-new brains. The pins and needles mean you will be very sharp."

The Scarecrow reached up and touched his head. His painted eyes seemed to sparkle. "I feel wise already," he said. "I can feel thoughts — real thoughts — forming inside my head."

"They were always there," the man said quietly. "You just needed something to believe in."

The Scarecrow did not hear this. He was too busy thinking.

Next came the Tin Woodman. The man cut a small hole in the Tin Woodman's chest and placed inside it a pretty heart made of red silk, stuffed with sawdust.

"Is it a real heart?" the Tin Woodman asked, pressing his hand against his chest.

"It is a very kind heart," the man said. "And that is what matters."

A tear rolled down the Tin Woodman's cheek. "I can feel it beating," he said. "I am so happy."

"You were always kind," the man said. "The heart is just a reminder."

But the Tin Woodman was too moved to listen. He stood with his hand on his chest, feeling the silk heart shift inside him, and he smiled wider than Dorothy had ever seen him smile.

Last came the Cowardly Lion. The man poured a green liquid into a golden dish.

"Drink this," the man said.

"What is it?" the Lion asked nervously.

"Courage," the man said. "Pure, liquid courage. It has been in my cupboard for years. Drink it, and you will be full of courage."

The Lion lapped it up. He stood taller immediately. His mane seemed to bristle with new energy. His eyes grew bright and fierce.

"I feel brave!" the Lion roared. "I feel like I could fight a hundred enemies!"

"You always could," the man said. "You simply did not believe it."

Dorothy watched all of this with mixed feelings. She was happy for her friends — truly happy. The Scarecrow was walking around making observations about everything, delighted with his "new" brains. The Tin Woodman was so full of feeling that he cried every few minutes and had to be oiled. The Lion strutted through the palace like a king.

But Dorothy knew the truth. The brains were bran and pins. The heart was silk and sawdust. The courage was colored water. The man from Omaha had given them symbols, not magic.

And yet — her friends were different. Not because the gifts had changed them, but because they finally believed in themselves. The Scarecrow had always been clever. The Tin Woodman had always been kind. The Lion had always been brave. They just needed someone to tell them so.

Was that magic? Dorothy was not sure. But it worked.

"Now," Dorothy said, turning to the man. "What about me? How will you send me home?"

The man rubbed his chin. "I have been thinking about that," he said. "I came here in a balloon, and I believe I can leave in a balloon. If I build one large enough, we can fly over the desert that surrounds Oz and land in Kansas — or somewhere near it."

"You would come with me?" Dorothy asked, surprised.

"I would very much like to go home," the man said. His voice was soft and sad. "I have been pretending to be a wizard for so long that I have nearly forgotten who I really am. I am just a man from Omaha, and I miss it."

Dorothy understood that feeling perfectly.

"Then build your balloon," she said. "And let us go home."

Chapter 18: The Balloon

The man from Omaha set to work building a hot-air balloon. He had done it many times in the circus, and his hands remembered the craft even after all these years.

He cut strips of green silk — there was plenty of green silk in the Emerald City — and sewed them together into a great bag, wider at the top and narrow at the bottom. He coated the inside with thin glue to keep the hot air from leaking out. Then he attached the bag to a large wicker basket, big enough for two people and one small dog.

While he worked, word spread through the city that the great Wizard of Oz was leaving. The people were upset, but the man calmed them.

"I am going away on a journey," he announced from the palace balcony. "While I am gone, the Scarecrow will rule over you. He has brains now, and he will govern you wisely."

The people cheered. They loved the Scarecrow, who had helped kill the Wicked Witch.

The Scarecrow was nervous. "I don't know anything about ruling a city," he whispered to Dorothy.

"You'll figure it out," Dorothy said. "You always do."

On the morning of the launch, the balloon was inflated in the great square before the palace. A fire underneath heated the air inside, and the green silk bag swelled and rose, tugging at the ropes that held it down. The whole city turned out to watch.

Dorothy hugged her friends one by one.

"Goodbye, Scarecrow," she said. "You were always the smartest person I knew — even before the brains."

"Goodbye, Dorothy," the Scarecrow said. "I will miss you terribly. But I will try to think of cheerful things, since I now have brains to think with."

"Goodbye, Tin Woodman," Dorothy said. "You have the biggest heart of anyone, and you always did."

The Tin Woodman was crying so hard he could not speak. Dorothy oiled his jaw so it would not rust, and he managed to whisper, "You are the best friend I ever had."

"Goodbye, Lion," Dorothy said. "You are the bravest creature in all of Oz."

"Only because you believed in me," the Lion said. His great golden eyes were wet. "I was nothing before you came along."

"That is not true," Dorothy said firmly. "You were always brave. You just didn't know it."

The balloon strained against its ropes. The man from Omaha climbed into the basket and called to Dorothy. "Hurry! The balloon is ready! We must go now, before it lifts off without us!"

Dorothy turned to get Toto — but the little dog had spotted a cat in the crowd and had run off barking. Dorothy chased after him.

"Come back, Toto! Come back!"

There was a snap. One of the ropes broke. Then another. The basket lurched upward.

"Dorothy!" the man cried. "Hurry!"

But Dorothy was still chasing Toto through the crowd. She caught him, scooped him up, and turned back — just in time to see the last rope break.

The balloon rose into the air.

"Come back!" Dorothy shouted. "Wait for me!"

"I can't!" the man called down. His voice was already faint. The balloon was rising fast, caught by the wind. "I don't know how to bring it down! Goodbye, Dorothy! Goodbye!"

The balloon sailed up and up, growing smaller against the blue sky, until it was nothing but a green speck, and then nothing at all.

Dorothy stood in the square, holding Toto, and watched it disappear.

The man from Omaha was gone. And Dorothy was still in Oz.

She did not cry. Not right away. She stood very still, feeling the weight of it — the closed door, the vanished hope, the enormous distance between her and Kansas.

Then the Scarecrow put his stuffed hand on her shoulder. The Tin Woodman stood on her other side. The Lion pressed his great warm body against her back.

"We will find another way," the Scarecrow said.

"We will not give up," the Tin Woodman said.

"We are with you," the Lion said.

Dorothy looked at her three friends — the ones who were supposed to need her help, who had turned out to be the ones helping her — and she allowed herself one small, tired smile.

"All right," she said. "Another way."

Chapter 19: The Journey South

The next morning, the four friends set out from the Emerald City heading south. The Guardian of the Gates unlocked their green spectacles, wished them well, and watched them walk away down the road.

"I hope you find what you are looking for," he called after them.

"So do I," Dorothy said quietly.

The countryside was lovely at first — green fields, gentle hills, and sparkling streams. But as they went further south, the road disappeared entirely. There were no more yellow bricks, no more fences, no more signs. Just wild, untamed land stretching in every direction.

"We must keep going south," the Scarecrow said, studying the position of the sun. "Glinda's country is on the southern border of Oz. If we walk straight, we should reach it."

They walked through tall grass and across shallow streams. The Lion led the way, his great nose sniffing the air for danger. The Tin Woodman kept his axe ready. The Scarecrow watched the sun and kept them heading in the right direction.

Then they reached the forest.

It was not like the dark forest near the Munchkin country. This forest was alive — aggressively alive. The trees were thick and gnarled, with branches that hung low across the path like arms.

"These trees look unfriendly," the Tin Woodman said.

He was right. When the Scarecrow tried to walk between two of them, the nearest tree swung a great branch like a fist and knocked him flying. He tumbled through the air and landed in a heap ten feet away.

"Ow," the Scarecrow said. Then: "Well, not ow exactly, since I cannot feel pain. But that was very rude."

The trees thrashed their branches, blocking the path completely. Every time someone tried to pass, the trees struck at them. The Lion growled and swiped at a branch, but another tree hit him from behind and sent him stumbling.

"Let me try," the Tin Woodman said.

He marched toward the nearest fighting tree, axe raised. A branch whipped toward his head. He ducked and chopped it clean off. Another branch swung at his legs. He chopped that one, too. A third branch came from the side — chop. A fourth from above — chop.

The trees recoiled, pulling their remaining branches tight against their trunks. They had learned very quickly that hitting a man made of tin with an axe was not a winning strategy.

"Run!" the Tin Woodman shouted, holding the trees back.

The others dashed through the gap. The Tin Woodman followed, chopping one more branch for good measure. Behind them, the fighting trees shook with fury, but they could not follow.

"That was magnificent," the Lion said.

"Thank you," the Tin Woodman said. "I may not have real muscles, but tin is very hard, and this axe is very sharp."

"Brains, heart, and courage," the Scarecrow said, brushing dirt from his clothes. "We have everything we need between us."

"We always did," Dorothy said.

They pressed on through the forest, and the trees did not bother them again.

Chapter 20: The Dainty China Country

Beyond the fighting trees, the four travelers found something none of them expected.

The ground changed suddenly from dirt and grass to something smooth, hard, and white — like the bottom of a teacup. Dorothy knelt down and touched it. It was china.

"How strange," the Scarecrow said.

They walked forward carefully. Tiny houses appeared along the road, no taller than Dorothy's waist. They were made of painted china — delicate, beautiful, with tiny chimneys and tiny doors and tiny windows with tiny curtains. The roofs were glazed in bright colors: red, blue, yellow, and green.

And the people were china, too.

They were small — no higher than Dorothy's knee — with painted faces, painted clothes, and bodies so thin and fragile that Dorothy could see the light shining through them. China milkmaids carried china pails. China shepherds tended china sheep. A china princess in a china gown walked through a china garden, and when she saw the travelers, she froze in alarm.

"Please," the princess said in a voice like a tiny bell, "please be very careful. If you step on us, we will break. And once we are broken, we are mended with glue, but we are never quite the same."

"We will be very careful," Dorothy promised. She picked up Toto and held him tightly so he would not chase the china animals.

They walked through the china country one step at a time, placing each foot with enormous care. The Tin Woodman was the most cautious of all. He lifted each foot high and set it down gently, terrified of crushing some tiny creature.

"I could not bear to break something so beautiful," he said. His hand was pressed against his chest, over his silk heart.

The Lion had the hardest time. He was enormous — his great paws could have crushed a china house in a single step. He held his breath and tiptoed, which looked absurd for a creature his size, but no one laughed. The stakes were too high.

"Tell me about your country," the Scarecrow said to the china princess as they walked.

"We live quiet lives here," the princess said. "We do not travel. We do not fight. We spend our days in our gardens and our houses, being careful. That is the most important thing in the china country: being careful. Because we are fragile, and the world is not."

"That sounds lonely," Dorothy said.

"Perhaps," the princess said. "But it is also peaceful. No one here is in a hurry. No one here is angry. We have learned that the most important things in life — kindness, beauty, gentleness — are also the most breakable. So we protect them."

Dorothy thought about this long after they left the china country. She thought about Aunt Em, who had once been young and cheerful but had been worn down by the harsh Kansas wind. She thought about the Tin Woodman, whose heart had been taken away piece by piece. She thought about how easy it is to break things — and how hard it is to put them back together.

The Lion's tail, swishing as he walked, accidentally knocked over a tiny china church. The Tin Woodman stopped, knelt down, and carefully set it upright again. The china minister inside shook his tiny fist, but no one was hurt.

"Sorry," the Lion murmured, tucking his tail between his legs.

When they finally reached the far edge of the china country, they stepped off the smooth white ground and back onto dirt with enormous relief. The Scarecrow looked back at the delicate world behind them.

"I think that was the most important place we have visited," he said.

"Why?" the Lion asked.

"Because it reminded me that strength is not the only thing that matters. Gentleness matters, too. And sometimes the most powerful thing you can do is simply be careful with the world around you."

Dorothy squeezed the Scarecrow's stuffed hand. "For someone who says he has no brains," she said, "you are very wise."

"I know," the Scarecrow said, surprised at himself. "I really am."

Chapter 21: The Country of the Quadlings

South of the china country, the land changed again. The wild forest gave way to gentle, rolling hills covered in soft red grass and dotted with red flowers. The houses they passed were painted red, and the fences were red, and the people they met wore red clothes and had kind, cheerful faces.

"This must be the country of the Quadlings," the Scarecrow said. "Just as the Munchkins love blue, the Quadlings love red."

The people they met were friendly and welcoming. When Dorothy asked the way to Glinda's castle, they smiled and pointed south. "Follow the road," they said. "You cannot miss it. Glinda's castle is the most beautiful building in all of Oz."

They were right.

The castle rose from a hill like something out of a dream. Its walls were deep red, studded with rubies that caught the afternoon sun and threw light in every direction. Three young women in red uniforms stood guard at the gates, and behind them, Dorothy could see gardens filled with red roses and paths of smooth red stone.

"State your business," one of the guards said.

"My name is Dorothy, and I have come to ask Glinda for help getting home to Kansas."

The guard studied her for a moment, then nodded. "Glinda will see you. She has been expecting you."

"Expecting us?" Dorothy said, surprised. But the guards were already leading them through the gates and into the castle.

The throne room was nothing like the Wizard's. There was no booming voice, no locked doors, no tricks. The room was open and airy, filled with light from tall windows, and on a throne of rubies sat a woman of extraordinary beauty.

Glinda had red hair that fell in curls to her shoulders, and eyes that were a deep, warm blue. She wore a white gown — pure white, in a country of red — and her smile was so genuine that Dorothy felt, for the first time in a long while, completely safe.

"Welcome, Dorothy," Glinda said. Her voice was gentle but strong, like a bell. "I know who you are, and I know what you have done. You killed the Wicked Witch of the East with your house and the Wicked Witch of the West with your bucket. The land of Oz owes you a great debt."

"I didn't mean to kill anyone," Dorothy said. "Both times were accidents."

"Sometimes the most important things happen by accident," Glinda said, smiling. "Now, tell me what you wish."

"I want to go home," Dorothy said. "I want to go back to Kansas, to Uncle Henry and Aunt Em. I love Oz, and I love my friends, but Kansas is my home, and I miss it."

"Even though Kansas is gray?" Glinda asked. There was no judgment in the question — only curiosity.

"Even though Kansas is gray," Dorothy said. "Home is not about what a place looks like. It is about the people who love you."

Glinda nodded slowly. "You are wiser than you know, Dorothy. But before I help you, let me help your friends. Each of them has somewhere to go, too."

She turned to the Scarecrow. "The people of the Emerald City love you and want you to rule them. Will you return and be their leader?"

The Scarecrow straightened up. "I will," he said. "I have brains now, and I will use them to govern wisely."

She turned to the Tin Woodman. "The Winkies of the western land have asked me to find them a ruler. They loved how you protected Dorothy, and they want you to be their emperor. Will you go?"

The Tin Woodman pressed his hand to his chest. "Emperor?" he said. "Of the Winkies?" His tin eyes shone. "I would be honored. I will rule them with all the kindness in my heart."

She turned to the Lion. "The animals of the great forest south of here have no king. They need someone brave and strong to protect them. Will you return and rule them?"

The Lion lifted his great head. "I will," he said. "I will be the bravest king the forest has ever known."

Dorothy looked at her three friends — each of them standing tall, each of them ready for the life ahead of them — and she felt a deep, bittersweet pride. They had come so far. The Scarecrow who could not stand without falling. The Tin Woodman who cried every time he felt anything. The Lion who trembled at his own shadow. They were not those creatures anymore.

"But how will they get to their new homes?" Dorothy asked.

"The Golden Cap," Glinda said. "You still have it, yes?"

Dorothy took the cap from her basket. "I have two commands left."

"Use one to send the Scarecrow to the Emerald City, the Tin Woodman to the Winkies, and the Lion to the great forest. The Winged Monkeys can carry them all at once."

"And the last command?"

Glinda smiled. "Give the cap to me. I will use the final command to set the Winged Monkeys free, so they will never be slaves again."

Dorothy's heart swelled. "Yes," she said. "They deserve to be free."

"Now," said Glinda, leaning forward, "let me tell you about the silver shoes."

Chapter 22: Glinda Grants Dorothy's Wish

Glinda sat on her throne of rubies and looked at Dorothy with warm, knowing eyes.

"Before I tell you how to get home," Glinda said, "let me help your friends find their places in the world."

She turned to the Scarecrow. "The people of the Emerald City have asked me to find them a good ruler. They love you, Scarecrow. You helped defeat the witches, and you governed wisely while the Wizard was... away. Will you return and lead them?"

The Scarecrow straightened up. His painted face seemed to glow. "I will," he said. "I have brains now, and I will use them to make the Emerald City the wisest place in all of Oz."

Glinda turned to the Tin Woodman. "The Winkies of the western land need a ruler, too. They remember how you fought for Dorothy, and how gently you treated them after the Witch was destroyed. Will you be their emperor?"

The Tin Woodman placed both hands over his silk heart. "Emperor?" he said softly. "Of the Winkies?" He stood taller than Dorothy had ever seen him stand. "I would be honored. I will rule them with all the kindness I have."

Glinda turned to the Lion. "And the animals of the great forest near here have no king. They need someone brave and strong to protect them from danger. Will you return and rule them?"

The Lion's mane seemed to bristle with new energy. "I will," he said. "I will be a good king. I will protect every creature in the forest, large and small."

Dorothy looked at her three friends — each of them standing tall, each of them exactly where they were meant to be. The Scarecrow who had been stuck on a pole. The Tin Woodman who had been rusted in a forest. The Lion who had been too afraid to leave his den. They had come so far.

"But how will they reach their new homes?" Dorothy asked.

"The Golden Cap," Glinda said. "You have two commands left, yes?"

Dorothy took the cap from her basket. "Yes."

"Use the first to send all three of your friends to their new homes. The Winged Monkeys can carry the Scarecrow to the Emerald City, the Tin Woodman to the Winkies' land, and the Lion to the great forest — all at once."

"And the second command?"

"Give the cap to me," Glinda said. "I will use the final command to set the Winged Monkeys free forever, so that no one can ever force them to be slaves again."

"Yes," Dorothy said at once. "They have been slaves long enough."

Dorothy put on the Golden Cap and spoke the magic words. The sky darkened, the air hummed, and the Winged Monkeys appeared, circling above the ruby castle.

"What do you command?" their leader asked.

"Please carry my friends to their new homes," Dorothy said. "The Scarecrow to the Emerald City, the Tin Woodman to the land of the Winkies, and the Lion to the great forest."

"It shall be done," the monkey king said.

This was the moment Dorothy had been dreading. The goodbyes.

She went to the Scarecrow first. He was trying not to look sad, but his painted eyes gave him away.

"You are the best friend I ever had," the Scarecrow said. "Before you came along, I was stuck on a pole, and the crows laughed at me. You gave me a life."

"You gave yourself a life," Dorothy said. "I just helped you off the pole." She hugged him tightly. He smelled like clean straw and sunshine.

The Tin Woodman was next. He was crying so hard that Dorothy reached for the oil can without even thinking. She oiled his jaw, his cheeks, the hinges around his eyes.

"Let them rust," the Tin Woodman whispered. "Some things are worth rusting for."

Dorothy oiled him anyway. Then she hugged him, and his tin arms held her carefully, and his silk heart pressed against her cheek.

The Lion knelt so Dorothy could wrap her arms around his great maned neck. His golden fur was warm, and his chest rumbled with a sound that was half purr, half sob.

"You made me brave," the Lion said.

"No," Dorothy said. "You were always brave. I just noticed."

The Winged Monkeys lifted her three friends into the air. Dorothy watched them grow smaller against the sky — a man of straw, a man of tin, a great golden lion — until they were specks, and then nothing.

She stood in the courtyard of the ruby castle, holding Toto, and allowed herself to cry.

Then she wiped her eyes, took a breath, and turned back to Glinda.

"Now," Dorothy said. "Tell me about the silver shoes."

Chapter 23: There's No Place Like Home

"The silver shoes," Glinda said, "have a very powerful magic. They can carry you anywhere in the world in three steps."

Dorothy looked down at the shoes on her feet. She had been wearing them since the very beginning — since the moment she took them from the Wicked Witch of the East on her first day in Oz. Through forests and poppy fields, through the Emerald City and the Witch's castle, through every danger and every mile of yellow brick road — the answer had been on her feet the entire time.

"You mean," Dorothy said slowly, "I could have gone home from the very start?"

"Yes," Glinda said.

The room seemed to tilt. Dorothy felt dizzy with the weight of it. She could have gone home on the first day. She could have avoided the dark forest, the deadly poppies, the Wicked Witch, the humbug Wizard. She could have clicked her heels and been in Kansas before she ever set foot on the yellow brick road.

But then she never would have met the Scarecrow. Or the Tin Woodman. Or the Lion.

She never would have learned that the Scarecrow was the cleverest person she knew. She never would have watched the Tin Woodman weep over a crushed beetle. She never would have seen the Cowardly Lion stand between his friends and danger, shaking with fear but refusing to back down.

She never would have learned that a wizard could be a fraud and still teach a lesson, or that a bucket of water could defeat what an army could not, or that the smallest mouse and the fiercest lion could both be heroes.

She never would have learned what home really meant.

"I would not trade this journey for anything," Dorothy said.

Glinda smiled. "I know. That is why I did not tell you sooner. Some things cannot be taught. They must be lived."

"How do I use the shoes?" Dorothy asked.

"Click the heels together three times," Glinda said. "And with each click, say where you wish to go."

Dorothy held Toto close against her chest. She could feel his small heart beating fast. She looked at Glinda — the most powerful and the most honest person she had met in all of Oz — and said, "Thank you."

"Thank yourself," Glinda said. "You did this, Dorothy. All of it."

Dorothy closed her eyes. She thought of Aunt Em's face. She thought of Uncle Henry's quiet hands. She thought of the flat, gray prairie and the small wooden house and the smell of bread baking in the oven.

She clicked her heels together.

"Take me home."

Click.

"Take me home."

Click.

"Take me home."

Click.

The world dissolved. The ruby castle, the red flowers, the warm light — all of it swirled and blurred and spun away. Wind roared past her ears. She felt herself flying, tumbling, spinning through an impossible distance. Toto pressed against her chest, and Dorothy held on.

Then — silence.

She was sitting in the grass. Real grass. Brown, dry, Kansas grass, warm from the sun.

She opened her eyes.

In front of her was a farmhouse — a new one, because the old house had flown to Oz. Uncle Henry had built it while she was gone. It was small and plain, just like the old one. No emeralds. No rubies. No green marble or yellow brick.

It was the most beautiful thing Dorothy had ever seen.

And running across the yard toward her, arms outstretched, was Aunt Em.

Chapter 24: Home Again

"My darling child!" Aunt Em cried, sweeping Dorothy into her arms and holding her so tightly that Toto yelped between them. "Where in the world did you come from?"

"From the land of Oz," Dorothy said into Aunt Em's shoulder. "Oh, Aunt Em, I am so glad to be home!"

Aunt Em pulled back and looked at Dorothy's face as though she could not quite believe she was real. Her gray eyes were shining — with tears, with relief, with a fierce, burning love that Dorothy had always known was there, even when Aunt Em had been too worn down by the prairie to show it.

Uncle Henry came out of the barn. He stopped in the doorway and stared. Then he walked across the yard, slowly, as though Dorothy might disappear if he moved too quickly. He knelt down in the dirt and put his weathered hands on her shoulders.

"Dorothy," he said. Just her name. But he said it in a way that held everything.

Then he did something Dorothy had never seen him do before.

He smiled.

Dorothy looked around. The Kansas prairie stretched flat and brown to the horizon. The wind blew dust across the yard. The sun baked the earth the same dull gray it had always been. There were no emerald cities, no yellow brick roads, no fields of scarlet poppies or countries made of china.

But Aunt Em was there, warm and real. Uncle Henry was there, steady and solid. And Toto was barking and jumping in circles, thrilled to be on solid ground again.

Dorothy looked down at her feet. The silver shoes were gone — lost somewhere in the sky between Oz and Kansas, tumbled away during the flight. She felt for them for a moment, a pang of loss. Those shoes had carried her so far.

But she did not need them anymore. They had done their job.

"Come inside," Aunt Em said, wiping her eyes. "I have just taken bread out of the oven. You must be starving."

The kitchen of the new house smelled like warm bread and butter. It was small and plain — one room, a stove, a table, chairs. No green marble. No ruby thrones. But as Dorothy sat down at the table and Aunt Em set a thick slice of bread in front of her and Uncle Henry poured a glass of milk, Dorothy felt something settle inside her chest like a key turning in a lock.

This was it. This was the thing she had traveled hundreds of miles to find, the thing the Wizard could not give her, the thing even Glinda could not create. It was not a place, exactly, though Kansas was part of it. It was not a feeling, exactly, though love was part of it. It was something bigger and simpler than either of those things.

It was home.

Not a perfect home. Not a dazzling home. Not a home made of emeralds or rubies or magic. Just a small house on a flat prairie with two people who loved her and a little black dog who made her laugh.

That was enough. That had always been enough.

Dorothy ate her bread. Toto lay at her feet. Aunt Em sat across the table, watching Dorothy with eyes that would not look away, as if she were memorizing her.

"Aunt Em," Dorothy said.

"Yes, dear?"

"There is no place like home."

Aunt Em reached across the table and took Dorothy's hand. She did not ask what the words meant. She did not need to.

Outside, the Kansas wind blew across the prairie, and the sun began to set, painting the gray sky with gold. It was not emerald and it was not ruby. But it was warm, and it was real, and it was hers.

Dorothy was home.
