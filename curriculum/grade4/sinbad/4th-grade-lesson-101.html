The Seventh Voyage: The King's Letter

After the sixth voyage I was truly done, said Sinbad. I was home. I was rich. I was comfortable. I had been shipwrecked more times than I could count on one hand. I was finished with the sea. Then the Caliph of Baghdad summoned me. The Caliph had heard of the kingdom of Serendib — its beauty, its wealth, the kindness of its king. He wanted to send a letter and gifts to the king of Serendib. He needed someone who knew the way. I knew the way. I tried to explain that I was retired. The Caliph looked at me. When the Caliph of Baghdad looks at you in a particular way, you pack your bags. I packed my bags. The voyage to Serendib was actually fine. No storms. No shipwrecks. No strange islands. I delivered the letter and the gifts to the king, he received them with great joy, and I prepared to sail home. Easy. Done. On the way home, pirates found us. Our ship was large and well-armed, but the pirates had three ships and they attacked from three sides at once. Some of my companions were killed. The rest of us were taken prisoner. The pirates brought us to an island and sold us at a market. I was purchased by an ivory merchant named Khasib, who was a kind enough master as masters go — he fed me well and did not beat me, which I will acknowledge as the minimum possible decency but also better than the alternative. The merchant had a problem. The elephants in this region had been killing his hunters. He needed someone to shoot the elephants from a tree — high enough to be safe, skilled enough to aim. He had run out of local men willing to do it. I was desperate enough to try. I climbed trees and I aimed carefully and I was a reasonable shot, which I had not known about myself until I absolutely needed to know it. The elephants noticed me. This is the part of the story that I find most remarkable. One morning an enormous old elephant came to the base of my tree and waited. When I climbed down, it did not attack me. It walked away and looked back. I followed it. I followed it for an hour through the jungle, which was either very wise or very foolish, and I genuinely did not know which. It led me to a place in the hills where the bones of hundreds of elephants lay piled — the elephants' graveyard, the place they went to die. There was more ivory in that valley than a hundred merchants could carry. The elephant looked at me. I believe it was saying: take the ivory. Stop killing us. There is enough here. I went back to Khasib and told him what I had found. He brought everyone to the graveyard. We loaded ship after ship with ivory. It took weeks. Khasib was so grateful that he freed me, paid me a large share of the ivory's value, and sailed me home personally. I arrived in Baghdad the second time with enough wealth to live on for the rest of my life, with money left over to give away. Sinbad looked at Hindbad across the long table. "That was the seventh voyage. That was the last voyage." "The elephants," said Hindbad. "They showed you the way." "They trusted me with it," said Sinbad. "After everything I had done." He shook his head. "I think about them often." He refilled Hindbad's cup for the final time. "Now you have heard the whole story. Does my fortune seem easily come by?" Hindbad looked at the fine house around him, the carpets, the food, the lamps burning late into the night, and then he looked at Sinbad — the bright eyes, the white hair, the hands that had gripped a piece of meat while an eagle lifted him out of a pit and clung to the leg of the largest bird in the world. "No," he said. "It does not." Sinbad smiled. "Then we understand each other." He gave Hindbad a purse of gold when he left. More than a porter earned in a month. More than he expected. Hindbad walked home slowly, thinking about the sea, thinking about questions the world asks you that you never hear if you never go to look for them. He went home to his family and his work and his ordinary life. But for the rest of his life, whenever his load felt heavy and the sun felt too hot and the world felt unfair, he thought about a man clinging to an eagle's foot while a valley of diamonds spun far below him, and he thought about what it cost to deserve the fine house at the end of the story. And he picked up his load. And he walked on. ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

Pirates and Slavery: The Dark Side of Sea Trade

Piracy was a constant threat on trade routes. Pirates would attack merchant ships, steal the cargo, and either ransom the crew back to their families or sell them as slaves.

The Indian Ocean had several notorious pirate havens where captured goods could be sold and sailors could be enslaved. Some coastal rulers even supported pirates because they got a share of the stolen goods.

Slavery was unfortunately common in this period. People captured in raids, wars, or pirate attacks could be sold in slave markets in various port cities. Some slaves were treated relatively well (as servants or craftsmen), while others were forced into harsh labor.

Wealthy families sometimes paid ransoms to free captured relatives. This is why Sinbad mentions being valuable enough to ransom — his reputation as a successful merchant meant his friends or family might pay for his release.

Some enslaved people eventually earned or bought their freedom and rebuilt their lives. Others spent years or their entire lives in slavery. It was one of the cruelest aspects of medieval trade.

💡 The Lesson

Sinbad's stories gloss over some harsh realities of his time. Piracy and slavery were real dangers that affected millions of people. Understanding this history helps us appreciate how much safer modern trade and travel are.

✏️ Grammar

Week 21 Focus: Quotation Marks in Dialogue

📚 What You're Learning:

When someone speaks, put their exact words inside quotation marks. The comma or period at the end goes inside the quotation marks. Start a new paragraph when a different person starts speaking.

✨ Examples:

'I am Sinbad,' the old man said. 'Please sit down.'

Hindbad looked at his hands. 'I didn't know anyone could hear me.'

The servant said, 'My master heard you. He would like you to come inside.'
