📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

Back to the Beginning

Let me start again, said Sinbad, more slowly this time. You have heard the shape of the seven voyages. But the shape of a thing and the feel of it are different. Tonight I will tell you what it was actually like to be twenty-three years old and broke and boarding a ship for the first time. I was terrified. I would not have admitted this then. Young men rarely admit to being terrified because being terrified feels like weakness and weakness feels like something to be ashamed of. I understand now that being terrified is simply information — it means you are about to do something that matters. I stood at the dock in Basra with my bales of fabric and my small sacks of spice and I looked at the ship. It was old. The wood was dark with water and salt. The crew were hard-faced men who did not look at passengers with much warmth. The captain was a man named Ibrahim, and he ran his ship well and treated us fairly, and I will always be grateful to him because the first captain you sail with sets your idea of what a captain is. If he had been cruel or incompetent, I might have been afraid of every ship for the rest of my life. The sea on that first day was calm and blue and vast and I thought: I understand why men love this. The ship moved through the water with a sound like breathing. The sails were full. The horizon was endless. After three weeks we stopped at islands I did not know the names of. Strange trees, strange birds, strange fruits that tasted nothing like anything in Baghdad. I bought things cheaply at one island and sold them for more at the next. I was learning how trade worked on the sea — that distance is money, that what is common in one place is rare in another, that patience and attention are worth more than luck. I was good at this. I was surprised to discover I was good at it. Then we stopped at what looked like an island and I fell asleep on the grass and the island moved. You already know this part. But what I have not told you is what it felt like to be in the water afterward. Cold. The ocean is much colder than it looks from a ship's deck. The whale had gone down and the water it displaced had churned everything into foam and I was in the middle of it with no direction and no idea which way was the ship. I found the washtub not because I was clever but because it bumped into my arm. I held on. I shut my eyes and held on and did not think about what was below me in all that dark water, which is a very important skill that I recommend developing if you plan to go to sea. Two days. Two days on that washtub. The sun was very hot. I had nothing to drink. I talked to myself to stay awake during the second night, because I was afraid that if I fell asleep I would let go. And then the island appeared. And the horses. And King Mihrjan, who asked no suspicious questions and gave me a job and treated me as a person rather than a problem. The kindness of strangers, said Sinbad, is a real thing. I have encountered cruelty and I have encountered kindness, and I cannot tell you which I have encountered more. But the kindness of strangers is what makes the world crossable. Never forget to be kind to the stranger who washes up on your shore. He looked at Hindbad. "That is why I invited you in, when I heard you on my steps. You reminded me of someone I used to be." Hindbad was quiet for a moment. "I didn't expect that," he said. "Most things worth having are unexpected," said Sinbad. "Come back tomorrow." ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 21 Focus: Synonyms and Word Choice

📚 What You're Learning:

Synonyms are words that mean almost the same thing, but good writers choose specific words that fit the situation. 'Big' and 'enormous' are synonyms, but 'enormous' creates a stronger picture.

✨ Examples:

Instead of 'walked,' Sinbad 'dragged himself' onto the beach (shows exhaustion)

Instead of 'bad,' he says 'the worst day' (emphasizes how terrible it was)

Instead of 'scared,' the sailors 'ran screaming' (shows panic and terror)

🎯 Your Turn:

1. Replace 'big' with a better word:

"The bird was big — its wings blocked out the sun."

2. Replace 'said' with a more specific word that shows how Sinbad felt:

"'The sea has its hooks in me,' Sinbad said."

3. Rewrite this sentence using more specific, powerful words:

"The whale moved and went down into the water."
