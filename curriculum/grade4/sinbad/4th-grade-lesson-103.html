📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Ship That Did Not Wait

Here is something nobody tells you about going to sea, said Sinbad. Ships do not wait. This seems obvious. But it is the kind of obvious thing that you do not fully understand until a ship fails to wait for you and you are standing on an island watching the sails disappear. On the second voyage, the island we stopped at was genuinely beautiful. There was a stream with cold water and flat rocks to sit on and shade from old trees. Several of us went ashore to rest. The captain announced we would leave after the midday meal. I found my shady spot and my book and I was reading and then I was asleep, which I have mentioned before. But what I have not told you is that I woke up not all at once but slowly, gradually, the way you wake when no one is shaking you, when the sun has just moved and the light has changed. For a moment, half-asleep, I thought: what a pleasant afternoon. Then I thought: it is afternoon. Then I sat up and looked at the shore. The ship was a small shape on the horizon. Still visible. Getting smaller. I ran to the beach and shouted. I waved my arms. I ran along the shore as if I could somehow chase it. The ship got smaller. I sat down on the beach. Here is what I want you to understand about that moment: the worst part was not fear. Fear came later. The worst part was embarrassment. I had done this. I had fallen asleep knowing we were leaving after the midday meal. I was not a careless person — I thought of myself as careful and smart and good at paying attention. And I had simply fallen asleep and let the ship leave without me. The embarrassment was followed by fear, and then by the practical question: what do I actually do now? I could sit here and feel sorry for myself. That was one option. I could explore the island and understand my situation. That was another. I explored. That is how I found the dome. That is how I found the Roc. The thing about terrible situations is that they force you to pay attention in a way you do not when things are going well. When I was safe on the ship, reading my book in the afternoon, I was not thinking very hard about anything. When I was stranded on the island alone, I noticed every single thing — the height of the trees, the direction of the wind, the shape of that white dome, the shadow that fell from the sky. Necessity sharpens the mind considerably. I am not saying it is good to be shipwrecked. It is not good to be shipwrecked. But I will say that every time I found myself in a desperate situation, I discovered something about myself that I did not know before. On the whale island, I discovered I could endure two days alone in the open ocean without giving up. In the diamond valley, I discovered I could think creatively when I was frightened. On the giant's island, I discovered I could organize frightened people into doing something brave. None of these things would I have learned in a comfortable house in Baghdad. Sinbad paused. The candles had burned low. Somewhere in the house a door closed softly. "I am not saying," he said carefully, "that you should go looking for disasters. I am saying that if you find yourself in one, it will show you things about yourself that nothing else will. And those things are worth knowing." Hindbad looked at his hands in the dim light. "What did you discover," he asked, "that you wish you hadn't?" Sinbad was quiet for a long moment. "That I can leave people behind," he said, "when leaving them is the only way to survive. I have never decided if that is strength or cowardice. Perhaps it is neither. Perhaps it is simply what survival requires." He blew out the nearest candle. "Come back tomorrow." ~ ~ ~

✏️ Grammar

Week 21 Focus: Quotation Marks in Dialogue

📚 What You're Learning:

When someone speaks, put their exact words inside quotation marks. The comma or period at the end goes inside the quotation marks. Start a new paragraph when a different person starts speaking.

✨ Examples:

'I am Sinbad,' the old man said. 'Please sit down.'

Hindbad looked at his hands. 'I didn't know anyone could hear me.'

The servant said, 'My master heard you. He would like you to come inside.'
