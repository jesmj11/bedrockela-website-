📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

What the Giant Taught Me

I have thought about the giant many times, said Sinbad. Not the terror of him — though that was real — but the strangeness of what he was. He was not evil, exactly. He was not cruel in the way that a person who knows what cruelty is can be cruel. He came to the beach and he looked at us and he chose the largest man and he ate him. He did this the same way a lion does it. With complete calm. Without malice. It was simply what he did. This did not make it better. I want to be clear about that. Eight of my companions died because of him. I do not look back on those days with anything except grief and anger. But here is the thought that has stayed with me: he was not doing something wrong, from his own perspective. We were on his island. He was hungry. We were available. We were the strangers who arrived in a place where we were not welcome and where we were not prepared for what lived there. Now. I am not saying we deserved what happened to us. No one deserves to be eaten by a giant. What I am saying is that every sea voyage, every journey into a place you do not know, requires you to hold in your head the possibility that you are the stranger, and that the rules of the strange place may be entirely different from the rules of your home, and that things that seem safe or obvious or normal at home may not be safe or obvious or normal there. The fourth voyage, in the city with the strange food — I survived because something in me refused to eat without thinking first. My companions ate because the food smelled good and they were hungry and eating is what you do when you are hungry. I asked myself: why is this food here? Who prepared it? Why are these people being so generous to strangers? Questions. Just questions. Questions kept me alive. Ask questions, said Sinbad. Always ask questions. When something seems too convenient — a meal that appears, a path that opens, a helpful stranger — ask why. Not rudely. Not suspiciously. But ask. Hindbad was quiet. "Did you ask questions," he said, "before you picked up the Old Man of the Sea?" Sinbad laughed, a short rueful sound. "No. I did not. He looked pitiful and I felt sorry for him and I crouched down before I thought. Which is why I ended up with him on my back for a month." He shook his head. "I was not consistent. No one is consistent. You learn a lesson and then you forget it in the moment when it counts, and then you learn it again more painfully." "Do you ever stop making mistakes?" "I am ninety years old and very rich," said Sinbad, "and I still make mistakes. The difference is that most of them now happen in a comfortable house rather than on a life raft in the middle of the ocean." He smiled. "That is probably the best outcome a person can hope for." ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 21 Focus: Synonyms and Word Choice

📚 What You're Learning:

Synonyms are words that mean almost the same thing, but good writers choose specific words that fit the situation. 'Big' and 'enormous' are synonyms, but 'enormous' creates a stronger picture.

✨ Examples:

Instead of 'walked,' Sinbad 'dragged himself' onto the beach (shows exhaustion)

Instead of 'bad,' he says 'the worst day' (emphasizes how terrible it was)

Instead of 'scared,' the sailors 'ran screaming' (shows panic and terror)

🎯 Your Turn:

1. Replace 'big' with a better word:

"The bird was big — its wings blocked out the sun."

2. Replace 'said' with a more specific word that shows how Sinbad felt:

"'The sea has its hooks in me,' Sinbad said."

3. Rewrite this sentence using more specific, powerful words:

"The whale moved and went down into the water."
