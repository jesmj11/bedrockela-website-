📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The City Under the Mountain Sky

When I woke up on the other side of the mountain river, said Sinbad, I did not know where I was. I knew I was alive. I knew there was sunlight. I knew the raft was still under me and still moving, which meant the river was still carrying me somewhere. I lay flat on the raft and looked at the sky and did not move for quite a long time. Eventually I sat up. The river was wide and slow and the banks were covered in tall green trees. Birds called from the branches — birds I did not recognize, with brighter colors than Baghdad birds. The air smelled different. Wet and green and alive. I had gone into a mountain through a cave in darkness and come out into a different country. I am a practical man and I am not given to thinking in symbols and meanings, but I will admit that lying on that raft in that new light, I thought: this is what it feels like to be given another chance. The farmers who pulled my raft to shore were kind and curious. They gathered around me asking questions in a language I did not know, pointing at the mountain, looking very concerned. I answered in Arabic and they understood none of it. I pointed at the mountain. They spoke to each other urgently. Eventually someone arrived who spoke a little Persian, which I spoke a little of, and between us we managed to communicate the basic facts: I had come through the mountain river from the other side. This was, apparently, something no one had done before. They gave me food and dry clothes and a place to sleep. In the morning they took me to the city. The city of Serendib was the most beautiful city I had ever seen. Not the largest — Baghdad was larger, and Basra, and the cities of Persia. But beautiful in a particular way, as if the people who built it had thought carefully about what would make it pleasant to be in rather than simply functional. Trees in the streets. Fountains at every corner. Colored tiles on the walls. The king received me in a hall open on three sides to gardens full of flowers. He was a man of middle age, serious and warm, with the kind of face that listens when you speak. I told him my story. He listened to all of it without interrupting. When I finished, he said: "You have survived things that should not be survivable. I would like to hear why you think that is." I thought about it. "Because I kept thinking," I said. "And because I was lucky. Both things together." "Most men," he said, "when they are frightened, stop thinking. You did not." "I was frightened every time," I said. "I was terrified. But terror is just energy that hasn't been pointed anywhere yet. I tried to point it somewhere useful." The king nodded as if I had said something he already knew but was pleased to hear someone else say. He kept me in Serendib for three months. He showed me the island, which was as beautiful as the city. He introduced me to his court, his scholars, his astronomers who charted the stars from a tower at the edge of the sea. When I left, he gave me more gifts than the ship could comfortably carry. I arrived home richer than I had ever been, said Sinbad. And I was certain — truly certain, this time — that I was finished with the sea. He was quiet for a moment. "And then came the letter," said Hindbad. "And then came the letter," said Sinbad. ~ ~ ~

✏️ Grammar

Week 22 Focus: Pronouns and Antecedents

📚 What You're Learning:

A pronoun takes the place of a noun. The antecedent is the noun the pronoun refers to. Make sure it's clear what each pronoun refers to!

✨ Examples:

Sinbad told his story. HE spoke for hours. (He = Sinbad)

The captain saw the whale. HE cut the anchor rope. (He = captain, not whale)

Hindbad was a porter. HIS back hurt from carrying heavy loads. (His = Hindbad's)
