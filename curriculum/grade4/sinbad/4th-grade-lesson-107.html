📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Letter the Caliph Sent

The Caliph of Baghdad was a man who got what he wanted. This is not a criticism. The Caliph was, on the whole, a good ruler who wanted reasonable things and usually got them through fair means. But when he wanted something, he wanted it, and the matter of the king of Serendib had been on his mind for months. "I have heard of this king," the Caliph said to me, when I stood before him in the great hall. "I have heard of his kindness and his beautiful island and his wisdom. I would like to send him greetings. And gifts." "A fine idea," I said carefully. "I'm sure one of your experienced ambassadors could make the voyage." The Caliph looked at me. "You know the way." "I am retired," I said. "I have been on seven — well, six at that point — I have been on six extremely eventful voyages, and I have come home from all of them, and I feel that pressing my luck a seventh time is unwise." The Caliph continued to look at me. "I will of course be honored to serve," I said. The voyage out was good. The sea was kind, the winds were favorable, and I arrived at Serendib to find the king exactly as I had left him — warm, thoughtful, pleased to see me, delighted by the Caliph's letter and gifts. I stayed for a week. I enjoyed every moment of it. The gardens, the fountains, the tower where the astronomers watched the stars. The king fed me excellent food and we talked long into the evenings about everything — trade, philosophy, the differences between Baghdad and Serendib, the strange things the sea brought to both of our shores. On the last evening he gave me his reply to the Caliph's letter, sealed and wrapped, and more gifts for the Caliph: rubies, fine cloth, a carved sandalwood box with a message inside. I said goodbye. I boarded my ship. We set sail. Three days later, pirates. They came from behind an island, three ships to our one, and they hit us from three sides at once. Our crew fought. Two of the pirates' ships had more men than ours. The third came in from the far side while we were occupied with the first two. It was over very quickly. The pirates were not particularly cruel — they wanted cargo and captives, not a fight. They took us alive, which at the time I was grateful for and later came to have more complicated feelings about. They sailed us to an island market and sold us. Standing in a market being sold is a very particular kind of humiliation. I had been shipwrecked and stranded and lost at sea, but I had never been a piece of goods on a table before. The pirates described our skills — this one is strong, this one is a navigator, this one is a merchant from Baghdad with useful knowledge of trade routes. They sold me to a man named Khasib. He bought several of us. He was, as I said, a reasonable master. He did not beat us. He fed us adequately. He explained what he needed from us clearly and then let us get on with it. What he needed was someone willing to sit in a tree and shoot elephants. He explained this to me as if it were an entirely ordinary thing to ask. I thought about my options. I did not have many. "I can shoot," I said. ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 22 Focus: Cause and Effect Relationships

📚 What You're Learning:

A cause is WHY something happens. An effect is WHAT happens. Signal words like 'because,' 'so,' 'since,' 'therefore,' and 'as a result' show cause and effect.

✨ Examples:

CAUSE: Our cooking fires warmed the whale. EFFECT: It woke up and dove.

CAUSE: Sinbad tied himself to the Roc's leg. EFFECT: The bird carried him off the island.

CAUSE: Hindbad was exhausted and frustrated. EFFECT: He complained about unfairness.
