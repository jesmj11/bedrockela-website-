📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Elephant and the Secret Valley

I learned to hunt elephants, said Sinbad, out of necessity. I want to be honest that I am not proud of this. They are intelligent animals — the most intelligent I have ever encountered other than people, and sometimes more trustworthy than people. Khasib's hunters had been killed by elephants protecting their young. He needed someone to shoot from high in the trees, where the elephants could not reach. He had run out of local men willing to try. I was not a man with choices. I climbed trees. I learned to be very quiet and very still for long periods, which is a skill I had not needed before. I learned to read the forest — which sounds told you an elephant was nearby, which sounds meant it was agitated, which meant it was calm. I learned which trees were strong enough to hold a person and also high enough to be safe. I shot from those trees for several weeks. Then the elephants started watching me. Not in the way of an animal that is angry or afraid. In the way of an animal that is figuring something out. They would stand at the base of my tree and look up. Not charging. Not threatening. Just looking. I found this more unnerving than if they had charged. One morning the old elephant came. I had seen it before — huge, ancient, with worn tusks and eyes that looked at you with a weight that made you feel observed rather than simply looked at. It stood under my tree and waited. When I climbed down, it did not move. When I took a step, it turned and walked away and looked back. I want to tell you that I was brave and confident when I followed it. I was not. My hands were shaking. Every part of my mind that is reasonable was saying: do not follow an elephant through a jungle. Every part of me that was desperate and had no other ideas said: follow the elephant. The desperate part won. We walked for an hour. The elephant moved at a pace I could keep up with, which I noticed — it was not running from me or marching ahead, it was walking at a person's pace, checking that I was still following. The valley it led me to was in a fold of hills that you would never find unless you were led to it. The bones lay thick across the valley floor — hundreds of them, maybe thousands. Skulls. Ribs. Enormous curved tusks, creamy white, worth a king's ransom each. The old elephant stopped at the edge of the valley. It turned its head and looked at me. I understood. The elephants knew I was after ivory. They had brought me to more ivory than I could ever take, and they had done it to show me: here is what you want. Take it. It is ours to give. Stop hunting us. I stood at the edge of that valley for a long time. Then I went back to Khasib and told him what I had found. His face when he understood what I was describing — a valley full of tusks, given freely, more than a hundred hunters could have gathered in a lifetime — was a remarkable thing to see. "You followed an elephant," he said slowly. "I did." "Into the jungle." "Yes." He stared at me. "Why?" I thought about it. "Because it asked me to," I said. "And because it seemed to have something to say." Khasib looked at me as if he was not entirely sure I was sane. But he came to the valley. He saw it. He filled his ships. He freed me. ~ ~ ~

✏️ Grammar

Week 22 Focus: Pronouns and Antecedents

📚 What You're Learning:

A pronoun takes the place of a noun. The antecedent is the noun the pronoun refers to. Make sure it's clear what each pronoun refers to!

✨ Examples:

Sinbad told his story. HE spoke for hours. (He = Sinbad)

The captain saw the whale. HE cut the anchor rope. (He = captain, not whale)

Hindbad was a porter. HIS back hurt from carrying heavy loads. (His = Hindbad's)
