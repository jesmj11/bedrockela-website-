📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Long Way Home

There is a difference between going somewhere and coming home, said Sinbad. Going somewhere, you are full of what might happen. You do not know the place. You do not know the people. Every morning is a mystery waiting to be opened. Even on the terrible voyages — even on the islands where things went wrong in every direction — there was a quality of aliveness in not knowing what the day would bring. Coming home, you know what you will find. Your house. Your friends. The streets you grew up on. The smell of Baghdad in the morning — bread and horses and river water and the ten thousand different things that make up the smell of a city you have known all your life. I have come home six times from sea voyages. Each time was different. The first time, I was relieved. Just that — pure relief, like putting down a very heavy thing. I had survived. I was back. The world I knew still existed. The second time, I was grateful. I had been to the bottom of a valley of diamonds and the top of a cliff carried by an eagle and I had seen things no one in Baghdad had seen, and I was grateful to be back among people who understood what I was saying when I spoke. The third time, I was grieving. Eight men had died. I could not celebrate coming home when eight men had not come home. The fourth time, I was different. I had been a slave in a hole underground and I had crawled out in darkness and found a crack in the wall and a ship in the bay, and when I walked into Baghdad I felt as if I had been given the city as a gift. I noticed it more. The light on the river. The way the markets smelled. Children playing in the courtyards. The fifth time, I was angry. The Old Man of the Sea had taken weeks of my life, weeks I will never have back, and I was home but I was still angry at myself for the mistake that had cost them. The sixth time, through the mountain river, I came home quietly. I did not celebrate. I sat in my house by myself for three days and thought about the beach covered in shipwreck bones and the men who had ended their lives there. The seventh time — the last time — I came home a free man who had been a slave and a hunter and an interpreter between an old elephant and a frightened merchant, and I had the Caliph's reply from the king of Serendib in my bag, and Khasib's generous payment in my purse, and I walked through the gates of Baghdad knowing I was home for the last time. There was no triumph in it. Not exactly. More like a completion. Like a long sentence that has finally reached its end. I bought back my father's house. I hired good people to run it well. I invited my friends and fed them generously. I sat in the courtyard on warm evenings and listened to the sounds of the city. Hindbad had been listening with his chin in his hand. "Do you miss it?" he asked. "The sea." Sinbad considered this carefully. "I miss the question it asks," he said finally. "The question of what you're capable of. But I am old now, and I have answered that question often enough. It is enough to remember the answers." "What were the answers?" "More than I expected," said Sinbad. "Every time, more than I expected." ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 22 Focus: Cause and Effect Relationships

📚 What You're Learning:

A cause is WHY something happens. An effect is WHAT happens. Signal words like 'because,' 'so,' 'since,' 'therefore,' and 'as a result' show cause and effect.

✨ Examples:

CAUSE: Our cooking fires warmed the whale. EFFECT: It woke up and dove.

CAUSE: Sinbad tied himself to the Roc's leg. EFFECT: The bird carried him off the island.

CAUSE: Hindbad was exhausted and frustrated. EFFECT: He complained about unfairness.
