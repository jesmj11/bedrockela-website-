📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

What Wealth Actually Costs

I want to tell you something about this house, said Sinbad. Hindbad looked around at the carpets and the lamps and the carved furniture. "Every fine thing in it cost something it does not look like it cost," said Sinbad. "The carpets came from trade goods bought with money earned in the diamond valley. The diamond valley was a pit I was dropped into on the back of an enormous bird after being abandoned on an island. The lamps were bought with profit from the sixth voyage's salvage. The sixth voyage ended with me floating through a mountain in the dark on a raft I built from shipwreck timber. The chair you are sitting on was purchased the year I came home from the ivory valley. The ivory valley was shown to me by an elephant who had watched me kill other elephants for weeks and then decided to offer me a better option." He paused. "Nothing in this house cost money, exactly. Everything in it cost time and fear and the willingness to keep going past the point where stopping felt completely reasonable." Hindbad looked at the chair differently. "I am telling you this," said Sinbad, "not to make you feel that your work is less than mine. A porter who carries loads honestly and comes home to his family every evening and feeds his children is doing something real and valuable and worthy of respect. I am not looking down at you from this chair." "Why are you telling me, then?" "Because you sat on my steps and said the world was unfair. And you were not wrong. The world is not fair — rich men have easier lives than poor men in a dozen obvious ways, and that is genuinely unjust in many of those ways. I do not argue with that." He leaned forward. "But you said it as if there was nothing to be done. As if the distance between your steps and my front door was fixed and permanent and uncrossable. And that part I would push back on." "You had advantages I don't have," said Hindbad. "You could afford trade goods." "I sold my furniture," said Sinbad. "I had a table and three chairs and some bed linens and a cooking pot. That is all I had when I first went to sea. It was not much." "But you were willing to go," said Hindbad. "Not everyone can do that." "True," said Sinbad. "Not everyone can leave what they know. And I am not saying everyone should. I am saying — " He paused, thinking. "I am saying that the distance between where you are and where you want to be is made up of choices and work and some luck, and the choices and work are the part you can affect." He sat back. "That is all. It is not a comfortable answer. I know it is not comfortable. The world should be more fair and I am an old man who got lucky seven times, and if I had died on the whale island none of this would exist and no one would be telling inspiring stories about Sinbad the Sailor." "I know," said Hindbad. "But I didn't die on the whale island," said Sinbad. "And the choices I made after I was in the water — those were mine." Hindbad was quiet for a while. "I will think about that," he said. "That," said Sinbad, "is all I can ask." ~ ~ ~

✏️ Grammar

Week 23 Focus: Possessive Nouns

📚 What You're Learning:

Possessive nouns show ownership. For most singular nouns, add apostrophe-s ('s). For plural nouns ending in s, just add an apostrophe (').

✨ Examples:

Sinbad's house (the house belonging to Sinbad)

The sailor's story (the story told by one sailor)

The sailors' ship (the ship used by multiple sailors)
