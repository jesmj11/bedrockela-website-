📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The King of Serendib Remembers

Let me tell you about the king of Serendib, said Sinbad, because he is worth knowing. His name was Maharaj, and he had ruled his island for thirty years. He was a fair king — his people were well-fed and safe, his courts handled disputes honestly, his roads were kept clear of bandits. He was not remarkable in the way of conquerors or warriors. He was remarkable in the way of someone who does ordinary things extraordinarily well. When I arrived the first time, through the mountain river, half-drowned and barely able to walk, he asked me how I had gotten there. I told him. He asked me what it had felt like to go into the cave in the dark. I told him that too — the cold water, the sound of the walls close on both sides, the way I had kept my hands out to feel for obstacles. "Were you afraid?" he asked. "Terrified," I said. "What made you go in anyway?" I thought about it. "The beach," I said. "The beach was worse. I knew what the beach would eventually be. The river at least went somewhere." He nodded slowly. He had the expression of someone filing something away. Later, when I was rested and fed and wearing clean clothes and sitting in his garden, he came to find me. "I have ruled this island for thirty years," he said. "I have never been to sea. I was born here, and here I will die. I have never had the experience of going into the cave because the beach was worse." "It is not an experience I recommend," I said. "No," he said. "But you describe it as something that made you capable of going into the next cave, and the one after that. A skill acquired through necessity." "I suppose so." "That is something I cannot give my children," he said. "Security is good. Safety is good. But a person who has never been in a cave has never had to find out if they have the courage to go through the dark." He was quiet for a moment. "I think about this when I look at my sons. They are kind boys. They are educated. They have never been hungry or afraid." "That is not entirely a problem," I said. "No," he agreed. "But it is a little bit of one." I thought about this conversation for a long time on the voyage home. I thought about my own father, who had left me money that I wasted, and about what he might have done differently. Probably nothing. Probably I needed to waste the money in order to be desperate enough to go to sea, and the sea was what made me who I am. Perhaps, said Sinbad, some caves have to be chosen. And some are simply the only door left open. He looked at Hindbad. "Did you choose your work?" "No," said Hindbad. "I had a family to feed." "And did it make you capable?" Hindbad thought about it. About the loads he had carried, the distances, the heat, the weight. About what his body could do now that it could not do when he was young. "Yes," he said slowly. "I suppose it did." ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 23 Focus: Theme: What the Story Teaches Us

📚 What You're Learning:

Theme is the lesson or message of a story. It's not what happens (plot) — it's what the story teaches about life. Themes are usually stated as full sentences.

✨ Examples:

PLOT: Sinbad survives dangerous adventures. THEME: Fortune requires risk and perseverance.

PLOT: Hindbad complains about unfairness. THEME: Success often comes from hard work and sacrifice, not luck.

PLOT: Sinbad keeps going back to sea. THEME: Some people need adventure and challenge to feel alive.
