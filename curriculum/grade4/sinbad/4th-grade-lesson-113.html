📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Shape of the Sea

Here is something I learned about the sea that I did not expect, said Sinbad. The sea changes shape. Not the water itself — the ocean does not have a fixed outline any more than a river does. I mean the sea changes what it looks like from a ship's deck depending on the weather and the time of year and the part of it you are in. The sea I sailed in summer was blue-green and clear and you could see fish moving twenty feet below the surface. The sea in winter storms was gray-black and opaque and moved like something alive and angry. The sea in the early morning, before the sun was fully up, had a particular silver quality that made it look like hammered metal. I never got tired of looking at it. This surprises people. After everything the sea did to me — the storms, the shipwrecks, the islands, the whale, the pirates, everything — you would expect me to hate it. I do not hate it. I am no longer able to sail on it, at my age, and I miss it. Not the danger. I do not miss nearly dying. But the quality of the sea — its size, its aliveness, its absolute indifference to whether I lived or died — that I miss. The sea does not care about you. It does not wish you well or ill. It simply is. It is enormous and old and it will be here long after every person on every ship has returned to the earth. There is something restful about that, when you accept it. The worst sailors I met were the ones who took the sea personally. When a storm came they said: why is this happening to me? When the wind died they said: why has it abandoned us? The sea was not punishing them and the sea had not abandoned them — the sea was simply being the sea. The best sailors I met accepted this and worked with it rather than against it. The weather is doing this — what can we do now? The wind is from this direction — what does that mean for our course? Questions again, said Sinbad. Always questions. The sea is also generous, when you are paying attention. It gives you fish if you know where to look. It gives you current if you know how to read it. It gives you wind if you set your sails right. It is not your enemy. It is simply a very large environment with its own rules, and the rules can be learned. The same is true of most things in life that seem frightening from the outside. Hindbad had stopped eating and was simply listening. "You talk about the sea the way you talk about a person," he said. "I know," said Sinbad. "After seven voyages, it felt like one to me. Strange and difficult and occasionally dangerous, but a person you could come to know." He smiled. "I have known worse people." Hindbad laughed. It was the first time he had laughed in several nights. "Good," said Sinbad. "You needed that." ~ ~ ~

✏️ Grammar

Week 23 Focus: Possessive Nouns

📚 What You're Learning:

Possessive nouns show ownership. For most singular nouns, add apostrophe-s ('s). For plural nouns ending in s, just add an apostrophe (').

✨ Examples:

Sinbad's house (the house belonging to Sinbad)

The sailor's story (the story told by one sailor)

The sailors' ship (the ship used by multiple sailors)
