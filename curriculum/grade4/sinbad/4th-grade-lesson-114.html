📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

What Was Lost

There are things I have not told you, said Sinbad. Not because I wanted to hide them. Because they are heavy things and I was waiting until we knew each other well enough for heavy things. On the third voyage, eight men died. I told you that. I was the one who organized the attack on the giant — the stakes, the fire. I was the one who decided we could not wait any longer to act. If I had decided differently, perhaps some of those men would have survived. If I had decided differently, perhaps none of us would have. I do not know. I have never known. I know that I made the choice I thought was right with the information I had at the time. I know that eight men went into that sea when the giant threw the boulders and did not come out. I know their names. I have not forgotten a single one of them. Yusuf, the carpenter from Basra. Hamid, who had a daughter he was saving money for. Tariq, who was the best cook on any ship I ever sailed. Five others. I knew all of them. On the second raft, the one that was not mine, there were six men. The boulder hit them directly. There was nothing left of the raft or the men to retrieve. I held this for a long time before I could say it to anyone. I still hold it. A wealthy man in a fine house in Baghdad carries the names of eight men who followed his plan and died for it. This is what wealth actually costs, Hindbad. Not the storms. Not the fear. The weight of the names. Hindbad was very still. "I'm sorry," he said. "Thank you," said Sinbad. "I do not often hear that." He was quiet for a moment. "I funded their families when I returned. It was not enough. It could never be enough. But it was something I could do, and I did it." He looked at the lamp. "I went back to sea again, on the fourth voyage, partly because I could not sit in my comfortable house with those names. The sea asked me questions that distracted me from the ones I could not answer." He looked at Hindbad. "Do you understand that?" Hindbad thought about it. He thought about the days after his own father died, when he had taken every difficult job he could find — the heaviest loads, the longest distances — because moving was easier than sitting still. "Yes," he said. "I understand that." "I thought you might," said Sinbad. They sat together in the quiet for a while, which was its own kind of conversation. ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 23 Focus: Theme: What the Story Teaches Us

📚 What You're Learning:

Theme is the lesson or message of a story. It's not what happens (plot) — it's what the story teaches about life. Themes are usually stated as full sentences.

✨ Examples:

PLOT: Sinbad survives dangerous adventures. THEME: Fortune requires risk and perseverance.

PLOT: Hindbad complains about unfairness. THEME: Success often comes from hard work and sacrifice, not luck.

PLOT: Sinbad keeps going back to sea. THEME: Some people need adventure and challenge to feel alive.
