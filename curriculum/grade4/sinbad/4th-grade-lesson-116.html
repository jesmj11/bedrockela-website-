📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Merchants He Met

Let me tell you about the people I met, said Sinbad. Not the kings and the monsters and the strange customs — you have heard plenty of that. Let me tell you about the ordinary people. On the first voyage, on King Mihrjan's island, there was a spice merchant named Farouk who had been trading on those routes for forty years. He knew every island, every current, every reliable captain on the eastern sea. He had a small shop near the harbor where he sorted and packaged his goods in the evenings, and he let me sit with him and ask questions. I learned more from Farouk in three months than I had learned in twenty-three years in Baghdad. He taught me which spices were worth what, and why, and how to tell the difference between fresh and old, and how to negotiate without appearing to negotiate. He taught me that most successful trade is just careful observation: knowing what something costs here and what it sells for there, and finding a reliable way to move it between the two places. "The sea is just a road," said Farouk. "A difficult one. But still a road." He was the first person who made me see my voyages as trade rather than adventure. That shift in perspective changed how I did everything. On the fourth voyage, in the city with the strange food, there was an old woman who spoke to me in a language I mostly didn't understand, but who clearly understood that I was not eating the food I was supposed to eat and was choosing not to say anything about it. She brought me plain bread and clean water every evening, left at the threshold of my room. I never found out who she was. I never learned her name. She saved my life with bread and water and silence. On the sixth voyage, on the beach of shipwrecks, there was a man who had been there for eleven years. He had survived eleven years on a beach that was supposed to be a place you died in. He had built a shelter, found a freshwater spring, learned which plants were edible, organized the other survivors into something like a community. When I arrived and started talking about the river in the mountain, he was the first person who listened carefully. "The river goes somewhere," I said. "It goes underground," he said. "Into the dark." "Somewhere," I said. "Eventually it goes somewhere." He helped me build the raft. He was too old and too weak to go through the river himself. He knew this. He helped me build it anyway. His name was Daoud. I got home. I do not know if he is still on that beach or if anyone ever went back for him. I have thought about going back. I have thought about it many times. I did not go. Sinbad was quiet for a moment. "The people who helped you," said Hindbad. "Did you ever repay them?" "Farouk I sent goods to, from Baghdad, for years. The bread woman — I never found her again. Daoud — I sent a ship to that beach, three years later. The beach was empty. I choose to believe he found his own way out." "Do you believe it?" "I choose to," said Sinbad. "There are some things you choose to believe because the alternative is heavier than you can carry." He refilled their cups. "But the merchants," he said. "The ordinary working people on those islands, trading their goods and feeding their families and knowing every current on the eastern sea — they are the ones who made the voyages possible. Not the adventures. The quiet, reliable, useful people." He looked at Hindbad steadily. "Not unlike a porter who shows up every morning and carries what needs to be carried." ~ ~ ~

✏️ Grammar

Week 24 Focus: Sentence Fragments vs. Complete Sentences

📚 What You're Learning:

A complete sentence needs a subject (who/what) and a predicate (action). A fragment is missing one of these. Fragments can be effective for style, but you need to know the rule before you can break it!

✨ Examples:

COMPLETE: Sinbad told many stories. (subject + predicate)

FRAGMENT: For two days in the ocean. (missing: who? what happened?)

COMPLETE: I held on for two days. (subject + predicate)
