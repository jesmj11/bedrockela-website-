📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Last Thing I Learned

On the seventh voyage, said Sinbad, after Khasib freed me, I had a long journey home. Khasib sailed me to the nearest large port, which was still several weeks from Baghdad by land. He gave me money for the journey and enough ivory value in trade goods to make me comfortable, and he shook my hand and said: I have never met a man like you, and I hope my path crosses yours again. It has not. I hope he is well. I traveled home slowly through places I had not seen before, trading as I went, buying and selling small things, sleeping in caravanserais with merchants from a dozen different countries. I spoke Arabic and some Persian and a little of the eastern island languages and enough bad Turkish to get by, and I ate what was offered and slept in whatever bed was available and I was happy. Just happy. Quietly, ordinarily happy. Not because of the ivory or the money or the thought of coming home. Happy because I was a person moving through the world in a useful way, talking to people about useful things, buying a bolt of red cloth in one town and selling it in the next town where red cloth was wanted. The road from the sea to Baghdad took six weeks. I spent six weeks being a perfectly ordinary traveling merchant, and it was one of the best six weeks of my life. I had spent my voyages learning to survive extraordinary things. The last thing I learned was how to be content with ordinary ones. This is the hardest thing, said Sinbad. Not the Roc. Not the giant. Not the Old Man of the Sea. The hardest thing is to be happy when nothing remarkable is happening, when every day is similar to the last, when no eagle is lifting you from a pit and no river is carrying you through a mountain. Most of life is ordinary. You wake up. You do your work. You eat your food. You sleep. The adventures are few and far between and mostly shorter than they seem in the telling. The question is whether you can be grateful for the ordinary things. The bread. The bed. The clean water. The road that is just a road, not a test or an adventure, just a path from one place to another. I could not see this when I was young and broke and angry. When you have nothing, ordinary feels like deprivation. Once I had seen enough extraordinary things — once I had been in enough valleys of diamonds and burial chambers and giant-haunted islands — ordinary started to look different. Ordinary meant safe. Ordinary meant alive. Ordinary meant home. Hindbad was quiet. "I think I understand," he said. "But I think it is easier to appreciate ordinary when you have chosen it. When it is all you have, it feels like no choice at all." "Yes," said Sinbad. "You are right. I cannot argue with that." He paused. "What I can say is that the ordinary things in your life — your family, your work, the streets of Baghdad, the weight on your shoulders that tells you you are useful — those things are real and worth having. Even when you did not choose them." Hindbad thought about his children's faces when he came home in the evening. "Even then," he said. "Even then," agreed Sinbad. ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 24 Focus: Character Traits: Show, Don't Tell

📚 What You're Learning:

Good writers SHOW character traits through actions and words instead of just TELLING us. Instead of 'Sinbad was brave,' show him tying himself to a Roc's leg.

✨ Examples:

TELLING: Hindbad was tired and frustrated.

SHOWING: Hindbad sat in the dust and complained. His back hurt. Even his elbows hurt.

SHOWING: Sinbad laughed and invited Hindbad inside. He offered food and listened.
