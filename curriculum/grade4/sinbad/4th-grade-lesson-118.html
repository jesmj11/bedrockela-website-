📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

Morning at the Sailor's House

On the last morning Hindbad arrived early, before the other guests. He knocked at the gate, not sure if it was too early. A servant opened it immediately — servants in a house like Sinbad's were always awake before the household needed them — and brought him into the garden. The garden was cool in the early morning, the air still holding the night's quiet. A fountain ran at the center. Doves called from somewhere in the old fig tree. Sinbad was already there, sitting in a low chair with a glass of mint tea, watching the light change on the fountain. He looked smaller in the morning light, somehow. Older. Just an old man in a garden, with a teacup. Then he looked up and smiled and was Sinbad again. "You came early," he said. "I wanted to ask you something before the others arrive," said Hindbad. "Before the story ends properly." A servant brought Hindbad tea. He sat down across from Sinbad. "Ask," said Sinbad. "Was it worth it?" said Hindbad. "All of it — the voyages, the danger, the men you lost, the years of your life on ships and islands and caves. Was this —" He gestured at the house, the garden, the fountain. "Was this worth all of that?" Sinbad held his teacup in both hands and looked at the water. "I cannot answer for the men I lost," he said. "That is not mine to answer. For them, clearly, no — they paid a price that was not fair and did not lead to any reward for themselves. I carry that." He paused. "For me — yes. But not because of the house. This house would mean nothing if it were all I had to show for seventy years. It would just be a building." "What does it mean, then?" "It means I know what I am capable of," said Sinbad. "I know that I can think clearly when I am frightened. I know that I can endure being alone and lost and hungry and humiliated and still find the way out. I know that when a door closes, I will look for the crack in the wall. I know that when an elephant shows me something, I should follow." He smiled. "I know the names of more fish than any person in Baghdad. I know what a hundred different harbors smell like. I know which stars to follow to find east, and south, and home. I know how to build a raft from driftwood and how to tie a knot that holds in wind and how to stay awake for two days when awake is the only option." He looked at Hindbad. "That knowledge is in me. The house could burn and I would still have it." "That," said Hindbad slowly, "is the answer I was hoping for." The sounds of the house were beginning — doors, voices, the smell of bread. The other guests would arrive soon. The last morning of the story was ending. "Thank you," said Hindbad. "For the food and the stories and the —" He paused. "For treating me as if my questions were worth answering." "They were," said Sinbad simply. ~ ~ ~

✏️ Grammar

Week 24 Focus: Sentence Fragments vs. Complete Sentences

📚 What You're Learning:

A complete sentence needs a subject (who/what) and a predicate (action). A fragment is missing one of these. Fragments can be effective for style, but you need to know the rule before you can break it!

✨ Examples:

COMPLETE: Sinbad told many stories. (subject + predicate)

FRAGMENT: For two days in the ocean. (missing: who? what happened?)

COMPLETE: I held on for two days. (subject + predicate)
