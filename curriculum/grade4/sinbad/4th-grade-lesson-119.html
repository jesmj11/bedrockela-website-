📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Purse of Gold

After the other guests had come and eaten and the final morning's feast was done, Sinbad stood at the door to see everyone out. He pressed a purse into each guest's hand as they left — a generous gift, a custom of hospitality that said: you honored my house by eating in it. When Hindbad reached the door, Sinbad pressed a larger purse into his hands. Hindbad looked up. "You deserve more for your patience," said Sinbad, "than a man who came for an afternoon." Hindbad felt the weight of the purse. It was heavy. "I can't accept this," he said. "You can," said Sinbad. "It is not charity. It is payment for being an honest listener. I have told these stories to many people over many years. Most of them listen for the excitement — the whale and the giant and the eagle and the diamonds. You listened for something else." "What?" "For what they cost," said Sinbad. "You asked the right questions. Every night you pushed past the adventure and asked: and then what? And what did that do to you? And were you afraid? That is a harder kind of listening and I am grateful for it." Hindbad held the purse. He looked at the street — the ordinary street, the sunlight on the stones, the other people going about their ordinary days. "I'm going to think about the river in the mountain," he said. "For a long time." "Good," said Sinbad. "That is the right one to think about." "Going into the dark because the beach was worse," said Hindbad. "Yes." "It is a good way to think about — a lot of things." "I hoped you would find it useful," said Sinbad. They looked at each other for a moment — the old sailor in his fine house and the porter with his load — and something passed between them that was not friendship exactly, because they did not know each other well enough for friendship, but was something in the same family as friendship. Recognition, maybe. The acknowledgment that the other person had seen something real. "Go well," said Sinbad. "You too," said Hindbad. He walked home through the streets of Baghdad with the purse in his belt and the stories in his head. The morning was already hot. His next load was waiting for him at the warehouse by the river. He picked it up. It was heavy. It was always heavy. But he thought about a raft going into a dark cave because there was no better option, and the light that appeared on the other side, and he carried his load down the long hot street toward the river, and the weight of it, somehow, felt a little different. Not lighter. But different. And that was enough. THE END ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 24 Focus: Character Traits: Show, Don't Tell

📚 What You're Learning:

Good writers SHOW character traits through actions and words instead of just TELLING us. Instead of 'Sinbad was brave,' show him tying himself to a Roc's leg.

✨ Examples:

TELLING: Hindbad was tired and frustrated.

SHOWING: Hindbad sat in the dust and complained. His back hurt. Even his elbows hurt.

SHOWING: Sinbad laughed and invited Hindbad inside. He offered food and listened.
