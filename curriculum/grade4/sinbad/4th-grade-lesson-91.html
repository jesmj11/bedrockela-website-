📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Porter and the Sailor

Hindbad the porter was having the worst day of his week. He was carrying a load so heavy it felt like someone had stacked a donkey on top of another donkey and then asked him to carry both. The sun was blazing. The streets of Baghdad were packed with people who kept bumping into him. His back hurt. His feet hurt. Even his elbows hurt, which he hadn't known was possible. He stopped to rest on the steps of the grandest house on the street. Cool air drifted from inside. He could smell roasting lamb and fresh bread and something sweet he couldn't name. Music was playing — a lute, expertly played, somewhere deeper in the house. Hindbad sat down in the dust and said several things under his breath about the unfairness of the world. A servant appeared at the door. "My master heard you," the servant said. "He would like you to come inside." Hindbad was sure this was going to go badly for him. He stood up anyway, because when a rich man's servant invites you inside a rich man's house, you go inside. The room was cool and enormous and covered in carpets so fine that Hindbad felt embarrassed to walk on them. At the head of a long table sat an old man — white-haired, richly dressed, with bright eyes and the kind of face that has seen a great deal and decided to find most of it interesting. "I am Sinbad," the old man said. "Sinbad the Sailor. Please sit down." A servant put food in front of Hindbad before he could say anything. He ate. The food was extraordinary. "I heard what you said on my steps," said Sinbad. "You said the world is unfair. That some men sit in fine houses while others break their backs in the street." Hindbad looked at his hands. "I didn't know anyone could hear me." "I can always hear that," said Sinbad. "I used to say the same thing, when I was young and poor and angry about it." He leaned back in his chair. "I was born in Baghdad. My father was a merchant who left me money when he died — enough to live on comfortably. I spent it all. Every coin. Feasts and fine clothes and all the usual ways a young man destroys a fortune." He paused. "When the money was gone, I had nothing. I was poor and I was embarrassed and I needed to do something about it. So I went to sea." "You chose to go to sea?" said Hindbad. "I did. Seven times." Hindbad looked around at the fine house, the food, the carpets, the musicians barely visible through an archway. "And this is what the sea gave you?" "This is what the sea cost me," said Sinbad. "There is a difference." He refilled Hindbad's cup. "Stay tonight. I'll tell you about the first voyage. Then you can decide whether my fortune came easily." Hindbad stayed. And Sinbad began. ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

Baghdad: The City of Sinbad

When Sinbad lived in Baghdad (around 800-1200 AD), it was one of the greatest cities in the world. It had libraries, schools, hospitals, and markets filled with goods from across Asia and Africa.

The city was the capital of the Abbasid Caliphate and sat on the Tigris River, which made it perfect for trade. Ships could sail down the river to the Persian Gulf and then across the Indian Ocean to India, China, and East Africa.

Baghdad had over a million people during its golden age — more than any European city at the time! The city was divided into circular rings with the caliph's palace at the center. Merchants, craftsmen, and scholars lived in different districts.

The markets (called souks or bazaars) sold spices from India, silk from China, ivory from Africa, and pearls from the Persian Gulf. A merchant like Sinbad could buy goods in one place, sail across the ocean, and sell them for many times the original price in another city.

💡 The Lesson

Sinbad's adventures took place during the Islamic Golden Age, when Baghdad was the center of world trade, science, and learning. The wealth and opportunities Sinbad found weren't fantasy — they were the real possibilities for successful merchants of his time.

✏️ Grammar

Week 19 Focus: Compound Sentences with Coordinating Conjunctions

📚 What You're Learning:

A compound sentence joins two complete thoughts (independent clauses) with a comma and a coordinating conjunction. The seven coordinating conjunctions are: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (FANBOYS).

✨ Examples:

Sinbad was poor, so he went to sea.

The island looked safe, but it was actually a whale.

Hindbad could complain about unfairness, or he could listen to Sinbad's story.
