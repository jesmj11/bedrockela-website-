📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The First Voyage: The Island That Moved

I was twenty-three years old when I first went to sea, said Sinbad. I had nothing. My father's money was gone and I was ashamed of how I'd wasted it. I sold what little I had left — a few pieces of furniture, some clothes — and bought trade goods with the money. Fabric. Spices. Small things that took up little space but could be sold for more than I'd paid for them. I found a merchant ship leaving for the islands of the east and bought passage. The sea was not what I expected. I had grown up in the desert heat of Baghdad. The ocean was vast in a way that land is not — you could stand at the rail and look in every direction and see nothing but water and sky, and the ship was the only solid thing in all of it. I loved it immediately. That was my first mistake, because loving the sea makes you stop being careful around it. After several weeks we came to a beautiful island. Green trees, white sand, freshwater streams running down to the shore. The captain anchored and we went ashore to rest and cook and enjoy the land for a day. Some men swam. Some gathered fruit. I found a soft patch of grass under a shady tree and fell asleep. The island moved. I don't mean it shifted slightly. I mean it lurched, tilted, and began to sink. Because it was not an island. It was a whale — an ancient whale of enormous size that had been floating still for so long that soil had built up on its back and trees had grown from the soil, and it had looked exactly like an island because it had been still for so long. Until our cooking fires warmed it. And it woke up. Men ran screaming for the ship. The captain, to his credit, cut the anchor rope immediately and began maneuvering to pick up survivors. The whale dove slowly, taking its trees and its grass and its freshwater stream and me with it. I grabbed a wooden washtub that was floating nearby. I held on. The ship disappeared. The whale disappeared. I was alone in the middle of the ocean on a wooden washtub, which is perhaps the least dignified vessel ever used for an ocean crossing. I held on for two days. Wind and current pushed me toward an island — a real one this time, solid rock and old trees that had definitely not grown on a whale's back. I dragged myself onto the beach and lay there for a while being grateful. I found horses on that island. Beautiful horses, being tended by grooms who worked for a king named Mihrjan. They fed me and gave me dry clothes and brought me to the king. He was generous and kind, and when he heard I was a merchant from Baghdad he gave me a position overseeing the harbor — recording the names and cargoes of ships as they came and went. I did that work for months. I was grateful for it. But every time a ship came in from Baghdad I felt something pull in my chest like a hook. One day a ship arrived, and the merchant overseeing its cargo was dealing with goods that looked exactly like the goods I had lost when the whale surfaced. I went closer. I read the labels on the bales. My name was on them. My goods. Packed and labeled by my own hands. The captain of the ship came down the gangplank and I recognized him. He had thought I was dead. He had logged my goods as cargo of a drowned man and intended to sell them and distribute the profit to my heirs. I told him I was not dead. He stared at me for a long time. Then he laughed and embraced me and gave me everything back. I said goodbye to King Mihrjan and sailed for home. I arrived in Baghdad with more goods than I had left with, because I had traded carefully during my months at the harbor. I sold them at a profit and bought my father's house back and furnished it well. And then, said Sinbad, I sat in my comfortable house for exactly six months before I started thinking about the sea again. Hindbad looked at him. "You went back?" "Of course I went back," said Sinbad. "That is the nature of the sea. Now go home and sleep. Come back tomorrow. I'll tell you about the second voyage." ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

Sailing Ships of the Arabian Sea

Sinbad would have sailed on a type of ship called a dhow. These wooden ships had triangular sails that could catch wind from almost any direction — much better than the square sails used in Europe at the time!

Dhows were built without iron nails. Instead, shipbuilders sewed the wooden planks together using coconut fiber rope! This made the ships flexible so they could bend with the waves instead of breaking apart.

Arab sailors were experts at using the stars, sun, and wind to navigate. They used an instrument called a kamal to measure the angle of stars above the horizon, which told them their latitude (how far north or south they were).

Trade routes connected the Arabian Peninsula to East Africa, India, Southeast Asia, and China. A voyage could take months, and sailors had to plan around the monsoon winds — winds that blow one direction half the year and the opposite direction the other half.

💡 The Lesson

Arab sailors and shipbuilders were some of the best in the world during Sinbad's time. Their innovative ship designs and navigation skills made long ocean voyages possible centuries before Europeans mastered similar techniques.

🎨 Language Skills

Week 19 Focus: Context Clues: Using Sentences to Figure Out Word Meanings

📚 What You're Learning:

When you don't know a word, look at the words around it for clues. The sentence often gives hints about what the unknown word means.

✨ Examples:

The captain cut the anchor rope and began maneuvering to pick up survivors. (Maneuvering = moving the ship carefully)

I was alone in the ocean on a wooden washtub, which is perhaps the least dignified vessel ever used. (Dignified = respected or impressive)

The whale dove slowly, taking its trees and its grass with it. (Dove = went down underwater)
