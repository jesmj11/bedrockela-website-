📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Second Voyage: The Valley of Diamonds

Hindbad came back the next day, and Sinbad continued. The second voyage, said Sinbad, began the same way the first one had: I had a comfortable life, I got restless, I bought trade goods and found a ship. We stopped at a beautiful green island to rest. I found a shady spot by a stream and fell asleep reading, which I mention only to say that I am capable of making exactly the same mistake twice. When I woke up, the ship was gone. I want to be clear about this. The ship had simply left. Whether they forgot to count heads or assumed I had already reboarded or simply did not notice — I cannot say. But I climbed the tallest tree I could find and looked in every direction. Water. Just water. No sail anywhere on the horizon. In the center of the island I found a white dome — perfectly round, perfectly smooth, taller than a house and with no door or window anywhere. I walked around it several times trying to understand what I was looking at. Then a shadow fell over me. The shadow of something very large. The sun disappeared. I looked up. The largest bird I have ever seen or imagined was descending from the sky. Its wingspan blocked the light completely. It was a Roc — a bird from old stories, a bird I had assumed was exaggerated for dramatic effect. It was not exaggerated. It landed on the dome, which was its egg. It settled itself and tucked its enormous head under one wing. I thought very quickly. I unwound my turban — I always wear a turban with a good length of cloth — and tied myself carefully to the Roc's leg. If there was a way off this island, this was it. At dawn the Roc rose straight up. I held on with everything I had and kept my eyes shut until the wind told me I was very high up. We flew for what felt like half a day. Then the bird descended, fast and steep, and landed. I untied myself and looked around. I was at the bottom of a valley surrounded by cliff walls so sheer and so high that there was no possible way to climb them. The valley floor was covered in diamonds. Not small ones. Handfuls of them, everywhere I stepped. I also noticed the snakes. Large snakes. Enormous snakes. Snakes thick as tree trunks, coiled in the shade of the cliff walls. They were sleeping. I discovered later that they slept during the day and hunted at night. It was currently day. That was the good news. The bad news was that I was at the bottom of a pit of diamonds with no way out. Then something came down from the sky — a large piece of fresh meat, thrown from the cliff top. Then another. Then several more. I understood: this was how the diamond merchants of the region worked. They threw down meat. The diamonds stuck to the fat and blood. Eagles swooped down to carry the meat back to their nests at the top of the cliffs. The merchants then collected the diamonds from the nests. I filled my pockets with diamonds. Then I found the largest piece of meat I could and tied myself to it, as firmly as my turban would allow. An eagle lifted me. Very fast. Very high. At the top of the cliff, the eagle landed in its nest and a merchant shouted and waved his arms at it to drive it away from the meat — which was when he found me, tied to his dinner, covered in diamonds, somewhat shaken. He stared. I untied myself. "The strangest thing," he said finally, "that has ever come up from that valley." I gave him a share of the diamonds. He gave me passage to the nearest city. I traded the remaining diamonds for an enormous profit and found a ship home to Baghdad. Sinbad smiled at Hindbad across the table. "That is the second voyage." "You tied yourself to a piece of meat," said Hindbad. "I did." "And rode an eagle out of a pit." "I did." "And you went to sea a third time." Sinbad laughed. "Oh, the third time was far worse. Come back tomorrow." ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

The Spice Trade: Why Merchants Risked Everything

Spices like pepper, cinnamon, cloves, and nutmeg were worth more than gold in medieval times. A small bag of spices could buy a house! That's why merchants like Sinbad were willing to risk dangerous ocean voyages.

Most spices grew only in specific places: pepper in India, cinnamon in Ceylon (Sri Lanka), cloves in the Molucca Islands (Indonesia). If you could bring these spices to Europe or the Middle East, you could become incredibly wealthy.

Spices weren't just for making food taste better. They were used as medicine, to preserve meat (before refrigeration existed), and even as currency. People paid taxes in peppercorns!

The spice trade created the first global economy. Goods from Indonesia might pass through a dozen merchants before reaching Europe, with each merchant adding their profit. The final price could be 100 times what the original farmer was paid.

💡 The Lesson

Sinbad's willingness to risk his life for trade goods wasn't foolish — spices and rare items were genuinely worth fortunes. The wealth he gained from one successful voyage could support him for years.

✏️ Grammar

Week 19 Focus: Compound Sentences with Coordinating Conjunctions

📚 What You're Learning:

A compound sentence joins two complete thoughts (independent clauses) with a comma and a coordinating conjunction. The seven coordinating conjunctions are: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (FANBOYS).

✨ Examples:

Sinbad was poor, so he went to sea.

The island looked safe, but it was actually a whale.

Hindbad could complain about unfairness, or he could listen to Sinbad's story.
