📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Third Voyage: The Giant's Island

The third voyage, said Sinbad the next evening, taught me something about the difference between looking dangerous and being dangerous. We were a week from port when we saw the island. Low and green, with a high rocky hill at the center. We went ashore for water. From the hill, something watched us. Something large. It came down the slope toward us with the slow confidence of an animal that has never needed to hurry because nothing has ever been a serious threat to it. It was shaped like a man but the size of a house — black skin, one eye in the center of its forehead, teeth like stone pillars, fingernails like iron hooks. It walked through the shallow water to our ship as if wading through a stream. It picked up our captain and looked at him the way you look at a fruit when you're deciding whether to eat it. It put him down. It walked around the ship slowly, studying us all. Then it walked back up to its cave and went to sleep. We held a council on the ship. The sensible thing was to sail away immediately. We had plenty of water. We could simply leave. Nobody suggested this quickly enough. By morning the giant was back. This time it chose. It picked up our largest sailor — the strongest, fattest man among us — and ate him for breakfast, right there on the beach in front of everyone. Then it went back to its cave. I will not describe the next few evenings. We could not sail because the wind was against us. Each morning the giant came and ate one man. We could not sleep. We could barely eat. On the third night, when the giant was sleeping, we built two large rafts from timber we cut from the ship. We carried sharpened stakes to the cave. We heated the stakes in the fire until the tips glowed red. Ten of us drove the stakes into the giant's single eye at the same moment. The scream was unlike any sound I have heard before or since. We ran for the rafts. The giant stumbled out of the cave, blind and roaring, grabbing at anything in reach. We pushed off from shore. He couldn't see us. But he could hear us. He picked up boulders and threw them by sound. He was extraordinarily accurate. Two of our rafts — and most of our men — were crushed. My raft and one other survived. We drifted for two days. We landed on another island. We were exhausted and half-dead with thirst and grief for our companions. That night, a serpent as thick as a house beam came from the trees. It swallowed one of my two remaining companions whole. We could hear him inside it. On the second night it came back. It swallowed the other man. I spent the third night in the highest tree I could find, tied to the branches with my turban, too frightened to sleep. At dawn I came down and built a raft and pushed off before the serpent woke. I was very tired by the time I reached Baghdad. Sinbad was quiet for a moment. "Eleven men sailed with me on the third voyage," he said. "Three of us came home. I have never stopped thinking about that." Hindbad said nothing. "I went back a fourth time," said Sinbad. "But not for a year. Come back tomorrow." ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

Monsters, Myths, and Maps: What Medieval Sailors Believed

Medieval sailors genuinely believed in sea monsters, giant serpents, and islands that moved. Maps from this time period show dragons, giant fish, and warnings like 'here be monsters' written in the ocean.

Some of these legends came from real animals misunderstood. Giant squids (which can grow up to 40 feet long) probably inspired kraken legends. Manatees (large sea mammals) might have been mistaken for mermaids from a distance.

Sailors also believed in magnetic islands that could pull the nails out of ships, whirlpools that could swallow entire fleets, and fog that made ships disappear forever. Some of these were based on real dangers (like dangerous currents or reefs) that sailors didn't fully understand.

The Roc (giant bird) in Sinbad's stories was based on legends of enormous eagles. Some historians think these legends started when people found fossils of extinct giant birds like the Elephant Bird of Madagascar, which really existed but died out around 1000 AD.

💡 The Lesson

Many 'monsters' in stories like Sinbad came from sailors trying to explain real things they encountered: strange animals, dangerous weather, or natural phenomena they didn't have scientific knowledge to understand.

🎨 Language Skills

Week 19 Focus: Context Clues: Using Sentences to Figure Out Word Meanings

📚 What You're Learning:

When you don't know a word, look at the words around it for clues. The sentence often gives hints about what the unknown word means.

✨ Examples:

The captain cut the anchor rope and began maneuvering to pick up survivors. (Maneuvering = moving the ship carefully)

I was alone in the ocean on a wooden washtub, which is perhaps the least dignified vessel ever used. (Dignified = respected or impressive)

The whale dove slowly, taking its trees and its grass with it. (Dove = went down underwater)
