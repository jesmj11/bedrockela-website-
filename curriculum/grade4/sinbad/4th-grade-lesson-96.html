📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Fourth Voyage: The Underground Kingdom

The fourth voyage, said Sinbad, is the one that taught me the most useful thing I know. On the fourth voyage, I was captured. Our ship was caught in a sudden storm — not a natural storm, but a storm that seemed to grow and grow and never end, driving us onto rocks, smashing the ship, scattering us into the water. I woke up on a beach with half a dozen survivors and no ship, no cargo, no supplies. People found us on the beach. They were kind people — they brought us food and water and took us to their city, which was beautiful and strange. For several days everything was fine. Then they brought us food. I cannot explain it precisely. The food smelled wonderful. My companions ate it without hesitation. Something in me refused. The food made my companions hungry. The more they ate, the more they wanted, and the more they ate the less they could think about anything except eating. Within a week they could not carry on a proper conversation. Within two weeks they had forgotten their own names. I pretended to eat. I hid the food when no one was watching. I stayed thin and quiet and sharp. The people of the city fed my companions until they were enormous and then took them to a field and left them there. I never saw them again. The same people found me. I was thin and clearly useless for what they wanted. They didn't know what to do with me. While they were deciding, I slipped away into the countryside. I found a road. I followed it. I kept walking. Eventually the road led me to a city where the people were normal and sensible and very surprised to see a half-starved sailor from Baghdad stumbling out of the wild lands to the east. I told my story to their king. He was fascinated. He gave me clothes and food and a horse. In that city, no one used saddles. The king asked if I could make one — he had heard of saddles but never seen one. I built a saddle from wood and leather. The king was delighted. His nobles wanted saddles too. Within a month I was the most successful saddlemaker in the city, which is a sentence I had not expected to say about my life. The king gave me his daughter in marriage. I will stop there for a moment because Hindbad's expression had become quite startled. Yes, said Sinbad. The king gave me his daughter in marriage. We were happy. I had a fine house and a good income and a wife I was very fond of, and I thought: this is where I will stay. The sea is done with me. Then my wife got sick and died. I was devastated. And then I discovered a custom of that city that I found deeply alarming: when a husband died, he was buried with his wife. When a wife died, she was buried with her husband. This was considered respectful. This was considered proper. I was lowered into a burial chamber with a small lamp, a small amount of food and water, and my wife's body. The lamp burned down. The food ran out. I waited in the dark for something to happen, because something had to happen — I refused to simply sit there and die in a hole while I was still perfectly capable of being alive. I heard something. Scratching. Movement. An animal — some kind of wild creature that had found its way into the burial chambers through a crack in the rock. I followed the sound. I crawled in the dark in the direction of the scratching for what felt like hours. Air moved against my face. Cold, fresh air. I found the crack. I squeezed through it. I came out on the sea cliff in the light of morning. There was a ship in the bay. I waved my shirt and shouted until they saw me. Sinbad poured more tea. The useful thing I learned, he said, is this: always notice what you will not eat. Other people's customs are not always safe. A man who thinks clearly in a crisis will sometimes survive situations where a man who acts normally will not. And the crack in the wall is always there if you look hard enough in the dark. Hindbad thought about this for a long time. ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

What Happened to Sailors Who Died at Sea?

Different cultures had different beliefs about sailors who died during voyages. In Sinbad's culture (Islamic tradition), burial at sea was allowed only if returning the body to land was impossible.

Many ships carried specific prayers and rituals for sailors who died at sea. The crew would wash the body, wrap it in clean cloth, weight it so it would sink, and commit it to the ocean with prayers.

Some cultures believed that sailors who died at sea couldn't rest peacefully because they weren't buried in the ground. This made long voyages even scarier — you were risking not just your life but your soul.

Merchant ships often carried extra money to send back to the families of crew members who died. A good captain would record the names of every lost sailor and make sure their families received their share of any profits from the voyage.

Sinbad mentions this custom in his stories — he feels responsible for the men who died on his voyages and supports their families afterward.

💡 The Lesson

Death was a constant risk for sailors in Sinbad's time. The customs around burial and supporting families show that maritime cultures took these risks seriously and tried to honor those who didn't make it home.

✏️ Grammar

Week 20 Focus: Past Tense Verbs: Regular and Irregular

📚 What You're Learning:

Regular past tense verbs add -ed (walked, jumped, played). Irregular past tense verbs change form in different ways (go→went, think→thought, find→found). Sinbad's stories are told in past tense because they already happened.

✨ Examples:

REGULAR: I walked around the dome. I climbed the tallest tree.

IRREGULAR: The Roc flew into the sky. I held on. The bird descended.

IRREGULAR: I found a shady spot and fell asleep. The ship left without me.
