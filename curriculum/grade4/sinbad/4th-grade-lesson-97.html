📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Fifth Voyage: The Old Man of the Sea

On the fifth voyage, said Sinbad, I made the worst mistake of my life. I was kind to someone who did not deserve it. I know that sounds wrong. Let me explain. We landed on a beautiful island to take on fresh water. While exploring the island I found an old man sitting at the edge of a stream. He was very old, very frail, very thin. His clothes were rags. His eyes were large and sad. "Can you help me?" he said. "I am very weak. I need to cross this stream. I cannot do it alone. If you carry me on your shoulders to the other side, I will be most grateful." He was old. He was clearly harmless. I felt sorry for him. I crouched down and he climbed on my back. I crossed the stream. I tried to let him down on the other side. He would not let go. His legs, which had appeared too weak to walk with, wrapped around my neck and chest with the strength of iron bands. His arms gripped my head. I pulled at his legs. I bent forward trying to shake him off. I spun around. He held on. He was not going to let go. I understood this within the first few minutes. He stayed on my back. I had to walk with him on my back. I had to find food for him, eat food myself, sleep with him still holding on — he slept too, but his grip did not loosen even in sleep. If I fell down from exhaustion, he kicked my sides to make me get up. This went on for weeks. My shipmates, once they understood what had happened, were sorry for me and helpless to assist. He simply could not be removed. One afternoon I found a patch of wild grapevines. The grapes were ripe. I squeezed juice from them into a large gourd shell and left it in the sun. After a few days, the juice had fermented into wine. I drank some. I felt better. Warmer. Lighter. He wanted some. I gave him the gourd. He drank deeply. His grip loosened. He laughed — a strange, rusty laugh — and drank more. The wine was strong and he was old and small. His arms went slack. I threw him off my back. I ran. I did not look back. My shipmates found me on the beach, thinner than when I had left, with marks on my neck where his legs had held. I told them what had happened. The captain was not surprised. "The Old Man of the Sea," he said. "Everyone on these islands knows to avoid him. You are the first person I have ever heard of who got free." We sailed away quickly, before he woke up. The lesson of the fifth voyage, said Sinbad, is that kindness is good and important, but wisdom is knowing who actually needs your help and who is simply very good at appearing to need it. I should have asked more questions before crouching down. A genuine old man who cannot cross a stream cannot also hold on through weeks of struggling. He shook his head. A very long month. Hindbad, who had been holding his own neck sympathetically, nodded. ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

Survival Skills: How Sailors Stayed Alive on Long Voyages

Fresh water was the biggest challenge on long sea voyages. Ships carried large clay jars of water in the hold, but the water would often go bad (turn green and smelly) after a few weeks.

Sailors ate a lot of dried and preserved foods: salted fish, dried meat (called jerky), hard biscuits (ship's biscuit or hardtack), dates, and nuts. Fresh food was a luxury only available near land.

Scurvy (a disease caused by lack of vitamin C) killed many sailors on long voyages. Sailors didn't know this was caused by not eating fresh fruits and vegetables — they just knew that after months at sea, men would get sick, their teeth would fall out, and many would die.

Fresh water sources on islands were precious. Sailors would go ashore to refill water jars from streams or wells. This is why Sinbad and his crew often landed on islands — they needed fresh water and food.

Navigation was done by watching the stars, sun, and moon. Expert sailors memorized the position of dozens of stars and could tell their location by which stars were visible each night.

💡 The Lesson

Every voyage Sinbad survived required knowledge of sailing, weather, navigation, and survival. The dangers were real, and many sailors died from causes that had nothing to do with monsters — just the harsh realities of life at sea.

🎨 Language Skills

Week 20 Focus: Idioms and Figurative Language

📚 What You're Learning:

An idiom is a phrase that means something different from what the words actually say. 'Break your back' doesn't mean your back literally breaks — it means working very hard.

✨ Examples:

Hindbad was breaking his back carrying heavy loads. (idiom = working extremely hard)

I sat in the dust and said things about the unfairness of the world. (figurative way of saying he complained)

The sea cost me everything. (figurative — not money, but danger and hardship)
