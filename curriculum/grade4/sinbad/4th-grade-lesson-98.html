📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

The Sixth Voyage: The River Under the Mountain

The sixth voyage began with a shipwreck, said Sinbad. This was not unusual at this point in my sailing career. What was unusual was where I ended up. Our ship hit rocks in a bad storm and came apart. Those of us who survived made it to the shore of an island. The island had cliffs on all sides — high, steep, impossible to climb. The beach was narrow. There was no way off. Let me tell you about this beach. It was covered in things from shipwrecks. Decades of shipwrecks. Centuries of shipwrecks. Timbers, ropes, anchors, chests, barrels, rigging, broken masts. Every ship that had ever hit those rocks in those waters had ended up on that beach. And everyone who had survived those shipwrecks had also ended up on that beach. The other survivors were there when I arrived. Old survivors. Very old. Too weak to do much besides point at things when I asked questions. There were bones too. Many, many bones. I understood the situation. I had arrived in a place people came to but did not leave. I spent several days thinking and exploring. The island had a river that came out of the mountain, ran across the beach, and disappeared back into the mountain through a cave. A river has a source. A river has a place it goes. Rivers meet the sea eventually. I built a raft from shipwreck timber. I loaded it with the best of the goods scattered on the beach — there was treasure there, from all those lost ships and lost merchants. I lashed myself to the raft so I would not fall off in the dark. I pushed off into the cave. The river pulled me in. It was dark. Completely dark. I could hear the water and feel the walls close on both sides and the ceiling low overhead. I could not see my hands. I held on. The river moved fast. I hit my head on the ceiling twice and decided to lie flat and stay flat. The raft bounced and spun. At some point — I do not know how long; it could have been hours — I fell asleep from sheer exhaustion. I woke up in daylight. I was drifting on a wide river in the sunshine. Trees on both banks. Birds in the trees. A road visible through the trees. A city on the horizon. Farmers saw me floating past and pulled my raft to the bank. They asked where I had come from. I pointed at the mountain behind me. They spoke to each other in low voices and gave me very concerned looks. I was in the kingdom of Serendib, which is one of the most beautiful places in the world. The king heard about a merchant who had come through the mountain river — which was considered impossible — and summoned me to his court. I told him my story. He gave me gifts. I gave him gifts from what I had salvaged. I went home rich and grateful and absolutely certain I would never go to sea again. You know how that turned out, said Sinbad with a smile. Come back tomorrow. Hindbad shook his head slowly. "Six voyages. And you went a seventh time." "The seventh time," said Sinbad, "was actually the king of Serendib's fault. But that is tomorrow's story." ~ ~ ~

📰 Informational Text

Learn About the Real Arabian World

Merchant Networks: How Trade Really Worked

Medieval trade wasn't like going to a store today. Merchants formed networks of trust across thousands of miles. If you met another merchant in a distant port, you might do business based only on reputation and word of honor.

Most merchants specialized in certain trade routes or types of goods. Some focused on spices from India, others on silk from China, others on ivory from Africa. Sinbad seems to have traded in many different goods.

Merchants often formed partnerships or caravans for safety. One merchant might provide the ship, another the goods, another the local connections in a foreign port. They'd split the profits (and the risks).

Credit and loans were common. A merchant might borrow money to buy goods, sail to another city, sell the goods, and return to pay back the loan with profit. If the ship sank or was captured by pirates, the merchant lost everything and went into debt.

Caravanserais (inns for travelers) existed along major trade routes. Merchants could rest, store goods, meet contacts, and hear news from other travelers. These were crucial meeting points for trade networks.

💡 The Lesson

Sinbad's success wasn't just about surviving dangerous adventures — it was about understanding how trade networks worked, building trust with other merchants, and knowing where to buy low and sell high.

✏️ Grammar

Week 20 Focus: Past Tense Verbs: Regular and Irregular

📚 What You're Learning:

Regular past tense verbs add -ed (walked, jumped, played). Irregular past tense verbs change form in different ways (go→went, think→thought, find→found). Sinbad's stories are told in past tense because they already happened.

✨ Examples:

REGULAR: I walked around the dome. I climbed the tallest tree.

IRREGULAR: The Roc flew into the sky. I held on. The bird descended.

IRREGULAR: I found a shady spot and fell asleep. The ship left without me.
