📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

A Night Off: Hindbad Asks Questions

On the seventh night Sinbad did not tell a story. Instead he said: you have been listening for six nights and eating my food every night, Hindbad, and I have done all the talking. Tonight I would like to know what you think. Hindbad set down his cup. "About what?" "About the voyages. About luck and skill and whether my fortune was earned or given." Hindbad considered this. He was a thoughtful man, though he rarely got to show it because carrying loads did not leave much time for thinking. "The whale island," he said. "That was luck. Bad luck first, then good luck. You did nothing wrong and almost died, and then you did nothing special and were found." "That's fair," said Sinbad. "The diamond valley — that was you. You thought of the turban and the meat and you did it. Another man would have sat down and despaired." "Many men did exactly that. Their bones are in that valley." "The giant's island was you again. The stakes. The rafts. You thought of those things and organized the others." "I did. Though I lost eight men doing it." "The fourth voyage — the burial chamber — that was mostly you. Listening in the dark. Following the crack." "And luck that there was a crack," said Sinbad. "And luck that there was a ship in the bay." "Everything is partly luck," said Hindbad. "But luck only helps if you are still alive and still thinking when it arrives. And you always were." He paused. "The old man of the sea — that was bad luck and a mistake." "Definitely a mistake," said Sinbad. "I was too kind and not careful enough. I have regretted it." "The river under the mountain — you made that path. You built the raft. You went into the dark." "Yes." Hindbad looked at his hands — the porter's hands, calloused and broad. "So the answer is: some luck, a great deal of thought, and a willingness to do the frightening thing when there is no other option." "And the willingness to go back," said Sinbad. "After everything. To load the goods and find the ship and go again." "Why?" said Hindbad. "After six voyages like those — why do it again?" Sinbad was quiet for a long time. "The sea asks you a question every voyage," he said finally. "Can you solve this? Can you survive this? Can you find the way out of this particular impossible situation? Most men never hear that question. Most men live and die in one city on one street and never know what they are capable of." "And you needed to know." "I could not rest until I did," said Sinbad. "I still cannot explain it better than that." Hindbad nodded slowly. He thought he understood. "Tomorrow," said Sinbad, "the seventh voyage. And then you will know the whole story." ~ ~ ~

🎨 Language Skills

Week 20 Focus: Idioms and Figurative Language

📚 What You're Learning:

An idiom is a phrase that means something different from what the words actually say. 'Break your back' doesn't mean your back literally breaks — it means working very hard.

✨ Examples:

Hindbad was breaking his back carrying heavy loads. (idiom = working extremely hard)

I sat in the dust and said things about the unfairness of the world. (figurative way of saying he complained)

The sea cost me everything. (figurative — not money, but danger and hardship)
