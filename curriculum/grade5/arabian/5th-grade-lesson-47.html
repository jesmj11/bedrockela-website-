The Ebony Horse • Part 1 of 4

Three inventors came to the Persian king's festival, each with something extraordinary. The first brought a golden peacock that spread its tail and marked the hours. The second brought a trumpet that sounded by itself when enemies approached the gates. The third — an old Indian craftsman with quiet eyes and the patient manner of someone who has made one very good thing and knows it — brought a horse. It was carved from black ebony wood, inlaid with gold and set with jewels, life-size, perfect in every detail. The most beautiful object in the room. The king asked what it could do. "This horse," said the craftsman, "can carry a rider anywhere in the world. Through the air, faster than any bird. The peg on the right shoulder sends it upward. The peg on the left brings it down." The king's son, Prince Firouz Shah, had been watching with the bright eyes of someone who has already made a decision and is just waiting for permission to act on it. Before anyone could discuss further, he stepped forward and asked to try it. "There is a third control—" the craftsman began. The prince had already turned the right-hand peg. The horse rose straight into the air. The court watched, open-mouthed, as prince and horse climbed into the sky, shot forward over the city walls, and vanished. Everyone turned slowly to look at the craftsman. "There is a third control?" the king said. "A small knob inside the horse's mane," the craftsman said carefully. "It must be engaged to descend properly. Without it, the left peg only slows the horse. The rider needs to know to reach inside the mane." There was a silence. High above the earth, Firouz Shah was discovering this problem in real time. He was thrilled at first — the wind rushing past, the earth spread out below in patches of brown and green, the city already small as a game tile. He could see mountains he had only read about. He could see the sea. He tried the left peg. The horse slowed. It did not descend. He pushed further. It slowed more. He reversed. It shot upward. Firouz Shah had been trained well. His tutors had drilled one principle above all: when frightened, think. He worked through the horse's controls systematically, trying each peg in each direction, noting every response. He discovered that the right peg had variable pressure — not just on or off, but degrees. Perhaps the left peg did too. He searched the carved mane with his fingers, methodically, section by section. There was a small knob, perfectly smooth, almost impossible to find unless you were looking. He pressed it. The horse responded. He brought it gently down. He had landed in a garden of a palace he didn't recognize. It was night — he had traveled far enough to leave the daylight behind. He secured the horse, found the lock mechanism, explored the garden. He was in Bengal. The garden belonged to the king of Bengal. And in a pavilion at the garden's edge, he found the king's daughter, Princess Shams al-Nahar, asleep in the warm night air. He should have left. He knew this with perfect clarity. He was a stranger in a foreign kingdom with no invitation and no explanation for how he had arrived, and waking the princess up was not a reasonable next step. He woke her up anyway. She was not frightened — she was curious, which told him immediately that she was someone worth talking to. He told her about the horse. She told him about Bengal. They talked until the sun came up, and when he finally left, he had promised to come back. He came back. Several times. He brought her a gift each time. He was falling in love with the particular carefulness that falls over people when they realize they have found something they don't want to lose. Her father found out. He objected, strenuously, in the way that kings object to things — which is to say with armed guards. The guards had not seen the ebony horse. Firouz Shah put the princess on the horse and turned the right peg. They arrived in Persia. His father the king was relieved to have his son back and delighted to meet the princess and approximately three seconds away from arranging a wonderful wedding when the old craftsman appeared and complicated everything. He wanted his payment — the princess, which had been the original deal he had proposed for the horse, before Firouz Shah had launched himself into the sky without hearing the terms. The king, who had forgotten entirely about this arrangement, had to admit the craftsman had a point. The craftsman did not get the princess. What he got was a dungeon, because the king was also a king and kings have options. The princess went to a comfortable wing of the palace to rest. The craftsman, from his dungeon, sent word to the king of Bengal that his daughter was in Persia. The king of Bengal arrived with an army, as fathers tend to do in these circumstances. Firouz Shah put the princess back on the horse, told her exactly how all three controls worked, and suggested she might enjoy a brief flight while the adults sorted out the army situation. She landed the horse perfectly on the first try. She had paid attention. The army situation resolved itself when everyone agreed that the prince and princess should simply get married, which had been the obvious solution from approximately the first night they'd talked until sunrise in her father's garden. The ebony horse stood in the palace courtyard. Both of them knew how it worked now. They went on many journeys together, to places neither of them had seen, landing in gardens and on hilltops and once, memorably, on the deck of a ship full of extremely surprised merchants. The craftsman eventually got a pardon and a considerable amount of money, which was more useful than the princess anyway. Curiosity, the horse seemed to say, standing in the courtyard with its jeweled eyes and its three controls — curiosity is good. Curiosity without preparation is how you end up very high in the air with no way down. Know where the third control is before you turn the first peg.

Reading Comprehension

💭 Understanding the Story

Answer these questions about "The Ebony Horse":

Question 1

Firouz Shah turned the first peg before he heard about the third control — and spent a terrifying time very high in the air with no way down. The story ends with the line: 'Know where the third control is before you turn the first peg.' Can you think of a time when acting before you had all the information caused a problem? What did you learn from it?

Question 2

When Firouz Shah was stuck high in the air and panicking, his training kicked in: when frightened, think. He worked through the horse's controls one by one, methodically, until he found the solution. How is that different from what most people would do in a scary situation? Why is staying calm and thinking so difficult — and so important?

Question 3

The princess, Shams al-Nahar, listened carefully when Firouz Shah explained all three controls, and landed the horse perfectly on her first try. She had paid attention. What does it say about her character that she listened so carefully? How is paying attention a kind of power?

Informational Text

📰 The Spice Trade: Treasures from the East

Black pepper, cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg, ginger — these spices were once worth more than gold. In medieval Europe, a pound of saffron cost as much as a horse. A ship full of spices could make a merchant wealthy for life.

Why were spices so valuable? They preserved food before refrigeration existed. They flavored food in an age when fresh ingredients were seasonal. They made medicine taste better. And they demonstrated wealth and status — only the rich could afford meals flavored with Eastern spices.

Most spices came from specific places: black pepper from India's Malabar Coast, cinnamon from Sri Lanka, cloves and nutmeg from Indonesia's Spice Islands. Arabian merchants controlled the trade routes, buying spices in Eastern ports and selling them at enormous profit in Mediterranean markets.

The spice trade shaped history. European explorers like Columbus and Vasco da Gama searched for sea routes to bypass Arabian middlemen. This quest for spices led to the 'discovery' of the Americas and the European colonization of Asia.

Today, we can buy any spice cheaply at the grocery store. But for thousands of years, these fragrant treasures traveled thousands of miles, passed through countless hands, and changed the course of civilizations.
