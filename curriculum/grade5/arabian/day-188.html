Ali Cogia, the Merchant of Baghdad • Part 1 of 4

He had saved one thousand gold pieces over many years. He wanted to make the pilgrimage to Mecca — the great journey every person of faith hopes to make once in a lifetime — but he couldn't take his savings on the road. Bandits. He needed somewhere safe. He had a large clay jar in his shop. He put the gold at the bottom, covered it with olives — a jar of olives attracts less interest than a jar of gold — sealed it, and carried it to his neighbor's house. Hagi Hassan was a man he had known for twenty years. A merchant. Respected. The kind of man you trust with a jar of olives because you have known him for twenty years. "Keep this for me until I return," Ali Cogia said. Hagi Hassan agreed without hesitation and stored the jar without giving it another thought. At home, Hagi Hassan had not thought about the jar in years. Then one evening his wife wanted olives for dinner and the market was closed. He remembered the jar. He opened it expecting olives past their best. He found gold. A thousand pieces of it, under a layer of dried olives. Hagi Hassan sat on the floor of his shop with a thousand gold pieces around him and had a very long conversation with himself. He was a respected man. He was a trusted neighbor. He had known Ali Cogia for twenty years. He took the gold. He refilled the jar with fresh olives, resealed it, and put it back exactly where it had been. He told himself, several times, that if Ali Cogia came back he would simply say he'd known nothing about any gold. He told himself this until it almost felt true. "Of course!" Hagi Hassan said warmly. "Here it is, exactly as you left it." "There should be a thousand gold pieces at the bottom," he said. Hagi Hassan widened his eyes in perfect surprise. "Gold? But you said it was olives! I stored a jar of olives! I know nothing about gold!" The Cadi was troubled. He could not decide. But outside the judge's window, some children were playing a game in the street. One of them — a boy of perhaps ten — had set himself up as a judge, with two other children playing the merchant and the neighbor. He had heard the case being talked about in the market, the way children absorb the conversations of adults, and was now holding his own trial. The pretend judge called for a jar of olives. He had the pretend merchant describe how old the olives were when the jar was sealed. He had someone taste the olives currently in the jar. He asked how long olives lasted before going bad and whether seven-year-old olives would taste like the fresh ones in the jar. The real Cadi had been watching from his window. He called the boy inside. The boy explained his reasoning without being the least bit intimidated, which takes either courage or the particular fearlessness of someone who doesn't fully understand they should be nervous. The Cadi summoned experts — olive merchants, traders, people who knew their olives — and asked the question the child had asked: could olives in a sealed jar remain fresh for seven years? They could not. The olives in the jar were fresh. The olives should have been seven years old. Hagi Hassan, who had thought he was very clever, had not considered that olives have a memory. He confessed. He returned the gold. He received the judgment that went with theft and betrayal of trust in a city that took trust seriously. The boy went back to playing in the street. The lesson is not only that justice can come from unexpected places. It is also this: the simplest question — asked without agenda, just to understand — can see through the most careful lie. Children ask simple questions. Adults would do well to keep that habit.

Reading Comprehension

💭 Understanding the Story

Answer these questions about "Ali Cogia, the Merchant of Baghdad":

Question 1

Hagi Hassan was a respected man who had been Ali Cogia's trusted neighbor for twenty years — and he stole from him anyway. The story says he told himself the lie until he almost believed it. Have you ever convinced yourself that something wrong was okay? What made it feel justified — and what made it still wrong?

Question 2

The Cadi — the judge — couldn't solve the case. A ten-year-old child playing a pretend trial in the street found the answer by asking a simple question about olives. What does that tell us about how we solve hard problems? Why might a child sometimes see something clearly that adults miss?

Question 3

Ali Cogia trusted his neighbor with his life savings because he had known him for twenty years. Hagi Hassan betrayed that trust the moment it was profitable to do so. What does it mean to truly trust someone — and what does this story suggest about how well we actually know the people around us?

Informational Text

📰 The Scholar's Life in Medieval Baghdad

In 9th-century Baghdad, education was prized above almost everything else. Wealthy families hired tutors for their children. Poor families sent sons to mosque schools where they learned to read, write, and memorize sacred texts — all for free.

Bright students could continue their education at advanced academies called madrasas, where they studied mathematics, astronomy, medicine, law, philosophy, and literature. The best students received scholarships and living expenses. Education wasn't just for the rich.

Scholars often specialized in multiple fields. A physician might also be a mathematician and a poet. An astronomer might study philosophy and law. Knowledge was seen as connected — understanding one field helped illuminate others.

The House of Wisdom in Baghdad employed scholars to translate important works from Greek, Persian, Sanskrit, and other languages into Arabic. They didn't just copy — they corrected errors, added commentary, and built on earlier ideas. Original manuscripts that would have been lost to history survived because Arabian scholars preserved them.

Scholars debated in the caliphs' courts, wrote letters to colleagues across the empire, and taught students who would carry their ideas forward. Their writings traveled along trade routes, eventually reaching European universities. Modern science, mathematics, and medicine build on foundations these scholars laid over a thousand years ago.
