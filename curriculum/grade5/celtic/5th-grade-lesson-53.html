📚 Reading (Part 1 of 4)

Taliesin, the Greatest Bard

Ceridwen loved her son. That is where this story starts, and it matters.

Her son's name was Morfran, and she could see clearly — because she was a powerful enchantress and saw things clearly — that the world was not going to be kind to him. He was not handsome, not charming, not gifted in any of the ways people reward. He would struggle his whole life unless she did something about it.

She decided to give him wisdom. Real wisdom — the deep, undeniable kind that cannot be dismissed or overlooked.

She began to brew it in a great cauldron by the edge of a lake. The recipe required exactly one year and one day of continuous stirring, with the right herbs added at exactly the right times, the cauldron watched without a single moment's pause. At the very end, three drops of liquid would hold all the power of everything brewed: three drops of total wisdom, for her son.

She hired a farm boy named Gwion Bach to stir while she gathered herbs and slept. He was entirely ordinary — the kind of boy no one notices, which is sometimes useful and sometimes not. He stirred faithfully for nearly a year, understanding nothing about what he was making.

On the last day, three drops of liquid leaped from the cauldron and landed on Gwion's hand.

Without thinking — the way these things always happen — he put his hand to his mouth.

The world shifted.

He knew everything the potion had been brewed to contain. He understood the past and glimpsed the future, and the first thing the future showed him was Ceridwen's face when she came back.

📚 Reading (Part 2 of 4)

Taliesin, the Greatest Bard

Ceridwen returned and knew immediately. The rest of the liquid was poison now — the three drops of power had been the heart of it, and without them the cauldron cracked and spilled. She turned her rage in the direction Gwion had gone and gave chase.

What followed has no equal in Welsh mythology.

Gwion turned himself into a hare. Ceridwen became a greyhound.

He turned himself into a fish and leapt into the river. She became an otter.

He turned himself into a bird and rose into the air. She became a hawk.

He looked down at a threshing floor covered in grain and turned himself into a single kernel among thousands. She landed, became a black hen, and began to work through the pile systematically.

She swallowed him.

Nine months later, Ceridwen gave birth to a child. The child was Gwion, reborn — and he was so extraordinary to look upon that she found she couldn't bring herself to destroy him, though she had fully intended to. She wrapped him in a leather bag and set him in the water to float wherever the current chose.

The bag was found on the first of May — a lucky day — by a young man named Elphin, the unluckiest person in Wales. He had gone to check his father's fish trap, which was always empty, and found it empty again. He was beginning to suspect the universe had a personal interest in his misfortune.

📚 Reading (Part 3 of 4)

Taliesin, the Greatest Bard

He opened the leather bag floating beside the trap.

There was a baby inside, with a shining, remarkable forehead.

"What a radiant brow," Elphin said, which is a reasonable thing to say when you find a baby where you expected fish.

"Taliesin," the baby said clearly.

Elphin stared at the baby. The baby looked back at him with ancient, completely untroubled eyes.

Elphin brought him home. Taliesin grew quickly, strangely, in the way of magical things — and from the beginning he could do what no one else could. Not just sing, though he sang beautifully. He could make words do the thing that only the very best words can do: reach inside the listener and find what was truest in them and give it shape. He sang of the beginning of the world and the nature of stars and the feeling of grief and the fact that spring always returns, and everyone who heard him felt that the words had already been living somewhere inside them, waiting for someone to say them.

When Elphin fell into trouble — as the unlucky reliably do — and was imprisoned by a king who thought him worthless, Taliesin went to court to argue his case.

He was a boy walking into a great hall full of the king's finest poets and scholars. He walked in as though he'd been expected, sat in front of the king, and waited.

The court poets, annoyed, tried to mock him. He answered in verse so precise and so honest that they couldn't find a handhold to argue back. He made his foster father's case in a poem so moving and so cleverly constructed that the king, despite himself, could not find the argument wrong.

📚 Reading (Part 4 of 4)

Taliesin, the Greatest Bard

Elphin was released before sunset.

Taliesin became the greatest bard Wales ever knew. His poems were considered so powerful that people copied them carefully and guarded the copies for centuries, and some of them survive today — you can read them, if you want; they are still there. He was welcomed at the courts of kings not because he flattered them but because he told the truth, and truth told beautifully is one of the rarest things in any world.

He remembered everything. He remembered being Gwion Bach, the nobody farm boy who stirred a pot he didn't understand. He remembered the chase — the hare, the fish, the bird, the grain. He remembered the nine months of waiting in the dark, and the cold water, and Elphin's surprised hands opening the leather bag.

He never pretended the farm boy wasn't part of who he was.

The greatest bard in Wales had started as the boy no one noticed.

He always said that was the part that mattered most.

📰 Informational Text

The Bards of Celtic Britain

Bards were professional poets and musicians in Celtic society, and they held enormous power. Unlike entertainers today, bards were highly educated, politically important, and sometimes feared. They studied for years learning poetry, history, music, and law. A skilled bard could memorize hundreds of poems and genealogies (family histories) going back generations. Kings employed bards to record their deeds, praise their victories, and preserve their family histories. But bards could also satirize — write cruel poems mocking someone — and a well-crafted satire could ruin a king's reputation. Some believed a bard's curse could cause physical harm. Because of their power over reputation and memory, bards had to be treated with respect even by the most powerful rulers.

Taliesin was a real historical figure — a Welsh bard who lived around 550 CE and wrote poems that survive today. But he also became a legendary figure with magical origins, and the story of Ceridwen's cauldron was added later to explain his extraordinary talent. The chase sequence where Gwion and Ceridwen change shapes repeatedly is called a 'transformation chase' and appears in myths worldwide. Each animal represents different qualities: hare for speed, fish for adaptability, bird for freedom, grain for humility. Ceridwen swallowing Gwion and giving birth to Taliesin represents transformation and rebirth — the idea that true wisdom destroys your old self and creates someone new. The greatest poems attributed to Taliesin claim he has existed since the beginning of time and witnessed everything, which makes sense if you've gained all knowledge from a magical potion. His story teaches that talent plus opportunity can come from unexpected places.
