📚 Reading (Part 1 of 3)

Chapter 4: Escape

I decided I had to escape. I could wait no longer.

I had noticed that the Count sometimes climbed the outer wall of the castle — headfirst, like a lizard, moving with horrible ease along the sheer stone face. He seemed to have powers far beyond those of any human being.

But the wall was the only way out that was not locked. I waited until I was sure the Count was away from that side of the castle, took a deep breath, and climbed out the window.

The stone was old and rough, with plenty of handholds. I am a reasonably capable climber. I made it down and found a door at the base of the wall, hidden by ivy. It opened onto a long passage cut into the rock.

📚 Reading (Part 2 of 3)

Chapter 4: Escape

At the end of the passage, I found something terrible.

A large cellar. And in the cellar, boxes — dozens of them, filled with fresh dark earth. And in one of the boxes, the Count himself. Lying perfectly still. His eyes were closed, but his skin had a warm color to it, as if he had recently — fed.

I ran.

I did not find a way out of the outer walls that night. I ended up back in my room, shaking, and lay awake until dawn.

📚 Reading (Part 3 of 3)

Chapter 4: Escape

Over the following days, I observed the Count carefully and kept making attempts to find a way out. I saw him loading those boxes onto a wagon through a back gate, late at night. Whatever he was planning in England, those boxes were part of it.

Finally, one morning before dawn, I found a window on the far side of the castle that had no lock. The drop was steep, but there was a gutter pipe that reached almost to the ground. I tied my journal carefully in oilcloth and tucked it inside my coat.

Then I climbed, and dropped, and ran.

I ran until the castle was out of sight. I did not stop running for a very long time.

📰 Informational Text

Connect Story to Reality

Ancient Vampire Legends: Before Dracula

Vampires didn't start with Bram Stoker's Dracula. Legends of blood-drinking creatures have existed in nearly every culture throughout human history — long before anyone wrote them down in novels.

In ancient Mesopotamia (modern-day Iraq), around 4,000 years ago, people feared a demon called Lilitu who attacked sleeping people and drank their blood. Ancient Assyrian clay tablets describe rituals to protect against her. Similar creatures appear in Persian, Hebrew, and Greek mythology.

Eastern European folklore had the most developed vampire traditions. In places like Romania, Serbia, and Poland, people believed that certain dead people — those who died violently, committed suicide, or were buried improperly — could rise from their graves at night. These creatures were called different names: 'strigoi' in Romania, 'vrykolakas' in Greece, 'upir' in Russia.

These folklore vampires were quite different from Stoker's elegant Count Dracula. They were described as bloated, dark-skinned corpses with blood on their mouths. They didn't dress in fine clothes or live in castles. They returned to their own villages to attack family and neighbors, spreading disease and death.

To protect against vampires, people used garlic, holy symbols, and certain woods like hawthorn. They believed sunlight, running water, and religious faith could repel them. Some villages dug up suspected vampires and drove wooden stakes through their hearts, or cut off their heads, or burned the bodies — practices that seem shocking now but made sense in a time when people couldn't explain why plagues spread through communities.

Many vampire legends probably came from misunderstanding decomposition. Before people understood what happens to bodies after death, natural processes — gases causing bloating, blood around the mouth, skin darkening, nails appearing to grow — looked supernatural and frightening.

Other cases may have been rare diseases like porphyria (which causes sensitivity to sunlight) or rabies (which causes fear of water and aggressive behavior). Frightened communities, lacking scientific explanations, created stories to make sense of what they saw.

Stoker took these ancient, scattered folklore traditions and wove them into one compelling character: a vampire who was sophisticated, intelligent, powerful, and terrifying in a completely new way.

✏️ Language Skills

Week 16 Focus: Greek and Latin Roots: 'Trans-' and 'Morph'

📚 What You're Learning:

Many English words come from Greek and Latin roots. 'Trans-' means 'across' or 'beyond' (Transylvania = beyond the forest). 'Morph' means 'form' or 'shape' (metamorphosis = change in form).

✨ Examples:

Transport = carry across, Transform = change form across states

Transfusion = transfer (of blood) across from one person to another

Morphology = study of forms and shapes
