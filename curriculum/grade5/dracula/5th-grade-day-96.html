We approached. The great door, as Jonathan had described, was unlocked from outside — meant to keep people in, not out. Van Helsing and I went through it.

Inside, the castle was cold and empty. Our footsteps echoed on stone floors. Van Helsing moved through each room methodically, examining everything, leaving holy water at every doorway and window.

In the cellar, we found what we were looking for.

Three boxes. Three of the boxes that the creature women at the castle used as resting places.

Van Helsing was thorough and gentle and respectful in his work, even here. He treated each box with care, speaking quietly, as though performing a religious ceremony.

📰 Informational Text

Connect Story to Reality

The Role of Faith: Religion in Dracula

Christian religious symbols — crucifixes, holy water, communion wafers — are powerful weapons against Dracula. But the novel's treatment of faith is more complex and interesting than just 'good magic versus bad magic.' What is Stoker really saying about religion's role in fighting evil?

First, it's worth noting that Stoker was Irish Protestant, writing in Victorian England where religion was central to social life and moral identity. Church attendance was expected; religious doubt was controversial. The novel reflects this context but also questions it in subtle ways.

Professor Van Helsing is the most explicitly religious character. He's Dutch Catholic (unusual in a predominantly Protestant British story), and he explains vampire lore through Christian theology. He believes Dracula is a 'creature of the devil,' corrupted soul trapped between life and death. The religious weapons work, he suggests, because they represent sacred power against unholy evil.

But Van Helsing also values scientific observation, medical knowledge, and rational investigation. He doesn't just pray — he experiments, records data, and develops strategy. His faith and his science work together; he sees no contradiction between them.

This dual approach reflected Victorian debates about faith and science. Darwin's theory of evolution (published in 1859) had challenged literal biblical interpretation. Geological discoveries showed Earth was far older than biblical timelines suggested. Many Victorians struggled to reconcile religious faith with scientific evidence. Van Helsing represents a solution: using both faith and reason, seeing them as complementary rather than opposed.

The religious symbols in the novel work through belief and will as much as inherent power. When characters hold crucifixes with genuine faith, they're effective. When they're used mechanically without understanding or conviction, they're less potent. This suggests that it's the spiritual commitment behind the symbol, not just the object itself, that matters.

Interestingly, Dracula himself has religious knowledge. He's lived for centuries in a Christian culture. He knows what the symbols mean and why they repel him. He's bound by religious rules — unable to enter homes without invitation, unable to approach the sacred Host, weakened by holy ground. His evil is defined in relation to the sacred; he's not an alien force but a corruption of the religious order itself.

The novel also shows faith as community. The prayers, the shared rituals, the mutual support — these bind the heroes together and give them strength. When they sanctify Lucy's tomb or surround Mina with protective symbols, they're not just performing magic; they're affirming shared values and commitment to each other.

Modern readers from various religious backgrounds (or none) might interpret the religious elements differently. Some see them as metaphorical — representing moral clarity, courage, and the power of good versus evil. Others read them literally, as Stoker probably intended. Either way, the novel suggests that what you believe in — what you're willing to sacrifice for and stand against evil for — matters deeply.

✏️ Grammar

Week 19 Focus: Using Commas in Complex Sentences

📚 What You're Learning:

When a dependent clause comes BEFORE the independent clause, use a comma to separate them. When it comes AFTER, usually no comma is needed. Example: 'Although Jonathan was frightened, he continued forward.' vs 'Jonathan continued forward although he was frightened.'

✨ Examples:

When the Count appeared in the doorway, Jonathan felt a chill run down his spine.

Jonathan felt a chill run down his spine when the Count appeared in the doorway.

Because Mina organized all the records, the group could see patterns they had missed before.
