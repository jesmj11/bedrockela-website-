📰 Informational Text

The Egyptian Concept of Ma'at

The battle between Horus and Set represents more than just a fight for power — it represents the Egyptian concept of ma'at versus chaos. Ma'at was one of the most important ideas in ancient Egypt, meaning truth, justice, balance, and cosmic order. The goddess Ma'at was depicted with an ostrich feather, which would later be used to weigh human souls in the afterlife. Egyptians believed that keeping ma'at was essential for the world to function properly. When pharaohs were crowned, they swore to uphold ma'at, which meant ruling justly, telling the truth, maintaining order, and keeping Egypt in balance. Set represented isfet — chaos, disorder, violence — but even isfet had a purpose. The Egyptians understood that a world without any chaos would be weak and stagnant.

The Eye of Horus, which Set tore out during their battles, became one of the most powerful symbols in Egyptian culture. Called the wedjat, it represented healing, protection, and wholeness. Egyptians wore it as jewelry, painted it on ships and buildings, and placed it in tombs to protect the dead. The eye had mathematical significance too — different parts of the symbol represented fractions that ancient Egyptian doctors used in measuring medicines. The story of Horus defeating Set but not destroying him shows Egyptian wisdom: strong leaders need opposition to stay sharp, and chaos in small doses keeps order from becoming tyranny. Every pharaoh who ruled Egypt for three thousand years believed they were the living Horus, and when they died they became Osiris, continuing the eternal cycle the gods had established.
