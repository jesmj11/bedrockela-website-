📰 Informational Text

Mummification and the Egyptian Afterlife

Isis's restoration of Osiris became the model for Egyptian mummification. When the Egyptians embalmed their dead, they were recreating what Isis did for Osiris — preserving the body so the soul could use it in the afterlife. The process took seventy days and involved removing internal organs (except the heart, which was needed for judgment), drying the body with natron salt, wrapping it in linen, and performing ritual magic spells. The brain was removed through the nose and discarded because Egyptians thought thinking happened in the heart. Rich Egyptians were buried with food, furniture, servants' statues (shabtis), and detailed instructions (the Book of the Dead) to help them navigate the underworld. Poor Egyptians received simpler burials but still followed the same basic principles.

When a person died, Egyptians believed the soul split into several parts. The ka was your life force. The ba was your personality (shown as a bird with a human head). The akh was what you became if you passed Osiris's judgment. In the Hall of Ma'at, Osiris would weigh your heart against Ma'at's feather of truth. If your heart was light — meaning you lived justly — you passed into paradise (the Field of Reeds), which looked like Egypt but perfect: unlimited food, no work unless you wanted it, reunited with loved ones. If your heart was heavy with wrongdoing, the monster Ammit would devour it and you'd cease to exist. Egyptians believed this system was perfectly fair because Osiris had lived, died, and understood what it meant to be human before becoming their judge. The story of Osiris gave them hope that death wasn't the end, just a transformation.
