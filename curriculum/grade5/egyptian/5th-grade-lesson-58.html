📰 Informational Text

The Egyptian Sun God Ra

Ra (also spelled Re) was one of the most important gods in ancient Egypt. His daily journey across the sky represented order, light, and life itself. Egyptians believed Ra sailed across the sky in a solar boat called the Barque of Millions of Years. At sunset, he descended into the underworld (the Duat) and spent the night traveling through twelve hours of darkness, each hour bringing different dangers. The most dangerous was Apophis (also called Apep), a giant chaos serpent who tried to swallow Ra's boat and end the sun forever. Set, despite being a god of chaos and violence, protected Ra during this nightly journey — fighting Apophis so the sun could rise again. Every morning when the sun appeared, Egyptians celebrated Ra's victory over darkness. This daily cycle was so central to Egyptian religion that later pharaohs added 'Ra' to their names (like Amenhotep became Amenhotep-Ra).

Ra had many forms representing different times of day: Khepri (the scarab beetle) at dawn, Ra at noon, and Atum (the old man) at dusk. The Egyptians believed Ra created himself from the primordial waters of Nun by speaking his own secret name. By speaking more names, he created the other gods and all of existence — which is why names had such power in Egyptian magic. Knowing something's true name gave you power over it. Magicians and priests guarded their true names carefully and used magical nicknames in spells. Ra was sometimes shown merged with other gods: Ra-Horakhty (Ra combined with Horus), or Amun-Ra (Ra combined with the hidden god Amun of Thebes). As Egypt's political center moved from city to city, local gods would be elevated by association with Ra. The persistence of Ra worship shows how central the sun was to Egyptian life — without it, the Nile valley would be uninhabitable desert. Ra was literally life itself.
