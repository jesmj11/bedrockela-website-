Heracles and the Twelve Labors

Heracles and the Twelve Labors A Tale from Ancient Greek Mythology Heracles was the strongest man who ever lived. His father was Zeus, and the divine strength ran through him like current through wire — always present, always dangerous, capable of anything. Hera hated him for the simple fact of his existence and made his life difficult from birth. When he was eight months old, she sent two enormous serpents into his crib. They found him already awake. He strangled them both and was found in the morning playing with the bodies, which tells you everything you need to know about Heracles as an infant. He grew up and married and was genuinely happy — which was when Hera sent the madness. A cloud of confusion so total that he did not know himself or anyone around him. In that terrible fog, he caused harm to his own household. When the madness lifted and he understood what he had done, it broke something in him. He went to the Oracle at Delphi and asked what he must do. The Oracle sent him to his cousin King Eurystheus: twelve years of service, twelve labors, whatever was commanded. Eurystheus was a coward who was afraid of Heracles and designed the labors to be impossible. He was wrong about what impossible meant. The first labor: the Nemean Lion, a creature whose skin no weapon could pierce. Heracles figured this out quickly, dropped his weapons, and wrestled it to death with his bare hands. He used its own claws to skin it and wore the hide as armor forever after. The second: the Lernaean Hydra, which grew two heads for every one cut off. Heracles brought his nephew Iolaus, who cauterized each stump with a torch as Heracles cut. It took both of them. Heracles was not too proud to admit that. The third: the Ceryneian Hind, a golden-horned deer sacred to Artemis. He could not harm it. He chased it for a full year until it tired enough to catch, then carried it back alive and released it unharmed after showing it to Eurystheus, who had not specified that catching it would be sufficient but discovered it was going to have to be. The fourth: the Erymanthian Boar. Captured. The fifth: the Augean Stables — three thousand cattle, thirty years of accumulation, clean it in a day. Heracles looked at the stables and then at the two rivers nearby and diverted both of them to flow through. Done before sunset. The sixth: the Stymphalian Birds, man-eating birds with bronze feathers and beaks. He borrowed bronze castanets from Athena, crashed them together to startle the birds into the air, and shot them with arrows as they rose. The seventh through twelfth: a fire-breathing bull, the man-eating mares of Diomedes, the belt of Hippolyta queen of the Amazons, the cattle of Geryon at the edge of the world, the golden apples of the Hesperides guarded by an unsleeping dragon, and finally the three-headed dog Cerberus himself — dragged up from the underworld alive, shown to Eurystheus, and returned. Eurystheus received news of each completed labor in a large bronze jar, which he hid inside when Heracles came to report, because Eurystheus had been terrified of Heracles from the beginning and twelve successful impossibilities had not improved the situation. The twelve labors were complete. Heracles served his penance. The debt, as the Oracle had defined it, was paid. He went on to other adventures — there were always more adventures — and eventually, much later, was killed by a poisoned shirt sent by someone who thought it was a love charm. He died in agony on a funeral pyre he built himself. Zeus took him to Olympus. He became a god. He was reconciled, finally, with Hera, who gave him her daughter Hebe as wife. The Greeks told the twelve labors to show what a human being could do when pushed past every limit — not because they were easy or because Heracles was a god (he was half, which mattered), but because he kept going. The labors were designed to be impossible. He refused to agree. The Augean Stables took a day. The rivers are still there.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

Show what you understood

1. The madness Hera sent was the cause of everything — Heracles didn't choose what he did in that fog. But he still accepted responsibility and did the twelve labors to make amends. Do you think he had to? Is it possible to owe penance for something you did while you didn't know yourself?

2. One of the labors — the Hydra — took two people. Heracles needed his nephew Iolaus. What does it say about Heracles that he accepted help when he needed it, rather than insisting on doing everything alone? How is knowing when to ask for help its own kind of strength?

3. Heracles was made a god after death — reconciled even with Hera, who had made his life miserable. What do you think the Greeks were saying about the end of that story? Is there something satisfying about that resolution, or does it feel too easy? What do you think he deserved?

Week 35 Focus: Consistent Verb Tense

📚 What You're Learning:

Keep the same tense throughout unless time actually changes

✨ Examples:

CORRECT: Zeus ruled, Hera schemed, and Athena watched.

WRONG: Zeus ruled, Hera schemes, and Athena will watch.

CORRECT: After Prometheus stole fire, Zeus punished him.
