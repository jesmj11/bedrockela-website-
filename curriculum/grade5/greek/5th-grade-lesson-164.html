Pandora's Box A Tale from Ancient Greek Mythology After Prometheus gave fire to humanity, Zeus decided to send a gift of his own. He went to Hephaestus and asked him to make a woman out of clay — the first mortal woman, shaped by divine hands. Then he invited every god on Olympus to contribute something to her. Aphrodite gave her beauty. Athena gave her skill in weaving and the arts. Hermes gave her curiosity and a persuasive voice. Apollo gave her music. The Graces gave her charm. Hera gave her perseverance. The Seasons dressed her in flowers. Zeus named her Pandora — all-gifted — and gave her one more thing: a large sealed jar with very clear instructions attached to it. Do not open this. Then he sent her to earth, to be the wife of Epimetheus. Epimetheus was the brother of Prometheus, and his name meant Afterthought — because unlike his brother, who understood the future, Epimetheus understood things only after they had already happened. Prometheus had warned him: never accept a gift from Zeus. Epimetheus forgot this warning the moment he saw Pandora, which is perhaps the most Afterthought thing he ever did. They were happy together. The jar sat in the corner of their house, sealed, waiting. The problem was Hermes's gift. Curiosity is wonderful — it drives invention and discovery and the desire to understand — but it does not combine well with a sealed jar you've been told never to open. The jar was there every day. Every day Pandora walked past it. Every day the wondering grew a little larger and a little harder to ignore. What was inside? Why couldn't she open it? Zeus had given it to her — surely it was hers in some sense. Surely she had a right to know what she owned. One day she opened it. What came out was everything difficult about human life — all the things the world had not yet known: sickness, sorrow, envy, cruelty, conflict, old age, disappointment, grief, all the hard and painful things that make a human life difficult. They had been locked in the jar, and now they poured out like smoke, spreading into the world, into the air, into the future of every person who would ever live. Pandora slammed the lid down. In the bottom of the jar, still inside, was one thing that had not escaped. Hope. The Greeks argued about this for centuries, the way they argued about everything worth arguing about. Was hope left behind because Pandora caught it in time — something preserved for humanity in the disaster? Or was hope itself a kind of trap — the thing that keeps you suffering when you might otherwise give up? Was it a gift or one more complication? They never fully agreed. What they did agree on is this: Pandora was not evil. She was curious, which is one of the best things a person can be. She had been given curiosity as a gift, by a god who knew exactly what curiosity would do with a sealed jar and a prohibition. The question of who was really responsible for what got loose in the world — the woman who opened the jar, or the god who made the jar and put her within reach of it — is a question the Greeks considered worth asking. They were, in this way, honest about their gods. The jar is empty now. Everything that was in it is already out in the world. But hope is still there, in the bottom, if you look for it.
