Persephone and the Seasons

Persephone and the Seasons A Tale from Ancient Greek Mythology Demeter was the goddess of growing things — grain and harvest and the patient work of the earth — and she had one daughter named Persephone who was the whole world to her. Persephone was joyful and curious, a young goddess who moved through meadows like spring itself, flowers turning toward her as she passed. She spent her days gathering flowers with the nymphs, running through tall grass, as glad to be alive as a creature can be. One afternoon she found a narcissus she had never seen before — a hundred blossoms on a single stem, its perfume drifting across the whole meadow. She reached for it. The earth opened. From the crack in the ground rose Hades, lord of the underworld, in a chariot of black horses. He had been watching Persephone. He had gone to Zeus and asked permission to make her his queen. Zeus — who was Persephone's father, though he was better at ruling the sky than paying attention to his children — had said yes. He had not mentioned this to Demeter. Before Persephone could cry out, the earth closed over her. The meadow was empty. The nymphs had seen nothing. Demeter searched for nine days and nine nights without stopping — no food, no sleep, a torch in each hand, calling her daughter's name across every hillside and shore. The other gods would not tell her what had happened. The earth would not answer. She was entirely alone in her grief, which is the worst kind. On the tenth day she found Hecate, who had heard a scream, and Helios, the sun god, who sees everything that happens on earth from his chariot. Helios told her the truth: Hades had taken Persephone, and Zeus had known. Demeter did not rage. She did something quieter and more absolute. She left Olympus. She walked the earth disguised as an old woman and refused to do her work. She was the goddess of the harvest, and nothing would grow while her daughter was gone. The fields dried and cracked. The orchards stopped bearing fruit. The grain didn't rise. Animals had nothing to eat. People had nothing to eat. The earth was dying. Zeus sent the gods to Demeter one by one, asking her to return. She said one thing: bring back my daughter. Zeus finally sent Hermes to the underworld to retrieve Persephone. Hades agreed, with a quiet smile and the manner of someone who has already arranged the ending he wanted. Before Persephone left, she ate six pomegranate seeds. Whether she was tricked, or hungry, or understood what she was doing — the stories don't quite agree. But the rule of the underworld was clear: anyone who ate its food belonged to it. She could not stay on earth. She could not stay entirely below. The compromise that was reached — and it was a compromise, which means no one got everything they wanted — was this: Persephone would spend part of each year below with Hades, and part of each year above with her mother. When Persephone returns from the underworld, the earth bursts into spring. Demeter's joy is visible in every flower, every green shoot, every warm morning after a long winter. When Persephone must go back down, Demeter lets the world go cold. She does not abandon it entirely — the work must continue, the cycle must turn — but she grieves, and the earth grieves with her. Every autumn, the world goes golden and then bare. Every spring, it comes back. The Greeks looked at the seasons and saw a mother's love and a mother's grief, cycling through the year like breathing in and breathing out, and they thought: yes. That is exactly what it feels like. That is exactly right.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

Show what you understood

1. Zeus gave permission for Persephone to be taken without telling Demeter. He was Persephone's father. What does his choice tell you about him? How do you think a parent should balance their own decisions with the wellbeing of their child?

2. Demeter's response to grief was not rage but withdrawal — she just stopped working, and the world began to die. Why do you think the Greeks made the goddess of the harvest's grief so powerful? What does it suggest about what love looks like when it has real weight behind it?

3. The seasons exist, the Greeks said, because Persephone travels between two worlds every year. What do you think the seasons feel like to you — does the story of Demeter and Persephone change how you see them? What would it feel like to carry grief and joy on a cycle like that?

✏️ Grammar

Week 34 Focus: Active vs. Passive Voice

📚 What You're Learning:

Active: subject does the action. Passive: subject receives the action

✨ Examples:

ACTIVE: Prometheus stole the fire.

PASSIVE: The fire was stolen by Prometheus.

ACTIVE: Zeus punished the Titan.
