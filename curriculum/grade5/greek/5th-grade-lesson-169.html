📚 Reading (Part 2 of 4)

Midas and the Golden Touch

Midas and the Golden Touch A Tale from Ancient Greek Mythology King Midas was not a bad man. He was friendly and generous and well-liked by his people, and his one weakness — a love of gold, its color and weight and the way it caught light — was, as weaknesses go, not the most dangerous kind. He had a treasury full of it and was always happy to see more. One morning his servants brought him an old man they'd found asleep in the royal garden. He was peculiar-looking — round red face, vine leaves tangled in his hair, the unmistakable air of someone who had been having an excellent party somewhere and wandered off. They'd tied him up, not knowing what else to do. Midas looked at him and recognized him immediately: Silenus, the companion and old tutor of Dionysus, the god of wine and celebration. He freed the old man at once, drew him a warm bath, had a proper meal prepared, and entertained him cheerfully for ten days before sending him safely back to Dionysus. Dionysus was delighted. He offered Midas any wish. Midas didn't have to think. "I wish that everything I touch would turn to gold." Dionysus looked at him. He was a god of joy, and he liked Midas, and he had offered the wish freely. He granted it. But the expression on his face when he did it was the expression of someone who already knows how a story ends. Midas went home full of excitement. He touched a twig — gold. A stone — gold. He plucked a flower and held a small, perfect, heavy golden rose, every petal preserved exactly. He went to sit down to dinner. He lifted his bread. It turned to gold in his hand, hard and cold and impossible to eat. He lifted his wine cup — gold. He reached for an olive — gold. He was sitting at a table covered in golden food he could not touch, in a golden chair that had stopped being a chair, in a palace that was rapidly becoming a very expensive problem. He was starving. His daughter came to comfort him when she saw his face. She turned to gold. She stood there — perfectly herself in every feature, her arms still extended toward him, her expression still full of concern — cold and solid and golden and gone. Midas left the palace and found Dionysus and didn't speak for a moment, because what do you say. "Take it back," he said. "Please. Take it back." Dionysus told him to go to the source of the river Pactolus in Lydia and wash his hands in it. Midas ran. He washed his hands in the headwaters and felt the power drain out of him and flow into the river — which is why, the Greeks said, the river Pactolus ran with gold dust for centuries afterward, washing out of the hills where Midas had washed away his wish. He went home. His daughter was herself again. He held her for a long time. After that, Midas lost interest in gold entirely. He gave most of his treasury away. He took to wandering in the hills and forests, which suited him much better. He became a devoted follower of Pan, the wild god of the woods, and spent his days listening to the wind in the reeds and watching the animals move through the trees, none of which was gold, all of which was exactly what it was. He was the richest man who ever lived for about four hours. He was much happier after he stopped being that. The Greeks told the story of Midas not as a warning against greed exactly — Midas wasn't greedy in a cruel way, just enthusiastic about something in a direction that happened to be wrong. They told it as a reminder that getting what you think you want has consequences you cannot fully imagine in the moment of wanting it. They also pointed out that his daughter came back. The gold in the river stayed. That part didn't undo itself. Be specific about what you ask for, and think it through first. Dionysus will not always be in a reversing mood.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

Show what you understood

1. Midas asked for the golden touch immediately, without thinking it through. Dionysus warned him with an expression — he didn't say anything, but his face showed he knew what was coming. Why do you think the Greeks gave Dionysus that knowing look? What responsibility do we have when we can see someone is about to make a terrible mistake?

2. Midas gave away most of his gold after the wish was reversed, and was happier wandering in the woods listening to the wind. What changed for him? What did the golden touch teach him that he couldn't have learned any other way?

3. Midas's daughter was turned to gold and then restored. Some versions of the story say she was never quite the same afterward, even after she came back. What might it mean to be restored but changed? Can you think of something in your own life that was restored but different?
