A Tale from Before the world existed, there were exactly two things. And they hated each other.

In the north: Niflheim. Ice. Darkness. Fog so cold it bit. Eleven rivers that crawled outward from a spring called Hvergelmir and froze solid before they got anywhere interesting. If you had stood at the edge of Niflheim — which you couldn't, because you didn't exist yet — you would have felt your eyelashes freeze off within seconds.

In the south: Muspelheim. Fire. Roaring, howling, everything-is-on-fire fire. A giant named Surt stood at its border holding a flaming sword, waiting for the end of the world with the patience of someone who has been waiting since before the world started and doesn't mind waiting until after it ends.

Between them: nothing. A yawning gap called Ginnungagap. The great empty nothing.

Then the cold crept south. The fire crept north. They met in the middle.

The ice melted.

And from that melting, something enormous hauled itself upright, shook the frost from its beard, and blinked.

His name was Ymir. He was the first frost giant — bad-tempered, enormous, and not particularly bright. While he slept, other frost giants sweated out of him the way breath fogs in cold air, and those giants had more giants, and suddenly the empty void was full of enormous bickering creatures.

Also crawling out of the melting ice was a cow. An enormous, untroubled cow named Audhumbla. She had four udders and she fed Ymir with her milk, and she fed herself by licking the salty ice.

As Audhumbla licked, something started appearing in the block she was working on. Day one: hair. Day two: a head. Day three: a full person stepped out of the ice, blinking, looking around at the frost giants with an expression that said clearly: I am not one of these.

His name was Buri. He was the first of the gods.

Buri had a son named Bor. Bor had three sons: Odin, Vili, and Ve. The three brothers looked at Ymir snoring in the middle of everything, frost giants multiplying in every direction, and had a conversation that went approximately like this:

"We should build something better."

His skull became the dome of the sky, which they propped up at the four corners with four dwarves named North, South, East, and West, who have been standing there ever since holding up the sky and presumably wishing they had said no.

His brains became clouds. His eyebrows became a great wall around the middle world to keep out whatever was left of the giants.

Then the brothers found two trees lying on the ground — an ash and an elm, just lying there, not doing much. Odin breathed life into them. Vili gave them understanding and the ability to move. Ve gave them senses, voices, color in their faces.

From the ash came a man named Ask. From the elm came a woman named Embla. They looked at each other. They looked at the world — the mountains made of bones, the sea made of blood, the sky made of a dead giant's skull — and somehow found it beautiful.

In the Beginning: The Norse Creation

The brothers built their own realm above: Asgard, the home of the gods, connected to the human world of Midgard by a rainbow bridge called Bifrost. They built great halls. They lit the sky with sparks from Muspelheim. They set the sun and moon running across the sky, chased by wolves who would catch them at the end of everything.

And holding all of it together — all nine worlds, from the fiery south to the frozen north, from the gods at the top to the dead at the bottom — was Yggdrasil, the World Tree. An ash so enormous that its branches sheltered the gods and its roots reached into three different wells. A squirrel named Ratatoskr spent every single day running up and down its trunk carrying insults between the eagle at the top and the serpent gnawing at the bottom.

Nobody asked him to do this. He just did it.

The world was built. The people were walking around in it. The gods were in their hall.

And somewhere at the border of Muspelheim, Surt was still standing, still holding his flaming sword, still waiting.

He was very patient.

📰 Informational Text

Connect Myth to Reality

How We Know About Norse Myths: From Oral Tales to Written Books

The creation story you just read wasn't written down until the 1200s — more than 200 years AFTER most Vikings had converted to Christianity! So how did these myths survive?

For centuries, Norse myths were passed down orally. Poets called 'skalds' memorized thousands of lines of poetry and recited them at feasts, ceremonies, and gatherings. They were the living libraries of Viking culture, preserving history, religion, and stories through careful repetition.

In 1220, an Icelandic historian named Snorri Sturluson wrote down the myths in a book called the Prose Edda. He was Christian himself, but he wanted to preserve the old stories so people could understand the ancient poetry that referenced these gods and tales. Without Snorri's work, we might have lost these myths forever.

Another source is the Poetic Edda, a collection of Old Norse poems discovered in an Icelandic manuscript from around 1270. These poems are older than Snorri's prose version and give us the original poetic form of many myths.

Archaeologists have also found Viking artifacts — carvings, runestones, and jewelry — that show scenes from these myths. A famous example is the Gosforth Cross in England, which depicts Ragnarök in carved stone, proving that these stories were important enough to be preserved in art and architecture.

💡 The Lesson

The Norse myths survived because people valued them enough to memorize, recite, write down, and carve into stone. Stories don't preserve themselves — humans have to choose to keep them alive.

2. Snorri Sturluson was a Christian who wrote down pagan myths in the 1200s. Why do you think he chose to preserve stories about gods he didn't worship? What does this tell you about his values as a historian?

✏️ Grammar

Week 28 Focus: Complex Sentences with Subordinating Conjunctions

📚 What You're Learning:

A complex sentence has an independent clause (complete thought) and a dependent clause (incomplete thought connected with words like 'because', 'although', 'while', 'when', 'if', 'since').

✨ Examples:

Ymir slept while frost giants sweated out of him.

The brothers made the world because they wanted something better than chaos.

Although Surt waited at the border, he never moved from his post.
