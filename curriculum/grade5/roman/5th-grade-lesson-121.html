Mars, Father of Rome A Tale from Roman Mythology The Greeks had Ares, and mostly found him alarming — chaotic, bloodthirsty, not the kind of divine presence you'd invite to a planning meeting. He was the god of the worst parts of war: the slaughter and the frenzy and the part where everything goes wrong at once. Even the other gods didn't like him much. The Romans had Mars, and they loved him. This difference tells you almost everything you need to know about the difference between Greece and Rome. For the Romans, war was not chaos. War was order, imposed by force. War was how Rome brought its system — its roads and aqueducts and laws and architecture and the long Roman peace — to people who did not yet have these things. The Roman army was the most disciplined fighting force the ancient world had ever seen, and it functioned not by ferocity alone but by engineering and logistics and relentless, methodical organization. Mars was that army's divine patron, and the Romans were proud of him. He was also, in their telling, the literal father of Rome. He fathered Romulus and Remus on a Vestal Virgin, and through Romulus he was the ancestor of every Roman. This was not incidental mythology. This was a foundational claim: Rome was descended from the god of war, and the capacity for organized, purposeful force was in Roman blood from the very beginning. The Romans built a theology around this, and then built one of history's largest empires around the theology. The month of March is named for Mars. His month was when the military campaign season traditionally began — when the frozen ground thawed and the roads were passable again and armies could move. The Roman legions received their sacred weapons back from his temple each March, carried through the city in procession: the season of war has begun. His temple in the Forum of Augustus was one of Rome's great buildings — tall white marble columns, a magnificent statue within, captured enemy standards brought here after every victory. Generals came to pray before departing on campaign. Young men received their adult togas in the Forum of Augustus, under Mars's eyes, because that transition from boy to citizen was also, in Rome, a transition toward the military responsibility of manhood. But Mars was older than the city, and before he was the god of legions he was the god of fields. He was an agricultural deity in his oldest form — the protector of crops and flocks from wolves and bandits and the violent unpredictability of nature. The early Romans were farmers who could pick up weapons when needed and put them down when the need was past. Cincinnatus, called from his plow to serve as dictator in a crisis, led Rome to victory and returned to his plow the moment the job was done. This was the Roman ideal: the citizen-soldier, farmer and fighter, the sword a tool like the plow, taken up in service and laid down without ceremony. Mars contained both — the man who worked the land and the man who defended it. The Romans saw no contradiction. The same steadiness that made a good farmer made a good soldier. The same discipline. The same willingness to do the necessary thing without fuss. He was not the god of bloodlust. He was the god of the thing that Rome believed made civilization possible: the willingness to fight for what you had built, when fighting was what the situation required. March is still his month. The martial arts, martial law, the martial virtues — all his name, still in the language, two thousand years after his last temple fell. He is still here.

Week 37: Subject-Verb Agreement

📚 What You're Learning:

Subjects and verbs must match in number (singular/plural)

✨ Examples:

Jupiter rules (singular)

The gods rule (plural)

Romulus and Remus were (plural)
