📚 Reading (Part 2 of 4)

Juno Queen of the Gods

Juno, Queen of the Gods A Tale from Roman Mythology Juno is often called jealous and difficult, and the myths give you plenty of evidence for this. She made Aeneas's life miserable for years. She sent obstacles and storms and delays against the destiny the Fates had already decided. But that is not the whole picture, and the Romans knew it. They worshipped her for three centuries as one of the three great gods of Rome. You do not build a temple on the Capitoline Hill — the most sacred hill in the city, home of Rome's most important worship — for a goddess you find merely difficult. Juno was the queen of the gods. Not Jupiter's assistant or decoration — his queen, with her own authority and her own sphere and her own fierce intelligence. She was the patron of women and of marriage. In Rome, a woman's most important legal and social transitions happened under Juno's protection. The month of June is named for her, and the Romans considered it the luckiest month for weddings because she presided over it. Brides invoked Juno. Pregnant women prayed to her. Women facing difficult decisions came to her temples. She was also, in her aspect as Juno Moneta — Juno the Warner — the patron of the Roman mint. Her temple on the Capitoline Hill, where sacred geese were kept, was where the Romans stored their money. The words money and monetary both come from her name. You carry Juno in your pocket every time you carry coins. The geese at Juno's temple saved Rome once. In 390 BC, the Gauls attacked the city at night, climbing the Capitoline Hill in darkness and silence. The Roman sentinels were asleep. The sacred geese of Juno were not. They woke with alarm calls so loud that they roused the garrison in time to repel the attack. The Romans credited Juno with saving the city she was known to have complicated feelings about, which they found entirely consistent. Her anger at Aeneas was real, and the Romans explained it: she had invested herself in the Greek cause during the Trojan War and in Carthage's future, and the Fates were going to undo both at once through one Trojan's descendants. She could not stop it. She could fight it every step of the way. The Romans found this understandable. She was not acting from simple spite. She was fighting for what she had built and what she loved, against a future she had seen coming and could not prevent. The outcome was fated. But Juno had too much dignity to submit without a fight, and the Romans respected dignity. She reconciled with Rome eventually. She had to — the Fates had her cornered from the beginning. But she reconciled with full honors, her temples magnificent, her month the wedding month, her name on the coins, her geese still honking on the Capitoline Hill. The Romans understood something about difficult women that is worth noting: they made one the queen of the gods, kept her there through centuries of worship, named a month after her, and credited her with saving their city. Jealous and difficult, yes. Also magnificent. Both things were true. That was the point.
