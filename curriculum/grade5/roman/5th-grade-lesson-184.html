Venus Mother of Rome

Venus, Mother of Rome A Tale from Roman Mythology Venus was the goddess of love. The Romans meant this differently than we usually do. Love, in Venus's domain, was not only the soft, private feeling between two people. Love was the force that held the world together — the thing that made atoms cohere and plants grow toward light and creatures care for their offspring and civilizations build beautiful things when they could simply have built functional ones. Lucretius, the Roman poet, opened his great poem about the nature of the universe with an address to Venus: she was, he said, the pleasure of gods and men, the one through whom all living things were brought into being. This was the goddess who was also Rome's mother. Her son Aeneas was the ancestor of Rome. Through Aeneas's descendants, the Julian family claimed direct divine descent — Julius Caesar and Augustus presenting themselves as the children of Venus, not metaphorically but in the serious genealogical way the Romans took seriously. When Caesar stood in the Senate and when Augustus addressed the Roman people, they were, in the Roman understanding, literally speaking as the children of a goddess. Caesar built the Temple of Venus Genetrix — Venus the Ancestor, Venus the Origin — in the center of Rome, dedicating it in 46 BC. The statue inside showed her in full divine authority: not draped in soft romance but standing in the dignity of a divine mother, the origin of the Roman people displayed in marble for everyone who entered the Forum to see. Augustus, building his own Forum beside Caesar's, placed Mars and Venus as the two presiding figures — the war god and the love goddess, the divine father of Romulus and the divine mother of Aeneas. The Romans chose these two as their ancestors. Not wisdom alone, not power alone — war and love, force and beauty, the capacity to destroy and the capacity to create, held together. They believed this combination was civilization. Force without love was tyranny. Love without force was helplessness. Together, directed by reason and law, they made Rome. Venus in the stories was clever and protective and not above arranging things to suit her purposes. She used Cupid to make Dido fall genuinely in love with Aeneas, because her son needed safe harbor and she was not going to leave that to chance. She came to Aeneas in disguise on the Libyan shore and told him honestly that his road was going to be hard and that she would be watching. The Romans did not find this troubling. Mothers do what they must for their children. Divine mothers have more options. She was also the patron of beauty in every form — of the art and music and the beautiful public spaces and the magnificent buildings that Rome filled the world with, at enormous expense, when purely functional buildings would have served. The Romans, who were justly famous for their engineering, were equally famous for making their engineering beautiful, and they credited Venus with that impulse. Beauty was not weakness. Beauty was divine. April is her month — the month of opening, of blossoming, of things unfolding into what they were always going to be. The Romans named a day of the week after her: Friday. Vendredi in French, Viernes in Spanish — Venus's day, still. She is still everywhere. She always was.
