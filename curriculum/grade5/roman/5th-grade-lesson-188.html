🎯 Vocabulary Practice

domain

The area of power or responsibility that belongs to a particular god or ruler.

sufferance

Permission or tolerance given by someone with power — being allowed to do something by someone who could stop you.

trident

A three-pronged spear — Neptune's symbol of power, capable of splitting the earth or stilling the seas.

Neptune and the Sea

Neptune and the Sea A Tale from Roman Mythology Neptune was not the god you prayed to when everything was going well. He was the god you prayed to when the sky turned dark and the waves came up and your ship was very small and the sea was very large and there was nothing between you and the bottom of the ocean except the wood under your feet and whatever goodwill the god of the sea chose to show. In this way, Neptune was one of the most honestly worshipped gods in the Roman world. You did not pray to him out of gratitude or civic duty. You prayed to him because you needed him, specifically and urgently, right now, and you knew it. He was Jupiter's brother — one of the three sons of Saturn who had divided the world between them. Jupiter took the sky. Pluto took the underworld. Neptune took everything wet: the sea, the rivers, the lakes, the underground springs, and the earthquakes that shook the land when the water beneath it moved. His trident could split the earth open or raise storms or still the seas with equal authority. He drove a chariot across the ocean drawn by hippocampi — creatures with a horse's head and a fish's tail — and where he passed dolphins leaped around his wheels. He was passionate and proud and had a remarkable gift for being second. He competed with Minerva for the patronage of Athens. He drove his trident into a rock on the Acropolis and a saltwater spring poured out. Minerva planted an olive tree. The gods voted: the olive tree, which gave oil and food and wood and shade and lasted for centuries, won over the saltwater spring. Neptune sent floods to the region to express his feelings. The city was named for Minerva's Greek counterpart anyway. He competed for Corinth. He lost. He competed for Aegina. He lost. He competed for several other cities and lost those too. What he did not lose was the sea itself, which was more than enough. Rome was a Mediterranean power, and everything Rome ever traded, conquered, or supplied that crossed water crossed Neptune's territory. The grain ships from Egypt that fed Rome's millions. The war galleys that enforced Roman power from Britain to Persia. The merchant vessels loaded with wine and oil and amber and silk. All of them sailing on Neptune's sufferance. He held his festival, Neptunalia, in late July — the hottest and driest time of year in Rome, when rivers ran low and springs dried up. The Romans built shelters from tree branches, feasted outdoors, and prayed for water. They gathered to celebrate the god of water in the season when they missed him most, which is the kind of honestly desperate worship that produces genuine feeling. He also created the horse, which the Romans found slightly mysterious — what did the god of the sea have to do with horses? They decided that in the very beginning, when Neptune first struck the earth with his trident, what came out was not water but a horse. His power was force released from depth: whether that was a wave or a galloping horse was, at the fundamental level, the same thing. When your ship is small and the sea is large, you pray to Neptune. When the prayer works, you are grateful. When it doesn't, the sea is Neptune's too, and he answers to no one. That was always the honest deal. The Romans understood it and prayed anyway.

✍️ Comprehension Questions

1. Neptune was a god you prayed to when you genuinely needed him — not out of habit or civic duty but out of real fear. The Romans considered this honest worship. Is there something more real about prayer in a crisis than prayer when everything is fine? What does urgency do to belief?

2. Neptune lost competition after competition for city patronage — Athens, Corinth, Aegina — but he kept the sea, which was the most important thing. Do you think he would have traded the sea for any of the cities? What does it mean to lose the small things and keep the one thing that matters?

3. The Romans held Neptune's festival in July, the hottest and driest time of year — celebrating the god of water when they missed water most. What is the wisdom in celebrating something at the moment of its absence rather than its abundance? How does missing something change how you value it?

Week 38: Appositives

📚 What You're Learning:

An appositive renames or explains a noun, set off by commas

✨ Examples:

Jupiter, king of the gods, ruled the sky.

Rome, the eternal city, lasted 1,000 years.

Romulus, the founder of Rome, killed his brother.
