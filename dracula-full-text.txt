DRACULA
Based on the novel by Bram Stoker
Retold for Young Readers

Chapter 1: The Road to the Castle
My name is Jonathan Harker, and I am a young lawyer from London. I am writing this in my journal because I want to remember everything — the good days and the strange days alike. And the days I am about to describe were among the strangest of my life.
My employer, Mr. Hawkins, sent me on an important assignment. A nobleman from a distant country called Transylvania wished to buy a house in England. He had chosen an old estate called Carfax, near London, and he needed a lawyer to handle the paperwork. Mr. Hawkins thought I was just the man for it — young, hardworking, and eager to prove myself. He was right that I was eager.
I traveled by train across France and into Eastern Europe, watching the landscape change through the train window — from flat farmland to rolling hills to jagged mountains draped in pine forest. The food changed, the language changed, and the people changed too. They were warm and welcoming, but there was an uneasiness that I could not quite explain.
When I asked about my destination — a castle owned by a Count Dracula — people's faces changed. They fell quiet. An old woman pressed a string of garlic flowers into my hands and made a sign that I did not understand.
"Do not go," she whispered.
I thanked her politely and kept going. I was a professional. I had a job to do.
A coach met me at a mountain pass called the Borgo Pass. The driver wore a dark cloak and a wide hat pulled low over his face. He barely spoke. The horses were enormous and pale, and they seemed to know the road perfectly, even in the dark.
We rode higher and higher into the mountains. A wolf howled somewhere in the trees. Then more wolves — many of them — calling back and forth like a conversation in a language I couldn't understand.
The driver laughed softly. I did not find it funny.
At last, through the trees, I saw the castle — black against the stars, tall and jagged, perched on the edge of a cliff. Not a single light showed in any window.
My heart beat faster. But I told myself I was being foolish.
I was a lawyer. I was here for paperwork.

Chapter 2: Welcome to Castle Dracula
The coach stopped before a great arched doorway. The driver vanished — I don't know exactly how, one moment he was there and the next the coach was empty — and before I could wonder about it, the castle door opened.
A tall man stood in the doorway, holding a lamp. He was very tall — nearly as tall as the doorframe. His face was pale and angular, with a sharp nose, heavy dark eyebrows, and a thin, precise mustache. His eyes were dark red, like old rubies. He was dressed entirely in black.
"Welcome to my house," he said. His voice was deep and perfectly controlled. "Come in freely, of your own will."
I thought the phrasing was a little strange, but I stepped inside.
Count Dracula shook my hand, and I was startled by how cold his grip was — cold and very strong. He guided me through stone corridors lit by flickering candles, up a sweeping staircase, and into a warm sitting room where a fire blazed and supper was laid out on a long table.
I was very hungry after the long journey. The Count gestured for me to eat. I noticed he did not eat anything himself. He sat in a tall chair and watched me with those dark eyes, asking polite questions about my journey and my plans.
I stayed for several days. The Count was a gracious host. He had a vast library filled with books about England — its history, its customs, its geography. He was fascinated by everything English and asked me endless questions.
But there were things I began to notice.
The Count had no servants. I never saw him eat. I noticed there were no mirrors in the castle — and one morning, when I was shaving with my small travel mirror, the Count approached behind me, and I realized with a jolt that he cast no reflection at all.
I said nothing. I kept shaving.
But that night, I walked to the great front door to get some air, and found that it was locked from the outside.
I was a guest who could not leave.

Chapter 3: A Prisoner in the Castle
I tried to remain calm. Perhaps the door was locked for my safety — the Count had mentioned wolves in the area. Perhaps there was a simple explanation.
But as the days passed, I found more things that troubled me.
I discovered that the Count never appeared during daylight hours. I explored the castle during the day and found that many rooms were locked, but those that were open showed no sign that anyone lived in them. No clothes, no personal items, no food, no dishes.
One afternoon, I was exploring a part of the castle I had not visited before when I found a room where the windows faced west. The afternoon sun came through these windows, and they were not shuttered. I sat down and wrote in my journal. It was the safest I had felt since arriving.
Then I heard footsteps in the corridor.
But it was not the Count's step. It was lighter, quicker. And then three women appeared in the room — young and beautiful, with pale skin and bright eyes. They moved silently, without sound, like things in a dream.
They came toward me with smiling faces, and I felt strangely unable to move, as though my limbs had forgotten how to work.
But before they reached me, a dark shape swept between us. Count Dracula. He was furious in a way that his calm face could barely contain.
"This one belongs to me," he said to them in a voice so low I barely heard it.
The women withdrew like smoke in a wind. The Count turned to me, and for just a moment, I saw something behind his polished manner — something cold and ancient and deeply strange.
He smiled his careful smile and led me back to my room.
That night, I wrote everything in my journal. I did not know who might find it someday, but I wanted someone to know the truth.
I wrote: I am a prisoner in Castle Dracula, and I do not know what my host intends to do with me.

Chapter 4: Escape
I decided I had to escape. I could wait no longer.
I had noticed that the Count sometimes climbed the outer wall of the castle — headfirst, like a lizard, moving with horrible ease along the sheer stone face. He seemed to have powers far beyond those of any human being.
But the wall was the only way out that was not locked. I waited until I was sure the Count was away from that side of the castle, took a deep breath, and climbed out the window.
The stone was old and rough, with plenty of handholds. I am a reasonably capable climber. I made it down and found a door at the base of the wall, hidden by ivy. It opened onto a long passage cut into the rock.
At the end of the passage, I found something terrible.
A large cellar. And in the cellar, boxes — dozens of them, filled with fresh dark earth. And in one of the boxes, the Count himself. Lying perfectly still. His eyes were closed, but his skin had a warm color to it, as if he had recently — fed.
I ran.
I did not find a way out of the outer walls that night. I ended up back in my room, shaking, and lay awake until dawn.
Over the following days, I observed the Count carefully and kept making attempts to find a way out. I saw him loading those boxes onto a wagon through a back gate, late at night. Whatever he was planning in England, those boxes were part of it.
Finally, one morning before dawn, I found a window on the far side of the castle that had no lock. The drop was steep, but there was a gutter pipe that reached almost to the ground. I tied my journal carefully in oilcloth and tucked it inside my coat.
Then I climbed, and dropped, and ran.
I ran until the castle was out of sight. I did not stop running for a very long time.

Chapter 5: Mina's Worry
My name is Mina Murray, and Jonathan was supposed to be home weeks ago.
I am writing in my own journal now, because writing helps me think, and I need very much to think clearly.
Jonathan and I were engaged to be married. He left for Transylvania in good spirits — excited about his first important assignment, a little nervous, but confident. He said he would be back inside a month.
Three months have passed. His letters stopped halfway through his journey. I have written to his employer, to the train companies, to every contact I can think of. No one has heard anything.
I tell myself there are reasonable explanations. The mail is slow in that part of the world. He may have had to stay longer. He is resourceful and sensible, and nothing bad has happened to him.
I tell myself this every night before I go to sleep.
My dearest friend in the world is a woman named Lucy Westenra. She is bright and funny and the most good-natured person I know. Lucy has three young men all very much in love with her — a doctor named John Seward, a good-natured American named Quincey Morris, and an English nobleman named Arthur Holmwood. She has chosen Arthur, and they are newly engaged.
I went to visit Lucy at her family home by the sea, in a Yorkshire town called Whitby. The air there is bracing and salty, and the cliffs are beautiful, and it helped a little.
But then strange things began to happen in Whitby.
A great ship came in during a storm, sailing wildly, with no living crew aboard. At the helm, lashed to the wheel, was the body of the captain. In the ship's hold were fifty boxes of dark foreign earth.
Lucy began walking in her sleep.

Chapter 6: Something Wrong with Lucy
The night of the great storm, I woke to find Lucy gone from our shared room. I searched the house and then ran out into the night in my dressing gown, following the path I knew she sometimes walked in her sleep.
I found her on the clifftop, in the ancient churchyard that overlooked the sea. She was sitting on a bench, her head tilted back, her eyes closed, her white nightgown bright in the moonlight. And behind her, bending over her, was a dark shape — tall and thin, with what looked like a pale face turning to look at me.
I ran toward her calling her name, and the shape was gone.
Lucy was cold to the touch and confused. She did not remember being outside. I brought her home, put her to bed, and sat up watching her for the rest of the night. In the morning, I found two small marks on her neck, like insect bites.
I thought nothing of them at first. This was my mistake.
Over the following weeks, Lucy grew pale. Her appetite faded. She slept too much and then could not sleep at all. The marks on her neck did not heal.
Her mother, who was ill herself, did not notice. But I noticed. And when Doctor Seward was called, he noticed too. He said he had never seen anything quite like it, and he called in an old friend — a professor named Abraham Van Helsing, who had come from Amsterdam.
Van Helsing was an unusual man. He was small and energetic, with a sharp mind that seemed to dart in several directions at once. He looked at Lucy for a long time without speaking.
Then he asked to speak with Doctor Seward privately.
I was not in the room, but I saw Van Helsing's face as he left. He was trying to hide that he was frightened.

Chapter 7: Professor Van Helsing
Professor Van Helsing returned to Whitby several times over the following weeks, and each time he brought flowers. Garlic flowers, specifically — bunches and bunches of them, which he arranged around Lucy's room, hung above the windows, and wove into a wreath for her neck.
Lucy's mother thought this was eccentric. Lucy thought it was funny, and teased Van Helsing gently about his "smelly decorations."
But I noticed that on the nights when the garlic was in place and the windows were closed, Lucy slept soundly. Her color improved. She seemed more herself.
Then one night, Lucy's mother — not understanding the reason for the garlic — opened the windows to let in fresh air and moved the flowers because she thought they smelled too strong.
The next morning, Lucy was worse than she had ever been.
Van Helsing arrived and did not say "I told you so." He simply began again, re-hanging the garlic, closing the windows, sitting beside Lucy's bed. He looked at her for a long time, then asked that Arthur be sent for.
Lucy needed a blood transfusion — her blood had grown dangerously thin. Arthur gave blood willingly, and it helped. But Lucy continued to weaken.
I finally asked Van Helsing directly what was happening to her.
He sat with me for a long time in silence. Then he said, very carefully: "Miss Murray, I need you to tell me — do you believe there are things in the world that science does not yet fully explain?"
I thought of Jonathan, lost somewhere in Eastern Europe. I thought of the dark shape in the churchyard.
"Yes," I said. "I believe that."
Van Helsing nodded. "Good. Because what is attacking your friend is very old, and very real, and we must be clear-eyed about what it is before we can stop it."

Chapter 8: Good News and Bad
Two wonderful things happened in the same week.
Jonathan was found.
He had collapsed in the street in Budapest, in Hungary, and had been taken to a hospital run by nuns. He had a fever and was confused, and could remember very little of what had happened to him. He had his journal, which he refused to let the nurses take.
A nun named Sister Agatha wrote to me, because she found my name in his belongings. I took the first train east.
When I arrived, Jonathan was sitting up in bed. He was thin and pale, and his hair had gone partly white — he was only in his twenties, and this shocked me more than anything. But his eyes, when he saw me, were the same eyes I remembered. He reached for my hand.
We were married quietly in the hospital chapel, and it was the happiest moment of my life despite everything surrounding it.
Jonathan told me he did not want to read his journal. He said he feared that what was in it might have been a sickness — a dream, something that had broken in his mind. He asked me to keep it safe but not tell him what was in it. Not yet.
I said yes. But I read it myself.
And then I understood why Van Helsing was frightened.
The second thing that happened that week was terrible. Lucy died.
She had declined rapidly after Jonathan was found, as if whatever was drawing her away had grown bolder and more powerful. Arthur was at her side at the end. Van Helsing was there too, his face grave.
I received the letter with the news as Jonathan and I traveled back to England, and I wept for most of the train journey.
But Van Helsing had written too, in a careful hand: Do not bury Lucy yet. Wait for me. There is something more to be done.

Chapter 9: The Undead
The children in the neighborhood near Lucy's family home began to disappear at night. They were always found in the morning — unharmed, but with small marks on their necks, confused and unable to remember where they had been.
They described being led away by a "beautiful lady."
Van Helsing gathered the small group of people who knew the truth — Doctor Seward, Arthur, Quincey Morris, Jonathan and me — and told us plainly what we were dealing with.
"Lucy is not at rest," he said. "What you call a vampire — un-dead — it is not a legend. It is a real creature, one that has existed for many centuries. It feeds on the living. It can move at night in the mist and shadow. It commands wolves and rats. And it has made Lucy one of its own."
The room was very quiet.
"Lucy is gone," Van Helsing said gently, looking at Arthur. "What walks in her body is not your Lucy. But you can free her. You can give her peace. And then we must find the one who made her this way, and stop him before he does this to anyone else."
Arthur was pale as chalk. "What must we do?"
Van Helsing explained. It was not easy to hear. It was not easy to do.
But we went to the tomb together that night — all of us, with lanterns. And when it was over, Arthur knelt for a long time in silence, and when he stood up again, his face was different. Older. But also, in some way, peaceful.
"She looks like herself," he said quietly. "She looks as if she's really sleeping."
She did. Lucy, whatever had been wearing her face, was gone. And Lucy herself — the real Lucy — was finally at rest.
Van Helsing put a hand on Arthur's shoulder. "Now," he said, "we have a greater task. The one who did this is in London, and he has plans."

Chapter 10: The Count in London
With Van Helsing's guidance, we began to piece together what Count Dracula was doing in England.
Jonathan's journal, which I shared with the group, was invaluable. It described the boxes of earth that Dracula had loaded onto a wagon at the castle — fifty boxes, each filled with the dark soil of Transylvania. Dracula needed this soil to sleep in. Without it, he could not rest and regain his strength.
The boxes had been shipped to a property called Carfax, right next door to Doctor Seward's asylum. Carfax was old, sprawling, and half-ruined — a place where no one would look closely and no one would ask questions.
Van Helsing explained that our best strategy was to find those boxes and destroy them, one by one. Every box we rid of its earth was a resting place Dracula lost. Make enough of them useless, and he would grow weaker and more desperate.
Meanwhile, I worked at organizing all our journals and letters into a single record. I was the fastest typist, and I believed that having everything written down and ordered might reveal patterns or clues we had missed individually.
Jonathan threw himself into research with a thoroughness that made me proud. The fear that had aged him had also sharpened him. He was determined now in a way he had never been before the journey to Transylvania.
Doctor Seward provided his asylum as our base of operations, partly because of its proximity to Carfax. He was a careful, methodical man who had trained himself not to feel what he was feeling, but I could see the grief for Lucy behind his professional manner.
Quincey Morris was quiet and steady. He never complained and never hesitated, and he had a way of making everyone around him feel slightly braver.
And Arthur — Lord Godalming now, after his father's death — had turned his grief into purpose.
We were six people against one very ancient and very powerful enemy. But we were together, and we knew the truth, and we were not afraid. Not entirely.

Chapter 11: Breaking into Carfax
On a cold autumn night, the men of our group went over the wall into Carfax while Doctor Seward and I kept watch from the asylum.
Jonathan told me afterward what they found.
The house was dark and thick with dust. Cobwebs hung from every doorway. Rats scattered at the sound of their lanterns. The smell was terrible — old earth and something else, something they could not quite name.
They found the boxes in the chapel. Not all fifty — only twenty-nine were still there. Dracula had been moving the rest, distributing them to other hiding places around London. Van Helsing had expected this.
They set to work consecrating each box — treating the earth with holy water, making each one useless as a resting place. It was exhausting work, box after box in the dark and the smell, with the rats getting bolder and the sense that something might return at any moment.
"Did you feel it?" Quincey said quietly to Jonathan, on the walk back.
"Yes," Jonathan said. "Like being watched."
They had not seen Dracula. But they had felt him — his presence, cold and heavy, somewhere in the dark above them. Watching.
The next morning I typed up their report and added it to our record. As I worked, I felt a strange sensation — a heaviness in my head, a difficulty focusing. I told myself I was tired.
Van Helsing arrived in the afternoon and looked at me carefully. His eyes lingered on my neck. He asked if he could examine me.
I agreed. He looked at my neck for a long time without touching it, his expression very still.
"What is it?" I asked.
He sat back. "Mina," he said carefully, "I believe Dracula has been visiting you."

Chapter 12: The Count's Mark
The news was terrible and frightening, but Van Helsing told me everything — clearly, without softening it. I demanded that he do so. I could not help the group if I did not understand the full truth.
Dracula had found his way into the asylum at night. He had come to me while I slept, and had begun the same process he had used with Lucy. If it continued, I would eventually become one of the undead.
There was, Van Helsing said, one difference between my situation and Lucy's. We had caught it earlier. We knew what was happening. And there was a connection — a strange mental link that Dracula could use to find me, but that we might be able to use in reverse to find him.
Van Helsing used hypnosis — a kind of deep, focused sleep — to access this link. Each morning at sunrise, just before I fully woke, he would speak to me softly, asking what I could sense. And in that state between sleeping and waking, I could sometimes describe what Dracula was experiencing — the movement of water beneath him, the creak of a ship, the darkness of a box.
It became our compass.
Jonathan was very quiet after he learned what had happened to me. I held his hands and made him look at me.
"I am still here," I told him. "I am still myself. And I intend to stay that way."
He nodded. His jaw was set. He had faced something terrible in that castle, and he had survived it, and now he channeled everything that experience had taught him into protecting the people he loved.
The group added new protections around my room every night. I was never left alone. They watched over me with such fierce, loving care that even in the middle of all this fear, I felt fortunate.
"We will end this," Jonathan told me. "I promise you that."

Chapter 13: The Chase Begins
Through my early morning trances, we pieced together where Dracula was going.
After we had destroyed or consecrated most of his boxes in London, Dracula had panicked. He had one box remaining — his last refuge. We found it through a shipping record that Jonathan and Doctor Seward tracked down. It was on a ship called the Czarina Catherine, headed east, back to Transylvania.
Dracula was running home.
Van Helsing gathered us and laid out the situation plainly. If Dracula reached his castle before we caught him, he would be safe behind his own walls and in his own country, with all of the old powers that came with that. He would be able to recover, rebuild his strength, and eventually return to England or try elsewhere.
We could not let that happen.
We split into two groups. Jonathan, Doctor Seward, Arthur, and Quincey would follow the ship's route by land, traveling hard across Eastern Europe to try to intercept the box before it reached the castle. Van Helsing and I would travel more directly to Transylvania.
Preparing to leave, there were small, meaningful moments — Jonathan checking my coat, Arthur shaking everyone's hand with both of his, Quincey Morris who never wasted words saying simply, "We'll see each other on the other side of this."
I was afraid. I will not pretend otherwise. But fear, I had learned, was not the same as hopelessness.
Jonathan and I embraced at the train station. He held on for a moment longer than usual.
"Whatever happens," he said, "you are the bravest person I have ever known."
I kissed his cheek. "Come back to me."
"I will," he said. And he meant it.

Chapter 14: Mina and Van Helsing
Professor Van Helsing and I traveled east together, crossing France by train and into Eastern Europe by a series of coaches. Van Helsing was excellent company — sharp-minded, curious about everything, full of unexpected humor at odd moments.
But as we traveled, my morning trances grew more vivid and harder to control. Van Helsing had to be very careful about how he guided me — asking just enough to find direction, but not so much that the connection deepened.
"You are resisting it," he told me one morning, looking pleased. "That is good. Your mind is strong."
I asked him once what would happen if we failed — if we could not destroy Dracula.
He was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "Then we would have to find a different way. There is always a different way. The difference between the brave and the merely stubborn is knowing which way to keep fighting."
We crossed into Transylvania as autumn deepened toward winter. The mountains were covered in the first snow. The roads became rough and difficult. Our driver grew more reluctant with each passing mile.
I used my morning trances to track the progress of the Czarina Catherine upriver. The box was still on board. It had not yet been unloaded.
We were ahead of it.
Van Helsing and I pressed on toward the castle, moving through forests that grew darker and quieter with each hour. At times I felt the mental pressure I associated with Dracula — a pulling sensation, as if something were trying to reach through my thoughts and turn me around.
I kept walking.
"Remarkable," Van Helsing said, watching me.
"I have a very good reason to keep going," I said.
He smiled. "Yes. I know."

Chapter 15: Castle Dracula at Last
Van Helsing and I reached the castle in the late afternoon. It was even more forbidding than Jonathan had described — massive and dark, jutting from the cliff edge like a broken tooth. The sky behind it was the pale gold of a winter sunset.
The driver would not come past the bend in the road. He handed down our bags and turned his horses around almost before we were fully off the coach. We stood alone on the mountain road.
Van Helsing took a moment to study the castle.
"I expected to feel afraid," he said calmly. "But I find I feel rather determined. Interesting."
We approached. The great door, as Jonathan had described, was unlocked from outside — meant to keep people in, not out. Van Helsing and I went through it.
Inside, the castle was cold and empty. Our footsteps echoed on stone floors. Van Helsing moved through each room methodically, examining everything, leaving holy water at every doorway and window.
In the cellar, we found what we were looking for.
Three boxes. Three of the boxes that the creature women at the castle used as resting places.
Van Helsing was thorough and gentle and respectful in his work, even here. He treated each box with care, speaking quietly, as though performing a religious ceremony.
Afterward, we went back to the upper rooms and built a fire. Night was coming, and with it, the cold. Van Helsing drew a careful circle around our fire using salt and herbs, and explained that within this circle, nothing could harm us without our invitation.
"We wait here," he said. "The others will come tomorrow."
I nodded. I could feel the link in my mind pulling faintly, like a current.
"He is close," I said.
"Yes," Van Helsing agreed. "But so is tomorrow."

Chapter 16: Racing Against Sunset
Jonathan's account of the final chase:
We rode hard for three days, pushing the horses as far as they dared go on the mountain roads. Doctor Seward had procured a map from a local guide, and we tracked the river where the Czarina Catherine was moving. The box was still on board. We had to reach the point where the road and the river would converge before the ship docked.
Quincey Morris rode like a man who had been on horseback his whole life, which he had. He never complained about the cold, the pace, the rough terrain, or the endless uncertainty. He simply rode.
Arthur rode with the same grim quiet that had come over him since Lucy. Doctor Seward checked his medical bag each evening as though making sure the contents were still there gave him some comfort.
I thought about Mina constantly. I thought about what Van Helsing had told us — that the mark on her throat would fade when Dracula was destroyed, and would spread if he was not. I rode for her.
On the third day, the snow was deep and the road was nearly impassable. We could see the river below, and on it, a dark shape — the Czarina Catherine, pushing through the ice-choked water.
Gypsies on horseback were riding along the riverbank, clearly heading for the same point we were — a landing, where they would unload the box.
"They're going to unload it before sunset," Quincey said.
I looked at the sky. The sun was sitting on the mountain peaks, blood-orange and sinking fast.
"Then we ride now," I said.
We drove our horses down the hillside, sliding on the snow, toward the bend in the river where the ship was heading to dock.

Chapter 17: The Final Battle
What happened in those last minutes before sunset is burned into my memory.
We reached the landing as the gypsies were lifting the box from the boat. There were many of them and only four of us, but they were not our real fight and I think they sensed something of what was happening. They watched us warily as we rode in.
"Leave the box," I said.
They did not leave it. But they hesitated.
Quincey came off his horse and onto the box in a single motion, forcing it open. The gypsies shouted. Two of them moved toward him with knives. In the struggle, Quincey was stabbed in the side.
He kept moving.
Jonathan pulled the lid from the box. Inside, in the dark earth, Dracula lay. His eyes were open. They were bright with rage, and underneath the rage, fear — the sun was barely above the mountain now, and he could not fully move yet, not until the sun was fully down.
Jonathan had his knife ready. He did not hesitate.
The effect was instantaneous. The ancient face crumbled. The red eyes went wide, then blank. The whole body seemed to turn to dust and ash, scattering in the cold mountain wind.
Gone.
The gypsies pulled back. The horses, which had been stamping and frightened, went still.
I ran to Quincey.
He was sitting against a rock, his hand pressed to his side, breathing carefully. He looked up at me with a slight smile.
"Is it over?" he asked.
"Yes," I said. "It's over."
He closed his eyes and exhaled slowly. "Good," he said. "Tell Mina—" But he stopped, because I was already here.
I had arrived with Van Helsing just in time. I was standing right in front of him.
Quincey Morris looked at me, and he smiled, and then he was gone.

Chapter 18: After the Storm
Mina's account:
When Dracula was destroyed, something changed in me.
I felt it immediately. The pulling sensation in my mind — that dark current I had felt for weeks, the thing that had been trying to draw me in some direction I did not want to go — simply stopped. The silence it left behind was extraordinary, like a ringing in your ears going suddenly quiet.
I looked at Van Helsing, and he was watching my face. He saw the change happen.
"It is done," he said.
We buried Quincey Morris on a hillside outside a small village. The priest who performed the ceremony did not know the full story, but he was kind and dignified and said the right words. Arthur stood with his hat in his hands and did not cry. Jonathan held my hand very tightly.
In the days that followed, we began the long journey home.
We traveled together as far as Budapest, and then by degrees our group began to separate — Doctor Seward back to his practice, Arthur back to England and the responsibilities he had inherited. Quincey's family in Texas would be told that he had died in a foreign country doing something brave, which was perfectly true.
Van Helsing traveled with Jonathan and me as far as Paris. At the train station, he shook Jonathan's hand for a long time without saying anything. Then he kissed my hand very formally, put on his hat, and walked away through the crowd.
"Thank you!" I called after him.
He raised one hand without turning around, and then he was gone.

Chapter 19: Going Home
Jonathan and I came back to England in early winter, arriving in London on a gray and misty afternoon that nevertheless seemed beautiful to me.
We were quiet on the long train ride north — not the silence of people who have nothing to say, but the silence of two people who have been through something enormous together and are only now, slowly, beginning to let themselves feel it.
I thought about Lucy. I thought about what Van Helsing had said — that she was at peace, truly herself again. I hoped it was true. I believed it was.
Jonathan had been changed by everything. He was more serious than the young man who had left for Transylvania two years earlier, but he was also more present — more alert to what was real and what mattered. He noticed things. He was kind in small practical ways. He was not broken by what had happened to him. He had worked very hard not to be.
And me?
I was different too. I had learned that I was stronger than I had known. I had learned that fear does not disqualify you from being brave — that bravery is often just deciding to keep moving while afraid. I had kept records, organized information, put the pieces together when no one else could see the pattern. That had mattered. I had mattered.
I did not want to forget any of it.
Not the fear, not the grief, not the wonder of what we had faced and defeated together. All of it was real. All of it was mine.
The train pulled into our station. Jonathan reached up to lift our bags from the rack.
Outside the window, people moved through the familiar streets. An ordinary winter evening.
I was glad to be home.

Chapter 20: Seven Years Later
A note added to these records:
Seven years have passed since the events described in these pages. Jonathan and I have a son. We named him Quincey.
He is six years old and has his father's curiosity and, I am told, my stubbornness. He has very little idea why his parents occasionally go quiet when certain topics come up in conversation, or why our home contains more garlic than any household strictly requires.
We have not told him. Not yet. He is too young, and the world should be ordinary for him as long as it can be.
But I have kept all the records — organized, bound, labeled. Every journal, every letter, every account. They are real. They happened. I believe they should not be forgotten.
Doctor Seward still practices in London. He married a few years ago and seems genuinely happy, which is everything we could have wished for him. Arthur and I exchange letters every Christmas. He has become an extraordinary man — someone shaped by loss into something very fine.
Professor Van Helsing wrote to us last year from Amsterdam. He is well. His letter was full of observations about the latest developments in biology and chemistry, and a long, excited paragraph about a paper he had read on the nature of electricity. He was delighted by the world, as he always was.
Jonathan read all of these records once, some years ago, when Quincey was a baby. He sat with them for a long evening, reading carefully. When he finished, he was quiet for a while.
"We did that," he finally said, looking a little amazed.
"We did," I agreed.
He looked at Quincey sleeping in his cradle. Then he put his arm around me, and we sat together as the fire burned low.
Outside the window, England was perfectly ordinary and perfectly beautiful and perfectly safe.
We had helped make it so.
