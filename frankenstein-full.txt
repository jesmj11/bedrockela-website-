FRANKENSTEIN
Based on the novel by Mary Shelley
Retold for Young Readers

Chapter 1: A Letter from the Ice
My name is Robert Walton, and I am an explorer. I have always dreamed of reaching the very top of the world — the frozen North Pole, where no person had ever stood before. I gathered a crew, bought a ship, and set sail across the icy Arctic seas.
The farther north we sailed, the colder the air became. Ice crept along the sides of the ship, and our breath made little white clouds in front of our faces. Fog rolled in thick and heavy, hiding the horizon.
One morning, my crew spotted something strange through the mist. A large sled was racing across the ice in the distance, pulled by enormous dogs. The figure driving it was huge — bigger than any man we had seen. Then the fog swallowed them whole, and they were gone.
The next morning, we found another sled — but this one was almost destroyed. Its dogs were dead, and only one runner remained. A man lay across it, barely alive. He was thin as paper, with hollow cheeks and wild eyes that still burned with something fierce. We brought him aboard.
For days, he could not speak. We fed him warm broth and wrapped him in blankets. Slowly, color returned to his face.
When he was well enough to sit up, he asked us where we were going.
"North," I told him. "I want to discover what no one has discovered before."
The man looked at me with great sadness. "That hunger," he said quietly, "that burning need to know more and go further than anyone else — I know it well. It nearly destroyed me."
He closed his eyes for a long moment. Then he said, "Let me tell you my story. Perhaps it will save you."
His name was Victor Frankenstein.

Chapter 2: The Boy from Geneva
Victor Frankenstein grew up in Geneva, Switzerland, in a happy and loving family. His father, Alphonse, was a wise and generous man. His mother, Caroline, was gentle and kind. Victor was their only child for many years, and they gave him everything he needed — books, a warm home, and most importantly, their time.
When Victor was about five years old, his parents took in a little girl named Elizabeth. She was an orphan with golden hair and bright green eyes. From the moment she arrived, Victor thought of her as his most precious companion.
"She was not just my sister," Victor told Captain Walton. "She was my light."
Victor was a curious boy. While Elizabeth loved poetry and drawing, Victor loved science. He wanted to know how the world worked — what made lightning strike, what kept the planets spinning, what was hidden inside every living thing.
When he was thirteen, Victor's family traveled to a place near the mountains. One evening, a tremendous thunderstorm rolled in from the peaks. Victor stood at the window, unable to look away. A bolt of lightning struck an old oak tree in the yard — and in an instant, the entire tree was blasted to splinters.
Victor stared in amazement. What force could do that? What power could tear a tree apart in a single second? He wanted to understand it. He needed to understand it.
His father tried to explain that lightning was simply electricity — a natural force that scientists were only beginning to study. Victor became hungry to learn everything there was to know about electricity, about nature, about the mysterious forces that held the world together.
"That is where my trouble began," Victor said quietly. "With wanting to know too much."

Chapter 3: The Dream of Creating Life
Victor's mother became ill when he was seventeen years old. The whole family gathered around her, hoping she would recover. But she did not. She died peacefully one evening, holding Elizabeth's hand.
Victor was heartbroken. He could not eat. He could not sleep. And in his grief, one dark thought began to grow inside him: What if death could be defeated? What if a person could be brought back? What if life itself could be created?
He left Geneva and went to study at a university in a city called Ingolstadt. He thought that throwing himself into his studies would dull the pain of losing his mother. But the dream would not leave him.
At the university, Victor discovered two professors who changed his life. The first, Professor Krempe, told Victor to throw away all his old books and start fresh with modern science. The second, Professor Waldman, gave a lecture that set Victor's heart racing.
"The ancient dreamers of science imagined the impossible," Professor Waldman said. "But the scientists of today have achieved wonders. They have revealed the hidden workings of nature. There is no boundary to what they might yet discover."
Victor felt as if every word was aimed directly at him. After the lecture, he went to Professor Waldman's office and peppered him with questions until the old man smiled and offered to help him.
Victor began to study harder than he had ever studied before. He worked in the laboratory from morning until long after dark. He read every book he could find on chemistry, biology, and the nature of life. Slowly, an idea began to form in his mind — a wild, impossible, wonderful idea.
What if he could take lifeless matter — bones and muscle and tissue — and make it breathe?
What if he could create a living being?

Chapter 4: Secret Work in the Dark
Victor Frankenstein threw himself into his work completely. He stopped writing letters home. He forgot about his friends. He did not sleep properly for months at a time.
He spent long hours in graveyards and in the university's anatomy labs, studying how the human body was put together. He read everything written about the nature of life. He experimented late into the night while the rest of the city slept.
Slowly — terribly slowly — he began to believe it was possible.
The human body, he decided, was like a very complicated machine. If you could gather the right parts and find the right force to start it — the spark of life, the hidden energy in electricity — perhaps that machine could be made to run.
Victor began to build his creature in a small workshop he rented at the top of a tall building. He worked only at night, by candlelight. He gathered materials from laboratories and kept his windows covered so no one could see what he was doing.
He chose to build the creature large — nearly eight feet tall — because it was easier to work with bigger parts. He did not think much about what it would be like to look at such a creature when it was finished. He thought only about whether it would work.
Two years passed. Victor barely noticed. He wrote to his family less and less. He lost weight. His eyes became sunken and dark from too many sleepless nights. When old friends from the university tried to visit him, he made excuses and turned them away.
"I told myself I would rest when it was finished," Victor said to Captain Walton. "I told myself everything would be worth it. That I was doing something wonderful — something that would change the world."
He paused and looked down at his hands.
"I was wrong about nearly everything."

Chapter 5: The Creature Wakes
One night in November, after more than two years of secret work, Victor Frankenstein was finally ready. Rain hammered the windows of his workshop. Thunder growled in the distance. He stood before his creation — a giant figure stretched out on the table, enormous and still.
Victor leaned over his equipment with trembling hands. He took a deep breath and completed the final connection.
For one long moment, nothing happened.
Then — a finger moved.
Victor stepped back. The creature's chest rose. And fell. Rose again. Its eyes — pale and watery, like old glass — fluttered open.
Victor stared.
He had been so focused on the science, on whether it would work, that he had never truly imagined this moment. Now that it was here, he felt a wave of terror wash over him. The creature was enormous. Its skin was stretched tight over its body, yellowish and strange. The stitching where different parts had been joined together was clearly visible. Its lips were black. Its eyes stared blankly up at the ceiling.
Victor ran.
He stumbled down the stairs, burst out into the rainy street, and kept running until he reached the courtyard of the university. He paced back and forth, his heart hammering. After a while, the cold rain calmed him slightly. He told himself he had been foolish. He was a scientist. He should go back.
He went back to the workshop.
The creature was gone.
Victor did not know whether to feel relieved or more frightened than before. He went to his bedroom, wrapped himself in blankets, and eventually fell into a terrible, restless sleep full of dark dreams.
When morning came, his old friend Henry Clerval appeared at his door, having just arrived in Ingolstadt to study. Victor burst into relieved laughter and hugged his friend. For a moment, everything felt almost normal.
Then Victor collapsed. He had pushed himself too far, for too long. He fell into a long fever, and it was Henry who nursed him back to health over the following months.
Victor told Henry nothing about what he had built in that workshop.

Chapter 6: Letters from Home
Victor spent several months recovering from his illness, with Henry Clerval at his side. Henry was everything Victor had stopped being — cheerful, curious about the world in a healthy way, interested in people and languages and stories. Being around Henry helped Victor feel like himself again.
As Victor grew stronger, letters began arriving from Geneva. His father wrote faithfully, always asking when Victor was coming home. Elizabeth wrote long, beautiful letters full of descriptions of the garden, the lake, and the neighbors she saw every day.
Victor read these letters and felt a deep ache. He had been away for years. He had barely written. He had shut out everyone who loved him in order to focus on his work, and now, reading their warm words, he was ashamed.
Elizabeth's letters were especially precious to him. She described a young girl named Justine who lived with their family as a servant, almost like a younger sister. She wrote about the mountains changing color with the seasons. She urged Victor to take care of his health and to come home soon.
Henry also received wonderful news — he had been accepted to study at Ingolstadt, just as he had always hoped. He moved into the city, and for the first time in years, Victor had a real friend nearby.
Together, they took long walks through the countryside. Victor began to sleep properly again. He started to eat real meals. The shadow that had lived over him during his years of secret work began to lift.
He almost managed to stop thinking about the creature.
Almost.
Sometimes, in the space between waking and sleeping, he would see those pale, watery eyes staring up at him from the table in his workshop. He would hear the creak of the floor, the low sound of something large moving through the dark.
And he would pull his blankets tighter and try to think of Elizabeth's letters instead.

Chapter 7: Terrible News
Just when life seemed to be returning to normal, a letter arrived that shattered everything.
Victor's youngest brother, William, was dead.
He had been strangled in the hills outside Geneva during an evening walk. The family was devastated. Their father begged Victor to come home at once.
Victor packed immediately. Henry offered to come with him, but Victor said he needed to go alone. He climbed into a coach and rode hard toward Geneva, watching the mountains grow closer with every hour.
It was late at night when he reached the gates of the city — too late to enter, for the gates were locked. Victor decided to walk in the hills and wait for morning. The night was dark and stormy, with lightning flashing between the mountain peaks.
As Victor walked, lightning split the sky — and in that flash of white light, he saw a figure on the hillside.
His breath stopped.
It was enormous. Eight feet tall, moving with a strange and powerful ease among the rocks. It turned, and for just a moment, Victor caught a glimpse of that pale face, those blank eyes.
The creature. His creature.
Victor stood paralyzed as the figure disappeared into the darkness. His mind raced. William had been killed here, in these same hills. And the creature was here, in these same hills. Victor's stomach turned over with a horrible suspicion.
Had he caused his brother's death? Had his creation — brought into the world by his own hands — taken the life of an innocent child?
He was shaking when he finally entered Geneva in the morning. His father embraced him. His family wept together over William. And then they received news that made things even worse.
A young woman named Justine — the same Justine Elizabeth had written about so fondly — had been accused of killing William. A small portrait that William had been carrying was found in her pocket.
Victor said nothing about the figure he had seen on the hillside. He told himself he was not sure. He told himself there was no proof.
But deep down, he knew the truth.

Chapter 8: The Wrong Person Blamed
Justine Moritz was brought to trial for the murder of little William Frankenstein. The whole town seemed convinced of her guilt, and even some members of Victor's own family began to have doubts.
But Elizabeth did not waver for a single moment.
"I know Justine," she said firmly. "She loved William as if he were her own little brother. She is innocent."
Elizabeth stood up in court and spoke in Justine's defense. Victor sat in the courtroom and listened, and inside he was being torn apart.
He knew. He was almost certain that his creature had killed William. He was almost certain that Justine was innocent. But he had no way to prove it without revealing the terrible secret of what he had done in that workshop — the creature he had created and then fled from, the monster that was now wandering free.
Who would believe him? And if they did believe him, what would they think of him? They would know that everything that had happened — William's death, Justine's trial — had begun with Victor Frankenstein and his desperate ambition.
Justine herself, when questioned, remained calm and gentle. She did not understand how the portrait had gotten into her pocket. She could not explain it. But she refused to confess to something she had not done.
In the end, the court found her guilty.
She was put to death.
Victor returned home carrying a weight he could barely stand beneath. He had created a being that walked free in the world, and that being had now taken two innocent lives — William's, and Justine's. He had done this. His ambition, his secret, his pride had done this.
Elizabeth wept for Justine for many days. Victor sat in silence and could not weep at all. He felt that he deserved no comfort.

Chapter 9: Victor's Despair
After Justine's death, a heavy darkness settled over the Frankenstein household. Elizabeth moved quietly through the house, her laughter gone. Victor's father watched his eldest son with worried eyes. Victor himself barely spoke.
He began taking long, solitary walks in the mountains. He needed to be away from the city, away from the house that seemed full of the grief he had caused. He climbed higher and higher into the Alps, pushing himself up rocky paths through rain and wind, as though he could outwalk his own guilt.
One gray morning, he climbed to the edge of a great glacier called the Mer de Glace — the Sea of Ice — a vast frozen landscape stretching between the mountain peaks. It was spectacular and terrifying, like the surface of another world. Great columns of ice rose around him, blue-white and ancient.
Victor stood at the edge of the glacier, the cold wind cutting through his coat, and cried out into the empty air.
"Why am I alive? What good has my life brought to anyone?"
The mountains gave no answer.
Then he heard something.
Footsteps. On the ice. Coming fast.
Victor turned.
A figure was crossing the glacier, moving with impossible speed over the treacherous frozen ground. It was enormous. It leaped across cracks in the ice that Victor would never have dared cross. And it was heading straight toward him.
Victor wanted to run. His legs locked beneath him.
The creature stopped a short distance away and looked down at him. Its face was scarred and strange. Its eyes were filled with pain. When it spoke, its voice was low and careful, like someone who had taught himself to talk in secret, alone.
"I knew I would find you here," it said. "I have been looking for you for a long time."
"Monster!" Victor cried. "Did you kill my brother?"
The creature's expression shifted — not angry, but sad. "I will not deny what I have done," it said. "But before you condemn me, you must hear my story. You made me, Frankenstein. You owe me that much."

Chapter 10: The Creature's Story
Victor stood frozen on the ice as the creature began to speak. Despite his rage and fear, he found himself listening.
"When I first woke," the creature said, "I knew nothing. I was like a newborn — but with no one to guide me, no one to explain the world. You had run away. I was alone."
The creature described wandering out into the night after Victor fled. The world was overwhelming — bright, loud, cold, confusing. People who saw him screamed and ran, or threw things at him. He learned quickly to stay hidden during the day and move only at night.
He found shelter near a small cottage at the edge of a forest. Through a crack in the wall, he could watch a family inside — an old blind man named De Lacey, and his adult children, Felix and Agatha. They were poor but kind to each other. The creature watched them for months, learning everything they did.
He taught himself to speak by listening to them. He taught himself to read by finding their books. He read a book called Paradise Lost, about an angel who became an outcast. He felt that he understood it deeply.
He helped the family secretly — chopping wood for them at night, clearing paths through the snow — and felt a small flame of happiness in doing it.
"I wanted to be known to them," the creature said. "I wanted to tell them I was there, that I had cared for them. I wanted a friend."
Finally, the creature chose a moment when only the old man was home. Blind, De Lacey could not see him. And when the creature spoke to him, the old man was kind. He listened. He did not scream.
But then the others returned. They saw the creature crouched beside their father. Agatha fainted. Felix attacked the creature with a stick.
The creature ran.
When he returned the next day, the family was gone. They had packed everything and fled in the night.
"I had done nothing wrong," the creature said, and his voice cracked. "I had only wanted to be known. And they left because of what I look like. Because of what you made me look like."

Chapter 11: The Creature's Request
The creature's voice was steady, but Victor could see the pain behind his eyes — pain that had hardened, over time, into something colder.
After the family abandoned the cottage, the creature had wandered further. He came to Geneva. He did not know why, exactly — he only knew that somewhere in the world, there was one person who was responsible for his existence. One person who might owe him something.
And then he had seen William.
Victor tensed. The creature looked away.
"The boy saw me," the creature said. "He screamed, and I tried to quiet him — I was not thinking. I was so angry, Frankenstein. I had been cast out of everywhere. I had been met with nothing but fear and hatred. I did not mean to harm him. But I did."
Victor's jaw was tight. "And Justine?"
"I found a portrait in the boy's pocket. A woman's portrait. And I thought — here is someone beautiful and kind-looking, someone who will never look at me without horror — and in my bitterness, I placed it in her pocket while she slept. I knew she would be blamed."
The creature paused. "I am not proud of what I did. But I want you to understand what made me this way. I was not born cruel. I became cruel from loneliness."
He turned and looked directly at Victor.
"I have one request."
Victor said nothing.
"Make me a companion," the creature said. "Create another being like me — a female, someone who will not fear my face or my size, someone who will understand what I am. I do not ask for much. I ask only not to be entirely alone in the world. Give me a companion, and I will go far away — to the jungles of South America, somewhere no humans live — and you will never see either of us again."
Victor stared at him for a long time.
"And if I refuse?"
The creature's eyes were steady. "Then I cannot promise what my loneliness will make me do."

Chapter 12: A Terrible Promise
Victor stood on the edge of the glacier for a long time, thinking.
Every part of him wanted to say no. The creature had killed his brother. It had caused the death of Justine. How could he trust anything it promised?
But the creature's words had burrowed deep into his mind. I was not born cruel. I became cruel from loneliness.
Victor thought about those first moments after the creature woke — how he had run away in horror. He had built a living being, given it life, and then immediately abandoned it to the dark. The creature had come into the world with no one to explain it, no one to care for it, no guide or parent or friend. It had been driven away from every human it encountered.
Was the creature entirely responsible for what it had become? Or was Victor?
These thoughts twisted together in his head until he could no longer think clearly.
Finally, Victor agreed.
He would build a companion for the creature. When it was done, both of them would disappear forever. There would be no more harm done.
The creature's face — that strange, scarred face — showed something Victor had not expected to see there.
Relief.
"You will not regret this," the creature said. Then it turned and leaped back across the glacier, disappearing among the columns of blue ice with startling speed.
Victor walked down from the mountains alone, his mind already working through the terrible task ahead. He would need to return to his work, to those long dark nights in the laboratory. He had sworn he would never go back to that world.
But he had made a promise now. And somewhere beneath his dread, he felt the old familiar pull — the hunger to create, to discover, to push further than before.
He hated himself for feeling it.

Chapter 13: Travels and Doubts
Victor told his father he needed to travel to England for his studies. His father agreed readily — he was glad to see Victor taking an interest in something again. It was arranged that Henry Clerval would travel with him, as Henry also wished to pursue connections with scholars in London.
Victor and Henry traveled first through France and into England. London was a wonder — enormous, loud, full of ships and smoke and people from every corner of the world. Henry loved every moment of it. He kept tugging Victor's sleeve to point out a new sight, a new building, a new remarkable person they passed on the street.
Victor tried to enjoy it. But his mind was already on the work that waited.
He had decided to go north — to Scotland, to one of the distant islands where he could work in secret and be far from anyone who knew him. He gathered materials and equipment quietly along the way, telling Henry nothing.
In Scotland, Victor found a remote cottage on a small island off the northern coast. The island was wild and rocky, with almost no trees. The wind came in off the sea cold and constant. He set up a new laboratory in a small stone outbuilding.
But as he began to work, doubts crept in.
He was about to create a second creature. What would she be like? She had not agreed to this arrangement — she had no say in whether she would go to South America and hide from the world. And what if the two of them had children? He could be creating not just two beings, but a whole new race, living in hiding, filled with resentment.
What if the second creature was even more dangerous than the first?
Victor pushed these thoughts down and kept working. He had made a promise.
But the thoughts came back. Every night. Every time he looked at the half-finished form on his worktable.

Chapter 14: The Promise Broken
One night, while Victor was working late in his island laboratory, he looked up from his worktable — and found the creature watching him through the window.
Even after all this time, the sight still sent a jolt through Victor's whole body. The creature's face was pressed against the glass, its expression unreadable in the dark.
Victor looked from the creature to the half-finished form on the table, and all his doubts came crashing down at once.
What was he doing?
He was creating another being — another living thing — to be sent off to a remote jungle with a creature who had already killed two people. This second creature had no say in it. No choice. No knowledge of what was being decided for her. Victor was about to bring a life into the world and hand it over without question.
And what if they were not happy together? What if the second creature hated the first? What if they chose not to keep their promise, and instead turned on humanity together?
Victor's hands moved before he fully decided. He grabbed the form on the table and tore it apart.
A sound came from outside — a horrible, anguished cry, like the howl of a wounded animal. Victor rushed to the window. The creature was gone.
Later, in the night, the creature returned. It came to the door of the cottage, and its voice was low and shaking with rage.
"You have done this on purpose," it said. "You promised. I trusted you."
"I cannot do it," Victor said. "I will not create another being when I don't know what will happen."
"Then you have chosen this," the creature said. There was a long silence. "I will be with you on your wedding night, Frankenstein."
Then it was gone, vanished into the dark.
Victor sat alone in the cottage for a long time, shaking.

Chapter 15: More Tragedy
Victor dismantled his Scottish laboratory and traveled back to meet Henry in Edinburgh. But Henry could see that something was badly wrong. Victor looked pale and shaken, and he would not speak of what had happened on the island.
They began traveling back south. But near the coast of Ireland, Victor was arrested.
A man had been found murdered on a beach nearby. A boat matching Victor's had been seen in the area. The local magistrate looked at Victor's pale and guilty face and decided he was suspicious.
Victor was brought to see the body.
It was Henry Clerval.
Henry — kind, cheerful, wonderful Henry, who had left his family and his plans to travel with Victor, who had nursed him back to health, who had been his truest friend — lay dead on the wooden table. There were marks on his neck.
Victor collapsed.
He fell into another long fever, this time worse than before. For weeks he raved in his cell, seeing visions — his creature, Henry, William, Justine, all of them crowding into his dark dreams. The jailer's wife fed him soup and tried to keep him comfortable.
His father came at last, having heard of Victor's arrest. He was aged now, his hair white, his hands trembling. He sat by Victor's bed and held his hand, and the sight of his father's face made Victor weep.
Eventually, with his father's help, Victor was cleared of the murder. There was no real evidence against him. They were free to go home.
Victor knew who had killed Henry. He carried that knowledge like a stone inside his chest.
The creature had done this. This was what Victor's broken promise had cost.
He traveled home with his father in silence. The world outside the carriage window seemed very far away.

Chapter 16: The Wedding
Back in Geneva, Victor's father had one wish — he wanted Victor and Elizabeth to marry. He believed it would heal something in Victor, bring back the light that had gone from his eyes. Elizabeth wanted it too. She had waited patiently, writing her faithful letters all these years, never losing hope.
Victor agreed. But the creature's words had never left him: I will be with you on your wedding night.
He told himself the creature meant to attack him — to fight him, perhaps to kill him. And he thought: I will be ready. I will be armed. The creature will find that I am not afraid.
He never once thought the threat was aimed at Elizabeth.
The wedding was small and sweet. Elizabeth wore white flowers in her hair. Victor's father smiled through tears. Even Victor felt, for a few hours, as though the darkness might lift.
Afterward, they traveled to a little inn by the lake, where they planned to spend the night before journeying further. The evening was beautiful — the lake flat and silver, the mountains dark against the sky.
Victor was alert, watching every shadow. When Elizabeth went to their room to rest, he walked the corridor and the grounds, hand on the small pistol he had hidden in his coat.
And then he heard it.
A scream.
He ran.
He burst through the door of their room. Elizabeth lay across the bed, still and pale, her dark hair spread around her. She was gone. Through the open window, Victor caught one last glimpse of a huge figure on the lake, standing in a small boat, watching him.
The creature raised one hand — not in triumph, but in something that looked almost like grief.
Then the darkness swallowed it.

Chapter 17: The Hunter and the Hunted
When Victor's father heard what had happened to Elizabeth, the grief was too great. He had lost his youngest son, and Justine, and Henry, and now Elizabeth — all of them connected to his eldest son's secret. He died a few weeks later, quietly, in his sleep.
Victor was alone.
He went to the magistrate of Geneva and told him everything — the creature, the laboratory, all of it. The magistrate listened with wide eyes. He said he believed Victor, but that he did not know how to hunt a being so powerful and so far beyond normal experience.
Victor would have to be the hunter.
He returned to the graves of Elizabeth, and William, and his father, and knelt in the cold grass. He made a vow — not of grief, but of purpose. He would find the creature. He would chase it to the ends of the earth if he had to. He would not rest.
The creature seemed to understand this, because it began to leave signs for Victor. Scratches on trees. Notes carved into rock, pointing the way. It was leading Victor somewhere — taunting him, keeping just out of reach.
North. Always north.
Victor followed. He bought sled dogs. He hired guides until the land became too desolate even for guides. He survived on meat and frozen water. He grew lean and tough and driven.
And the creature's trail grew fresher.
"I believe it wanted to be followed," Victor told Captain Walton, back on the ship. "It had caused so much suffering. Perhaps it could not rest, any more than I could. Perhaps it needed an end, too."

Chapter 18: Lost on the Ice
Victor chased the creature for months across the frozen north. Every time he thought he had closed the distance between them, the creature surged ahead, tireless and powerful, its great legs carrying it over snow and ice that Victor's dogs and sled could barely manage.
The cold was extraordinary. Victor had not known cold like this was possible. His breath froze in his beard. His hands stopped feeling the reins. At night, he curled in his sleeping skins and dreamed of Elizabeth, of Henry, of his father's garden in Geneva.
But every morning, he got up and kept going.
He was not entirely sure anymore what he would do when he caught the creature. A fight, perhaps. An end — his, or the creature's, or both. He had stopped thinking about what happened after.
The ice cracked beneath him one day, and he lost two of his dogs and most of his food. He was stranded on a floating sheet of ice, unable to go forward or back. The creature had vanished ahead.
For two days, Victor drifted on the ice, growing weaker.
Then he saw the ship.
He flagged it down with strips torn from his coat, waving them overhead until the crew spotted him. They sent a boat across to bring him aboard.
Captain Walton sat with Victor as he was warmed and fed. Victor was obviously very ill — worn to nothing by months of cold and hunger and grief. But his eyes still burned.
"Is there a large figure on the ice?" he asked the captain. "Ahead of us? Going north?"
"Yes," Walton said, surprised. "My men saw such a figure yesterday."
Victor almost smiled. "Then I am not too late."
But that night, the fever returned. Victor knew it was worse this time.

Chapter 19: Victor's Final Words
Victor Frankenstein lay in the ship's small cabin and knew that he was dying. He had pushed himself past the edge of what his body could endure. His voice grew weaker each day.
But he kept talking to Captain Walton.
"I want someone to know what truly happened," Victor said. "All of it. Not as a story of glory — I was not glorious. I was foolish and proud. I wanted to do what no one had ever done. I did not stop to ask whether I should."
He told Walton about the creature's loneliness. About William and Justine and Henry. About Elizabeth.
"I am responsible," Victor said. "I made a being and gave it life, and then I ran from it because it frightened me. It was as lost and alone as any creature could be. And I never once truly tried to understand it until it was far too late."
He paused to rest, his eyes closing.
"I am not telling you this to excuse what it did. It did terrible things. But I did terrible things too. I played at being a god, and I had none of the patience or love a god would need. I made a person and refused to be its parent."
Walton leaned forward. "But you devoted your life to setting things right."
Victor looked at him. "Did I? Or did I spend the rest of my life running from my guilt, telling myself that if I could just catch the creature, it would balance all the harm I had caused? Catching something is not the same as taking responsibility."
He turned his face toward the wall.
"Do not become what I became," he whispered. "Whatever it is you are chasing — whatever discovery waits at the top of the world — ask yourself whether it is worth the cost. And whether you are willing to face the consequences."
Three days later, Victor Frankenstein was dead.

Chapter 20: The Creature's Farewell
Captain Walton sat alone in Victor's cabin after the end. He was struggling to understand everything he had heard. He felt the weight of it — the terrible and sad waste of so many lives, all unraveled from one man's unchecked ambition and one being's bottomless loneliness.
Late that night, he heard a sound in the cabin.
He turned.
The creature stood over Victor's body. It was enormous — everything Victor had described. Its scarred face was bent low, and it was weeping. The sound was strange, almost like the howling of wind, and very real.
Walton stepped back, too startled to speak.
The creature looked up. Its pale eyes found the captain. It did not attack him. It did not move toward him at all.
"I know what you are thinking," the creature said. Its voice was deep and careful, each word chosen with effort. "You are thinking I am a monster. You are thinking that everything that happened was my fault."
"Victor told me your story," Walton said carefully.
The creature looked down at Victor's still face. "He was the only person in the world who knew what I was. I hated him for what he did — for making me and leaving me. But he was still the only one who knew." It paused. "And now he is gone."
"What will you do?" Walton asked.
"End it," the creature said simply. "I will go north, further than anyone has gone, and build a fire, and that will be the end. I have no place in this world. I never did. But that was not entirely my fault."
It turned to go.
"Wait," Walton said. "I am sorry. For what happened to you."
The creature stopped. It did not turn around. For a moment, it was perfectly still.
Then it climbed out through the window and onto the ice, and moved away into the white distance, growing smaller and smaller until it was gone.
Captain Walton stood at the window for a long time, watching the empty ice.
He gave the order to turn the ship south.
