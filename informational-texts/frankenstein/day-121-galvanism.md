# Galvanism: Electricity and Life

In 1780, Italian scientist Luigi Galvani made a shocking discovery that would inspire Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* and launch the science of bioelectricity. He found that dead frogs' legs twitched when touched with metal during a thunderstorm. Had he discovered the spark of life itself?

## The Discovery

Galvani was dissecting frogs in his laboratory when a metal scalpel accidentally touched an exposed nerve. The dead frog's leg jerked violently. Galvani was astonished—what force made dead tissue move?

He experimented systematically, testing different metals, conditions, and animal parts. When he hung frog legs on iron hooks against brass railings during thunderstorms, they convulsed dramatically. Galvani concluded that animal tissues contained a special "animal electricity" that powered muscle movement.

## The Controversy

Alessandro Volta, another Italian scientist, disagreed. He believed the electricity came from the contact between different metals, not from the animal tissue. This led to a fierce scientific debate across Europe.

Volta was partially correct—the metal contact did create electricity. But Galvani was also right—animals do use electricity in their nervous systems. Both scientists had discovered important truths. Their dispute led Volta to invent the electric battery in 1800, one of history's most important inventions.

## Public Spectacle

Galvani's experiments became public entertainment. Scientists demonstrated that electric shocks could make dead animals twitch and even make executed criminals' corpses seem to come alive briefly. In 1803, Giovanni Aldini, Galvani's nephew, conducted a famous experiment on the corpse of an executed murderer.

When Aldini applied electricity to the dead man's face, the jaw quivered, an eye opened, and the face contorted. Witnesses reported that the corpse seemed about to sit up. Some spectators fainted. Others wondered if electricity might actually restore life to the dead.

Mary Shelley, writing *Frankenstein* in 1816, drew directly on these experiments. Her Victor Frankenstein uses electricity to animate his creature, reflecting the era's belief that electricity might be the "spark of life."

## Bioelectricity: The Real Science

Modern science confirms that electricity is fundamental to life—just not the way Galvani imagined. Your nervous system transmits signals through electrical impulses. Your heart beats because of electrical pulses. Your brain waves are electrical activity.

But this bioelectricity is chemical, not like current from a battery. Cells use ions (charged atoms) moving across membranes to create electrical signals. No external electrical shock can create life or consciousness—it can only trigger existing biological systems.

## Medical Applications

Though you can't bring the dead to life, electricity does have legitimate medical uses:

**Defibrillators** restart hearts that have stopped beating or fallen into dangerous rhythms. Controlled electrical shocks can reset the heart's natural electrical system.

**Pacemakers** use electrical pulses to regulate heartbeats in people whose natural heart rhythms malfunction.

**Electrotherapy** treats certain mental health conditions. Carefully controlled shocks can relieve severe depression when other treatments fail.

**Nerve stimulation** helps some chronic pain patients by interrupting pain signals with electrical pulses.

None of these "bring life" in the Frankenstein sense—they work with existing biological systems that use electrical signals naturally.

## Why the Myth Persisted

The idea that electricity could create life was so appealing because electricity itself seemed almost magical. It was invisible, powerful, and mysterious. Telegraph wires carried messages instantly across continents. Electric lights banished darkness. Arc lights created artificial "lightning."

If electricity could do all this, why couldn't it create life? This was the question Mary Shelley explored in *Frankenstein*—not as real science, but as a metaphor for the dangers of scientific ambition without ethical restraint.

## The Real Question

Galvanism's legacy isn't about whether electricity can create life. It's about the relationship between physics, chemistry, and biology. Galvani helped scientists understand that living things are not separate from physical laws—we're electrochemical machines following natural principles.

This realization was both exciting and disturbing. If life is just chemistry and electricity, what makes us special? Do we have souls, or are we just complex machines? These philosophical questions, raised by galvanism, remain relevant today as we study consciousness, create artificial intelligence, and manipulate genes.

Victor Frankenstein's horror at creating life reflects Galvani-era anxieties about science discovering—or creating—things that should remain mysteries. The novel asks: just because we *can* do something scientifically, *should* we?
