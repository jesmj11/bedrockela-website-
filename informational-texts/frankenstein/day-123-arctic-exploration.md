# Arctic Exploration in the 1800s

*Frankenstein* begins and ends in the Arctic, where Captain Walton's ship is locked in ice while he searches for the North Pole. This wasn't just atmospheric setting—Arctic exploration was a major scientific and national project in Mary Shelley's time, combining adventure, discovery, and often tragedy.

## Why the Arctic?

In the early 1800s, large portions of Earth remained unexplored. The Arctic represented one of the last great geographical mysteries. European nations sponsored expeditions for several reasons:

**Scientific knowledge**: Understanding Earth's geography, climate, and magnetic poles  
**National prestige**: Being first to the North Pole or finding the Northwest Passage  
**Commerce**: Finding shorter shipping routes between Europe and Asia  
**Adventure**: The thrill of discovering the unknown

## The Northwest Passage

European explorers sought a water route through the Arctic connecting the Atlantic and Pacific oceans—the fabled Northwest Passage. Such a route would shorten voyages to Asia by thousands of miles, making whoever controlled it wealthy and powerful.

Numerous expeditions searched for this passage from the 1500s onward. Many ended in disaster as ships became trapped in ice, crews suffered scurvy and starvation, and cold killed unprepared explorers. The passage exists, but wasn't successfully navigated until 1906, and isn't practical for shipping even now due to ice.

## Extreme Conditions

Arctic explorers faced brutal challenges:

**Cold**: Temperatures dropping to -50°F or lower, freezing exposed skin in minutes  
**Ice**: Ships could be crushed by shifting ice floes or trapped for years  
**Darkness**: Months without sunlight during polar winter  
**Isolation**: No communication with the outside world for months or years  
**Food shortages**: Difficulty preserving food; scurvy from lack of fresh vegetables  
**Navigation**: Magnetic compasses unreliable near the North Pole

Explorers wore fur clothing, used dogs to pull sleds, and built ice shelters. Yet many died from exposure, starvation, lead poisoning (from canned food), or accidents.

## Famous Expeditions

**William Edward Parry** (1819-1820) sailed farther north than anyone before him, reaching Melville Island. His ships spent the winter trapped in ice, and the crew survived by hunting, rationing food carefully, and staying active.

**Sir John Franklin** (1845) led the most famous—and tragic—Arctic expedition. His two ships, HMS Erebus and Terror, vanished with 129 men. Search parties spent decades looking for survivors. Eventually it was discovered that the ships became icebound, Franklin died, and the crew abandoned ship attempting to walk south. All perished from cold, starvation, and possibly lead poisoning.

**Roald Amundsen** (1903-1906) finally completed the Northwest Passage, though it took three years. By then, the Panama Canal was under construction, making the Arctic route commercially irrelevant.

## Scientific Discoveries

Despite the hardships, Arctic exploration generated valuable knowledge:

- Mapping previously unknown coastlines and islands  
- Understanding ocean currents and ice formation  
- Studying Arctic wildlife and its adaptations to extreme cold  
- Learning about Earth's magnetic field near the poles  
- Proving or disproving theories about polar geography

## Connection to Frankenstein

Mary Shelley set *Frankenstein's* frame story in the Arctic deliberately. Captain Walton's obsessive drive to reach the North Pole mirrors Victor Frankenstein's obsessive drive to create life—both pursue dangerous goals despite warnings.

The Arctic's extreme environment symbolizes isolation, the limits of human knowledge, and nature's power. Just as ice can trap and crush ships, Victor's ambition traps and crushes him. The setting reminds readers that some frontiers might be better left unexplored.

When Walton's ship becomes trapped in ice, his crew begs him to turn back rather than risk everyone's lives for his glory. Unlike Victor, Walton listens—he learns from Victor's mistake and chooses responsibility over ambition.

## Legacy

Arctic exploration taught important lessons about human limitations, the importance of preparation, and the value of indigenous knowledge. Explorers who learned from Inuit peoples about clothing, shelter, and food survived. Those who relied only on European methods often died.

Today, the Arctic faces new challenges from climate change. The ice that stopped Franklin's ships is melting, opening the Northwest Passage but threatening Arctic ecosystems. Modern exploration uses satellites and icebreakers instead of wooden ships—but the Arctic remains dangerous and mysterious.

Mary Shelley's decision to frame *Frankenstein* with Arctic exploration reminds us that pursuing knowledge and achievement involves real risks—and that wisdom means knowing when to stop pushing boundaries.
