# Isolation and Human Development

Victor Frankenstein's creature learns everything alone—language, reading, morality, and human nature—by secretly watching others. He develops intelligence and feelings but grows up in complete isolation. What happens when someone grows up without human connection? Science and sad real-world cases provide answers.

The creature represents an extreme thought experiment: what if someone developed without any direct human interaction? Would they still be human? Research on isolated children, known as "feral children," shows that human connection isn't optional—it's essential for normal development.

**Genie**, discovered in Los Angeles in 1970, had been locked alone in a room for 13 years. At age 13, she couldn't speak, walk properly, or understand social interaction. Despite intensive therapy, she never fully developed language or social skills. Her critical developmental windows had closed.

**Victor of Aveyron**, found in French woods in 1797, had apparently lived alone in the forest for years. Like Frankenstein's creature learning from the DeLacey family, Victor learned some skills from his teacher, but he never fully integrated into society.

These cases show that humans need interaction during critical periods to develop properly. The brain literally changes structure based on social experiences. Without human connection during childhood, certain abilities may never fully develop.

The creature in *Frankenstein* is more fortunate than real isolated children—he observes language and culture through the cottage wall. But observation isn't the same as participation. He understands humanity intellectually but can never join it. This makes his isolation even more painful: he knows exactly what he's missing.

Isolation doesn't just affect language and social skills—it affects mental health. Solitary confinement in prisons causes psychological damage even in adults with normal childhoods. Prisoners in isolation experience anxiety, depression, hallucinations, and cognitive decline. Humans are social animals; removing social contact harms us deeply.

The creature's violent turn stems partly from his isolation. Every attempt to connect fails. He can only watch others experience love, friendship, and acceptance while he remains forever outside. This kind of chronic rejection and loneliness can warp personality and morality.

Modern research confirms that loneliness is deadly. Chronic isolation increases risks of heart disease, depression, dementia, and early death. It literally shortens lives. The creature's demand for a companion isn't just emotional—it's a survival need.

Yet the creature also demonstrates remarkable resilience. Despite no formal education, he teaches himself to read with *Paradise Lost*, Plutarch's *Lives*, and *Sorrows of Young Werther*. He develops moral reasoning and self-awareness. This shows that while isolation damages development, human intelligence and will can partially compensate.

The tragedy is that the creature's intelligence makes his isolation worse. A less aware being might not suffer as acutely. But the creature understands exactly what he lacks: "I am alone and miserable; man will not associate with me." Awareness of his isolation is itself a form of torture.

Mary Shelley uses the creature's isolation to explore what makes us human. Is it our biology? Our ability to reason? Our capacity for emotion? Or our connections with others? The novel suggests that humanity emerges from relationships—we become fully human through interaction with other humans.

This has implications for how we treat isolated people today: prisoners in solitary confinement, elderly people living alone, individuals with social anxiety or disabilities that limit connection. If isolation damages humanity itself, we have ethical obligations to prevent unnecessary isolation and help isolated people connect.

The creature's story is a warning: abandonment creates monsters. Not because isolated people are inherently dangerous, but because isolation damages mental health, warps development, and creates desperate needs that might be fulfilled through destructive means. Victor's greatest crime isn't creating life—it's creating life and then denying it the human connection necessary for healthy development.
