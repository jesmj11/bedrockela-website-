Chapter 1
How Robin Hood Became an Outlaw
In the days of good King Henry the Second, when England was still wild with thick forests and long stretches of untamed land, there lived a young man named Robin Hood in the town of Locksley, in Nottinghamshire. He was eighteen years old, tall and strong, with sharp eyes the color of a summer sky and hair as brown as an oak leaf in autumn. He could shoot an arrow straighter than any man in the county, and he walked through the greenwood as quietly as a deer.
Robin lived a simple life with his father, a forester who served the king by tending the royal deer in Sherwood Forest. From his father, Robin had learned to love the woods — the way sunlight fell in golden patches through the canopy, the rustle of creatures in the underbrush, the sweet smell of moss after a rain. He knew every path and every stream for miles around, and by the time he was fifteen, there was no better archer in all of England.
His father had taught him to shoot with a longbow before he could barely lift one. "The bow doesn't care how strong you are, lad," his father would say, adjusting Robin's grip on the smooth yew wood. "It cares how patient you are. How still you can be. An arrow finds its mark when your mind is quiet." Robin practiced every day until his fingers were calloused and his arms ached, until the bow felt like an extension of his own body, until he could hit a target at two hundred paces without thinking about it at all.
One fine morning in May, Robin heard that the Sheriff of Nottingham was holding a great archery contest. The prize was a fine golden arrow, and Robin's heart leaped at the thought. He polished his best longbow until the wood gleamed, chose six of his straightest arrows from the quiver hanging by the door, and set off through the forest toward Nottingham town. His father called after him to be careful, but Robin just waved and laughed. He was eighteen years old and felt invincible, which is a dangerous way to feel.
The road wound through the king's forest, where great oaks stood like pillars holding up a green cathedral roof. Robin walked with a spring in his step, whistling a merry tune that startled the sparrows from the hedgerows. Wildflowers dotted the path — yellow primroses and white hawthorn blossoms — and the air was thick with the scent of fresh growth. Everything felt alive and promising. He was young, he was strong, and he could outshoot any man alive. What could possibly go wrong?
He had not gone two miles when he came upon a group of fifteen foresters sitting beneath a spreading oak tree, eating their midday meal and drinking ale from leather flasks. They were rough men, thick-necked and red-faced from too much drink, and they wore the green coats of the king's forest service. Their bows and quivers were piled carelessly beside them, and the remains of a roasted rabbit lay scattered on a cloth between them.
"Well now," called out one of them, a big fellow with a black beard and small, mean eyes, "where might you be heading, young pup, with that fine longbow on your shoulder? Going to hunt butterflies, are you?" His companions snickered and elbowed each other.
"I'm bound for Nottingham," Robin answered cheerfully, refusing to be baited. "To win the golden arrow at the Sheriff's contest."
The foresters burst into laughter. The black-bearded man slapped his knee so hard that ale sloshed from his flask. "You?" he howled. "A boy barely old enough to shave? You'll win the golden arrow? That's the finest joke I've heard all week!" He turned to his companions, tears of laughter running into his beard. "Did you hear that, lads? This little sparrow thinks he can shoot!"
Robin's face grew hot. He could feel the blood rising in his cheeks, and his hands tightened at his sides. He was not a boy to be laughed at. His father had always warned him about his temper — "Your pride will be your downfall, Robin" — but right now, with fifteen men laughing at him, his father's words seemed very far away.
"I'll take any wager you care to make," Robin said, his voice steady and cool despite the fire in his chest. "Name your target."
The black-bearded forester wiped his eyes and grinned a wolf's grin. "Twenty marks of silver says you can't hit a deer at a hundred paces." He pointed through the trees to where a herd of the king's deer grazed in a sunny meadow. The meadow was a good hundred and fifty paces away, not a hundred, but the forester didn't seem concerned about honesty. "See that fine hart there, the one with the great rack of antlers? Hit him, and I'll pay you twenty marks. Miss, and you owe me the same."
Now, Robin knew he should not have taken the bet. The king's deer were protected by the forest laws — harsh, terrible laws that the Normans had brought to England. To kill a deer without permission was a serious crime. A man could lose his ears for it, or his eyes, or his freedom. Men had been hanged for less. Robin knew all of this. His father had told him a hundred times.
But his pride had been stung, and when you are eighteen years old and your blood is hot and fifteen men are laughing at you, pride can make you very, very foolish.
Robin nocked an arrow, feeling the familiar click as the shaft settled against the string. He drew the bowstring back to his ear, the way his father had taught him — smooth and steady, no jerking, no rushing. The world narrowed to a single point: the hart standing broadside in the meadow, its great antlers catching the sunlight. Robin breathed in. Breathed out. And let fly.
The arrow sang through the air in a graceful arc, rising and falling with the inevitability of a stone dropped from a cliff. It struck the hart clean through the heart. The great animal took three staggering steps forward, its legs buckling, and fell heavily into the tall grass. The meadow went silent except for the startled bleating of the other deer as they scattered into the trees.
The foresters went silent too. Fifteen jaws hung open. The black-bearded man's grin had frozen on his face like a mask, and his small eyes had gone very wide.
"A wager is a wager," Robin said quietly. "Pay what you owe."
The forester's face twisted with sudden rage. The laughter was gone now, replaced by something ugly and dangerous. He had no intention of paying twenty marks to a boy — twenty marks was more than he earned in a month. "You fool!" he snarled, lurching to his feet so fast that his flask went spinning. "You've killed one of the king's deer! That's a hanging offense, that is! I'll see you dragged before the Sheriff in chains!"
"You made the wager," Robin said, standing his ground even as the bigger man advanced on him. "You pointed at the deer. You set the terms."
But the forester had already snatched up his bow. "Run, boy!" he shouted, and without another word, he nocked an arrow and let it fly at Robin's head.
Robin ducked, and the arrow hissed past his ear close enough to part his hair. He felt the wind of it on his skin, felt the cold shock of how close he had just come to dying. And something shifted inside him — something that had been tame and civilized went wild and fierce. Without thinking, acting on pure instinct, he pulled an arrow from his quiver, drew, and fired back.
His aim, as always, was true. The arrow struck the forester in the chest, and the big man staggered backward, a look of pure surprise on his face, and fell with a crash that shook the ground.
For a moment, everything was still. The birds had stopped singing. The wind had died. The other foresters stared at their fallen companion, then at Robin, their faces cycling through shock, disbelief, and then — slowly, terribly — rage. Robin saw their hands reaching for their bows, saw the murder gathering in their eyes, and he knew with absolute certainty that if he stood here one second longer, he would die.
He ran. He ran faster than he had ever run in his life, crashing through the underbrush, leaping over fallen logs, dodging between the ancient oaks. Arrows whistled past him, thudding into trees and kicking up clods of earth at his feet. One grazed his sleeve, tearing the fabric. Another shattered against a rock inches from his ankle. But Robin knew these woods better than any man alive. He ducked into a ravine the foresters didn't know existed, splashed through a stream to kill his trail, and scrambled up a bank thick with brambles. He crawled through a gap in a fallen oak that was invisible unless you knew it was there.
He ran and crawled and climbed until his lungs burned and his legs trembled and the shouts of the foresters had faded to nothing behind him, replaced by the quiet breathing of the forest.
When at last he stopped, leaning against a mossy oak with his heart hammering against his ribs, Robin Hood realized that his old life was over. He had killed a man. He had slain one of the king's deer. He was an outlaw now, and there was no going back. Not to Locksley. Not to his father's house. Not to the life he had known.
Robin slid down the trunk of the oak and sat on the forest floor, his back against the rough bark. For a long time he just breathed. His hands were shaking. There was another man's blood on his arrow, and nothing in the world could wash it away. He thought about his father, who would hear the news by evening. He thought about the archery contest he would never attend. He thought about the simple, good life that had been snatched away in the space of a single foolish moment.
But as he sat there in the green shade of Sherwood Forest, listening to the birdsong slowly returning and the gentle whisper of the wind through the canopy, something strange happened. Instead of despair, he felt a fierce, wild joy rising in his chest like a flame. The forest stretched around him in every direction, vast and deep and free. No walls. No master. No one to tell him what to do or where to go.
"Well then," Robin said aloud, and a grin spread across his face despite everything. "If I must be an outlaw, I'll be the best outlaw England has ever seen. I'll rob from those who have too much and give to those who have too little. And I'll make this greenwood my kingdom."
And that is exactly what he did.
Word spread quickly through the villages and farms around Sherwood Forest. The story of Robin's flight grew with each telling — by the time it reached Nottingham, Robin had supposedly fought off fifty men single-handedly. Before long, other men who had fallen afoul of the harsh Norman laws began to seek Robin out. They came quietly, slipping into the forest at dusk, following whispered directions from sympathetic villagers.
They were men who had been taxed until they had nothing left, men whose land had been stolen by greedy lords, men who had been thrown into prison for poaching a rabbit to feed their starving children. They were farmers and craftsmen, former soldiers and wandering laborers — ordinary men pushed past the breaking point by extraordinary cruelty.
One by one, and then in groups of two and three, they came to join Robin Hood in Sherwood Forest. Within a year, he had gathered a band of over a hundred men, all dressed in suits of Lincoln green, all deadly with the longbow, and all loyal to Robin Hood and to each other unto death.
Robin gave them rules. They would rob the rich — the corrupt lords, the greedy bishops, the dishonest merchants — but they would never harm the poor. They would take from those who had too much and give to those who had nothing. They would never kill except in self-defense. And they would treat every person, rich or poor, with courtesy and respect — right up until the moment they emptied their purses.
They called themselves the Merry Men, and the forest rang with their laughter. For the first time in many of their lives, they were free. And for the first time in his life, Robin Hood had found his purpose.
The Sheriff of Nottingham, meanwhile, ground his teeth and plotted his revenge. But that is a story for another chapter.


Chapter 2
Robin Hood Meets Little John
It was a golden afternoon in late summer when Robin Hood had his first great adventure as an outlaw. He had been living in Sherwood Forest for nearly a year, and his band of Merry Men had grown to a fine company of bold fellows who could shoot, fight, and vanish into the trees like woodland spirits. But Robin was restless. Camp life was comfortable enough — Much the Miller's Son kept them fed, the lean-tos were snug against the rain, and Alan-a-Dale's songs kept spirits high — but Robin Hood was not a man built for comfort. He needed excitement the way a fire needs air.
He had sent his men out in groups to watch the roads and relieve wealthy travelers of their excess gold, and he himself had gone wandering alone through the forest, following deer trails and old footpaths that wound through the deepest parts of the greenwood. He walked for hours, enjoying the solitude, the way the light changed as the canopy thickened, the distant drumming of a woodpecker somewhere high above.
He came at last to a narrow bridge over a rushing stream. The bridge was nothing more than a single log, a foot wide at most, spanning a gap where the water churned white over jagged rocks fifteen feet below. It was not the sort of crossing that invited leisurely strolling. You planted your feet, you kept your balance, and you moved quickly. That was the sensible thing to do.
Robin was halfway across, placing his boots carefully on the damp bark, when he saw a stranger coming from the other direction. And what a stranger he was.
The man was enormous — the tallest person Robin had ever seen, standing a full seven feet from the soles of his leather boots to the top of his shaggy brown head. He was broad as a barn door, with shoulders that would have blocked a hallway and arms that looked like they'd been carved from the same oak as the bridge. He wore a simple tunic of brown wool, patched at the elbows, and he carried a quarterstaff in his hands that looked more like a young tree than a walking stick — eight feet of solid, knotted oak, polished smooth by years of use.
They met in the middle of the bridge and stood face to face. Or rather, face to chest, since the stranger was nearly a foot taller than Robin. Neither one moved. The stream roared beneath them.
"Step aside," said Robin, trying to sound authoritative and finding it difficult when he had to crane his neck to make eye contact, "and let the better man cross."
The giant looked down at Robin with eyes that twinkled with amusement, like a friendly bear being confronted by a particularly bold squirrel. "Step aside yourself," he said, in a voice as deep as a drum. "I was here first, and I yield the path to no man. Not even one who thinks well of himself."
"Do you know who I am?" Robin asked, his hand moving to the bow on his shoulder. "I am Robin Hood, and these are my woods. Every tree, every path, every bridge. I could put an arrow through you before you could blink."
"Aye, you could," said the big man calmly. He didn't flinch, didn't step back, didn't even shift his weight. "And that would make you a coward, wouldn't it? Shooting an unarmed man with a bow while you stand out of reach. Where I come from, a real man settles things face to face, with good English oak." He tapped his quarterstaff on the bridge, and the log shuddered. "If you're half the man you claim to be, put down that bow and fight me fair."
Robin felt his face flush. The stranger had a point, and Robin knew it. There was no honor in shooting a man who only carried a staff. Robin's own code — the code he asked his men to live by — demanded fairness, even in a fight. Especially in a fight.
"Wait here," Robin said. He went back to the bank and found himself a stout oak branch, six feet long and thick as his wrist. He trimmed it quickly with his knife, tested its weight and balance, and returned to the bridge. It wasn't as fine a staff as the stranger's, but it would do.
"Now then," Robin said, planting his feet on the log and raising his staff. "Shall we see who takes a bath today?"
"After you," said the stranger, and he raised his staff with the ease of a man lifting a twig.
What followed was the finest quarterstaff fight anyone in Sherwood Forest had ever seen — though there was nobody there to see it but the squirrels, the fish, and a very startled heron who had been napping on a rock downstream. The two men went at each other like thunder and lightning, their staves cracking together so hard that splinters flew and the sound echoed off the rocks like gunshots.
Robin was fast and clever, a natural fighter who relied on speed and cunning rather than brute strength. He darted in with quick strikes aimed at the stranger's knees and ribs, then danced back out of reach before the big man could counter. His footwork on the narrow log was extraordinary — he moved with the balance of a cat, shifting his weight from one foot to the other without ever looking down.
But the stranger was something else entirely. He was strong as an ox, and his reach with that enormous staff was devastating. Every blow he landed sent shockwaves through Robin's arms. And despite his size, he was not slow — his staff moved in wide, sweeping arcs that covered the entire width of the bridge, giving Robin nowhere to hide.
Back and forth they fought on that narrow log, with the stream roaring beneath them and the spray wetting their faces. Robin landed a solid crack on the stranger's ribs that would have felled a normal man. The stranger grunted, said "Not bad," and answered with a blow to Robin's shoulder that nearly knocked him into the water. Robin staggered, recovered, and came back with a flurry of strikes that drove the big man back three steps.
For a quarter of an hour they battled, and neither one could gain the upper hand. Robin had never fought anyone this good. His arms ached. His hands were raw from the impact of staff against staff. Sweat ran into his eyes, and he blinked it away, not daring to lower his guard for even a second.
Then the big man did something unexpected. He feinted left — a convincing fake that drew Robin's staff to block — and then swung right with a speed that seemed impossible for a man his size. The end of his staff caught Robin square on the shoulder, right where the earlier blow had already left a bruise.
Robin's feet went out from under him. The world tilted sideways. He saw sky, then water, then sky again, and then with a tremendous, bone-chilling splash, he plunged into the icy stream.
The cold hit him like a fist. The current grabbed him and tumbled him over the rocks. He came up sputtering and gasping, his Lincoln green soaked through, his hair plastered to his face, and his pride soaked even worse than his clothes. His staff was gone, swept away downstream.
The stranger stood on the bridge above him, leaning on his staff and laughing so hard that tears ran down his face. He laughed with his whole body — great, booming peals of joy that shook the bridge and sent birds scattering from the treetops.
Robin couldn't help it. Despite the cold, despite his bruised shoulder, despite the fact that he had just been thoroughly and publicly beaten on his own bridge in his own forest — he started laughing too. He laughed so hard that he nearly went under again, and he had to grab a tree root to keep from being swept downstream. The absurdity of it — Robin Hood, the great outlaw, the terror of the Sheriff, sitting chest-deep in a freezing stream while a giant laughed at him — was too much to bear with anything but humor.
"By heaven," Robin said, pulling himself up onto the bank like a wet dog and wringing out his cap, "that's the best beating I've ever taken! My arms will ache for a week. What's your name, you great mountain of a man?"
"John Little," said the stranger, still chuckling. He planted his staff and leaned on it, studying Robin with those warm, twinkling eyes. "From Hathersage way. I'm a wanderer, looking for work. I heard there was a man in these woods who stood up for common folk, and I thought I'd come see for myself."
"You found him," Robin said, shaking water from his boots. "Though I'll admit I'm less impressive when I'm dripping."
"John Little!" Robin shook his head and laughed again. "Well, John Little, you've just beaten Robin Hood in a fair fight, which no man has done before. How would you like to join my band? I could use someone who can crack skulls as well as you do."
The big man scratched his chin thoughtfully, as if considering a complicated business proposition. "I've heard of Robin Hood and his Merry Men," he said. "They say you rob the rich and give to the poor. They say no honest man need fear you. They say you're the best archer in England." He glanced at the stream. "They didn't mention the swimming."
"Every word of it is true," Robin said, ignoring the jab. "Except the swimming. That was new."
"Then I'll join you gladly," said John Little, and he reached down a hand as big as a ham to pull Robin up from the muddy bank. His grip was warm and solid, and when Robin clasped it, he felt — with the certainty that sometimes comes to people in important moments — that he had just met someone who would change his life forever.
That evening, around a roaring fire in the heart of Sherwood Forest, Robin's men held a great feast to welcome their newest member. Venison turned on spits over the flames. Bread and cheese were passed from hand to hand. Ale flowed freely, and the laughter rose into the trees like smoke.
Will Stutely, Robin's sharp-witted right-hand man, stood up on a stump with a mug of ale and declared that a man as big as John Little deserved a proper outlaw name. "Now, we outlaws are a contrary bunch," Will announced, grinning at the assembled company. "We rob the rich but we're generous. We break the law but we're honest. So it stands to reason that the biggest man in England should have the smallest name."
He raised his mug. "I christen him Little John!"
The Merry Men roared with laughter, and Little John laughed loudest of all — a laugh so big and warm that even the owls in the treetops seemed to listen. He accepted the name with grace and good humor, and from that moment forward, he answered to nothing else.
Little John became Robin Hood's best friend, his most trusted companion, and the strongest arm in all of Sherwood Forest. Where Robin was quick and clever, Little John was steady and immovable. Where Robin sometimes let his pride push him into foolishness, Little John was there to pull him back with a dry word and a raised eyebrow. They balanced each other perfectly, like the two sides of a scale.
And whenever Robin got too full of himself — which was often, for he was a proud young man who had never quite learned the lesson his father tried to teach him — Little John would remind him of the day he went swimming in the stream, and Robin would grin and say, "Aye, you great oaf, but next time I'll dunk you!"
He never did. But the threat of it kept their friendship lively for many years to come.


Chapter 3
The Sheriff’s First Scheme
News of Robin Hood's growing band spread far and wide, and nowhere did it spread with more fury than in the halls of the Sheriff of Nottingham. The Sheriff was a stout, red-faced man named Roger de Rainault, and he was as greedy as he was cruel. He had small, sharp eyes that were always calculating, fingers that were always counting, and a voice that could curdle milk when he was angry — which was most of the time, these days.
The Sheriff had a comfortable arrangement before Robin Hood came along. He taxed the poor until they had nothing left to give, skimmed a generous portion for himself, and sent the rest to Prince John in London with a letter explaining how terribly expensive it was to maintain law and order in Nottinghamshire. Nobody questioned him. Nobody dared. The Sheriff had soldiers, and soldiers had swords, and swords had a way of ending arguments.
Then Robin Hood appeared, and everything went sideways.
Every time the Sheriff sent a tax collector through Sherwood Forest, Robin and his men would stop the cart, count the money with polite thoroughness, keep the portion that had been unfairly squeezed from the poor, and send the collector running back to Nottingham in his underclothes. The collectors always arrived at the Sheriff's door shivering, embarrassed, and carrying a note from Robin that said something cheerful like "Thank you for your generous donation!" or "Better luck next time!"
Every time a rich lord traveled through the greenwood, Robin would appear from behind a tree with a bow in his hand and a smile on his face and invite the lord to dinner. The dinners were excellent — Robin set a fine table — but the bill at the end was always steep. "Think of it as a tax on selfishness," Robin would explain, while Little John counted gold coins into a leather sack.
The Sheriff was losing money, losing face, and losing his temper. His superiors in London were asking pointed questions. His wealthy friends were refusing to travel through Nottinghamshire. Even his own soldiers were starting to whisper that maybe Robin Hood wasn't so bad after all, especially compared to their boss.
"This cannot continue!" the Sheriff bellowed at a meeting of his officers one gray November morning. He pounded the oak table so hard that his wine cup jumped and left a red stain on the wood. "This ragged outlaw is making me a laughingstock! Every peasant in the county whispers his name like he's some kind of saint! My own cook was humming one of those blasted ballads this morning — I had him whipped!"
His officers shifted uncomfortably in their seats. Several of them had already tried to capture Robin Hood. Sir Oswald had led a patrol into the forest and come home without his horse, his sword, or his trousers. Captain Grimsby had set an ambush at the bridge and waited three days, only to discover that Robin's men had been watching him the entire time and had robbed a merchant on the opposite road while Grimsby sat in the rain. The less said about Sir Geoffrey's attempt, the better — though the story about the beehive was still making the rounds in the taverns.
"I have a plan," said the Sheriff, lowering his voice to a conspiratorial whisper. "If we cannot catch the fox in his forest, we will lure him out. I will hold a grand archery tournament, with a golden arrow as the prize. Not just any arrow — a masterwork, crafted by the finest goldsmith in London. Every man in Nottingham knows that Robin Hood is the finest archer in England. His pride — his insufferable, boundless, maddening pride — will not allow him to stay away. And when he shows his face, my men will seize him."
The officers exchanged glances. It was, they had to admit, a better plan than the beehive.
"But sir," ventured Captain Grimsby, "won't Robin Hood know it's a trap?"
The Sheriff smiled his most unpleasant smile. "Of course he'll know it's a trap. That's the beauty of it. He'll come anyway, because he cannot resist proving that he's the best. That is Robin Hood's weakness, gentlemen. His pride."
Word of the tournament reached Sherwood Forest within the week. Robin's scouts brought the news back to camp with great excitement — posters had been nailed to every tree and signpost within ten miles of Nottingham, announcing the contest in grand terms. A golden arrow! Open to all archers! By order of the Sheriff!
"It's a trap," said Little John flatly. He was sitting on a log, mending a bowstring with his big, careful fingers, and he didn't even look up. "The Sheriff wants you to show up so he can grab you. A blind man could see it."
"Of course it's a trap," Robin agreed cheerfully. He was examining one of the posters, which featured a rather unflattering drawing of a generic archer. "But what kind of archer would I be if I let a little thing like a trap keep me from winning a golden arrow?"
Little John sighed the long, deep sigh of a man who knows he is going to lose this argument but feels compelled to make it anyway. "The kind of archer who stays alive?"
"Where's the fun in that?"
"Robin."
"John."
They stared at each other. Little John sighed again. "Fine. But we're going in disguise, and we're bringing backup, and if anything goes wrong, we leave immediately. Agreed?"
"Agreed," Robin said, in the tone of a man who had no intention of leaving immediately if things got interesting.
The plan was simple but clever. Robin would dress as an old beggar, with a patched cloak borrowed from the camp's costume collection (every good outlaw band keeps disguises on hand) and a hood pulled low over his face. He would stoop and shuffle and cough and generally behave like a man who had no business holding a bow, let alone shooting one. Little John would go as a traveling friar, which required very little acting since he already ate like one. Will Stutely and a dozen others would scatter through the crowd dressed as farmers, merchants, and tradesmen, each one armed beneath his disguise. If anything went wrong, they would be ready.
On the morning of the tournament, the streets of Nottingham were packed with people. Farmers had come from miles around, leaving their fields and flocks for the excitement of a contest. The town square was bright with banners — red and gold and blue — and alive with the sound of trumpets and drums. Food vendors hawked meat pies and roasted nuts. Children ran between the legs of the crowd, shrieking with excitement. It was, by all accounts, the finest day Nottingham had seen in years.
The Sheriff sat in a high seat draped in purple cloth, looking very pleased with himself. Beside him sat a velvet cushion, and on the cushion lay the golden arrow — a thing of extraordinary beauty, gleaming in the autumn sunlight. The Sheriff's soldiers lined the edges of the field in their chainmail and helmets, their eyes scanning every face in the crowd for Robin Hood. They had been given a description: young, brown-haired, sharp-eyed, likely wearing Lincoln green. They were watching for exactly that.
They did not look twice at the stooped old beggar who shuffled up to the archery line, leaning on a crooked walking stick and coughing into a rag. His cloak was moth-eaten, his hood was pulled low, and he moved with the painful slowness of a man whose best years were long behind him. When he picked up a bow, the soldier at the registration table barely stifled a laugh.
The contest began, and one by one the archers took their shots. There were some fine bowmen in Nottingham — the Sheriff had made sure of it, wanting the contest to look legitimate — and the crowd cheered as arrow after arrow thudded into the targets. But when the stooped old beggar stepped up to the line, pulled back his bowstring with arms that were surprisingly strong for such an ancient fellow, and sent his arrow sailing straight into the center of the target — dead center, splitting the bull's-eye like a knife through butter — a hush fell over the crowd.
Round after round, the old beggar shot with impossible accuracy. His arrows flew like they had eyes, finding the center every single time. The other archers fell away one by one, outclassed and bewildered, until only the beggar and a tall forester from the Sheriff's own guard remained.
The forester shot first. His arrow struck just a hair's width from the center — a magnificent shot that would have won any ordinary contest. The crowd roared. The Sheriff leaned forward in his seat, grinning.
Then the old beggar stepped up. He seemed to take forever, squinting at the target with one eye closed, muttering to himself, adjusting his grip. The crowd grew restless. A few people shouted encouragement. The Sheriff drummed his fingers on the arm of his chair.
The beggar drew back his string slowly, slowly, slowly... and let fly.
The arrow struck the forester's shaft dead center and split it clean in two. The wooden halves fell away on either side, and the beggar's arrow stood quivering in the exact spot where the forester's had been. A perfect shot. An impossible shot. A shot that only one man in all of England could have made.
The crowd erupted. People screamed and threw their hats in the air. Women grabbed each other. Children stomped their feet. Even the Sheriff's soldiers forgot themselves and cheered, because they were archers too, and they knew magnificence when they saw it.
The Sheriff had no choice but to award the golden arrow to the old beggar. He did so with a frozen smile and a jaw so tight that his teeth ached, still scanning the crowd desperately for Robin Hood. "Fine shooting, old man," he said through gritted teeth. "What is your name?"
"Just a poor wanderer, my lord," croaked the beggar, pulling his hood even lower. "Thank you kindly for the pretty arrow. I'll treasure it always." And he shuffled away into the crowd, hunched and slow, just another old man among hundreds.
The Sheriff didn't realize he'd been tricked until that evening, when a messenger arrived at his door carrying a letter. The letter was tied to one of Robin's green-feathered arrows, which had been shot clean through the Sheriff's bedroom window from the street below and had stuck in the headboard of his bed — one inch above his pillow.
The letter read: "Dear Sheriff — Thank you for the lovely golden arrow. The tournament was wonderful fun, and your hospitality was most generous. I especially enjoyed the meat pies. I do hope we can do it again sometime. Your humble and obedient friend, Robin Hood."
The Sheriff's roar of rage could be heard three streets away. His servants said later that he broke two chairs, a table, and his personal record for continuous swearing.
In Sherwood Forest, the Merry Men passed the golden arrow from hand to hand, marveling at its beauty, and laughed until their sides ached. Little John, despite himself, was grinning.
"All right," he admitted. "That was worth it."
Robin placed the arrow in a position of honor at the center of camp, where it caught the firelight and glowed like a small sun. "This arrow isn't just a prize," he told his men. "It's a message. It tells the Sheriff — it tells everyone — that we can go anywhere, do anything, and there's not a thing he can do about it."
He was right. And the Sheriff knew it. And that, more than anything else, was what kept Roger de Rainault awake at night.


Chapter 4
Friar Tuck and the River Crossing
Robin Hood had heard tales of a certain friar who lived near Fountain Abbey, beside the river that wound through the eastern edge of Sherwood Forest. This friar, the travelers said, was no ordinary man of the cloth. He was as round as a barrel of ale, as strong as a plow horse, and as fond of a good fight as he was of a good meal — which was saying a great deal, because this particular friar was extraordinarily fond of good meals.
"They say he eats enough for three men," reported Will Stutely, who had gathered the intelligence. "And they say he once knocked out a knight with a leg of mutton."
"Accidentally or on purpose?" Robin asked.
"On purpose. The knight insulted his cooking."
"I must meet this man," Robin announced to his band one morning, rising from the breakfast log with the look of a man who has just found a new purpose in life. "Any friar who can fight as well as he can pray is exactly the sort of fellow we need in this company."
"You said that about the last three people you recruited," Little John pointed out, not looking up from his porridge. "And two of them tried to rob you."
"Third time's the charm," Robin said, and off he went before Little John could raise further objections.
He found the friar sitting on the grassy bank of the river, in a patch of warm sunshine, eating a meat pie the size of his own considerable head. The man was enormous — not tall like Little John, but wide in every direction, with a belly that strained magnificently against his brown robes and arms that would have looked more at home on a blacksmith than a holy man. His bald head gleamed in the sunlight like a polished stone. A broadsword — an actual broadsword, heavy enough to cut through armor — hung at his belt, which was unusual for a friar and frankly alarming. Three dogs lay at his feet, watching the meat pie with the desperate, unwavering intensity that only dogs and hungry outlaws can achieve.
"Good morning, holy father," Robin called from the opposite bank, putting on his most charming smile. "I find myself in need of crossing this river, and my boots are new. Fine calfskin, just bought. It would be a terrible shame to ruin them. Would you be so kind as to carry me across on your back?"
The friar looked up from his pie. He had a round, ruddy face, small bright eyes, and the expression of a man who has been interrupted during something important and is trying to decide how annoyed to be. He studied Robin for a long moment — taking in the Lincoln green, the longbow, the confident stance — and seemed to arrive at some private conclusion.
Without a word, he tucked the remainder of his pie into a fold of his robe, brushed the crumbs from his belly, waded into the river, and crouched down with his broad back to Robin. Robin, delighted that his charm had worked so easily, climbed onto the friar's back.
The friar carried him across the river without a single word of complaint. The water came up to the friar's waist, but he moved through it as steadily as a barge, his sandaled feet finding solid ground on the rocky riverbed with practiced ease. When they reached the far bank, Robin hopped off and landed neatly on dry ground.
"Thank you, good friar. Most kind of you. Now, I was wondering if —"
But the friar held up one thick finger. "My turn," he said, in a voice like a friendly avalanche. "I find I need to go back across, and my sandals, while old, are extremely comfortable. I'd hate to get them wet. Carry me."
Robin stared at him. The friar must have weighed close to three hundred pounds. Carrying him across the river would be like carrying a small horse through a rapids. But Robin Hood was not a man to back down from anything — it was his greatest strength and his most persistent flaw — so he gritted his teeth, bent down, and the friar climbed onto his back.
The weight was staggering. Robin's knees buckled. His boots, which he had been so concerned about protecting, immediately sank six inches into the muddy riverbed. He staggered forward, the water swirling around his waist, his every step an exercise in pure stubbornness. The friar, perched on his back like an enormous, cheerful boulder, hummed a hymn and seemed entirely comfortable.
"Lovely day for a swim," the friar observed pleasantly as Robin nearly went under.
Robin couldn't reply. His teeth were clenched too tightly. But somehow, gasping and wobbling and making sounds that were not remotely dignified, he made it to the far bank and deposited the friar on dry ground. His arms were shaking. His boots were ruined. His pride was intact, but only barely.
"Wonderful," said the friar, adjusting his robe. "Now carry me back again."
"What?" Robin said, bent double and breathing hard.
"I've changed my mind. I need to be on the other side after all. Terribly sorry for the inconvenience."
Robin looked at the friar. The friar looked at Robin. There was a twinkle in the friar's small, bright eyes that suggested he was enjoying this immensely.
So Robin carried him back. And then the friar carried Robin. And then Robin carried the friar again. Back and forth, back and forth, across the river and back, like some absurd, exhausting game that neither man was willing to lose. By the third crossing, Robin's arms were shaking. By the fourth, his legs were jelly. By the fifth, he was fairly certain that he was going to die in this river, carrying a monk, and that his tombstone would read "Here lies Robin Hood — he couldn't say no."
The friar, meanwhile, showed no sign of fatigue whatsoever. Each time he carried Robin, he did so with the same steady, unhurried ease. And each time Robin carried him, the friar seemed to get heavier, as if he were secretly filling his pockets with river stones.
On the sixth crossing, Robin had had enough. He was in the middle of the river, knee-deep in rushing water, with three hundred pounds of friar on his back and absolutely no dignity left to lose. Something inside him snapped — not anger, exactly, but a sort of wild, desperate hilarity.
He shifted his weight, bent forward, and dumped the friar into the river with the most almighty splash either of them had ever heard. Water erupted in every direction. Fish fled. The three dogs on the bank began barking hysterically.
The friar came up like a whale breaching — roaring, water streaming from his bald head, his brown robes billowing around him like the sails of a very round ship. His face was a picture of outraged astonishment. And then, without a moment's hesitation, he drew his broadsword from his belt — where it had apparently been hanging, completely dry, this entire time — and let out a war cry that sent the birds scattering from every tree within half a mile.
"Right then!" the friar bellowed, his voice echoing off the rocks. "We'll settle this like men!"
Robin drew his own sword — thank heaven he'd worn it — and they fought right there in the middle of the river, splashing and slashing and shouting like madmen. The friar fought with an enthusiasm that was frankly terrifying. He swung his broadsword in great, sweeping arcs, sending up curtains of spray with each stroke. His footwork in the rocky riverbed was astonishingly sure for a man of his size, and his blade moved with a speed and precision that spoke of serious training.
Robin was hard-pressed. He was lighter, faster, and more technically skilled, but he was also exhausted from carrying three hundred pounds of friar back and forth across a river five times. His arms felt like overcooked noodles. His footwork, usually his greatest asset, was hampered by the current and the slippery rocks.
Still, they fought on — twenty minutes of the finest swordfight ever conducted knee-deep in a river, accompanied by the enthusiastic barking of three dogs who couldn't decide which human to support. Robin landed a cut on the friar's sleeve. The friar answered with a stroke that shaved a button off Robin's tunic. Back and forth they went, neither able to gain the upper hand, both unwilling to yield.
Finally, Robin held up his hand. "Enough! Enough! I yield — or at least I'd like to stop before I drown. I am Robin Hood, and I came here to ask you to join my band of Merry Men. But first, would you please stop trying to kill me?"
The friar lowered his sword. Water dripped from his blade. His eyes, which had been blazing with battle fury, softened into something warm and surprised. Then he threw back his head and laughed — a great, booming, joyful laugh that seemed to make the river itself dance.
"Robin Hood! Why didn't you say so in the first place? I've been wanting to meet you for months! Heard all about you from the villagers. The man who robs the rich and feeds the poor. The thorn in the Sheriff's backside." He sheathed his sword and waded forward, extending a hand the size of a dinner plate. "The name's Tuck. Friar Tuck. And I've been waiting for someone to come along and give me a reason to leave this riverbank."
"Will you join us?" Robin asked, shaking the enormous hand.
"Will there be food?" asked Friar Tuck, with the air of a man asking the only question that truly matters.
"The best venison in Sherwood Forest. And Much the Miller's Son is a fine cook."
"Then lead the way, my son. Lead the way."
As they climbed out of the river, dripping and bruised and grinning like fools, Robin asked the question that had been nagging at him. "Tuck — why did you make me carry you back and forth so many times? You knew who I was from the start, didn't you?"
Tuck smiled mysteriously and patted his round belly. "I had my suspicions. But I wanted to see what kind of man you were. Any fool can shoot an arrow or swing a sword. But a man who will carry a three-hundred-pound friar across a river five times without complaining — well, that's a man I can follow."
"I complained," Robin pointed out.
"Not as much as most would have. That's the point."
Friar Tuck became one of the most beloved members of Robin's band. He said prayers over their meals, tended their wounds with surprising gentleness, performed wedding ceremonies for those who needed them, settled arguments with wisdom and humor, and ate roughly twice as much as any other man in camp. He also turned out to be one of the finest fighters in the group — his broadsword work was exceptional, and his bare-knuckle boxing was even better.
His three dogs — Fury, Beauty, and Fangs — became the unofficial guardians of the camp. Fury was fierce and territorial. Beauty was sweet and gentle, unless you threatened her master, in which case she transformed into a snarling fury that would make her namesake proud. And Fangs was old and mostly deaf but had an uncanny ability to find anyone trying to sneak into camp, usually by falling asleep on top of them.
As for the river crossing, it became one of the most popular stories in camp. The Merry Men never tired of hearing how Robin Hood had been outsmarted by a fat friar with a meat pie and a broadsword. Robin bore the teasing with good grace, because he knew that the best stories are the ones where the hero doesn't always win.
And whenever anyone asked Friar Tuck why he'd made Robin carry him back and forth so many times, Tuck would simply smile, pat his belly, and say, "A little humility is good for the soul. Even Robin Hood's soul."
Especially Robin Hood's soul.


Chapter 5
The Rescue of Will Stutely
Not all of Robin Hood's adventures ended in laughter. There came a dark day when one of his dearest friends was nearly lost forever, and it taught every man in Sherwood Forest that the life of an outlaw, for all its freedom and merriment, carried a price that could be paid in blood.
Will Stutely had been with Robin from the very beginning — one of the first men to seek him out in Sherwood Forest after Robin became an outlaw. He was a lean, quick-witted man with a fox-sharp face, hair the color of a copper penny, and a tongue that could cut deeper than his sword. He was clever, he was brave, and he was utterly loyal to Robin Hood. He served as Robin's chief scout, the man who went ahead to spy out the roads, who kept watch when the camp slept, and who could disappear into the forest like a shadow melting into darkness.
Will had a gift for blending in. He could walk into any tavern in Nottingham, adopt the accent of a local farmer, and have the innkeeper telling him secrets within an hour. He had disguised himself as a merchant, a beggar, a traveling preacher, and once — memorably — as an elderly washerwoman, which had fooled everyone except a cat, who had bitten him on the ankle. The Merry Men still told that story at feasts.
But one autumn morning, Will's extraordinary luck ran out in the most ordinary way possible.
He had gone to the Blue Boar Inn in Nottingham, disguised as a farmer, to listen for news about the Sheriff's latest plans. He sat in a dark corner nursing a mug of ale, his farmer's hat pulled low, keeping his ears open and his head down. The inn was busy — farmers, merchants, a few off-duty soldiers — and Will was gathering useful information. He had already learned that the Sheriff was planning a new tax and that a shipment of gold was expected from London next Tuesday.
All was well until a ginger cat rubbed against his leg under the table. Will, who had never forgotten the ankle-biting incident, flinched and shifted his legs to push the cat away. In doing so, his cloak fell open — just for a moment, just a flash — revealing the Lincoln green tunic underneath.
A sharp-eyed serving girl saw the flash of green. Her name was Bess, and she had a cousin who worked for the Sheriff, and the Sheriff paid good money for information about Robin Hood's men. Bess put down her tray, slipped out the back door, and ran straight to the Sheriff's guardhouse.
Within minutes, a dozen soldiers burst through the front door of the Blue Boar with swords drawn and murder in their eyes. The inn erupted in chaos — tables overturned, mugs shattered, patrons scrambled for the exits.
Will fought like a wildcat. He threw his mug of ale into the first soldier's face, blinding him. He overturned a heavy table into the path of three more, sending them sprawling. He grabbed a stool and swung it with both hands, cracking it across the helmet of a fourth soldier with a sound like a church bell. He made it halfway to the back door before they overwhelmed him — six men piling on at once, bearing him to the ground under their combined weight.
They chained his hands behind his back and dragged him through the streets to Nottingham Castle, where the Sheriff was waiting with a smile that would have frozen a bonfire.
"At last!" the Sheriff crowed, pacing around Will like a cat circling a mouse. "One of Robin Hood's own men, caught red-handed — or should I say, green-handed?" He laughed at his own joke. Nobody else did. "We'll hang him at noon tomorrow, in the town square, where everyone can see. And when Robin comes to watch — because he will come, he always does — we'll catch him too."
Will Stutely stood in chains before the Sheriff, his face bruised and one eye already swelling shut from the fight, and he said nothing. He didn't beg. He didn't plead. He didn't give the Sheriff the satisfaction of a single word. He simply looked at the man who planned to kill him with steady, unafraid eyes, and the Sheriff — though he would never admit it — felt a chill.
News reached Sherwood Forest that very evening. A woodcutter named Thomas, who had been drinking in the inn when Will was taken, came running through the forest trails, breathless and pale with terror, and told Robin everything. He described the soldiers, the chains, the Sheriff's gloating.
Robin listened in silence. His face went white as ash. Then it went red as fire. He stood up from the log where he'd been sitting, and every man in the camp fell silent, because they had never seen Robin Hood look like that before. His eyes were burning. His hands were shaking. And his voice, when he spoke, was quiet and cold and utterly terrifying.
"We ride at dawn," Robin said. "Every man. Full weapons. Every bow strung, every blade sharpened. We are going to Nottingham, and we are bringing Will Stutely home."
Nobody argued. Nobody suggested caution or patience or alternative plans. Will Stutely was one of their own, and the code of the Merry Men was absolute: no one gets left behind.
Little John laid a heavy hand on Robin's shoulder. "We'll get him back," he said, his deep voice steady as stone. "I swear it on my life."
They spent the night preparing. Arrows were sharpened. Swords were cleaned. Disguises were chosen with more care than usual, because this time the stakes were not gold or pride — they were a man's life. Robin sat apart from the others, staring into the fire, and did not sleep. He kept seeing Will's face — that sharp, clever, loyal face — and imagining it at the end of a rope.
The next morning, an hour before noon, the town square of Nottingham was packed with people. A gallows had been erected in the center — a crude wooden structure, hastily built, with a thick hemp rope dangling from the crossbeam. Will Stutely stood upon the platform with his hands bound behind his back and a noose around his neck. His face was bruised and bloody from the beating the guards had given him overnight, but he stood straight and proud, refusing to slump or cower.
When the crowd fell silent, Will lifted his chin and said in a clear, ringing voice that carried to every corner of the square: "I am Will Stutely, a free man of Sherwood Forest, a Merry Man, and I am not afraid to die. Tell Robin Hood — tell him I said it was worth it. Every day in that forest, every adventure, every laugh. It was worth everything."
A woman in the crowd began to cry. Several men removed their hats.
The Sheriff sat in his high seat, surrounded by fifty armed soldiers. He was grinning with satisfaction, drumming his fingers on his armrest, scanning the crowd for Robin Hood. He gestured impatiently for the hangman to proceed. The hangman, a hooded figure in black, reached for the lever that would open the trapdoor beneath Will's feet.
And then an arrow came out of nowhere — singing through the morning air like a green-feathered bird — and severed the hangman's rope clean in two.
The rope fell in coils around Will's feet. The crowd gasped. The Sheriff leaped to his feet so fast that he knocked over his wine. And from every corner of the square — from behind market stalls, from inside carts, from beneath awnings and cloaks — men in Lincoln green threw off their disguises and drew their weapons. There were fifty of them, appearing as if by magic, bows drawn and arrows nocked.
Robin Hood stood on the roof of the inn overlooking the square, his bow in his hand and another arrow already fitted to the string. The morning sun was behind him, turning his outline golden. "Good morning, Sheriff!" he called down, his voice carrying with the clarity of a trumpet. "I believe you have something that belongs to me!"
What followed was brief, furious, and completely one-sided. The Sheriff's soldiers, though they equaled Robin's men in number, were caught utterly off guard. They had been watching for outlaws sneaking in, not for outlaws who had been standing next to them all morning. Arrows rained down from the rooftops where Robin's archers had positioned themselves before dawn. Little John waded into the soldiers like a bear among hounds, swinging his quarterstaff in great horizontal arcs that sent men flying in every direction. Friar Tuck drew his broadsword and held an entire street single-handedly, bellowing hymns at the top of his lungs as he fought, his three dogs snarling at his heels.
In the chaos, Robin leaped from the rooftop — a drop of twelve feet that he landed in a roll — sprinted across the square, vaulted onto the gallows platform, and cut Will's bonds with his dagger. The rope around Will's neck fell away. Will was weak, his knees buckling, but he managed a bloody, beautiful grin.
"Took you long enough," Will croaked.
"Traffic was terrible," Robin said, and he wrapped his arm around his friend's waist and half-carried him down the steps.
Little John was holding the escape route open at the east end of the square, his staff creating a zone of absolute destruction that no soldier dared enter. Robin pushed Will through the gap. Friar Tuck fell back in good order, still singing, his dogs providing rear-guard defense that consisted mostly of enthusiastic barking and strategic ankle-biting.
Within ten minutes, it was over. Robin's men melted back into the crowd like drops of water into an ocean and disappeared through the city gates and into the forest beyond. The Sheriff was left standing in the ruined square, surrounded by groaning soldiers, overturned market stalls, and cheering townsfolk — for the people of Nottingham loved Robin Hood, even if the Sheriff did not, and they had just witnessed the most daring rescue in living memory.
That night, around a great fire in Sherwood Forest, the Merry Men celebrated Will Stutely's rescue with the biggest, loudest, most joyful feast they had ever held. Venison and fresh bread, blackberry wine and apple cider, music and laughter and stories told three times over. Will, his wounds cleaned and bandaged by Friar Tuck's surprisingly gentle hands, sat in the place of honor and told the story of his capture again and again, each telling featuring more dramatic details and more heroic resistance.
Robin sat beside Little John, watching his friend laugh and eat and live, and felt a fierce, overwhelming love for every single person in this camp — every man and woman who had risked their lives that morning without hesitation, without being asked twice, because one of their own was in danger.
"No one gets left behind," Robin said quietly, watching the firelight dance on the faces of his people.
"No one," Little John agreed, his deep voice warm with feeling. "Not today. Not ever."
It was a promise they would keep for the rest of their days. And it was a promise that every new member of the Merry Men learned on their first night in camp: we take care of our own. Always. No matter the cost.


Chapter 6
Alan-a-Dale’s Wedding
Spring had come to Sherwood Forest, painting the world in shades of green so bright they almost hurt to look at. The bluebells were out, carpeting the forest floor in rivers of purple. The birds sang from dawn to dusk, competing with each other in volume and enthusiasm. And everything smelled of new growth and warm earth and possibility.
It was into this perfect spring morning that Robin Hood found a young man who was quite possibly the most miserable person in all of England.
Robin was walking the forest paths alone, enjoying the sunshine and the birdsong, when he came upon the young man sitting on a mossy stone beside the road, weeping into his hands. He was a slender fellow, not much older than twenty, with golden hair that curled at his temples and the long, graceful fingers of a musician. A lute hung on his back — a beautiful instrument, its dark wood gleaming — and his clothes, though travel-stained and rumpled from sleeping outdoors, were those of a gentleman. A fine embroidered doublet, good leather boots, a velvet cap that had seen better days. Everything about him spoke of someone who had once been comfortable and was now distinctly not.
"What's the matter, friend?" Robin asked, sitting down on the stone beside him with the easy familiarity of a man who has never met a stranger. "You look as though the world has ended."
"It has," said the young man, and he told Robin his story between sobs and hiccups and occasional nose-blowing into an increasingly inadequate handkerchief.
His name was Alan-a-Dale, and he was a minstrel — a traveling singer and musician who made his living performing at fairs, weddings, and the households of the minor gentry. He was, by his own modest assessment, the finest singer in three counties. Other people's assessments were even more generous — there were those who said that when Alan-a-Dale sang, the birds stopped to listen, and that his voice could make stone walls weep.
Alan had fallen deeply, hopelessly, completely in love with a maiden named Ellen, the daughter of a farmer in the village of Plympton, about five miles south of the forest. Ellen was kind and clever and laughed at Alan's jokes, which he considered the most important quality a person could have. She loved him back — loved him with the fierce, certain love of someone who knows exactly what she wants — and they had planned to marry in the spring.
But Ellen's father had other ideas. A wealthy old knight named Sir Stephen of Trent had offered a large bag of gold for Ellen's hand in marriage. Sir Stephen was sixty-three years old, sour-faced, miserly, and about as romantic as a tax bill. He wanted a young wife to manage his household, and Ellen's father wanted gold to pay his debts. The deal was struck over Ellen's protests, her tears, and her increasingly desperate pleas.
The wedding was set for that very afternoon, at the little stone church in Plympton. Ellen was to be married against her will to a man old enough to be her grandfather.
"Can you not stop it?" Robin asked, his brow furrowed.
"How?" Alan cried, fresh tears spilling down his cheeks. "Sir Stephen has twenty men-at-arms. Twenty! And I have nothing. Nothing but my lute and my voice and a heart that's breaking in two. I can't fight. I've never held a sword in my life. I can't even afford to hire someone who can. All I can do is sing, and what good is singing against swords and armor?"
Robin Hood looked at this young man — this skinny, weeping, utterly helpless musician who had nothing to fight with except love and music — and something stirred in his chest. Because Robin knew, better than most, that the world was full of powerful people who took what they wanted from those too weak to stop them. That was the world the Sheriff lived in. That was the world Robin had sworn to fight against.
"You'd be surprised what music can do," Robin said, and he smiled his most dangerous smile — the one that made Little John nervous and the Sheriff reach for his wine. "Come with me, Alan-a-Dale. I think I can help."
Two hours later, the little stone church at Plympton was full to bursting. Every pew was packed with villagers, farmers, and curious neighbors who had come to witness the wedding of the richest knight in the county to the prettiest girl in the village. The church was decorated with white ribbons and spring flowers, and candles burned in every alcove, filling the air with warm, honeyed light.
Sir Stephen of Trent stood at the altar, looking like a dried apple in a fur-trimmed cloak. He was thin, stooped, and perpetually sour, with a face that suggested he had never in his life found anything amusing. His twenty men-at-arms lined the walls of the church, their hands on their sword hilts, their faces blank and professional.
Ellen's father sat in the front row, his thick fingers wrapped around a purse of gold coins, counting them for the third time. He did not look at his daughter. Perhaps he couldn't.
Ellen herself stood at the back of the church in a white dress that should have made her glow. Instead, she looked like a ghost. Her face was as pale as the dress itself, her eyes red from crying, and she clutched a handkerchief so tightly that her knuckles were white as bone. Her mother stood beside her, patting her hand helplessly, her own eyes bright with unshed tears.
The bishop — a thin, nervous man who had been paid handsomely to perform the ceremony quickly and without awkward questions — opened his heavy book and began to read the marriage service. He had just gotten to the part about "if anyone present knows a reason why these two should not be lawfully joined together" when the church doors burst open with a bang that made every candle flame jump.
A friar the size of a small barn waddled in, beaming at the astonished congregation as if he had been personally invited. "Terribly sorry I'm late!" boomed Friar Tuck, his voice filling the little church like an organ. "Traffic on the forest road. Absolutely dreadful. Now, I couldn't help but notice that you're about to perform a wedding, and I thought to myself, 'Tuck, old boy, that bishop looks exhausted. Why don't you lend a hand?'"
"Who are you?" the bishop sputtered, clutching his book to his chest.
"Just a humble friar, here to serve the Lord and perform a wedding." Tuck positioned himself at the altar with the confidence of a man who has never once questioned whether he belongs somewhere. He gently but firmly moved the bishop aside — like a boulder redirecting a stream — and placed his enormous hands on the altar. "Now then, where's the bride?"
Sir Stephen's face had gone an alarming shade of purple, like a plum left too long in the sun. "This is outrageous! I am Sir Stephen of Trent! I demand — Guards! Remove this enormous, this ridiculous —"
But his guards had problems of their own. While every eye in the church had been fixed on Friar Tuck's magnificent entrance, two dozen men in Lincoln green had slipped in through the side doors, the vestry, and — in one impressive case — the window above the baptismal font. They now stood among Sir Stephen's twenty soldiers with drawn bows and exceedingly friendly smiles.
"I wouldn't move if I were you," Little John suggested pleasantly to the nearest guard, resting his quarterstaff on the man's shoulder with the gentle pressure of a falling tree. "Weddings should be joyful occasions. Let's keep it that way."
Robin Hood walked up the aisle, leading Alan-a-Dale by the hand. The minstrel was pale and trembling, but his eyes were bright. When Alan's gaze found Ellen's across the church, her face — which had been so deathly pale — bloomed like a rose in sunlight. She broke free from her mother and ran to him, throwing her arms around his neck, and for a moment the two of them existed in a world that contained only each other.
"Dearly beloved," Friar Tuck announced, his voice filling the little church like a trumpet on a clear morning, "we are gathered here today to join this young man and this young woman in the bonds of holy matrimony." He beamed at the congregation. "Does anyone object?"
He looked pointedly at Sir Stephen. Sir Stephen opened his mouth to deliver what would undoubtedly have been a very loud objection. Then he looked at the twenty-four arrows pointed at his head and closed it again. His face cycled through several more shades of purple before settling on a defeated gray.
"Wonderful!" said Tuck. "I do love a cooperative congregation. Shall we proceed?"
The wedding was the shortest on record. Alan and Ellen said their vows so fast that they tripped over the words, laughing and crying at the same time, their hands clasped so tightly together that their fingers went white. Tuck pronounced them married with a flourish and a grin. Alan kissed his bride, and the church erupted in cheers — because the common folk in the pews were absolutely delighted to see the greedy old knight thwarted and young love triumph.
Robin bowed to Sir Stephen, who was sputtering like a kettle. "My sincerest apologies for the inconvenience, Sir Knight. I'm sure this isn't how you planned your afternoon. But I think even you would agree — if you searched your heart very, very deeply — that this young lady deserves a husband who will make her smile. And I don't think that's you."
"You'll pay for this, Robin Hood!" Sir Stephen hissed, his thin lips white with fury. "Mark my words, I'll see you hang for this!"
"Put it on my tab," Robin said cheerfully, and he led the newlyweds out into the spring sunshine, where the birds were singing and the bluebells were blooming and the world, for once, felt exactly right.
Alan-a-Dale joined the Merry Men that very day, and he brought with him the greatest gift of all — music. From that day forward, the greenwood was filled with his songs. He sang of Robin's adventures and Little John's strength, of Friar Tuck's appetite and Will Stutely's wit, and of the love that had brought him and Ellen together against impossible odds.
His ballads spread across England, carried by travelers and sung in taverns from Nottingham to London. They made Robin Hood more than an outlaw — they made him a legend. Because there is something about a good song that goes where swords and arrows cannot: into the hearts of people, into their memories, into the stories they tell their children.
Some legends are born from great deeds and mighty battles. But the legend of Robin Hood? That one was born from a love song, sung by a skinny minstrel who had nothing to fight with except his voice, and who won the greatest battle of all.


Chapter 7
Robin Hood and the Bishop
The Bishop of Hereford was one of the richest men in England, and he had absolutely no patience for outlaws, legends, or anything else that interfered with the smooth accumulation of his personal fortune. He was a tall, thin man with a long nose that seemed designed for looking down, cold gray eyes, and hands that were always either counting coins or clasped in prayer — though his prayers, Robin suspected, were mostly about getting more coins to count.
The Bishop had heard the stories of Robin Hood — how the outlaw and his band of ruffians lurked in Sherwood Forest, robbing wealthy travelers and distributing the proceeds to the ungrateful poor. The Bishop considered this the most offensive concept he had ever encountered. The poor were poor because God intended them to be poor. The rich were rich because God loved them more. This was basic theology, and the Bishop was personally insulted that some outlaw in a green hat was disrupting the natural order.
"Stealing is stealing," the Bishop told his servants as his grand carriage rolled through the English countryside on a fine September morning, heavy with gold collected from the churches of three counties. "I don't care what fairy tales the peasants tell about this Robin Hood fellow. He's a common criminal, and if I ever meet him, I shall have him arrested on the spot and delivered to the Sheriff with my compliments."
His servants, who had more sense than their master, exchanged nervous glances. They were, after all, about to travel through Sherwood Forest.
The Bishop's carriage was impossible to miss. It was enormous, gilded, and painted with religious scenes in bright blue and gold. It was pulled by four white horses with silver bridles, and its wheels were larger than a man is tall. Behind it rode a dozen armed monks — not ordinary monks who spent their days in prayer and contemplation, but warrior monks who wore chainmail under their brown robes and carried swords and crossbows instead of rosaries. The Bishop believed in being prepared.
Robin Hood, as it happened, was sitting in an oak tree directly above the forest road, eating an apple and watching the Bishop's procession approach. He had been tipped off by Margaret the peddler, who had seen the carriage that morning and sent word through the network. Robin dangled his legs from the branch and took another bite of apple, thoroughly enjoying the spectacle.
"Little John," Robin whispered to his companion, who was somehow balanced in the tree beside him despite being approximately the size and weight of a small horse. The branch was creaking alarmingly. "Do you see what I see?"
"I see a man with more gold than sense," Little John whispered back, eyeing the gilded carriage. "And armed monks. That's new. I thought monks weren't supposed to carry swords."
"These are the Bishop of Hereford's monks. They carry whatever the Bishop tells them to carry." Robin grinned. "I think the Bishop needs an invitation to dinner. Our special dinner."
Little John groaned softly. Robin's "special dinners" for wealthy travelers had become legendary — not just for the food, which was excellent, but for the bills, which were steep. "The last time you held a special dinner, the guest tried to stab you with a bread knife."
"That was Sir Geoffrey, and he apologized later. Well, he apologized after Little John held him upside down over a bridge. Same thing."
An hour later, the Bishop's magnificent carriage rounded a bend in the forest road and came upon a peculiar sight. An old woman sat in the middle of the road, wailing at the top of her lungs. Her cart had overturned, and apples were scattered everywhere — dozens of them, rolling into ditches and under bushes and forming a treacherous carpet on the packed dirt road.
"Oh, mercy!" the old woman wailed, wringing her gnarled hands. "My cart is broken and I'll lose all my apples! Won't someone help a poor old grandmother? Is there no kindness left in the world?"
The Bishop's driver hauled on the reins. "Out of the way, old woman! We haven't time for this!"
But the Bishop, who wanted to appear charitable in front of his men — and who was also aware that the common folk were watching from the edges of the road — sighed theatrically and ordered the carriage to stop. "Help her quickly," he snapped. "We haven't got all day. Move those apples. Right the cart. And someone give the woman a penny so she'll stop that infernal noise."
His armed monks dismounted, propped their crossbows against the carriage, and went to help right the overturned cart. It was heavier than it looked — Much the Miller's Son had packed rocks under the apples — and it took all twelve of them, straining and grunting, to lift it. And the moment all twelve monks were on one side of the road, bent over and struggling, with their weapons twenty feet away and their attention completely absorbed by the stubborn cart, Robin Hood and thirty of his Merry Men stepped out of the forest on the other side.
"Good afternoon, your Grace!" Robin called cheerfully, doffing his feathered cap with a flourish. "Welcome to Sherwood Forest! I do hope you'll stay for dinner. We're having venison."
The old woman straightened up, pulled off her bonnet, and turned out to be Will Stutely in a dress and a wig of gray yarn. He gave the Bishop a cheeky curtsy that was entirely unconvincing and seemed very pleased with himself.
The Bishop's face underwent a remarkable transformation. It went white, then red, then purple, then a sort of grayish green that suggested his last meal was considering a return visit. His armed monks reached for their swords — only to find them surrounded by archers with arrows nocked and aimed with professional steadiness.
"I wouldn't do that if I were you," Friar Tuck said kindly to the nearest monk, man of the cloth to man of the cloth. "Professional courtesy and all that. Besides, dinner really is quite good. Much has outdone himself."
The Bishop was escorted, with elaborate politeness, to Robin Hood's camp in the heart of the forest. He protested vigorously the entire way. "You cannot do this! I am a man of God! I am the Bishop of Hereford! I am under the protection of the Church and the Crown! This is kidnapping! This is robbery! This is —"
"This is dinner," Robin said pleasantly. "And I promise you, your Grace, our hospitality is far more comfortable than anything the Sheriff could offer. Please, sit."
The camp had been prepared for the occasion. A long table had been set in the clearing, draped in white cloth (borrowed, not stolen — Robin was particular about the distinction), with candles and wildflowers and the finest wooden plates the Merry Men owned. Venison roasted over a great fire, filling the air with an aroma that would have made a dead man hungry. Much had also prepared fresh bread, roasted vegetables, forest mushrooms in butter, and — the masterpiece — a blackberry pie so beautiful it nearly made Friar Tuck weep.
The Bishop was given the seat of honor at the head of the table. He was served first, served most, and served with a degree of courtesy that gradually — against his will, against his principles, against every instinct he possessed — began to wear down his resistance. The food was extraordinary. The wine was excellent. Robin's men were surprisingly good company. Alan-a-Dale played his lute and sang ballads, and even the Bishop had to admit — silently, privately, and with great reluctance — that the boy had a magnificent voice.
After dinner, when the candles burned low and the conversation had mellowed from hostile silence to cautious small talk, Robin leaned forward with the look of a man about to discuss business.
"Now then, your Grace. The matter of your bill."
"My bill?" the Bishop squeaked, and the warmth that had been creeping into his expression vanished instantly.
"Dinner, entertainment, the pleasure of our company, fresh air, exercise — it all adds up, I'm afraid. But don't worry, I'm sure a man of your extraordinary means can afford it. Shall we see what's in that lovely carriage of yours?"
The Bishop's carriage, when opened, contained three hundred gold pieces in locked chests, a silver candlestick of exquisite craftsmanship, and a jeweled cross that would have fed a village for a year. Robin counted the gold carefully, his lips moving silently, while the Bishop watched with the expression of a man having a tooth pulled.
Robin counted out exactly half — one hundred and fifty pieces — and placed them in a leather sack. The rest he put back in the chest and returned to the Bishop, along with the candlestick and the cross.
"I'm only taking half," Robin explained. "Half of this gold you collected honestly, from churches that gave willingly to support the work of the Lord. That half is yours, and I wouldn't touch it. But this half" — he hefted the sack — "was squeezed out of poor parishes that could barely afford candles for their altars. Villages where children go hungry while the church roof leaks. That money was taken, not given. And it's going back where it belongs."
The Bishop stared at him. For a long, silent moment, something moved behind those cold gray eyes — something that might have been recognition, might have been shame, might have been the flickering ghost of the young man who had entered the clergy because he genuinely wanted to help people, before ambition and greed had buried that young man under layers of silk and gold.
Then the Bishop straightened his robes and huffed. "You are a scoundrel, Robin Hood. A thief and a scoundrel."
"So they tell me," Robin said, with a grin that held no malice. "But I'm a scoundrel who sleeps well at night. Can you say the same, your Grace?"
The Bishop was escorted safely back to his carriage and sent on his way, poorer by exactly one hundred and fifty gold pieces but unharmed, well-fed, and — though he would have died before admitting it — strangely thoughtful.
He never did answer Robin's question. But the gold was distributed to the poorest parishes in the county within the week. And the Bishop of Hereford, though he cursed Robin Hood's name loudly and publicly at every opportunity, never again collected more than his fair share from the churches under his care.
Some lessons are best taught with a sword. Some are best taught with a sermon. But the best lessons of all, Robin believed, were taught over dinner — with good food, good conversation, and a bill that makes you think about where your money really comes from.


Chapter 8
The Silver Arrow
The Sheriff of Nottingham was not a man who learned from his mistakes. If anything, each failure only made him more determined — and, his officers were beginning to suspect, more unhinged. The golden arrow incident had been humiliating enough, but it was the letter — that smug, cheerful letter, shot through his bedroom window and stuck in his headboard — that truly drove the Sheriff to the edge.
He hadn't slept properly since. Every night, he lay in bed staring at the hole in the wood where Robin's arrow had been, imagining another arrow coming through the window, this one aimed lower. He had ordered the window bricked up, then changed his mind and had bars installed, then changed his mind again and moved his bed to the far wall. His servants thought he was losing his mind. They weren't entirely wrong.
"Another tournament!" he announced one morning, pacing his great hall like a caged bear. His officers sat around the table, carefully avoiding eye contact. "But this time — this time — we will be ready. I want soldiers at every gate. I want men in plain clothes watching the crowd. I want archers on every rooftop. I want the best tracker in the county monitoring every road into Nottingham. Robin Hood fooled me once with that pathetic beggar disguise, but he will not fool me again."
"With respect, my lord," ventured Captain Grimsby, who had survived enough of the Sheriff's schemes to know when one was heading for disaster, "perhaps instead of another tournament, we could simply —"
"The tournament is happening, Grimsby." The Sheriff's eyes were slightly wild. "The prize will be a silver arrow — even finer than the golden one. I've already commissioned it. Robin Hood's pride will compel him to attend, and when he does, we will be ready."
The Sheriff was right about one thing: Robin's pride would indeed compel him to attend. But he was wrong about everything else.
News of the silver arrow reached Sherwood Forest within days. The Merry Men discussed it around the evening fire, passing the poster from hand to hand.
"He'll be expecting disguises this time," Robin said, studying the poster with narrowed eyes. "He'll have men watching for anyone who shoots too well in the early rounds. He won't make the same mistake twice."
"So don't go," Little John said, with the weary patience of a man making an argument he knows will be ignored. "It's obviously a trap. Again. For the second time. Do I need to say it louder?"
"I heard you. I'm going anyway."
"Robin."
"John."
Little John closed his eyes briefly, communing with some private reserve of patience. "Why?"
"Because the Sheriff thinks he can intimidate us. He thinks if he sets a big enough trap, we'll cower in the forest like frightened rabbits. And if we do that — if we let him dictate what we can and can't do — then we've already lost. The whole point of everything we do is that we're not afraid. The moment we start being afraid, it's over."
Little John stared at him for a long moment. Then he sighed. "Fine. But this time, I'm planning the escape route. Not you. You always plan the escape route last, and it usually involves jumping off something."
"That's a valid escape technique."
"It's a valid way to break your legs."
Robin's plan was more elaborate than last time. Instead of one disguise, he would use layers of misdirection. He divided his best archers into groups and gave each group a different identity and a different entry point into the city. Some would enter from the east as farmers. Others would come from the south as traveling merchants. A group of Robin's most forgettable-looking men would enter the day before the tournament and simply blend into the population.
Robin himself would go as a one-eyed tinker from Mansfield, with a leather patch over his eye, soot rubbed into his face, a false limp, and a set of rattling pans hanging from his belt. It was the most thorough disguise he had ever attempted — not just a costume but an entire character, complete with a backstory about a forge accident that had taken his eye.
But the real trick — the stroke of genius that made Little John raise his eyebrows in grudging admiration — was this: Robin would not shoot his best in the early rounds. He would shoot well enough to advance, but not brilliantly. Just another decent archer among dozens. Only in the final round would he reveal his true skill.
"And if they recognize you in the final round?" Little John asked.
"That's why you'll be standing by the east gate with thirty men and our fastest horses, ready to ride the moment things go wrong."
"When. When things go wrong."
"Such a pessimist."
The day of the tournament dawned bright and clear, without a cloud in the sky — perfect shooting weather. The Sheriff had indeed prepared more thoroughly this time. Soldiers stood at every corner of the town square, their eyes scanning the crowd with professional suspicion. Men in plain clothes — the Sheriff's spies — circulated through the audience, watching for Lincoln green, listening for familiar voices, studying every face. Archers entering the competition were checked and questioned before being allowed to register.
Robin, his face darkened with soot and a leather patch over one eye, shuffled through the east gate without attracting a second glance. His limp was convincing, his pans rattled authentically, and he smelled faintly of coal smoke, which he'd achieved by sleeping next to the camp forge the night before. He gave his name as Tom the Tinker from Mansfield and was waved through with a bored gesture.
The competition was fierce — over a hundred archers had entered, drawn by the promise of the silver arrow and the chance to compete in the most famous archery contest in England. The Sheriff's watchers monitored each one carefully. Any archer who seemed suspiciously skilled in the early rounds was pulled aside and questioned. Three of Robin's men were identified — not as outlaws, but as "persons of interest" — and had to quietly slip away into the crowd.
Robin shot carefully, deliberately holding back. His arrows hit the target solidly but without distinction — no bull's-eyes, no remarkable feats. Just a competent tinker having a decent day. The soldiers' eyes passed right over him. He was invisible.
By the final round, only five archers remained. Robin was one. Another was Gilbert of the Red Cap, the Sheriff's own champion archer, who was widely considered the second-best bowman in England and who very much wanted to prove he was the first. The other three were skilled marksmen from distant counties, lured by the prize.
The target was set at two hundred paces — a tremendous distance that made half the crowd gasp. At that range, the target looked like a coin. Wind, distance, and gravity all became factors. This was not a test of basic skill — it was a test of mastery.
One by one, the remaining archers shot. Two missed the target entirely — no shame at two hundred paces, but the crowd groaned in disappointment. The third struck the outer ring, a respectable shot that earned polite applause. Then Gilbert of the Red Cap stepped up.
Gilbert was good. He knew it, the Sheriff knew it, and the crowd knew it. He took his time, reading the wind, adjusting his stance. Then he drew and released in one fluid motion. His arrow struck within a finger's width of dead center — a magnificent shot that would have been the highlight of any other contest. The crowd erupted. The Sheriff leaned forward in his seat, grinning.
Then Tom the Tinker shuffled forward. He seemed nervous, fumbling with his bow, nearly dropping his arrow. A few people in the crowd snickered. The soldiers looked bored. This scruffy, one-eyed tradesman was clearly out of his depth.
Robin lifted his bow. He dropped the limp, straightened his spine, and in one smooth, perfect, breathtaking motion — the motion of a man who has been holding himself back for hours and is finally free — he drew and released.
The arrow flew two hundred paces in a flat, unwavering line and struck the dead center of the target with such force that it sank to the feathers and vibrated there like a tuning fork. It didn't just hit the center — it obliterated Gilbert's arrow, splitting the shaft and driving through to the target beneath.
For one perfect, suspended moment, the crowd was absolutely silent. Then they went berserk. The sound was like a wave crashing — a wall of cheering, screaming, stomping, hat-throwing pandemonium that shook the windows of the buildings around the square.
But the Sheriff had seen this shot before. He had seen it at the last tournament, when a stooped old beggar had split his champion's arrow in exactly the same way. He leaped to his feet, his face purple, pointing a trembling finger at the one-eyed tinker. "SEIZE HIM!" he screamed, his voice cracking. "That's Robin Hood! I know that shot! I'd know it in my sleep! SEIZE HIM NOW!"
Soldiers surged forward from every direction, drawing swords and closing in. Robin threw off his eye patch and grinned — that wild, reckless, infuriating grin that the Sheriff knew so well. "Thank you for the lovely arrow, Sheriff! Shall we make it annual?"
Then he turned and ran.
The Sheriff's soldiers chased him through the streets of Nottingham, but Robin's men were ready. At every corner, an outlaw in disguise stepped out and caused chaos — overturning a cart to block the road, releasing a pen of geese into the soldiers' path, pointing the pursuit in the wrong direction with helpful shouts of "He went that way!" Robin sprinted through back alleys and over walls, the silver arrow clutched in his hand, arrows clattering off the stones around him.
He almost made it clean away. Almost. As he vaulted over the town wall near the east gate — the escape route Little John had prepared — an arrow caught him in the arm, just above the elbow. The pain was sudden and blinding, a hot lance that shot from his fingertips to his shoulder. Robin stumbled on the landing and went to one knee.
But Little John was there, exactly where he'd promised to be. The big man caught Robin before he could fall, threw him over one massive shoulder like a sack of flour, and ran for the horses waiting in the trees. Behind them, the east gate slammed open and soldiers poured out, shouting — but they were too late. Robin's men were already mounted and riding hard for the forest, the thunder of hooves drowning out the Sheriff's distant, impotent screams.
They didn't stop until they reached the deep woods, two miles from the nearest road. Friar Tuck cleaned and bandaged Robin's wound while Little John paced back and forth, his face tight with worry and anger.
"That was too close," Little John said, his voice rough.
Robin winced as Tuck carefully extracted the arrowhead — a clean removal, thank God, with no barb. "But we got the silver arrow," he said, holding it up with his uninjured hand. It gleamed in the dappled sunlight, beautiful and defiant.
"You could have died, Robin. For an arrow."
Robin looked at his friend — at the worry etched into Little John's broad face, at the fear that the big man was trying to hide behind anger — and felt a wave of affection so strong it nearly overwhelmed the pain in his arm.
"It was never about the arrow, John. You know that. It's about showing the Sheriff that no matter how many soldiers he sends, no matter how many traps he sets, no matter how many walls he builds — he cannot break us. He cannot make us afraid. And as long as the people see that, they won't be afraid either."
Little John was quiet for a long moment. Then he sighed — a sigh that carried the weight of years of friendship, years of worry, years of pulling Robin Hood out of situations that should have killed him.
"You're impossible," Little John said. But he was smiling.
Robin leaned back against the trunk of an oak and closed his eyes. His arm throbbed, but he was alive, he was free, and he was home. The silver arrow was beautiful, but the real prize was simpler than silver: the knowledge that the Merry Men could walk into the lion's den and walk out again, laughing.
That was worth an arrow in the arm any day.


Chapter 9
Maid Marian
For all his bravery and cleverness, Robin Hood had one weakness that his men teased him about endlessly, mercilessly, and with great affection: he could not speak a sensible word in the presence of Maid Marian.
Marian Fitzwalter was the daughter of Sir Richard of Lea, a noble knight who lived in a modest manor on the eastern edge of Sherwood Forest. She had hair as dark as a raven's wing that she wore in a long braid down her back, eyes that sparkled like the river in sunlight, and a tongue as sharp as any sword in Robin's camp. She could ride a horse better than most of Robin's men, shoot an arrow nearly as well as Robin himself, and she had absolutely no patience for nonsense, flattery, or people who underestimated her because she happened to be a woman.
Robin had known Marian since they were children, running through the fields together and climbing the same trees in the summers when Marian's family visited Locksley. She had been a wild, fearless girl who could outrun Robin in a footrace and who once pushed him into a pond for suggesting that girls couldn't climb as high as boys. She climbed higher than him that very afternoon, just to prove her point, and refused to come down until he apologized.
But that was before Robin became an outlaw. Before the forest. Before everything changed. Now, years later, Robin watched her from the edges of the forest when she rode through on her white mare, and he sighed like a lovesick poet in one of Alan-a-Dale's ballads. He sighed at breakfast. He sighed at dinner. He sighed while polishing his bow, while planning robberies, while doing absolutely everything.
"Just go talk to her," Little John said one afternoon, as Robin sat in a tree watching Marian ride past on the road below. She sat straight and confident in the saddle, her dark braid swinging, and she looked — Robin thought miserably — like the most magnificent person who had ever lived.
"I can't just go talk to her," Robin said. "What would I say?"
"Hello. That's generally how people start conversations."
"She's not just anyone, John. She's Marian. She's clever and brave and beautiful, and I'm a man who lives in a tree and robs people for a living. She probably doesn't even remember me."
"You face the Sheriff's soldiers without blinking. You walked into Nottingham in a beggar's costume and stole a golden arrow. But you can't say hello to a girl you've known since you were ten?"
"It's different."
"It's really not."
"You're not helping."
"I'm helping enormously. You're just not listening."
As it turned out, Robin didn't need to find the courage to approach Marian. She found him first. And she did it in the most Marian way possible.
It happened on a misty morning in early autumn, when the trees were just beginning to turn gold and the air carried the first sharp bite of the coming cold. Robin was walking alone through the forest, following a deer trail that wound through a part of the woods he rarely visited. The mist hung in the air like curtains of white silk, muffling sounds and making the trees look like ghosts of themselves.
He came around a bend in the trail and found himself face to face with a young knight on horseback. The knight was slender and sat the horse well, with a sword at the belt and a dark cloak pulled up to hide the face beneath the helmet's visor. Something about the rider seemed familiar — the way the shoulders were set, the angle of the head — but Robin couldn't place it.
"Hold there, young knight!" Robin called, stepping into the center of the trail with one hand raised and the other resting on his sword hilt. "These are my woods, and all who pass through must pay a toll. It's a fair price — I promise you'll get your money's worth in scenery."
The knight reined in the horse and looked down at Robin. "And if I refuse?" The voice was muffled by the cloak, pitched low and careful. "What then?"
"Then we settle it the traditional way. With swords."
"Very well." The knight dismounted with athletic grace and drew a sword — a fine blade, well-balanced and recently sharpened. The knight's stance was correct and confident, the mark of someone who had been properly trained.
Robin drew his own blade, and they began to circle each other in the mist. For a moment, neither attacked. They were reading each other — watching footwork, studying stance, looking for openings. Then the knight lunged.
It was a textbook attack — fast, precise, and aimed at Robin's left side, forcing him to turn. Robin parried and countered, but the knight was ready, deflecting his riposte with a clean beat that showed real skill. Robin pressed forward with a combination of cuts and thrusts, and the knight retreated smoothly, giving ground without losing form.
This young knight was good. Very good. Robin's initial confidence began to give way to genuine respect. The knight's style was elegant and efficient — no wasted motion, no flashy tricks, just solid, classical swordsmanship executed with surprising speed.
They fought through the misty trees for several minutes, their blades ringing in the silence like bells. Robin tried every trick in his considerable repertoire — feints, false openings, changes of tempo — but the knight countered each one. The knight fought defensively, waiting for Robin to overcommit, then striking at the gaps in his attack with sharp, punishing counters that came within inches of connecting.
Then the knight executed a spinning parry-riposte that Robin had seen only once before, years ago. It was a specific technique — a flourish followed by a low cut that reversed into a high thrust — taught by an old swordmaster named Thomas who had come through Locksley the summer Robin was twelve. Thomas had spent three weeks teaching the children of the village, and his best students had been Robin and one other person. One specific person.
Robin froze. His sword dropped an inch. His eyes went wide behind his guard.
In that moment of recognition, the knight's sword caught Robin's blade in a disarming twist and sent it spinning out of his hand. It clattered on the rocky ground ten feet away. The knight's point came to rest at Robin's throat, steady and precise.
"Yield," said the knight.
Robin couldn't help it. Despite the sword at his throat, despite the embarrassment of being disarmed, despite everything — he started to laugh. "I yield, Marian."
The knight pulled back the hood and removed the helmet, and there she was — dark hair tumbling out in a cascade, eyes flashing with triumph and mischief, cheeks flushed from the fight. She was grinning — the exact same grin she'd worn the day she climbed higher than Robin in the old oak tree, twenty years and a lifetime ago.
"How did you know?" she demanded, sheathing her sword.
"Old Thomas the swordmaster taught you that spinning combination. He taught me the same one, the summer we were twelve. I'd know it anywhere." Robin picked up his sword and sheathed it, shaking his head in wonder. "You've gotten better."
"I've been practicing. While you've been playing outlaw in the woods, some of us have been keeping up with our training." She crossed her arms. "I've been riding through this forest for three weeks, Robin. Three weeks! Hoping to run into you. Do you know how frustrating it is to look for the most famous outlaw in England and not be able to find him?"
"Why were you looking for me?" Robin asked, and his heart was beating faster than it had during the swordfight — which, considering that a sword had just been at his throat, was saying something.
Marian's expression shifted from playful to grave. "Because the Sheriff is planning something terrible. My father heard rumors at court — the Sheriff has petitioned Prince John for permission to burn Sherwood Forest. All of it, Robin. Every tree, every bush. He would rather destroy a thousand acres of ancient woodland than let you hide in it."
Robin's smile vanished. Burn the forest? Sherwood was not just Robin's home — it was a living, breathing world. Thousands of creatures lived here. Dozens of villages bordered the forest, depending on its wood and game for their survival. Burning it would be an act of monstrous, spiteful destruction that would devastate the entire region.
"There's more," Marian said, her voice dropping. "The Sheriff plans to arrest my father. He's been collecting evidence — manufactured evidence, lies and forged documents — claiming that my father has been supplying you with food and intelligence. If the Sheriff gets his way, my father will lose his lands, his title, everything."
"Has your father been helping us?" Robin asked carefully.
Marian gave him a look that could have melted steel. "He's been leaving food at the forest's edge for three years. Every week, without fail. Bread, cheese, dried meat. Of course he's been helping you. He believes in what you're doing. But he's too proud and too stubborn to admit it, because he's a knight and knights don't consort with outlaws." She paused. "Apparently stubbornness is a quality I'm attracted to, because here I am."
Robin's heart performed a complicated maneuver in his chest. "Marian, it's too dangerous for you to be here. If the Sheriff discovers that you've been warning me —"
"Then he discovers it. I didn't ride through this forest for three weeks to be told to go home and be safe." She stepped closer, and her eyes — those extraordinary, fierce, beautiful eyes — locked onto his. "I know these lands better than anyone in your band. I can move freely between the court and the countryside. I have connections among the nobility that you don't. I can be your eyes and ears in places where you can't go. Let me help, Robin. Not because I need your permission, but because we'll be more effective as partners."
Robin looked at her — this brave, brilliant, fearless woman who had ridden alone through outlaw-infested woods, challenged him to a swordfight, disarmed him, and was now standing in front of him demanding to be treated as an equal — and he knew she was right. He also knew, with a certainty that went deeper than thought, something he had known since he was twelve years old and too shy to say: he was completely and utterly in love with her.
"Stay," he said. "Stay and help us. And Marian — I'm glad you found me. I should have found you a long time ago."
She smiled, and the misty forest seemed suddenly full of light.
From that day forward, Marian became Robin Hood's most valuable ally. She moved between the world of the court and the world of the greenwood like a ghost, carrying messages, gathering intelligence, and warning Robin of the Sheriff's plans before they could be set in motion. She established a network of informants that stretched from Nottingham to London, connecting sympathetic nobles, disgruntled servants, and ordinary people who were tired of living under tyranny.
The Merry Men adored her — partly because she could outshoot half of them and outsmart the other half, and partly because she was the only person alive who could make Robin Hood blush, stammer, and lose his legendary composure. Watching Robin try to be cool around Marian became one of the camp's favorite entertainments.
"You should just tell her how you feel," Little John said one evening, watching Robin trip over his own feet for the third time because Marian had smiled at him.
"I will. Eventually. When the time is right."
"The time has been right for about fifteen years."
"You're not helping, John."
"I'm helping enormously. You're just not listening."
And if Robin walked a little taller and sang a little louder in the days after Marian joined them — if his plans were bolder and his laughter came easier — well, even outlaws are allowed to be happy. Especially when the person who makes them happy can also beat them in a swordfight.


Chapter 10
The Poor Knight’s Debt
One rainy afternoon in late November, when the forest was gray and dripping and the Merry Men huddled around their fires in damp misery, Robin's scouts brought a traveler to camp. But this was no wealthy lord with jingling purses, no pompous bishop with a gilded carriage. This was the sorriest-looking knight Robin had ever seen.
His name, he said, was Sir Richard of Lea. His armor was so rusted that it had turned the color of dried blood. His horse was a thin, sad creature with ribs showing through its coat and a look in its eyes that suggested it had given up on life some time ago. The knight's clothes were patched and mended so many times that they were more thread than fabric — a patchwork of different colors and textures that made him look like a quilt that had learned to walk.
He sat at Robin's table with his head bowed and his hands trembling around the mug of warm cider that Much had pressed into them. Rain dripped from his gray hair onto his threadbare cloak. He was the picture of a man at the end of his rope.
"Forgive me," the knight said, his voice cracking like old leather. "I know this is your forest, and I know you stop travelers for payment. But I have nothing worth stealing. Nothing worth anything at all. I am ruined."
Robin studied the man with careful, compassionate eyes. He had seen poverty before — the Merry Men saw it every day in the villages they protected — but there was something about a knight brought so low that was especially heartbreaking. Knights were supposed to be strong, proud, unbreakable. This man looked as though a stiff wind would finish him.
"Tell me your story, Sir Knight," Robin said gently. "In Sherwood Forest, we don't judge a man by his purse. And no one leaves this table hungry. Much — bring our guest some food."
Much appeared almost instantly with a bowl of hot stew, fresh bread, and a wedge of cheese. The knight stared at the food for a moment, as if he couldn't quite believe it was real, and then ate with the desperate, grateful speed of a man who hadn't had a proper meal in weeks.
Between bites, Sir Richard told his story. It was a common tale in those harsh times, but no less heartbreaking for being common.
His only son, a young knight named Hugh, had gotten into terrible trouble at a tournament. During a jousting match, Hugh's lance had struck his opponent badly — a glancing blow to the neck that left the other knight paralyzed. It was an accident, a horrible piece of bad luck, but the injured knight's family had powerful friends and demanded justice. Hugh was arrested and thrown into prison, facing charges that could see him hanged.
To save his son's life, Sir Richard had gone to the only person who would lend him money: the Abbot of St. Mary's Abbey, a wealthy and pitiless man who saw an opportunity to acquire prime farmland at a fraction of its value. The Abbot had lent Sir Richard four hundred gold pieces — an enormous sum — with Sir Richard's lands as collateral. The terms were brutal: repay in full within one year, or forfeit everything.
Sir Richard had used the gold to pay Hugh's fine and secure his release. His son was free. But now the debt was due in two weeks, and Sir Richard didn't have a single penny.
"If I cannot pay," the knight said, his voice barely above a whisper, "the Abbot will take my lands. My family home. The fields my father farmed, and his father before him. My wife, my daughter — they'll be turned out into the street. Everything my family has built over three generations will be gone." He pressed his trembling hands together. "I've begged everyone I know. No one will help. No one wants to cross the Abbot."
Robin looked at Little John. Little John looked at Friar Tuck. Friar Tuck looked at the empty plate in front of Sir Richard and sighed — the sigh of a man whose heart was too big for his belly, which was already considerable.
"Four hundred gold pieces," Robin said slowly, turning the number over in his mind. He turned to Little John. "How much do we have in the treasury?"
Little John, who kept the accounts with the meticulous care of a man who understood the value of every coin, didn't need to check. "Four hundred and twelve pieces. Give or take a few pennies that Much borrowed for cooking spices."
"Lend it to him," Robin said. No hesitation. No calculation. No weighing of pros and cons. Just a simple, immediate decision.
"All of it?" Little John's eyebrows climbed toward his hairline. "Robin, that's our entire treasury. Everything we've saved over months of work. We need that money for food, for supplies, for —"
"For what?" Robin asked, not with irritation but with genuine curiosity. "For sitting in a chest while a good man loses his home? We rob the rich to help those in need, John. That's not just what we do — it's who we are. And right here, sitting at our table, is a man who needs help more than anyone we've ever met. What's the point of having money if we don't use it for this?"
Little John opened his mouth to argue. Then he closed it. He looked at the broken knight with the trembling hands and the threadbare cloak, and something softened behind those steady eyes. "Aye," he said quietly. "You're right. You're absolutely right."
They counted out four hundred gold pieces into a leather sack, each coin clinking against the next with a sound like small bells. Friar Tuck blessed the money with a prayer that was short on theology and long on sincerity. Will Stutely threw in an extra gold piece "for luck and road snacks." Alan-a-Dale contributed a lucky charm — a small wooden robin he had carved — that he insisted the knight carry in his pocket.
Little John personally cleaned and polished Sir Richard's rusted armor until it gleamed like new. It took him most of the evening, working by firelight with oil and sand, but he did it without complaint and without being asked. When he finished, the armor looked like it belonged to a man who still had his pride.
Much packed a saddlebag with food — enough for a week's travel, carefully wrapped in oilcloth against the rain. He also slipped in a small jar of honey, because Much believed that no one's journey should be entirely without sweetness.
"Take this to the Abbot," Robin told Sir Richard, placing the heavy leather sack in his hands. The knight's fingers closed around it like a drowning man gripping a rope. "Pay your debt. Save your lands. Keep your family together. And when you can — when your fortunes have recovered and you're back on your feet — pay us back. But only when you can. There's no rush. A year, five years, ten years — it doesn't matter. The debt is between friends."
Sir Richard stared at the gold, then at Robin, and tears rolled down his weathered cheeks. His shoulders shook. For a long moment, he couldn't speak.
"Why?" he finally managed, his voice broken. "You don't even know me. I'm a stranger. I walked into your camp with nothing, and you're giving me everything you have. Why?"
Robin put his hand on the old knight's shoulder. "Because you're a good man who's had bad luck. And that's not a crime, no matter what the Abbot or the Sheriff or anyone else says. Good people help other good people. That's how the world is supposed to work."
Sir Richard rode away the next morning on a horse that Little John had personally fed and groomed until it looked like a different animal entirely. The knight sat straighter in his saddle than he had in months, his polished armor catching the pale November sun. He looked, for the first time in a long time, like a knight.
Robin watched him go from the edge of the forest, his arms crossed, a quiet smile on his face.
"That was our entire treasury," Little John said, standing beside him.
"It was."
"We have enough food for maybe three weeks. After that, we'll need to start working the roads again."
"Then I suppose we'd better get back to work," Robin said, his grin widening. "I hear the Bishop of Hereford is traveling through next week. And a certain tax collector named Oswald is due to pass through on Thursday."
Little John shook his head, but he was smiling. "You're the most generous thief in England, you know that?"
"I'm not a thief. I'm a redistributor of wealth. There's an important difference."
"The Sheriff doesn't think so."
"The Sheriff doesn't think much of anything. That's his problem."
Six months later, on a bright May morning, Sir Richard of Lea returned to Sherwood Forest. He was barely recognizable. His armor gleamed. His horse was fat, glossy, and spirited. His clothes were new, his back was straight, and his eyes — which had been so hollow and defeated — were bright and full of life.
He carried with him not only the four hundred gold pieces he owed, but an extra hundred as a gift of gratitude. He also brought three barrels of the finest wine from his cellars, a wheel of cheese the size of a cartwheel, and a letter of thanks so heartfelt that Friar Tuck cried when he read it aloud.
"My son is free, my lands are safe, and my family is whole again," Sir Richard said, clasping Robin's hand with both of his. His grip was strong now — the grip of a man who had been given back his life. "You saved us, Robin Hood. Not just with gold, but with faith. You believed in me when I had stopped believing in myself. I will never forget it, and I will spend the rest of my days trying to be worthy of your generosity."
He also brought something else — a knowing smile and a message from his daughter.
"My daughter Marian speaks very highly of you," Sir Richard said, his eyes twinkling. "She tells me you are an honorable man. A kind man. A brave man." He paused. "She also tells me you are occasionally an idiot, but she says that with great affection."
"I — well — I try to — I mean —" Robin stammered, his legendary composure evaporating like morning dew.
Little John, watching from behind a tree, laughed so hard that acorns fell from the branches above him.


Chapter 11
The Tinker, the Peddler, and the Cook
Not everyone who joined the Merry Men arrived through a dramatic swordfight on a bridge or a daring rescue from the gallows. Some of the band's most valuable members came to Sherwood Forest through accidents, misunderstandings, and — in one memorable case — a deep and passionate disagreement about the proper way to cook venison.
Take the tinker, for instance.
His name was Edward, and he was the most suspicious, nervous, twitchy little man that Robin Hood had ever encountered. He was small and thin, with darting eyes, a nose that seemed to be permanently sniffing for danger, and a way of looking over his shoulder every three seconds that suggested the entire world was out to get him. He traveled the country roads mending pots and pans, and the sound of his approach — a constant jangling and clanking of the metal wares hanging from his pack — could be heard a quarter mile away. He was, in other words, the exact opposite of stealthy.
What Robin didn't know, on the afternoon he fell into step beside Edward on a forest road, was that the tinker was carrying something far more interesting than pots and pans. Tucked into his right boot was a warrant for Robin Hood's arrest, signed by the Sheriff of Nottingham himself, and the tinker had been promised five gold pieces for its delivery to the garrison at Mansfield.
Robin had no idea about any of this. He was simply walking through the forest, enjoying the autumn colors, and he saw a nervous little man with a jangling pack and thought he looked like he could use some company.
"Fine day for a walk," Robin said pleasantly, falling into step beside the tinker.
Edward nearly jumped out of his skin. He leaped sideways, dropped three pans with a tremendous clatter, and stared at Robin with eyes the size of dinner plates. "Who are you? What do you want? I haven't got any money! Well, I have a little, but not much. Well, actually I have five — I mean, I have nothing. Nothing at all. I'm destitute. Poverty-stricken. I may actually be dead. Please go away."
"Calm down, friend. I'm just a traveler, same as you. These roads can be dangerous — shall we walk together? Safety in numbers and all that."
The tinker eyed Robin with deep suspicion but apparently decided that one companion was better than whatever he imagined lurking in the trees. They walked together for an hour, during which Robin learned that Edward was from Chester, that his mother was a terrifying woman who once chased a tax collector out of town with a rolling pin, and that Edward himself had a phobia of large dogs, loud noises, and anything that moved unexpectedly.
Robin bought the tinker lunch at a roadside inn — bread, cheese, and a mug of ale that Edward drank with the desperate gratitude of a man who has been on the road for days. They talked about the weather, about the state of the roads, about the price of tin, and about Edward's mother (Robin was beginning to think the rolling pin story was an understatement). By the end of the meal, Edward had relaxed considerably and was telling Robin jokes — terrible jokes, mostly about blacksmiths and horses, but Robin laughed anyway because the little man told them with such earnest enthusiasm.
It was only when Little John appeared at the inn door, filling the entire frame with his enormous body, and called out "Robin! There you are!" that the tinker realized who he had been dining with.
Edward went white. His ale mug froze halfway to his mouth. His eyes traveled from Little John to Robin and back again, and Robin could practically see the gears turning behind those terrified eyes. The tinker's hand crept slowly toward his boot, where the warrant was hidden.
Robin watched this internal struggle with quiet compassion. He could see the fear, the calculation, and — beneath it all — the decency of a man who didn't really want to turn anyone in but was afraid of what would happen if he didn't.
Edward's hand hovered over his boot for a long, agonizing moment. The inn was silent. Little John stood in the doorway, his hand resting casually on his quarterstaff.
Then Edward pulled the warrant out of his boot, looked at it, looked at Robin, and very deliberately tore it into tiny pieces. He dropped them into his mug of ale, where they floated sadly on the surface like miniature shipwrecks.
"I never liked the Sheriff anyway," Edward said. "His mother's even worse than mine, and that's saying something."
He joined the Merry Men that afternoon and turned out to be surprisingly handy — not just with pots and pans, but with locks, hinges, traps, and any mechanical device. He became the camp's unofficial engineer, building everything from rain shelters to an elaborate alarm system that involved tripwires, bells, and a very startled badger. He was still nervous about everything, but he was their nervous man, and the Merry Men protected their own.
Then there was the peddler.
Margaret arrived at Robin's camp not by capture or coincidence but by deliberate, determined choice. She was a stout, no-nonsense woman of about forty, with arms like a blacksmith's, a voice that could be heard across a field, and a face that radiated the particular kind of authority that comes from decades of tolerating absolutely nobody's foolishness.
She sold ribbons, needles, thread, and small household goods from an enormous pack on her back, traveling from village to village on a circuit that covered most of Nottinghamshire. She knew every road, every market, and every person worth knowing in the county. She was, in other words, the most connected person for fifty miles.
She had been robbed — not by Robin's men, but by the Sheriff's own soldiers. Three of them had stopped her on the road, taken every ribbon, every needle, every penny she possessed, and laughed when she protested. "Be grateful we didn't take more," the sergeant had said, and his men had found this very amusing.
Margaret was not amused. Margaret was furious with a cold, patient fury that was far more frightening than any hot rage. She walked to Sherwood Forest, found Robin's camp through sheer determination and a willingness to shout "Robin Hood! I need to talk to you!" at the top of her lungs until someone answered, and presented herself before Robin with her arms crossed and her jaw set.
Robin replaced her stock from the camp's supplies and gave her a purse of coins to restart her business. Margaret looked at the money, looked at Robin, and said, "I'm not leaving."
"I'm sorry?" Robin said.
"You need someone who can go into every village and every market in this county without raising suspicion. A peddler goes everywhere, talks to everyone, and nobody looks twice. You need eyes and ears in places your men can't go, and you need someone who actually understands how the common people live. That person is me. I'm volunteering."
Robin opened his mouth to politely decline, but Margaret cut him off with a raised hand. "This isn't a request, young man. I'm telling you what you need. You can accept my help, or you can spend the next year stumbling around in the dark while I do it anyway on my own. Your choice."
Robin looked at Little John. Little John shrugged. "She's not wrong."
Margaret became Robin's most effective intelligence gatherer. She carried messages hidden in spools of thread, coded information woven into the patterns of her ribbons, and detailed reports of the Sheriff's movements disguised as inventory lists. Within six months, there wasn't a plan hatched in Nottingham Castle that Robin didn't know about within forty-eight hours.
And finally, there was the cook.
Much the Miller's Son was a round, cheerful, irrepressible young man who had worked in the Sheriff's own kitchen for three years. He was, by universal consensus, the finest cook in Nottingham — a master of roasting, baking, stewing, and any other technique that turned raw ingredients into something transcendent. His venison pies were legendary. His bread could make a grown man weep. His blackberry cobbler had once caused a minor riot at a harvest festival.
He had been perfectly happy in his position — well-fed, well-paid, and largely ignored by the Sheriff, who cared about food only insofar as it appeared on his table — until the day the Sheriff ordered him to poison a meal.
A visiting lord had spoken publicly in favor of Robin Hood, praising the outlaw's generosity to the poor. The Sheriff, incandescent with rage, ordered Much to add a particular powder to the lord's wine at dinner. Much looked at the powder, looked at the Sheriff, and said, "No."
The Sheriff was not accustomed to being refused, especially by cooks. "Do as you're told," he snarled, "or I'll have you thrown in the dungeon."
"I'm a cook," Much said, with the quiet dignity of a man who has found his line and will not cross it. "I create food. I don't destroy people with it. Find someone else to do your murdering."
The Sheriff had him thrown into the street within the hour, with nothing but the clothes on his back and a rolling pin he'd grabbed on the way out.
Much wandered into Sherwood Forest more by accident than design, following the smell of roasting venison that drifted through the trees on the evening air. He walked in a daze, hungry and homeless and uncertain of everything except that he had done the right thing. The smell led him through the undergrowth, past a tripwire (which he stepped over without noticing), past a lookout post (whose occupant was asleep), and straight into the heart of Robin's camp.
He took one look at the charred, blackened, mangled remains of what Friar Tuck was attempting to cook over the central fire, and he burst into tears.
"What — what has that poor piece of venison ever done to deserve this?" Much wailed, snatching the cooking spit away from a startled Tuck. "This isn't cooking! This is arson! This is a crime against food itself! Oh, give me that knife. Everyone out of my way. Everyone. Now."
An hour later, the Merry Men sat down to the finest meal they had ever eaten in the forest. The venison was tender and perfectly seasoned with wild rosemary, thyme, and garlic. There was fresh bread baked on hot stones — golden-crusted and soft within. A salad of forest greens dressed with a vinegar that Much had produced from somewhere about his person. And for dessert, a blackberry cobbler made with berries he had picked during the hour, sweetened with honey, that made grown outlaws weep with pleasure.
"You're hired," Robin said, through a mouthful of cobbler so good it bordered on the religious. "You're absolutely, permanently, irrevocably hired."
Friar Tuck handed over the cooking duties without a moment's argument. His talents were many and genuine, but cooking was not among them, and he had the self-awareness to admit it. "Thank the Lord," Tuck said, helping himself to a third serving. "My prayers have been answered."
Much the Miller's Son became the heart of the camp in a way that no fighter or scout or strategist ever could. Because at the end of a long day of robbing the rich and outwitting the Sheriff and dodging arrows and sleeping on the ground — at the end of all of it — what every outlaw really needed was a good meal and the warmth of a fire and the knowledge that someone cared enough to make the food taste wonderful.
And Much gave them all of that, three times a day, every day, without fail. He fed them when they were tired. He fed them when they were sad. He fed them when they came home from dangerous missions with empty hands and heavy hearts. He believed, with every fiber of his being, that food was love made visible, and he cooked accordingly.
The tinker, the peddler, and the cook. Not warriors. Not legends. Just ordinary people who found their way to Sherwood Forest and discovered that they had extraordinary things to offer. And that, Robin often reflected, was the real secret of the Merry Men: the band was strongest not because of its fighters, but because of the people who kept it running, kept it fed, kept it connected, and kept it human.


Chapter 12
The Sheriff’s Tax Collectors
It was the cruelest time of year in Nottinghamshire — tax season. The Sheriff had sent his collectors out across the county with orders to squeeze every last penny from the common folk, and his instructions were brutally simple: bring back the money, or don't come back at all.
The collectors were not gentle men. They traveled in groups of three or four, each accompanied by a squad of soldiers, and they carried lists of every household in their assigned district — lists that showed exactly what each family owed and exactly what could be taken if they couldn't pay. If a family was short on coins, the collectors took livestock. If there was no livestock, they took tools. If there were no tools, they took furniture, clothing, food — anything of value, and some things of no value at all, just to make a point.
Robin Hood had watched this happen every autumn for three years, and every year it made his blood boil hotter. But this year was different. This year, the Sheriff had raised the taxes yet again — for the third time in twelve months — and the villages were being bled dry. Families that had been scraping by were now being crushed. Robin had seen children in the villages with hollow cheeks and dull eyes, and he had seen their parents staring at empty cupboards with the helpless, hopeless look of people who have tried everything and failed.
"This stops today," Robin told his men, gathered around the morning fire on a cold October dawn. His voice was calm, but his eyes burned with a fierce, steady anger that every person in the camp recognized and respected. Robin was at his most dangerous when he was quiet. "The Sheriff is robbing people who have nothing left to give. Widows. Orphans. Families who can barely survive as it is. He's not collecting taxes — he's stealing the food out of children's mouths. And we are going to take every penny back."
The plan was bold, even by Robin Hood's standards. The Sheriff had three tax collectors working the county simultaneously, each covering a different district with a small guard of soldiers and a heavy cart for hauling away the seized goods. Robin would hit all three on the same day, at the same hour, so the Sheriff couldn't send reinforcements from one group to another. It was the kind of plan that required perfect timing, absolute coordination, and a healthy dose of insanity.
"Little John, you take the northern road and the collector working the Edwinstowe district. Will Stutely, the eastern road and the Blidworth district. I'll handle the western route and the villages around Papplewick. Friar Tuck, you stay at camp and prepare for guests — we'll be sending the townsfolk to you for their refunds."
"Refunds?" Tuck asked, eyebrows raised.
"We're returning their money. All of it. Every coin, every chicken, every sack of grain. We take it from the collectors and give it straight back."
Tuck smiled — a broad, beaming, deeply satisfied smile. "Now that is a sermon I can get behind."
Little John's group struck first, at the village of Edwinstowe, just as the church bell rang nine o'clock. The tax collector assigned to this district was a thin, pinch-faced man named Grimsby who had a reputation for cruelty that exceeded even the Sheriff's expectations. He took visible pleasure in making people cry — especially old people and children, who were easier to intimidate — and he had been known to confiscate family heirlooms, wedding rings, and even children's toys when the coins ran short.
He was in the middle of confiscating an old widow's only cow — dragging the protesting animal by a rope while the widow clutched her shawl and wept — when a shadow fell over him. Grimsby looked up. And up. And up.
Little John stood before him, seven feet of quiet fury, his quarterstaff resting across his shoulders.
"I believe that cow belongs to the lady," Little John said, his voice pleasant in the way that a thunderstorm is pleasant before the lightning starts.
Grimsby went the color of old cheese. "I — this is official business — I am here on the Sheriff's authority —"
"And I am here on the authority of common decency," Little John replied. "Which outranks the Sheriff, last I checked. Now, you can hand over the cow, the money, and everything else in that cart. Or I can tie you to a tree and let the village children throw acorns at you until Christmas. Your choice."
Grimsby, showing a rare burst of wisdom, chose option one.
On the eastern road, Will Stutely employed a strategy that was pure theater. He and a dozen of Robin's men had disguised themselves as a traveling troupe of players — actors and musicians who wandered the countryside performing for coins. They set up an impromptu show in the village square of Blidworth, right in the path of the tax collector's procession.
The performance was magnificent. Alan-a-Dale sang. Two of the Merry Men juggled (badly, but with great enthusiasm). Will himself performed a one-man play about a foolish sheriff who taxed his people into poverty and was eventually outwitted by a clever fox. The audience — which included the tax collector and his bored soldiers — was enthralled.
They were so enthralled, in fact, that nobody noticed when the "stagehands" — four of Robin's most light-fingered men — slipped behind the soldiers, unlocked the money chests, and quietly redistributed their contents to the delighted villagers standing at the back of the crowd.
The soldiers didn't realize what had happened until the "troupe" had packed up and vanished. Will left a note pinned to the empty chest: "Thank you for your generous donation to the Robin Hood Fund for the Prevention of Sheriff-Related Misery. Tax-deductible!"
Robin's own encounter on the western road was the most dramatic of the three.
The tax collector assigned to the Papplewick district was a man named Oswald — a brutal, heavy-set enforcer who was known throughout the county for one thing: he enjoyed his work. Where Grimsby was cruel out of petty malice and the Blidworth collector was merely following orders, Oswald genuinely liked hurting people. He was the Sheriff's fist, and he used that fist freely.
Robin found him standing over a young farmer in a muddy yard, his boot on the man's chest, his fist raised. The farmer's wife crouched in the doorway of their cottage, shielding three small children behind her skirts. The children were crying. The woman's face was white with terror.
Robin didn't announce himself. He didn't make a speech. He didn't offer a witty greeting or a charming smile. He simply stepped out of the trees and put an arrow into the dirt between Oswald's boots. The shaft buried itself to the feathers, humming like a plucked string.
"The next one goes higher," Robin said. His voice was ice.
Oswald turned slowly. He was a big man, scarred and mean-looking, with the flattened nose of a brawler and fists like hams. He was not easily frightened, and the look he gave Robin was more curious than afraid. "Robin Hood," he said, almost conversationally. "The people's champion. How noble. How heroic. How completely pointless."
"Let the man up," Robin said.
"These people owe the Sheriff, and debts must be paid." Oswald pressed his boot harder into the farmer's chest. "That's how the world works, Hood. The strong take from the weak. Always have, always will. You can't change that with a few arrows and a speech."
"Maybe not," Robin said. "But I can change what happens today. And today, you're going to take your boot off that man, hand over everything in that cart, and walk away. Because the only difference between you and me, Oswald, is that I'm honest about what I do."
Twenty of Robin's men rose from the hedgerows, bows drawn and aimed with the steady patience of men who have done this many times before. The soldiers accompanying Oswald counted the arrows, counted their own numbers, and quietly put their hands in the air.
Oswald looked at the arrows. He looked at Robin. He slowly, deliberately, lifted his boot off the farmer's chest.
The cart was emptied, and Robin personally returned every confiscated item to its rightful owner. The farmer's wife wept and tried to kiss Robin's hand. The three children stared at him with enormous eyes, as if a character from a bedtime story had just stepped off the page and into their muddy little yard.
"You have to eat," the farmer said, pressing a loaf of bread into Robin's hands. His face was bruised, his lip was split, but his eyes were bright with a gratitude so deep it was almost painful to look at. "Please. It's all we have, but you've given us so much more."
Robin tried to refuse — the whole point was to help these people, not take from them. But the farmer insisted with a quiet, dignified pride that Robin recognized and respected. Generosity goes both ways. Sometimes accepting a gift is as important as giving one.
He ate the bread on the walk back to camp, breaking it into pieces and sharing it with his men. It was rough bread, made from the cheapest flour, baked in a clay oven in a farmer's yard. But it tasted better than anything Much had ever made, because it had been given with love by a man who had nothing and shared it anyway.
That evening, the Sheriff received three very angry, very empty-handed tax collectors, all demanding hazard pay and threatening to quit. His screams of rage echoed through Nottingham Castle for the better part of an hour and reportedly frightened the pigeons off the roof for three days running.
In Sherwood Forest, the Merry Men feasted on venison and the knowledge that somewhere in the villages of Nottinghamshire, families were sitting down to meals they had been sure they would never eat, in homes they had been sure they would lose. And for the first time in months, children in those villages fell asleep with full stomachs and smiles on their faces.
That, Robin thought, watching the stars appear through the canopy, was worth more than all the golden arrows in the world.


Chapter 13
Robin Hood and Guy of Gisborne
Of all the enemies Robin Hood had made in his years as an outlaw, none was more dangerous than Sir Guy of Gisborne.
Guy was a knight — or at least he called himself one, though he had earned his title not through honor or valor but through a cold, systematic willingness to do whatever needed doing, no matter how brutal. He was the Sheriff's enforcer, the man who handled the jobs that were too dirty, too dangerous, or too morally questionable for ordinary soldiers. While the Sheriff schemed and blustered from behind his castle walls, Guy was the blade that did the actual cutting.
He was tall and lean, with a face like a hatchet and eyes the color of a frozen lake — pale gray, flat, and completely without warmth. He wore a cloak made from a horse's hide, rough and wild-looking, that gave him the appearance of something not entirely human — a beast that had learned to walk on two legs and carry a sword. He spoke rarely and smiled never, and when he entered a room, the temperature seemed to drop.
He was also, Robin had to grudgingly admit, an exceptional fighter. Robin had faced many opponents — Little John, Friar Tuck, the Sheriff's soldiers, even Marian — and he knew what genuine skill looked like. Guy of Gisborne had it in abundance. His swordwork was precise, economical, and utterly lethal. He wasted no motion. He showed no emotion. He fought the way a machine works — relentlessly, efficiently, without passion or hesitation.
The Sheriff had hired him with a single, specific instruction: bring Robin Hood's head. Not capture. Not arrest. The Sheriff was done with trials and dungeons and the possibility of escape. He wanted Robin Hood dead.
Robin learned of Guy's arrival through Marian's intelligence network. She delivered the news on a gray November evening, her face more serious than Robin had ever seen it. "He arrived in Nottingham three days ago," she said, sitting across from Robin at the council fire. "He met with the Sheriff privately. He's been studying maps of the forest. And Robin — he's not like the others. He's not a blunderer. He's not incompetent. The villagers who have seen him are terrified."
"I can handle one knight," Robin said, though a cold knot had formed in his stomach.
"Don't be arrogant." Marian's voice was sharp with worry. "Arrogance is what gets people killed. Guy of Gisborne has hunted outlaws before — in the forests of Yorkshire, in the Welsh marches. He always brings back his quarry. Always. Dead or alive, but usually dead."
"We have a hundred men. We have the advantage of —"
"He's not coming with an army, Robin. He's coming alone. One man in the forest. No soldiers to slow him down, no supply wagons to track. Just him. That's what makes him dangerous. You can't ambush an army that isn't there."
Robin looked at her, at the fear she was trying to hide behind pragmatism, and felt a cold finger trace down his spine. "All right," he said quietly. "We'll be careful. Double the sentries. Triple the patrols. Nobody goes anywhere alone."
For three days, nothing happened. The forest was quiet. Robin's scouts found no tracks, no sign of an intruder, nothing out of place. It was as if Guy of Gisborne had vanished into thin air — or had never entered the forest at all.
Then Little John disappeared.
He went out at dawn to check the perimeter traps, the same routine he followed every morning. He took his quarterstaff, his knife, and nothing else. He told Will Stutely he'd be back by noon.
Noon came and went. So did the afternoon. By sunset, Robin's stomach was a knot of ice. Little John never missed a check-in. Never. In five years of living in Sherwood Forest, through battles and blizzards and everything in between, Little John had never once failed to come back when he said he would.
Robin organized search parties. Every available man fanned out through the forest, calling Little John's name, searching for tracks, checking every hiding place and secret camp. They found nothing. No footprints. No broken branches. No sign of a struggle. No blood. It was as if the biggest man in England had been erased from existence.
Robin barely slept. He paced the camp in the dark, his mind racing through every terrible possibility. Little John was his best friend, his closest companion, the person he trusted above all others. The thought of losing him was like the thought of losing a limb — not just painful but fundamentally disorienting, a removal of something so essential that life without it seemed impossible.
On the third morning, a message appeared, carved into the bark of a tree at the edge of camp. The letters were deep and precise, cut with a very sharp knife by a very steady hand: "Come alone to the Hermit's Oak at dawn. Or your friend dies."
Robin stared at the message. Around him, his men gathered in grim silence.
"It's a trap," Will Stutely said. The words had become so familiar in Robin's life that they had almost lost their meaning.
"Of course it's a trap," Robin replied. "But John's in there."
"Then we all go," said Friar Tuck, his hand already on his broadsword. "Every last one of us."
"No." Robin's voice was iron — hard, cold, and absolute. "He said alone. Guy of Gisborne doesn't bluff. If he sees an army coming through the trees, he'll kill John before we get within arrow range. I know men like him. The threat isn't a negotiating tactic — it's a statement of fact."
"Robin —" Marian began.
"I'm going alone." He looked at each of them in turn — Will, Tuck, Marian, the rest — and his gaze was steady and calm, the gaze of a man who has accepted what he must do. "If I'm not back by midday, you'll know what happened. And then it's up to you."
He left before dawn, walking through the dark forest with only his sword and his bow. The air was cold and still, and the trees stood like dark pillars around him. His footsteps made no sound on the carpet of fallen leaves. Above him, the stars were fading into a sky that was just beginning to lighten at the edges.
The Hermit's Oak was the oldest and largest tree in Sherwood Forest — a massive, ancient oak that had been standing when the Normans came, when the Romans came, when the first people walked these hills. Its trunk was so wide that six men couldn't have encircled it with their arms. Its branches spread out like the arms of a giant, and its roots plunged deep into the earth like the foundations of a cathedral.
Guy of Gisborne was waiting in the clearing beneath the tree. The morning mist hung in the air like curtains of white gauze, and Guy stood motionless within it, his horse-hide cloak over his shoulders and a sword in his hand. He looked exactly like the predator he was — patient, still, and utterly focused.
Behind him, tied to the great oak with thick rope, was Little John. The big man was bruised and bloody — his lip was split, one eye was swollen shut, and there were dark marks on his arms that suggested he had fought hard before being overcome. But he was alive, and when he saw Robin emerge from the mist, his one good eye went wide with a mixture of hope and terror.
"Don't!" Little John shouted, his voice hoarse. "Robin, it's a trap! He's going to —"
"Quiet," Guy said. He didn't raise his voice. He didn't turn around. He simply spoke, and Little John went silent. There was something about Guy's calm — that absolute, inhuman composure — that was more frightening than any amount of shouting or threats.
Guy's pale eyes found Robin's and held them. "Robin Hood," he said. "I've heard so much about you. The great hero. The champion of the poor. The scourge of the Sheriff." He tilted his head slightly, studying Robin with the detached interest of a collector examining a specimen. "You're smaller than I expected."
"And you're uglier," Robin said, drawing his sword. "Shall we get on with it?"
They fought.
It was nothing like the playful quarterstaff fight with Little John on the bridge, or the splashing swordfight with Friar Tuck in the river. This was deadly earnest — blade against blade, skill against skill, with real hatred and real death behind every stroke. The mist swirled around them as they circled and struck, their swords ringing in the silence of the ancient forest.
Guy was fast — faster than anyone Robin had ever faced. His blade moved in short, vicious arcs that gave Robin no time to think, only react. His footwork was immaculate, always balanced, always positioned for the next strike. And his eyes never changed — they stayed flat and cold and calculating, reading Robin's movements the way a hawk reads the wind.
Robin fought with everything he had. Every trick, every technique, every ounce of skill he had built over years of fighting and training. He feinted left and struck right. He changed tempos, going from slow to explosive. He used the trees and the roots and the uneven ground to create openings. But Guy matched him move for move, countering every attack with a defense that was almost mechanical in its precision.
Guy's sword caught Robin's arm — a shallow cut, but it burned like a brand. Then a slice across the ribs that left a line of fire. Then a blow to his sword hand that nearly knocked the weapon from his grip. Robin was losing. He could feel it — the slow, inexorable tide of the fight turning against him, his strength fading, his options narrowing.
Robin stumbled on a root. His back hit a tree. Guy's sword came arcing down in a killing stroke aimed at Robin's head —
And Robin dropped. He simply let his legs go and fell flat to the ground, and Guy's blade bit deep into the tree trunk where Robin's skull had been a fraction of a second earlier. The sword stuck — just for a moment, but a moment was enough.
Robin rolled. He came up behind Guy, inside the man's guard, and drove his sword point into the gap between Guy's shoulder armor and his breastplate. The blade sank in three inches.
Guy gasped — the first sound of pain or surprise he had made during the entire fight. His sword fell from his hand. He sank to one knee, clutching his shoulder, and blood welled between his fingers.
Robin stood over him, breathing hard, bleeding from half a dozen cuts, his sword arm shaking with exhaustion. "It's over, Guy."
Guy looked up at him. For the first time, something moved behind those pale, flat eyes — not fear, but something like curiosity. As if Robin had surprised him. As if Guy hadn't believed it was possible for someone to beat him, and now that it had happened, he wasn't sure what the world looked like anymore.
"Kill me, then," Guy said, his voice flat. "I would, in your place."
Robin looked at the man kneeling before him — this cold, dangerous, pitiless hunter who would have killed him without a moment's hesitation — and lowered his sword.
"No," Robin said. "I'm not you. That's the whole point. That's always been the whole point."
He turned his back on Guy of Gisborne and walked to the ancient oak. He cut Little John's bonds with his dagger, and the big man sagged forward, exhausted and weak, but alive.
"You should have killed him," Little John said quietly, rubbing the raw rope burns on his wrists.
"Maybe," Robin said. "But if I kill a man who's already beaten, then everything I've been telling people — about justice, about mercy, about being better than the people we fight against — becomes a lie. And I won't do that. Not even for Guy of Gisborne."
Behind them, Guy pulled himself to his feet, retrieved his sword with his good hand, and stumbled to his horse. He mounted with difficulty, his wounded shoulder hanging useless at his side. He looked back at Robin one last time — a long, unreadable look — and then rode slowly out of the clearing and into the mist.
He would survive. But he would not return to Sherwood Forest.
Little John looked at his friend — this stubborn, brave, impossibly idealistic man who had just fought the deadliest swordsman in England and then let him go — and felt something deeper than loyalty, deeper even than love. He felt awe.
"You're a good man, Robin Hood," Little John said.
"Don't tell anyone," Robin said, managing a tired grin. "I have a reputation to maintain."
They walked home together through the morning mist, leaning on each other, and the ancient forest was quiet around them — quiet, and safe, and theirs.


Chapter 14
Little John Goes to the Fair
Little John was bored. This was a dangerous state of affairs, because a bored Little John was a Little John looking for trouble, and when a seven-foot man goes looking for trouble, he generally finds it in spectacular fashion.
It was a gray, drizzly Tuesday in late March — the kind of day when the forest was muddy and cheerless, the fire wouldn't stay lit, and everyone was snappish and damp. Robin was off meeting with Marian about intelligence reports. Will Stutely was nursing a cold. Friar Tuck was reading aloud from a book of sermons, which nobody wanted to hear. Even Much's cooking couldn't lift the mood, though his mushroom soup was, as always, miraculous.
"I'm going to the Nottingham Fair," Little John announced to the camp at large, standing up and stretching until his head nearly hit the branch above him.
Robin, who had just returned, looked up from the map he was studying. "The Nottingham Fair? The one happening inside the city where the Sheriff has a hundred soldiers actively looking for us?"
"That's the one."
"The one where there will be wanted posters with our faces on them?"
"I assume so."
"John, you're seven feet tall. You're probably the tallest man in England. What kind of disguise covers that?"
"A very large one." Little John pulled a leather jerkin from his pack, along with a truly horrifying false beard made of horsehair that looked like something a horse had coughed up. "I'll be Dodd of York. Traveling wrestler. Looking for fights."
"This is a terrible idea."
"You crashed the Sheriff's archery tournament. Twice."
"That's different."
"How?"
Robin opened his mouth, closed it, and sighed the sigh of a man who knows hypocrisy when he hears it. "Fine. But be careful. And if you get caught, I'm going to say I told you so."
"You won't need to." Little John grinned. "Because I won't get caught."
The Nottingham Fair was the biggest event of the spring season — three days of markets, entertainment, food, and competition that drew people from across the county. The streets were packed with vendors selling everything from ribbons to roasted nuts, and the air rang with the sounds of music, laughter, and the enthusiastic shouting of people trying to separate other people from their money.
Little John entered the city through the east gate, walking with a pronounced stoop that brought him down to a mere six and a half feet. His false beard, while not convincing up close, was adequate in a crowd. He gave his name as Dodd of York to the bored gate guard, who waved him through without a second glance.
The fair was magnificent. There were jugglers tossing flaming torches, a fire-eater with singed eyebrows and supreme confidence, puppet shows that made children shriek with laughter, and a pie-eating contest that Friar Tuck would have won with one hand behind his back. Little John wandered happily through the crowds, sampling roasted chestnuts and honeyed almonds, watching a troupe of acrobats, and generally having the best day he'd had in months.
Then he came upon the wrestling ring.
It was set up in the center of the fairground — a circle of packed earth, twenty feet across, surrounded by a rope fence and an enthusiastic crowd. In the center stood the Sheriff's champion wrestler, a man called Eric the Red, who had just thrown his fifth consecutive opponent to the ground with bone-jarring force.
Eric was enormous — not as tall as Little John, but twice as wide, with arms like ship's cables, a neck like a tree stump, and a chest that looked like it had been carved from a side of beef. His red hair — which gave him his name — was cropped close to his skull, and his face wore the bored expression of a man who hasn't been challenged in years.
"Who else dares challenge Eric the Red?" bellowed the announcer, a small man with an enormous voice. "The prize is ten gold pieces and a silver belt! Step up, step up! Who among you has the courage?"
Little John's eyes lit up like lanterns. He shoved through the crowd, vaulted the rope fence, and landed in the ring with a thud that made the ground shake. "I'll have a go," he said, remembering to hunch.
Eric the Red looked him over — the false beard, the leather jerkin, the stooped posture that wasn't quite convincing — and smirked. "This string bean? I'll finish him before my ale gets warm."
The crowd laughed. Little John smiled. It was not a reassuring smile, though only Robin would have recognized the danger in it.
The fight lasted exactly twelve seconds.
Eric charged like a bull, head down, arms wide, expecting to barrel into Little John and drive him to the ground through sheer momentum. It was a technique that had worked against every previous opponent, because nobody could stand against three hundred pounds of muscle moving at full speed.
Little John sidestepped. It was a small movement, barely a shift of weight, but it put him exactly where he needed to be — beside Eric, not in front of him. His enormous hand caught Eric's arm as the champion barreled past. He pivoted on one foot, using Eric's own momentum against him, and threw the champion over his shoulder with a hip toss that launched the big man six feet into the air.
Eric landed on his back with a crash that rattled windows in the buildings around the fairground. He lay there, staring at the sky, his mouth opening and closing like a beached fish, trying to figure out what had just happened.
The crowd went absolutely berserk. People screamed, stamped their feet, and threw their hats in the air. Nobody had ever beaten Eric the Red. Nobody had even come close. And this hunched stranger from York had done it in less time than it takes to sneeze.
Little John collected his prize money and was about to slip back into the crowd — mission accomplished, pride satisfied, fun had — when a hand caught his arm. He turned to find the Sheriff of Nottingham himself standing there, flanked by two guards, his small eyes wide with amazement and greed.
"Magnificent!" the Sheriff exclaimed, practically vibrating with excitement. "Simply magnificent! In all my years, I've never seen anyone handle Eric like that. Tell me, good fellow, what is your name?"
Little John's heart hammered, but his face remained calm. "Dodd. Of York. Traveling wrestler, at your service, my lord."
"Well, Dodd of York, how would you like a job? I need men of your talents in my household. I'll pay you twenty gold pieces a month, all the food you can eat, and a comfortable room in the castle." The Sheriff leaned closer. "Between you and me, I have an outlaw problem, and I could use a man who can throw people across a room."
Little John's mouth opened to refuse. Then it closed. And then, slowly, a magnificent, terrible, world-class bad idea formed in his mind. What if he accepted? What if he walked right into the Sheriff's castle, ate the Sheriff's food, slept under the Sheriff's roof — and sent every plan, every secret, every whispered scheme back to Robin through Marian's network?
"I'd be honored, my lord," Little John said, bowing as low as his stooped posture would allow. "Truly honored."
For the next two weeks, Little John lived inside Nottingham Castle as the Sheriff's personal bodyguard. He ate at the Sheriff's table three times a day. He attended the Sheriff's meetings. He stood behind the Sheriff's chair during private conversations with visitors, and he heard every plan, every scheme, every secret that passed through those cold stone halls.
He was perfect for the role. The Sheriff liked having an enormous, intimidating presence at his side — it made visitors nervous, which the Sheriff enjoyed. And "Dodd" was quiet, obedient, and always available, which made the Sheriff trust him more each day.
The intelligence Little John gathered was extraordinary. He learned that the Sheriff was planning a new tax that would specifically target the poorest villages — the ones most likely to support Robin Hood. He learned the exact schedule of every gold shipment entering and leaving Nottingham for the next three months. He learned the names and locations of every spy the Sheriff had planted in the villages around Sherwood Forest. And he learned, most crucially, that the Sheriff had written to Prince John requesting an army — a real army, a thousand men — to finally destroy Robin Hood once and for all.
Every piece of information was smuggled out through Margaret the peddler, who visited the castle kitchens regularly to sell ribbons and thread to the servants. Little John would pass her notes hidden in empty spice jars, and Margaret would carry them to the forest, where Robin read them with growing alarm and growing admiration for his friend's audacity.
Little John's cover was finally blown in the most spectacularly stupid way possible.
The Sheriff's cook was a man of enormous pride and catastrophic skill. He considered himself an artist, and his dishes — which were universally terrible — were his masterworks. Nobody dared complain, because the cook had the Sheriff's ear and a vindictive streak that made the Sheriff look charitable.
On his fourteenth evening in the castle, the cook served Little John a stew that tasted like boiled leather seasoned with regret. Little John had been eating Much the Miller's Son's cooking for five years. He had standards. And after two weeks of suffering in silence through meals that would have made Friar Tuck weep, something inside him finally snapped.
He stood up at the dinner table — slowly rising to his full, un-hunched, absolutely enormous height of seven feet — and declared, in a voice that carried through the entire great hall and probably into the courtyard beyond: "I have eaten better food from a pig trough! My friend Much the Miller's Son could cook circles around this slop with his eyes closed and one hand behind his back! This stew is an insult to stew! This bread is an insult to bread! This entire meal is an insult to the very concept of food!"
The great hall went dead silent. The cook stared. The servants stared. The Sheriff, who had been reaching for his wine, froze with his hand in midair.
The cook attacked. He came flying across the hall with a ladle in one hand and a rolling pin in the other, screaming obscenities in three languages. Little John defended himself with a chair, which shattered on impact. In the commotion — the shouting, the crashing, the cook's creative profanity — Little John's false beard came unstuck and fell to the floor with a soft, damning thud.
The Sheriff stared at the beard on the floor. Then at Little John's face — that familiar, enormous, unmistakable face. Recognition hit him like a physical blow.
"LITTLE JOHN!" the Sheriff screamed, his voice hitting a note that only dogs should have been able to hear. "GUARDS! SEIZE HIM! IT'S LITTLE JOHN!"
"Time to go," Little John said cheerfully, and he went.
He went straight through the kitchen door, pausing only to grab the Sheriff's personal strongbox from the shelf where it sat (he'd been eyeing it for two weeks). He went over the castle wall — a fifteen-foot drop that he landed in a roll. He went through the streets of Nottingham at a full sprint, his enormous legs eating up the distance while guards shouted and stumbled behind him. And he went straight into the darkness of Sherwood Forest, where the trees welcomed him like an old friend.
He arrived at camp barely winded, carrying the strongbox under one arm like a parcel. Robin was sitting by the fire, waiting, because Margaret had sent word an hour ago that Little John's cover was blown.
"How was the fair?" Robin asked, as if Little John had been gone for a pleasant afternoon.
"Wonderful," Little John said. "I brought souvenirs."
He opened the strongbox. It contained fifty gold pieces, the Sheriff's personal seal, and — best of all — a letter in the Sheriff's own handwriting detailing every secret plan for the next three months, including the request to Prince John for a thousand soldiers.
Robin stared at the treasure, then at Little John, then back at the treasure. His mouth fell open. "You absolute marvel," he said. "You magnificent, reckless, brilliant, insane marvel."
"I try," said Little John, and he went to find Much, because after two weeks of the Sheriff's cook, he needed something decent to eat more urgently than he had ever needed anything in his life.


Chapter 15
The Great Forest Chase
The Sheriff had finally had enough of clever plans and cunning traps and tournaments and schemes that always — always — ended with Robin Hood laughing at him. He was done with subtlety. He was going to crush Robin Hood with brute, overwhelming, irresistible force.
He gathered an army. Three hundred of the best soldiers in Nottinghamshire, hand-picked for their discipline and ferocity. Fifty on horseback with lances and heavy swords. Two hundred foot soldiers with crossbows, shields, and the grim determination of men who had been promised triple pay and all the ale they could drink if they captured Robin Hood. And leading them all was the Hound — the best tracker in England, a grim, silent, weather-beaten man who could follow a trail across bare rock, through running water, and over solid stone. He had tracked wolves through blizzards and fugitives across deserts, and he had never, in twenty years, lost a quarry.
The army marched into Sherwood Forest at dawn on a Tuesday, three hundred strong, their armor glinting in the pale morning light, their boots crushing the fallen leaves into mud. The sound of their approach — the rhythmic tramping, the clink of chainmail, the creak of leather — echoed through the trees like distant thunder.
Robin had been warned, of course. Marian's spies had reported the army's assembly days before it marched, and Robin had used every hour to prepare. But three hundred soldiers was a force too large to fight, even for the Merry Men. Robin had a hundred and twenty fighters. They were skilled, they were brave, and they knew the forest — but they were outmatched nearly three to one. A direct battle would be suicide.
"We can't stand and face them," Robin told his captains around a late-night fire, his face lit by the flickering flames, a map of the forest spread on his knees. "Not head-on. They'd overwhelm us in minutes. But we don't have to fight them — we just have to outlast them. This is our forest. We know every tree, every stream, every hidden hollow, every animal trail. They don't. And three hundred men in armor are slow, loud, and hungry. We're going to use every one of those disadvantages."
The plan was simple in concept and fiendishly complex in execution: don't be where the Sheriff expects you to be. Move constantly. Never stay in one place for more than a few hours. Strike from the shadows — not to kill, but to confuse, demoralize, and exhaust. Make the soldiers chase ghosts until they're too tired, too hungry, and too frightened to continue.
"We're going to haunt them," Robin said, a glint in his eye that Little John recognized with a mixture of admiration and resignation. "We're going to make them believe the forest itself is fighting them."
On the first day, the Sheriff's army marched confidently into the heart of Sherwood Forest, following the Hound's lead. The tracker found Robin's main camp within hours — but it was empty. The fires were cold. The tents were struck. The food stores had been carried away. All that remained was a note pinned to an oak tree with one of Robin's green-feathered arrows: "Sorry we missed you! We went for a walk. Beautiful day for it. — R.H."
The Sheriff, riding at the center of his column on a horse that seemed to share its rider's perpetual irritation, turned purple and ordered his men deeper into the woods.
On the second day, the army woke to discover that something had happened during the night. Every horse's saddle had been loosened — not removed, just loosened, so that they looked perfectly normal but would slide sideways the moment a rider mounted. When the cavalry attempted to mount up, fifty soldiers slid off their horses simultaneously, landing in a collective heap of armor, pride, and bad language that took twenty minutes to sort out.
Robin's men, hidden in the trees above, struggled to contain their laughter. Several of them had to stuff their fists in their mouths to keep from being heard.
On the third day, the army's supply wagon vanished. One moment it was there, trundling along at the rear of the column. The next, it was simply gone — along with all their food, their spare arrows, their cooking equipment, and their only map of the forest. They found the wagon that afternoon, on the far side of a ravine so steep and so deep that reaching it would have required ropes, pulleys, and approximately four hours of effort. Another note was pinned to the wagon: "You looked hungry! Help yourselves. Actually, we already did. — R.H."
By the fourth day, the soldiers were exhausted, hungry, hopelessly lost, and very, very nervous. The forest, which had seemed like an ordinary woodland on day one, had become something else entirely. It felt alive around them — watching, waiting, playing games. Arrows whistled out of nowhere, always missing the soldiers by inches — a warning, not an attack. Strange sounds echoed through the trees at night: wolf howls that seemed to come from every direction, owl screams that didn't quite sound like owls, and once, at three in the morning, the unmistakable sound of Alan-a-Dale singing a ballad so rude that several soldiers blushed in the dark.
The Hound tracked Robin's trail through a swamp that turned out to be deeper than it looked, over a cliff that required the soldiers to lower themselves on ropes, across a river where three men lost their boots, through the same swamp again (Robin had doubled back, just to be cruel), and into a dense thicket that turned out to be the home of a large and extremely territorial family of badgers.
The soldiers who followed the Hound into the thicket came out running, covered head to toe in mud, badger scratches, and a deep desire to be somewhere — anywhere — else.
"The outlaws are leading us in circles," the Hound reported to the Sheriff on the fifth morning, his grim face grimmer than usual. "Every trail is a trap. Every path is a dead end or a loop. I've tracked men through the Sahara Desert and the Scottish Highlands, and I have never — never — encountered anything like this. It's like the forest itself is moving around us."
"That's because it is," muttered a soldier, and several of his companions nodded vigorously.
On the sixth day, the Sheriff called a halt. His army was in shambles. Morale had collapsed. Half his soldiers had lost their weapons — dropped in the swamp, stolen in the night, or traded to invisible outlaws for food (three soldiers, too hungry to resist, had accepted sandwiches from a friendly voice in the bushes and only later realized they had traded their swords for lunch). A quarter of the men were nursing injuries from forest-related mishaps. And they hadn't seen a single outlaw the entire time.
Then Robin Hood appeared.
He walked out of the mist alone, unarmed, with his hands raised and a smile on his face. He looked rested. He looked well-fed. He looked, the soldiers thought with bitter resentment, like he had been on vacation while they suffered.
"Good morning, Sheriff!" Robin called. "How's the camping trip? I hope the badgers weren't too unfriendly. They're usually quite pleasant once you get to know them."
The Sheriff's face was a masterwork of barely controlled fury. His eye twitched. His jaw clenched. "Arrest him," he said, his voice trembling. "Arrest. Him. Now."
But his soldiers didn't move. They were looking at the trees — where, one by one, like flowers blooming in the morning sun, Robin's men were appearing. On every branch. Behind every trunk. On every rock and ridge. There were a hundred of them, maybe more, with arrows nocked and bows drawn and very steady hands. They had been there the whole time. The army had been surrounded for days.
Robin's smile didn't waver, but his voice, when he spoke, was firm. "Here's what I suggest, Sheriff. You turn around and take your men home. They're tired. They're hungry. They're no threat to us, and we're no threat to them. This was never their fight — it's yours. And you can't win it. Not here. Not in this forest. This is our home, and we know it the way you know the inside of your castle. You'll never find us. You'll never catch us. And you'll never break us."
The Sheriff looked at his exhausted, demoralized army. He looked at the hundred arrows pointed at his head from every direction. He looked at Robin Hood, standing alone and unarmed in the clearing, as calm and confident as if he were hosting a garden party.
For once in his miserable, grasping life, Roger de Rainault chose wisely. He turned his horse around and marched his men out of the forest.
Robin watched them go — the long, weary column of soldiers trudging back toward Nottingham, their heads down, their weapons drooping — and his smile faded. He knew this wasn't over. The Sheriff would come back. The letter Little John had stolen proved it — a thousand soldiers, an army that would make today's force look like a scouting party. The danger was growing, not shrinking.
"That was too close," Little John said quietly, appearing at Robin's side.
"It was brilliant," Robin said. "But you're right. It was too close. We can't keep doing this forever, John. Eventually, the Sheriff will bring enough men that even the forest can't save us."
"Then what do we do?"
Robin was quiet for a long moment. The mist was lifting, and shafts of sunlight were beginning to break through the canopy, painting the forest floor in gold. Somewhere in the distance, a robin was singing.
"We wait," Robin said. "We hold on. And we hope that when King Richard comes home — and he will come home, John, I believe that with everything I have — he'll set things right."
Little John put his hand on Robin's shoulder. "And if he doesn't?"
"Then we keep fighting anyway. Because that's what we do."
They walked back into the forest together, and the trees closed behind them like a curtain.


Chapter 16
The King Returns
Spring came early that year, and with it came rumors — wonderful, impossible, heart-pounding rumors that swept through England like a fire through dry grass.
King Richard was coming home.
The news arrived in fragments, each one brighter and more certain than the last. A merchant from Dover swore he had seen ships in the English Channel flying the royal standard — the three golden lions of England. A pilgrim claimed to have spoken to a knight from Richard's personal guard at an inn in Canterbury. Letters began to arrive at noble households across the country, sealed with the royal seal, confirming what everyone had hoped for and hardly dared believe.
Richard the Lionheart — warrior king, crusader, the most famous monarch in Christendom — had endured years of war in the Holy Land and months of imprisonment by the Duke of Austria on his way home. A ransom had been paid. He was free. And he was coming to reclaim his throne from his scheming brother, Prince John.
Prince John reacted to the news with predictable panic. He withdrew to London with his most loyal supporters, barricaded himself in the Tower, and began writing frantic letters to every ally he had, promising titles and lands and gold to anyone who would stand with him against his brother. Most of those allies, sensing which way the wind was blowing, politely declined.
The Sheriff of Nottingham, who had built his entire career on Prince John's patronage, found himself in an uncomfortable position. His patron was about to be overthrown. His crimes — the illegal taxes, the corruption, the cruelty — were about to be exposed. And the outlaw he had spent years trying to destroy was about to become a hero in the eyes of the returning king.
The Sheriff locked himself in Nottingham Castle and hoped, with the desperate optimism of a drowning man, that everyone would forget about him.
In Sherwood Forest, Robin Hood allowed himself, for the first time in years, to hope.
"If Richard is truly coming back," he told his men, gathered around the evening fire on a night when the stars seemed brighter than usual, "then everything changes. Richard is a just king — hard, sometimes, and proud, but just. He'll set things right. The illegal taxes will end. The corrupt officials will be removed. The people will be protected, the way they should have been all along."
"And us?" Little John asked the question that hung in the air like smoke. "We're still outlaws, Robin. Even under a just king, we've broken the law. We've stolen. We've fought. We've — well, you know the list."
Robin was quiet for a moment. It was the question he had been wrestling with in the dark hours of the night, the question that had no easy answer. He had killed a man. He had stolen from hundreds. He had defied the law openly and proudly for years, and no amount of good intentions could erase the simple fact that he had broken it.
A just king might pardon him. Or a just king might hang him. There was no way to know.
"We'll face it when it comes," Robin said. "Whatever happens, we face it together. That's always been our way."
The King's army reached Nottingham on a bright April morning that felt, to everyone who witnessed it, like the first day of a new world. Richard rode at the head of his column on a great white charger, and he looked every inch the king that legend said he was — tall, broad-shouldered, with a golden crown on his head and the lion standard snapping in the wind above him. His armor was dented and scarred from a hundred battles in foreign lands, and his face was weathered by desert sun and prison cold, but his eyes were fierce and bright and alive.
Behind him rode five hundred knights, battle-hardened and loyal, their armor gleaming and their lances standing straight as a forest of steel. The thunder of their horses' hooves on the road could be heard for miles.
The people of Nottingham flooded into the streets. They poured from every house, every shop, every alley. They climbed on walls and rooftops for a better view. Women wept. Men fell to their knees. Children were hoisted onto shoulders and told to remember this day, because they would tell their grandchildren about it. After years of Prince John's tyranny — years of crushing taxes and corrupt officials and the constant, grinding fear of saying the wrong thing to the wrong person — the true king had come home, and hope, which had been guttering like a candle in a draft, blazed up into a bonfire.
Richard rode straight to the castle gates. The gates, which the Sheriff had barred, were opened on the King's command — not by the Sheriff's men, but by the castle's own servants, who had been waiting for this moment and who threw the bolts with something approaching glee.
The Sheriff was brought before the King in the great hall — the same hall where he had feasted while his people starved, the same hall where he had plotted against Robin Hood while children went hungry in the streets below. He looked small now, Robin thought. Small and gray and frightened, like a rat caught in a lantern's light.
King Richard sat in the high seat and listened. He listened to the people of Nottingham — one by one, they came forward and told their stories. The widow whose home had been burned. The farmer whose tools had been confiscated. The merchant who had been bankrupted by illegal taxes. The mother whose son had been imprisoned for speaking Robin Hood's name.
The Sheriff tried to defend himself. He babbled about outlaws, about threats to public order, about the difficult decisions required of men in positions of authority. But his words were weak and hollow, and they died in the air like sparks from a dead fire.
Richard silenced him with a single look — a look that carried the weight of years of warfare and the moral authority of a king who had fought for what he believed in, however imperfect his wars had been.
"You were entrusted with the welfare of my people," the King said, and his voice was low and terrible, like thunder on the horizon. "That trust was sacred. And you betrayed it in every way a man can betray a sacred trust. You enriched yourself while they suffered. You punished them for the crime of seeking justice. You turned the law — my law — into a weapon against the very people it was meant to protect."
The Sheriff opened his mouth. Richard raised a hand, and the Sheriff's mouth closed.
"You are relieved of your office. Your lands are forfeit to the Crown. You will spend the remainder of your days reflecting on the harm you have done. And you will thank whatever God you pray to that I am a more merciful man than you deserve."
The Sheriff was led away by the King's guards, his fine clothes suddenly hanging on him like rags on a scarecrow. He did not struggle. He did not protest. He simply walked, head bowed, shoulders hunched, through the great hall and out of the story — not with a dramatic last stand or a defiant speech, but with the quiet, pathetic shuffle of a man who had built his life on cruelty and found, in the end, that it could not support his weight.
Then the King spoke the words that everyone had been waiting for.
"And where is Robin Hood? The outlaw who defied my brother, who stood against corruption, who protected my people when their own government would not? I wish to see him. I wish to see this man."
Robin had been standing in the crowd, dressed in his Lincoln green, with Marian on his right side and Little John on his left. He had entered Nottingham with the throng of citizens, just another face in the crowd, though his heart had been hammering so hard he was surprised it couldn't be heard.
Now, at the King's words, he felt every eye in the hall turn toward him — because the people of Nottingham knew where Robin Hood was. They always knew. And now, one by one, they stepped aside, creating a path from the crowd to the throne — a path made not of stone or carpet but of people, ordinary people, who parted like water to let their champion through.
Robin walked forward. People reached out as he passed — touching his arm, his shoulder, his hand. Small gestures of gratitude and love, wordless acknowledgments of everything he had done and everything he had meant to them. A woman pressed a flower into his hand. An old man nodded, tears on his cheeks. A child whispered, "It's him. It's really him."
Robin walked up the steps to the great hall, through the doors, and into the presence of King Richard the Lionheart. The hall was packed — nobles, soldiers, citizens, all watching in breathless silence.
Robin knelt. The stone floor was cold and hard under his knees.
"Rise," the King said.
Robin rose and met the King's eyes. Richard studied him for a long moment — this young man in his forest green, lean and scarred, with steady eyes and calloused hands and the weight of a thousand adventures on his shoulders. Not a nobleman. Not a knight. Just a man who had made a choice, years ago, to stand for something, and who had never stopped standing.
"You broke the law," the King said.
"Yes, your Majesty."
"You killed the King's deer. You robbed travelers on the King's roads. You defied the authority of my appointed officials and made a mockery of the King's justice."
"Yes, your Majesty. All of it."
"And you fed the hungry. You sheltered the homeless. You stood between the powerful and the helpless. You gave people hope when they had none, and courage when they had forgotten what courage looked like."
Robin said nothing. He couldn't. His throat had closed up, and his eyes were burning.
King Richard rose from his throne and descended the steps. He was half a head taller than Robin and twice as broad, and up close he radiated a physical power that was almost overwhelming. But his eyes, when they met Robin's, were not hard. They were warm, and tired, and deeply, surprisingly kind.
"I have heard your story from a hundred lips," Richard said. "Every village between Dover and Nottingham knows the name of Robin Hood. You gave people hope, Robin of Locksley. In the darkest time, when everything seemed lost, you showed them that one man — one stubborn, reckless, impossibly idealistic man — could make a difference. That is not a small thing. That is, in fact, the greatest thing a man can do."
He drew his sword. The crowd gasped. Robin's hand twitched toward his belt — old instincts — but the King shook his head slightly and smiled.
He laid the flat of the blade on Robin's right shoulder, then his left.
"I pardon you, Robin Hood. You and all your men. Every crime, every offense, every broken law — forgiven, as of this moment. Your lands are restored. Your name is cleared. And you are hereby appointed Lord Protector of Sherwood Forest, charged with the defense of its people, its creatures, and its ancient trees. Serve them well."
Robin sank to his knees again — not because he was expected to, not because protocol demanded it, but because his legs simply gave out. The relief, the joy, the sheer overwhelming weight of everything he had carried for years suddenly lifted from his shoulders, and for a moment he couldn't do anything but kneel on the cold stone floor and breathe.
The great hall erupted. The sound was unlike anything Robin had ever heard — a tidal wave of cheering, clapping, stomping, weeping, and laughing that shook the walls and rattled the windows. Little John's roar could be heard above everything else, a sound of pure, raw, uncontainable joy. Friar Tuck was sobbing openly and hugging anyone within reach. Alan-a-Dale had his lute out and was already composing, tears streaming down his face. Will Stutely was standing on a bench, punching the air. Marian was —
Marian was standing at the foot of the steps, looking up at him, and she was smiling. Not grinning, not laughing, just smiling — a quiet, radiant, profound smile that said everything words could not. She had believed in this moment when Robin hadn't. She had worked for it, fought for it, risked everything for it. And now it was here.
Robin stood up. He walked down the steps. The crowd parted. And he took Marian's hand.
"We did it," he said.
"You did it," she said.
"We," Robin insisted. "All of us. Every single person in this room, and a hundred more in the forest. This was never just my story, Marian. It was theirs. It was ours."
Outside, the bells of Nottingham began to ring — every church, every chapel, every bell tower in the city, ringing together in a symphony of celebration that carried across the fields and into the forest, where the ancient oaks stood tall and patient and enduring, as they always had.
In Sherwood Forest, the Merry Men who had stayed behind to guard the camp heard the bells. They looked at each other. And then they began to cheer — a cheer that rose through the canopy and scattered the birds and echoed off the hills, a sound of freedom and relief and joy that had been years in the making.
Robin Hood's story didn't end that day. There would be new adventures, new challenges, new villains to face and new friends to make. But the darkest chapter was over. The forest was safe. The people were free. And the outlaw who had become a legend had become, at last, what he had always truly been: a good man, in a green hood, standing up for what was right.
Long live Robin Hood. Long live the Merry Men. And long live the greenwood that sheltered them all.
